# Chapter 9

The next afternoon, Rack C shuddered under Ina's palms as she slapped the checksum-compare key twice and watched the right progress bar outrun the left on two machines that were supposed to mirror each other exactly. Heat pooled in the concrete annex under Jamsil; hot dust, instant coffee, and the electrical sourness of cheap power strips sat in her throat while the headset bit into her ear.

The venue crew called the room Broadcast Annex Three. Nobody called it a room. It was a concrete slot behind the loading bay, no windows, one swinging door that never closed all the way, and nine people pretending they could breathe calmly because there was still an hour before Park Ji-eun walked onto the stage.

On the left monitor, the lyric package from Ina's team sat where it belonged: `chorus_patch_v6`, checksum green, timestamped 15:31, signed by the council key they had used since the pilot rewrite in spring. On the right monitor, a package with the same signature and a cleaner upload trail had arrived at 15:42 from a server she did not authorize.

The file name looked harmless, almost polite.

`chorus_patch_v6_final`

Minjae leaned over her shoulder, smelling like wet pavement and nicotine even though he had not smoked all day. He had rubbed his thumb so hard along the knuckle of his index finger that the skin had gone shiny. When he got anxious he counted cables under his breath, not numbers out loud, just color names in order like he was laying out paint: blue, black, yellow, blue.

"That second one piggybacked on house sync," he said. "Not from our relay. Not from agency either. Somebody inside venue ops opened a side tunnel, then pushed with our cert."

"Can we kill the tunnel?"

"Already dead. Doesn't matter. Payload's in the scheduler now."

Across the table, Lee Jisoo from venue tech kept scraping the edge of her molar with a thumbnail until it clicked. She had a tiny silver fish pin clipped to her lanyard, bent at the tail where somebody had stepped on it and she had bent it back. Every five minutes she straightened that pin with two fingers like she could flatten the evening with it.

"I flagged duplicate authorities at fourteen minutes to load," Jisoo said without looking up. "Control told me to ignore and maintain cue sheet. I've got that in text, if anybody suddenly wants records."

Ina could not tell whether that was courage or preemptive self-defense. Probably both.

The song itself had been built to do one thing. The opening verses held safe romance cliches for sponsors and parents. The bridge slipped in their coded narrative: sibling separation, hunger, border rivers that remembered names better than governments did. The chorus was meant to pull thousands of throats into one line about reunion as care, not conquest. They had tested every word for heat maps and trigger phrases, shaved off anything that could be read as artillery weather.

The duplicate package replaced three lines in the bridge and all of the final chorus.

Ina read it once. Then again. She did not need a third pass.

The new lines were a mobilization script dressed up as nostalgia. They called for marching columns, purification, traitor lists. The vocabulary was old war-radio language translated into fan-pop cadence. Whoever wrote it knew both registers and wanted blood.

"That's not drift," she said. "That's an intentional conversion."

Minjae nodded. "Rival faction. Has to be the hardliners Kwon keeps calling enthusiastic volunteers. They finally got their toy."

A runner from stage management burst through the bad door with laminated passes swinging against his chest. He could not have been older than twenty-one, and he wore a rain poncho around his waist like a skirt because he hated carrying bags. He kept flashing a service smile, but every sentence came out on half a breath.

"Fifteen-minute warning for final patch lock. Main bowl's at seventy percent seated. Agency asks if we're clear for lyric mirror to app."

Ina did not answer him. She spoke to Minjae, and to the two clocks that mattered: stage clock and street clock.

"If we hold lock and push manual from local?"

"We can shove ours at T-minus five," Minjae said. "But scheduler will still run both unless we get root on venue core. Root's in Directorate hands tonight, not ours."

"Meaning Kwon."

"Meaning somebody wearing his pass."

Ina took off the headset and wiped sweat from the back of her neck with the hem of her shirt. Her fingers came away with makeup and dust. She looked at Jisoo.

"Who has physical access to venue core?"

Jisoo turned her laptop so they could see the live badge log. "Three active entries. Me. House engineer. Director Kwon's office credential, temporary uplift at 14:08, no sign-out."

The badge log gave her the answer and tucked a trap inside it.

Motives were still murky, but mechanics were not. The 14:08 root uplift belonged to Kwon's office credential, and without that token the contaminated package could have been purged in under a minute.

Ina's phone lit with `Director Kwon` before she touched it.

She put the call on speaker. "We have conflicting chorus payloads in scheduler. One is militarized. We need a halt."

Kwon's voice came smooth and low, as if he were dictating condolences at a funeral where he knew everybody's secrets. "I authorized the 14:08 uplift. Do not purge the second package."

"Then pull your credential from core and let us purge."

"No."

The single word dropped the room dead still; even Jisoo's thumbnail stopped clicking against her tooth.

"If we cancel now," Kwon continued, "forty thousand phones will capture a blackout, and every hostile office will call it intentional sabotage. That brings prosecutors on-site before midnight. You know this sequence."

Ina squeezed the bridge of her nose until she saw a white spark. "This isn't sequence management. This is incitement language."

"Language has always carried risk."

"Not like this."

"You are over-reading an unstable draft under deadline pressure. Keep the current scheduler output on every stadium screen and the app mirror. Ms. Park stays in the set until final bow. I will handle the press bulletin, sponsor counsel, and police briefing after the set."

He said briefing the way accountants said reconciliation after money had already disappeared.

Minjae mouthed, Hang up, and dragged his hand across his throat.

Ina ignored him. "Ji-eun doesn't have these lines. Her in-ear prompts are tied to our version. If the screens run yours, she will break live."

"She is a professional performer. She has weathered worse than a telemetry conflict."

"She's twenty-four and your contract debt has her sleeping three hours a night. Don't tell me what she can weather."

A pause. Kwon did not raise his voice. He never needed volume.

"Ms. Seo," he said, full formal address now, "you accepted that public operations require imperfect control. If you choose unilateral cancellation, you will bear sole legal ownership of resulting disorder under the Armistice communications statute. You have read the statute."

Kwon had wrapped the choice and labeled it for her: cancel and carry the criminal file, run it and carry the bodies.

"I read everything I sign," Ina said.

"Then you understand. Execute the event."

The line clicked dead.

No one spoke for five seconds. The server fans filled the silence with a mosquito whine.

Jisoo was first. "I can produce a fault report right now. It gives us maybe ten minutes of hard stop before somebody above me reverses it."

Minjae shook his head. "Ten minutes doesn't solve scheduler contamination. It just gives them time to install guards at every door and freeze logs."

"So what solves it?" Ina asked.

He pointed to a cramped diagram on the wall where somebody had taped cable routes in fluorescent marker. "Two options. One: I tunnel into app mirror and poison their lyric push with stale cache so audience phones lag. Two: you get Ji-eun to follow your manual cue pack and ignore screens."

"Her in-ear feed can get overridden."

"Yeah."

"Stage manager will panic and force script."

"Yeah."

"We're not in control."

Minjae gave her a look that had no comfort in it, only respect for ugly facts. "We haven't been in control since month one."

The runner was still in the doorway. He had gone pale, but he was listening hard, because listening was how kids in these buildings survived. Ina realized she was about to use him as if he were furniture. She did not.

"What's your name?" she asked.

"Seung-ho," he said.

"Seung-ho, close that door and send everyone not on technical authority out of this corridor. Nobody comes in without Jisoo's clearance. If anybody above you screams, tell them line sync is unstable and you're waiting for structural approval."

He swallowed. "If they ask whose order?"

"Mine. Spell it right when they write the report."

He almost smiled, then did not, then ran.

Minjae opened three terminal windows and started typing fast enough that the keys clacked like rain on tin. He worked with his left shoulder angled forward to protect the screen from anyone behind him, habit from years of doing illegal things in crowded rooms. Blue, black, yellow, he muttered.

Ina walked to the corner where Ji-eun's stage liaison stood with a makeup pouch and a clipboard. Yoon Sera wore a white blazer that had survived a monsoon commute because she kept a travel lint roller in her right pocket and treated every speck like personal insult. She rolled invisible dust off her sleeve now while pretending this was routine.

"You need to brief Ji-eun," Ina said. "There may be lyric drift on main screens. She sticks to cue cards and my hand signals, not visual feed."

Yoon's lips thinned. "She's rehearsed approved copy. We can't improvise tonight."

"If she follows screen feed and it's contaminated, she says words that can get people arrested by midnight."

"If she deviates, she breaches sponsor language and triggers a penalty schedule that could bankrupt her before dawn."

Ina could hear the math cinch: debt, penalties, sponsor clauses tightening around Ji-eun until even breathing sounded billable.

Ina stepped closer. "Her trainee recovery account is already three hundred twenty million won. You're keeping her one invoice away from collapse. Don't talk to me about bankruptcy like it's theoretical weather."

Yoon looked toward the badge scanner, checking who might hear. "I didn't write that contract."

"You enforce it."

"And you," Yoon shot back, quietly vicious, "wrote the script that put a political fuse under her face." She snapped the lint roller shut. "I will relay your note. No promises."

She left before Ina could answer.

By sound, the stadium above them shifted from pre-show hum to expectation. The bass test hit through the concrete floor in slow thumps. Crowd chants rose and dissolved. Somebody in the corridor laughed too loudly and then shut up when nobody joined.

Minjae swore at his screen. "App mirror's dirty too. They're distributing from three shell nodes, probably bought by a fandom bot farm. If I nuke one, two reroute."

"Can you at least delay?"

"Thirty to forty seconds, maybe a minute if routing gods love me for once."

"Take the minute."

He nodded once and kept typing.

Ina stood in front of the rack again and watched the two progress bars reach one hundred percent together. The conflict check was complete, and it was too late to reset.

She hated how calm her own body felt at that exact second, the false calm that came when choice narrowed to one bad lane and another.

She keyed her headset channel to stage left. "Ji-eun, this is Ina. We have possible lyric instability on venue screens. Repeat: possible instability. Stay with rehearsal text. Follow my count, not monitor."

Ji-eun's voice came back bright and professional, the version she used for microphones and men with clipboards. "Copy, unnie. Staying on your count."

Then a second voice came in, private sub-channel only, quiet enough that Ina had to press the cup tighter.

"My left hand won't stop shaking," Ji-eun said. "If it gets bad, give me a cue to spin so nobody sees."

Ina closed her eyes for one breath. "I see it. We will hide it in choreography."

"I hate this dress," Ji-eun said. "It grabs when I inhale."

"Rip it if you need air. Sponsor can sue me."

A tiny laugh in Ina's ear, frayed but real. "That is the first useful legal strategy anyone has offered me today."

Stage manager counted down in all channels, voice already too fast.

"Ten minutes to open."

Outside, thunder rolled somewhere over the river.

---

From the tunnel mouth, the stadium looked like a bowl of wet light. Rain had started and stopped twice, leaving the plastic seats glossy and the air thick enough to chew. Ina stayed in the wing behind a stack of spare LED panels, headset on, cue cards in hand, watching Ji-eun stand at center mark while dancers took their opening poses.

Ji-eun wore black satin with silver stitching along the ribs, military enough to sell the sponsor concept and soft enough to keep fan cams romantic. Her dyed dark hair was pulled high, exposing the line of her neck and the clear tape that held her in-ear pack. Every few counts her jaw locked and she bit the inside of her cheek, then pinched the glove seam with two fingers until the satin creaked. A beat later the camera smile came back, patient as prayer.

The intro track hit. Forty thousand wrists lifted lightsticks in coordinated white.

Verse one ran clean. Ji-eun's public voice came warm, playful at the edges. She flirted with the camera on line breaks, took the ramp, hit marks. Ina counted bars under her breath and fed cues at the exact beat. For ninety seconds, the night behaved.

At bar thirty-two, the main screen behind Ji-eun flashed a lyric card that did not match rehearsal text.

The first replacement line was subtle enough most casual listeners would miss it. River became border. Home became front.

Ji-eun blinked once, almost invisible, and stayed on rehearsal. Good.

Minjae came over comms, clipped and fast. "Hijack active on north and east screens. App lag at twenty-eight seconds. I'm losing the west wall."

"Hold delay," Ina said. "Jisoo, can you cut screen lyric layer entirely?"

Jisoo answered through static. "Denied from core. Manual override rejected with director credential."

Director credential again. Of course.

The bridge began. This was where the coded narrative lived, where people who had buried siblings across borders heard themselves inside pop music for once. Ji-eun took one step forward, inhaled, and delivered Ina's line about a mother keeping two bowls on one table even after one child disappeared.

The crowd answered with a roar that started in the lower bowl and climbed upward in a wave. People cried in public and did not hide it. Ina saw a man in section C embrace a stranger hard enough to knock both of their lightsticks sideways. For a handful of beats, she thought they might still steer this.

Then the second hacked card hit all four main screens at once.

The text strobed in white over red: ADVANCE SO THE BLOOD IS NOT WASTED.

A beat later, audience phones lit with the same line through the event app push.

Ji-eun's in-ear crackled. Someone on production channel shouted, "Read monitor copy, read monitor copy now."

Ina cut across, voice sharp enough to hurt her own throat. "Ignore that. Stay with me. Stay with rehearsal."

Ji-eun looked to stage left. Ina gave the hand cue for spin and reset. Ji-eun spun, landed, and missed a half beat because her heel caught in wet confetti glued to the stage floor. She recovered, but the crack had opened.

In the bowl, singing fragmented. Half the crowd followed Ji-eun's sung line, half chanted the screen text because the screen looked official and authority traveled faster than context.

"What the fuck is this?" Minjae said on comms.

"They're forcing call-and-response," Ina said. "If crowd repeats those lines, cameras clip it and it becomes proof."

The third hacked line replaced the chorus entirely.

It named traitors, not by person but by category: negotiators, journalists, and anyone who spoke caution.

You could hear the flip happen. Catharsis had a sound, like one body exhaling. Aggression had a different sound, sharp edges, separate throats. The bowl changed key in less than a minute.

A section near the south goal started chanting the new lines in rhythm with the kick drum. Another section booed. Plastic bottles arced through humid air. One hit a dancer in the shoulder and burst, spraying sticky soda across black satin.

Ji-eun clipped a consonant, swallowed hard, and the mic slid in her grip for half a beat before she trapped it against her chest. Her smile vanished. She sang anyway, forcing rehearsal lyrics over a crowd now splitting into two songs.

In Ina's headset, voices piled up.

"Police liaison requests message clarification."

"Sponsors are asking whether this is approved political content."

"Agency requests performer return to monitor text immediately."

"No," Ina said to all of it. "No."

She switched channels. "Ji-eun, listen to me. New plan. Skip to coda after next eight. Repeat coda twice, then exit on my count."

"I can't hear the count," Ji-eun said, suddenly bare voice, no media polish left. "Somebody keeps stepping on your channel."

Behind Ina, a production assistant vomited into a trash can and apologized while doing it.

The crowd on the concourse side began to surge toward one gate where a rumor had started that the screens were hacked by northern agents. Rumors in Seoul moved like humidity: invisible, then everywhere. Ina saw it on her secondary monitor, a live social feed clipped from fan phones. One post said Ji-eun was signaling invasion. Another said she was warning against it. Neither post included full lyrics. Both had ten thousand shares.

Minjae shouted over keys. "I can black out app lyrics for fifteen seconds if I burn our mirror credentials. After that we're blind for tomorrow's transport."

He said tomorrow because he planned in boxes. He meant routes. He meant if he burned this door, their next door narrowed.

"Burn them," Ina said.

"Copy."

On stage, pyrotechnic jets fired for the final chorus because the cue system had not gotten the memo that politics had turned into a knife fight. White flame columns rose while the screens screamed text about cleansing and marching and unnamed enemies inside the city.

Ji-eun saw the words. She did not sing them. She stopped singing altogether for two beats, chest heaving, eyes searching the dark wing where Ina stood.

Then she spoke into the mic instead, stumbled on the first syllable, swallowed, and forced the rest through.

"This song was about people finding each other," she said.

A producer somewhere yelled, "Cut her mic."

Her mic stayed live.

"Not this," Ji-eun said. "Not this wording."

The confession was tiny and catastrophic. To one side of the bowl, it sounded like refusal. To the other, it sounded like betrayal.

Someone threw a lightstick at the stage. It spun end over end and bounced off the edge near her boot.

Security moved toward the pit as if motion alone could reverse what a sentence had done.

Ina felt the pressure wave before she saw it: concourse doors opening all at once, people pouring out faster than staff could direct them, the kind of movement that crushed ankles and children first.

A schoolboy in soaked uniform stumbled through the stage-left tunnel with one shoe missing and a scalp split open above his temple. An usher half-carried him by the backpack straps and screamed for a medic who did not answer. Ina dropped her cue cards, caught the boy by the shoulders, and pressed her palm and lanyard against the wound. Warm blood slicked over her fingers and down his eyebrow into his eye. He grabbed her wrist with both hands and gasped, "My sister's still at Gate E."

Ina shoved her emergency badge into the usher's hand. "Take him through camera corridor to the medic station. Don't wait for clearance."

Pepper spray fog rolled into the tunnel behind them, burning Ina's throat and eyes. A woman in a yellow rain hat crashed against the wall, coughing hard enough to retch, with chemical tears striping her face.

The stage manager grabbed Ina's elbow. "We are shutting down performance now."

"Do it."

"Agency says keep her visible for control messaging."

"Do it anyway."

He hesitated one fatal second and looked over her shoulder, waiting for authority with a bigger badge.

Kwon's voice entered her ear on the executive channel, quiet as ever. "Keep Ms. Park on camera and keep the current lyric feed live until Gates E and F clear. If screens cut now, prosecutors will call this coordinated panic engineering."

"People are bleeding while you count camera angles," Ina said.

"If this fractures publicly, the legal response will be severe. You and Ms. Park are already vulnerable."

"You should have thought about that before loading war slogans into a pop set."

"Your voice is unsteady. Follow the emergency script."

Something in Ina snapped cleanly, not loud, not dramatic, just a line finally breaking under known weight.

"No," she said. "I'm done translating your violence into donor-friendly subtitles."

She yanked her headset free, tossed it onto the panel stack, and ran for stage stairs.

A security guard blocked her with one forearm. He was broad-shouldered, older, wearing cheap reading glasses on a cord around his neck because he hated admitting he needed them. "If you don't have a pass, you don't come through."

Ina shoved her pass in his face. "Then authorize me."

By the time she hit center stage, Ji-eun was crouched beside a fallen dancer whose ankle had folded wrong under a sprinting exit. The dancer's makeup had mixed with rain and blood into a pink smear across her cheek. Ji-eun held the mic in one hand and the dancer's wrist in the other, both shaking.

"Off," Ina said. "Now."

Ji-eun stared at her. Close up, her mascara had started to run into the corners of her eyes, and she looked younger than twenty-four for one brutal second.

"What did you put me inside?" she said.

She was not asking what had happened; she was asking what Ina had put her inside.

Ina tasted pepper spray and blood at the back of her throat, opened her mouth to lie, and decided she had no time left for that luxury.

"I wrote the bridge lines that put council code in your mouth," she said. "I signed your cue sheet after legal flagged the sedation clause, and when duplicate authority hit the scheduler I tried to patch from control instead of warning you offstage."

Ji-eun laughed once, a short sound with no humor. "So you wrote the words, locked me into them, and left me under those lights blind."

The house lights slammed to half. Music died mid-beat. Crowd noise surged louder without the track to absorb it.

"We move," Ina said. "Questions in the car."

"I'm not getting in any car with agency tonight."

"Not agency. Me."

Ji-eun looked at her for one heartbeat, then nodded.

They ran.

---

Backstage corridors had their own weather. The air behind the main stage was colder from overworked AC, but smelled like sweat trapped in fabric, hairspray, and panic. People moved in packs that kept breaking apart and reforming around whoever seemed to have answers.

Ina dragged Ji-eun toward Dressing Room B and found three people waiting outside: Yoon Sera with her lint roller clenched like a weapon, a contract lawyer named Bae Jin-woo in plastic shoe covers because he refused to scuff expensive loafers, and a medic with chipped green nail polish holding a silver case.

The silver case hit Ina like déjà vu from too many internal clips: smooth latch, clear syringe cap, compliance sold as care.

Yoon stepped in front of the door. "She's on camera in two minutes. We settle her, run the holding script, then drop her into the live apology slot."

"No," Ina said.

Bae gave her a courtroom smile. "Ms. Seo, you are not principal management. Under exclusive performance rider clause 7(b), agency medical staff may administer supervised anxiolytic treatment for broadcast stabilization."

Ji-eun recoiled at the phrase broadcast stabilization like it smelled rotten. "Say the drug name."

The medic answered because she was the only honest one in the line. "Midazolam injection, low dose. It will keep you from shaking on camera."

Pepper-spray residue still sat in Ina's throat. Hearing "keep you from shaking" made her think of a thousand phones waiting to call that silence professionalism.

"It will keep me from speaking," Ji-eun said.

Yoon's jaw tightened. "It'll keep the feeds from eating you alive."

Ji-eun took one step back until her shoulder hit the cinderblock wall. "My whole twenties have been your damage-control plan."

Ina had spent weeks itemizing harm in careful columns, pretending documentation could blunt impact. Watching Bae flip to pre-highlighted tabs, she felt the bookkeeping lie in her teeth.

Bae opened a folder with tabbed pages already highlighted. Of course he had prepared this before the show, before the so-called unexpected event. "If she refuses directive treatment and script compliance, we initiate breach acceleration. The recovery amount becomes immediately callable."

"How much?" Ji-eun asked, though she knew.

"Three hundred twenty million won plus penalties for sponsor loss."

Bae said the number and the hallway went still. Even the medic's case stopped rattling.

Ina felt her own ledger in her bag, paper corners digging into her hip. Names, dates, harm columns. She had spent weeks pretending numbers could be restitution. Here was another number that was just a weapon.

"You're not sticking a needle in her for optics," Ina said.

Bae did not bother masking contempt now. "And you are not licensed to interfere. Move."

He reached for Ji-eun's elbow. Ji-eun slapped his hand away hard enough to make the folder fall and scatter annotated pages across the wet floor.

On Ina's dead headset hanging around her neck, a channel crackled to life by accident. Kwon's voice, faint through static.

"Contain Park Ji-eun immediately. Do not permit unscripted contact."

Yoon heard it too and went pale.

Ina lifted the headset mic toward her mouth and spoke so all of them could hear.

"Director, we have active crowd injuries and your first concern is sedation language."

"My concern," Kwon said, "is preventing irreversible civic damage. Return Ms. Park to agency care."

"No."

"If you remove her from licensed custody, you expose yourself to kidnapping charges, contract interference claims, and national-security obstruction. Consider your father's housing status before you choose spectacle."

The mention of her father was deliberate. He knew exactly which tendon to press.

Ina did not shout. "You already spent me, Director. You don't get to spend her too."

She tore the headset cord from her collar and let it fall.

For one second nobody moved. Then everything moved at once.

Yoon lunged for Ji-eun's wrist. Ji-eun twisted, slipped on confetti paste, and slammed her hip into a rolling costume rack. Metal screamed against concrete. The medic swore and grabbed the silver case before it burst open. Bae yelled for security.

Minjae appeared from the service stairwell like he had grown out of the wall, breathless, rainwater darkening his shirt from chest to hem.

"North loading ramp's clogged with police," he said. "South janitor lane is open for maybe two minutes. Fire door alarm will buy us noise."

"Do it," Ina said.

He slapped the red break-glass lever at the end of the corridor. Sirens erupted, high and constant, drowning individual orders. Strobe lights kicked on overhead, painting everybody's face alternating red-white-red.

"This way," Minjae barked.

Ji-eun hesitated, looking between Yoon and Ina.

Yoon's grip whitened on the lint roller for the first time. "If you walk out now, they'll label you unstable by dawn and bury you."

Ji-eun dug her nails into her thigh until the muscle jumped, dragged one clean breath through her nose, and answered in her on-camera cadence with the sweetness stripped out. "They've already been doing that. They just called it training."

She went with Ina.

They sprinted through the props cage, ducked under hanging backdrops that smelled like mildew, and pushed through a door marked JANITORIAL, NO ACCESS. The alarm kept screaming. Behind them, footsteps multiplied.

The stairwell was concrete and wet. Ina's shoes slipped on the second flight and she caught herself with her scarred wrist against the wall, skin scraping rough paint. Ji-eun grabbed the back of her shirt to steady her and kept moving.

By the time they hit the service tunnel, Ji-eun was gasping from panic more than exertion. "I can't get enough air in this corset."

"Rip the side seam," Ina said.

"Sponsor will sue."

"Let them queue up."

Ji-eun hooked two fingers under the silver-stitched seam and tore downward. Fabric gave with a sound like paper splitting. She inhaled hard and coughed.

Outside the tunnel mouth, monsoon rain came sideways. Sirens from police and ambulance braided into one long mechanical howl. The loading yard lights threw everything into harsh yellow: puddles, crates, cable reels, two riot vans pulling into the wrong lane because no one had clear command.

Minjae pointed to a gray catering van idling with hazard lights on. "Driver's a cousin of a cousin. He thinks we're moving damaged wardrobe. He doesn't need details."

They almost made it.

At the van door, two private security men stepped out of shadow. One held a tablet with Ji-eun's contract photo open. The other had a plastic rain hood pulled tight around his face and kept wiping rain off his eyelashes in angry little jerks.

"Package is Park. She's returning to agency custody now," tablet man said.

Before anyone else moved, he thumbed his radio and spoke in clipped dispatch Korean. "Haneul Response Two to Stadium Containment Desk: unauthorized extraction at South Yard, target vehicle gray catering van, plate 83-mo-2714. Initiate Ring Two perimeter and camera lock."

Minjae moved half a step in front of Ji-eun. "She's done performing tonight."

"This is not your jurisdiction."

"Nothing about tonight has jurisdiction," Minjae said.

Tablet man reached for his radio. Ina moved first, grabbed the radio strap, and yanked it hard enough to spin him toward her. He cursed. The second guard lunged for Ji-eun and caught only torn satin as she ducked aside.

Minjae drove his shoulder into the second guard's midsection and both of them hit the wet pavement, sliding through a shallow puddle that splashed black water up Ina's legs.

The driver of the catering van stared through the windshield, frozen, hands at ten and two like he was in a driving exam.

"Open the door!" Ina shouted.

He did.

She shoved Ji-eun inside first. Ji-eun scrambled across stacked plastic bins labeled CUTLERY and folded over herself, one fist jammed to her sternum while she counted under her breath.

Minjae slammed the first guard's radio under his heel until it cracked, then hauled himself up, rain streaming off his hair into his eyes.

"Go," he told Ina. "I'll peel off and meet you at point two."

"You're bleeding," Ina said.

He looked down at his forearm where a jagged cut had opened against something metal. "Yeah. So are half the city. Move."

He shoved the van door shut from outside and slapped the panel twice.

The van lurched forward.

Inside, the air smelled like kimchi brine and bleach. Metal trays rattled with every pothole. Ji-eun sat on the floor between crates, rainwater dripping off her chin, mascara gone, one false eyelash stuck to her cheek.

The driver's dispatch tablet chirped and flashed red on the dash: `RING TWO CONTAINMENT ACTIVE - VEHICLE 83-MO-2714 FLAGGED - HOLD FOR POLICE INSPECTION.`

Ina ripped the power cable out and shoved the tablet under a crate, but the damage was already done. Their plate was in the city net, and Minjae's point-two route had burned with it.

For thirty seconds they said nothing, both listening for pursuit at every turn.

Then Ji-eun laughed again, thin and close to crying. "Broadcast stabilization."

Ina slid down the opposite wall onto the ribbed rubber mat, knees up, heart punching at her ribs as if it wanted separate escape.

"I'm sorry," she said.

"For which part?" Ji-eun asked. "The part where my face sold your message, or the part where your message got replaced by a call to beat each other in parking lots?"

"Both."

Ji-eun wiped her nose with the back of her hand. "Did you know before tonight that they could swap text like that?"

"I knew it was possible. I didn't know they had access at core level."

"That's not the same as no."

"No. It's not."

Rain hammered the van roof so hard conversation had to come in bursts. Ina pulled her phone from her pocket. The feed had already split into accusation loops: Ji-eun saying "Not this wording," then fists flying outside Gate E while the chorus blasted from somebody's phone speaker.

Ji-eun watched Ina's face while she scrolled. "Say it."

"They're blaming you," Ina said.

"Of course."

"They're also blaming northern agents, and paid protesters, and stage pyros, and weather. Everyone gets a theory before midnight."

"Does your Director get blamed?"

Ina locked the screen. "Not unless somebody names him with documents."

Ji-eun pressed her forehead to her knees. "I gave you contract pages last month because you said we were building a safer lane."

"I believed that when I said it."

"Belief doesn't pay debt." Ji-eun looked up, eyes sharp despite exhaustion. "Do you know what they do when we're in breach review? They lock phones. They switch us to meal cards with exact calories. They make us recite apology language until our mouths go dry and crack. Then they tell us we should be grateful because at least we still have faces people recognize."

She held out her left hand between them. The tremor had not stopped. "This started when I was nineteen and they made me drink espresso and diet pills before camera test days. They said shaking reads as passion if you smile right."

Ina looked at that hand and thought of the ledger, columns too neat for what they held.

"I can't erase tonight," she said. "But I can keep you out of their room for the next twelve hours. Then we move you south."

"Busan?"

"Maybe. Depends which exits are watched."

"If I vanish, they call me unstable. If I stay, they sedate me and script confession."

"Yes."

Ji-eun gave a dry, exhausted nod. "Then unstable wins."

The van slowed at a flooded underpass and stopped in a Ring Two choke point. Police buses blocked two lanes, floodlights washed the road white, and a riot line funneled cars into single-file inspection.

On the median, a teenage girl lay on an orange spine board with her school blazer bunched at her waist and her left knee folded in the wrong direction. A paramedic knelt over her, both palms on a soaked bandage, shouting for immediate transport.

Two meters away, a boy in the same uniform tried to crawl toward her with his wrists zip-tied behind his back, pepper spray slick on his cheeks. An officer planted a boot between his shoulder blades and shoved him flat.

"She is my sister. Let me sit up," he coughed.

"ID verification first," a lieutenant said without looking at either of them. He waved a camera team closer and pointed at the boy's wrists as if the plastic bite marks mattered more than the blood on the girl's socks.

The paramedic looked up, furious. "If we wait for your queue, she loses the leg."

"Secondary lane after processing."

A baton rapped the van door.

Ina slid the side panel open three inches and held up the catering manifest with her event badge. "Kitchen overflow, Hall C. Fermentation trays and dry goods."

The officer's flashlight swept over her face, paused on Ji-eun's hood, then dropped to the crates. Behind him the girl on the board screamed once when somebody tried to straighten her leg.

His radio cracked with overlapping orders. "Eighty-three MO two-seven-one-four hold and clear. Backlog at Gate F."

He slapped the van twice and waved them forward.

As they rolled past the median, Ina checked her phone again. One new message sat at the top from an unknown encrypted number.

`UNAUTHORIZED EXTRACTION NOTED. RETURN SUBJECT IMMEDIATELY.`

No signature needed.

Then another from Kwon, plain SMS this time, almost polite.

`You are converting a recoverable incident into structural failure.`

Ina typed back before she could rehearse herself.

`You converted a concert into a riot.`

She did not wait for response.

At the next intersection the driver looked back once, eyes wide in the mirror. He had a Tottenham keychain swinging from the ignition and a packet of roasted seaweed clipped above the radio, the kind of small domestic clutter that kept ordinary people sane while they unknowingly helped fugitives.

"Noona," he said to Ina, eyes fixed on the windshield, "where do I drop?"

She gave him Minjae's point-two address in Mapo first, then deleted it when she pictured Ring Two cameras reading their plate. She switched to a warehouse near Seongsu, then changed again to a church parking lot in eastern Songpa because both primary routes were now burned.

"Final location in two turns," she said. "Keep driving. If anyone asks, we're catering overflow."

He nodded too quickly. "I can do that."

Ji-eun leaned her head back against a crate and closed her eyes, but sleep did not come. Her mouth kept moving silently, running lines maybe, or apology scripts, or curses. Ina wanted to tell her it would slow down. It would not.

Blood from the schoolboy's scalp had dried in a dark crescent under Ina's thumbnail. Each time she flexed her hand, the skin on her palm pulled where his fingers had clamped down.

The scream from the median followed them through two intersections.

In the side mirror, Ina caught one last frame before the rain erased it: paramedics finally lifting the girl into an ambulance while officers kept three detainees on their knees in the runoff.

She had known exposure as theory. In the van, with wet shoes and somebody else's catering boxes under her knees, exposure became logistics.

She needed cash routes, quiet beds, burner phones that could survive three hops, and one honest doctor who would not sedate a witness for television.

She needed Hyejin's church network before dawn.

She needed Minjae not to get arrested in the loading yard.

Her phone buzzed with a voice note from him, twenty-three seconds, recorded over wind and sirens.

"I'm clear for now," he said, breathing hard. "Shoulder's fine. Point two is burned. They pushed your plate on Ring Two dispatch. Take Olympic-daero east then cut. Don't hit major stations. They're scanning agency badges and event staff IDs. I'll meet you at fallback three with dry clothes and two burners. And Ina..."

He coughed, spat, continued.

"This wasn't an accident. They wanted visible rupture. Don't let them turn her into the only story."

The message ended.

Ina replayed the last sentence once and put the phone away.

Ji-eun opened her eyes. "Was that him?"

"Yeah."

"Is he okay?"

Ina thought of blood on his arm and rain on pavement and the way he had said only story.

"He's moving," she said. "That's enough for tonight."

The van turned off the main road into a narrow lane behind a closed hardware market. Neon signs buzzed in the rain. Shutter doors rattled in wind. Nobody looked up as they passed because Seoul had learned how to ignore strange vehicles after midnight.

Ina touched the ledger in her bag again, then touched the torn headset cord still in her pocket like a severed vein.

The operation had asked her to translate pain into strategy and strategy into ratings. Tonight the machine had dropped its polite mask in front of forty thousand witnesses, and then tried to sedate the witness with the best camera angle.

She looked at Ji-eun, who sat barefoot now because one heel had snapped in the run, massaging her own shaking hand with small furious circles.

"Listen to me," Ina said. "From this minute, nobody gets to medicate you for narrative cleanliness. Nobody gets to write your mouth without consent. If they want statements, they go through me first."

Ji-eun studied her for a long moment, as if checking for the familiar lie shape.

"That sounds expensive," she said.

"Everything already is."

Ji-eun gave a tired snort that might have been a laugh. "Fine. Then let's make the bill worth it."

The van rolled to a stop in darkness behind the church lot, wipers still working at full speed against rain that had finally started falling for real.

Ina reached for the handle and knew, with the same false-calm clarity she had felt at patch lock, that she had crossed from plausible deniability into open breach.

She opened the door anyway.

They had one stop for dry clothes and burners, then Seoul Station for the 06:03 KTX before dawn.
