# Chapter 17

The security scanner barked at Ina's zipper for the third time, and the guard waved her back through with his palm flat, impatient. Bullhorn feedback ricocheted off Seoul Civic Center until the plaza sounded like three wars layered on top of each other, every speaker at full volume. At eight-thirty, family groups from the missing-person network had brought laminated photos and candles in glass cups they were not allowed to light. Council loyalists had come in matching navy windbreakers with stitched peninsula outlines over the left breast. A third crowd moved between them with no matching clothes and the same hard posture, men and women with bullhorns and perfect slogans who did not seem to know one another's names.

The morning wind cut through the shirt she had ironed at two in the morning. Her burn scar on her left wrist felt dry and papery, the skin tightening when she flexed her hand around the case handle. She had not slept enough for fear to feel dramatic. Fear had become bookkeeping: folder, timestamp, witness list, what she could prove, what would still drown her.

A woman in front of her dropped a box of nicotine patches from her tote, swore, and crouched to gather them while security guards shouted for people to keep moving. She wore a black clerical collar and one sneaker with a split sole taped shut with silver duct tape. She looked up at Ina, shrugged, and said, "I promised my congregation I'd quit by Lent. Today would've been a bad day to start anyway." Ina passed her three patch envelopes and the woman took them with a little embarrassed laugh.

Inside, the hearing room was kept cold enough that breath hung pale over the first two rows. The panel sat on a raised platform that had once hosted zoning appeals and parking disputes; someone had draped a national flag behind it to make collapse feel official. Chief Commissioner Choi Hyun-woo sat in the center with two lozenges lined next to his water glass like spare teeth. His tie had tiny embroidered pine trees, the sort of tie bought by a spouse who still hoped formal occasions might improve a man's mood.

To Choi's left sat Commissioner Yoon So-jin, who had steel-gray hair and a fountain pen she kept uncapping and recapping whenever testimony wandered. To his right sat Commissioner Ryu Min-cheol, broad-faced, former union lawyer, knuckle scar visible when he turned pages. Every chair behind them was full. Journalists stood along the wall with recording rigs balanced on one shoulder. The overhead lights buzzed faintly, a trapped-insect sound that kept climbing in Ina's ears.

The prosecution table had two stacks of binders, one white, one red. Senior prosecutor Im Seong-jun sat between them in a suit too expensive for his fraying cuffs. He rubbed his thumbnail against the edge of a callus on his index finger each time a camera clicked. On his legal pad he had drawn three perfect squares before writing a single note.

Kwon Taesik sat three seats away from Im with his counsel and a folded umbrella across his knees. He had chosen a charcoal suit with no pocket square, no ring, no visible watch. He wore the expression of a man attending an academy lecture he expected to find simplistic. When his lawyer leaned over to whisper, Kwon held up one finger without looking at him, asking for silence and receiving it.

Ina's chair had a cracked vinyl seat. The seam pinched through her skirt. She set the evidence case on the table, unlatched both metal clips, and lined up her documents in the order she had memorized in the taxi: funeral inventory photo from her mother's apartment, phone extraction record, checksum report, cassette digitization chain, ledger fragment scans, copy of Ji-eun's contract rider, seizure report from the Hongje detention flat, mirror-release logs from the night the archive went public.

When the clerk called her name, she stood, swore in, and gave her full legal identification. Her voice came out one register too high on the first sentence. She swallowed and tried again.

Im began with politeness sharpened into a blade.

"For the record, Ms. Seo: you were employed by the organization you now call the Restoration Council."

Employed. He wanted the verb to sound voluntary, maybe even grateful. She kept her jaw loose and chose payroll over apology.

"I was on their payroll, yes."

"You signed a secrecy agreement with criminal penalties, then entered controlled cooperation with the National Intelligence Service. Two institutions, two loyalties."

"Two coercive contracts," Ina said. "Different logos."

Im's pen stopped. "This panel did not ask for slogans."

"I'm not giving slogans. I'm giving sequence."

Choi lifted one hand. "Ms. Seo, answer the prosecutor directly."

"I accepted both agreements," she said, eyes still on Im. "One before detention, one after."

"Then why should this panel treat your archive as anything more than an edited survival narrative?"

The room made a low sound, not quite a groan. Ina saw Choi lift one lozenge and set it down again.

The phrase edited survival narrative stung because it was almost true. If she defended motive, he could drown her in confession theater; if she answered with mechanics, he had to touch the evidence.

She kept her hands flat on the table. "Because survival narratives don't come with hash values, source-device IDs, and independent custody witnesses who hate each other."

Im gave a small smile for the cameras. "Fine. Let us test that claim. Chain of custody, first transfer, no speeches."

She did. She described finding the green lyric notebook in her mother's rice bin, pages wrapped in wax paper that still smelled faintly of sesame and mildew. She described photographing each page that night with a phone she had not yet registered to her own name, then saving raw files to two offline drives. She described the first drive copied in a Seongdong print shop back room with Ji-eun present and the second drive sealed in an envelope by Aunt Hyejin's church office stamp, then hidden inside the hymnbook circulation box as insurance.

Im interrupted before she reached the second paragraph of notes. "No forensic officer observed that first duplication."

"No. At that point I was trying not to get disappeared by the people now asking why I did not file cleanly."

"Strike the editorial. Did any forensic officer observe it?"

"None. Not one."

He nodded as if she had conceded something larger. "Proceed."

She moved through the cassette, the old hiss on the tape, the studio engineer's extraction log, waveform fingerprint comparison against archived broadcast clips of her mother. She described the shadow ledger: printed entries, handwritten corrections, names of civilians harmed after specific drops, family contacts, hospital dates, money transfers routed through church meal accounts when official relief never came.

Im leaned in. "This ledger includes entries you wrote yourself."

"Yes."

"Entries based on your interpretation."

"Some are mine. Most are corroborated by receipts, witness calls, and distribution logs."

"Logs that you accessed while cooperating with NIS."

"Logs that existed before I ever saw an interrogation room."

"But you curated what was released."

Legal language queued up in her throat, precise and evasive. She pictured school photos taped to funeral tables and cut the sentence down to what mattered.

"I redacted minors and active route identifiers so people wouldn't be killed for helping me."

"You redacted strategically."

Ina felt the old reflex kick in, the one that made her over-qualify until language softened into apology. "I redacted with a legal review matrix built from-"

She stopped herself. She could hear how it sounded, how quickly she could drown the room in process.

"I cut names of children," she said. "I kept names of adults who gave orders. That's the strategy."

Commissioner Ryu finally spoke. His voice had Busan gravel in it. "Ms. Seo, answer this for me. Why did you wait until after Mercury Night?"

The air seemed to narrow. Ina could see one of the journalists already framing his headline in the tilt of his phone.

"Because they had me in a windowless flat with lights on for forty-one hours and my father's landlord's eviction photos on a tablet," she said. "Because I gave them one backup server under that pressure. Because the only path I had left was the hymnbook dead-drop, and that took time to confirm. Because if I had posted half a package and it failed, they would've erased the rest and called me unstable."

Im lifted a document. "You are describing coercion by the Council. Yet according to NIS memorandum, you accepted immunity-linked cooperation during the MiraeWave raid case. Was that coercion too, or was that opportunism?"

The NIS liaison at the second table, Director Lee Mi-jung, drew a hard bracket around one paragraph, capped her pen, and angled her microphone stem a few centimeters away from Im. She wore a hearing aid that looped clear around her right ear, nearly invisible until she tilted her head.

Ina turned to Im and let the answer land without softening. "At NIS they put my contract on the table, then my father's medical debt file, then espionage language from the armistice statute. They said choose cooperation terms today or detention without charge while they froze everything. If that's your definition of free choice, keep it."

"Did you benefit financially from either side?"

"I was paid by the Council."

"Monthly stipend."

"Yes."

"And you diverted campaign funds."

Murmurs rose. Choi banged the gavel once, thin wood, sharp crack.

Ina answered before her lawyer could object. "I diverted money to families on the detention list when restitution requests were denied."

Im's eyes flicked up, surprised she had stepped into it. "So you admit embezzlement."

"I admit theft. Call it what you want."

"And you expect this panel to trust your moral framing."

She heard her own pulse in the buzzing lights. She thought of coins counted on Aunt Hyejin's kitchen table, of her father's hospital invoice folded and unfolded until the paper tore at the crease.

"I don't need your trust," she said. "I need the records read in order." She tapped the binder spine with one finger. "If these are fake, prove that with forensic analysis. Don't ask me to be pure first."

Commissioner Yoon asked for the chain exhibits. She read the timestamps silently for almost a minute, uncapping and recapping the pen with quick precise clicks. "Director Lee," she said without looking up, "did your service alter any source material in these exhibits?"

Lee adjusted her hearing aid and spoke into the microphone. Her voice was quiet and made the room lean in. "Our analysts validated file integrity against device extractions available at seizure points. There is no evidence of post-seizure fabrication in the materials submitted here."

Im turned. "No evidence is not proof of authenticity."

Lee did not look at him. "No. It is not. It is, however, the answer to the question I was asked."

Kwon's counsel requested cross-examination and was denied by Choi, who reminded everyone this was an emergency civic inquiry, not trial procedure. Kwon raised his hand anyway.

"Commissioner, with your permission."

Choi hesitated, then nodded.

Kwon stood, buttoned his jacket, and spoke as if he were delivering a lecture to graduate students who had disappointed him by arriving underprepared.

"Ms. Seo is articulate," he said. "Articulation often wins the first hour of a national panic. It does not settle institutional truth. This republic survived war years, currency collapse, and mass dislocation by insisting on process before purge. Exhibit C-12 moved through a church office without sealed forensic intake. Ledger L-3 contains handwritten edits by the principal witness. Mirror release logs were uploaded from devices already in intelligence contact. These are chain fractures, not a mandate to liquidate an institution in one afternoon."

His voice stayed level and almost clinical, but people in the gallery straightened as if the room had dropped another degree.

She asked for permission to respond. Choi gave it.

"Director Kwon just called survival contamination," she said. "Your team held me in Hongje for forty-one hours, seized half that ledger, and left seizure receipts with your signatures. The records we kept before detention still checksum against what was posted. Ji-eun's rider clause was your switch for feed manipulation. Families are in this room because your chain hurt bodies, not because my chain hurt your pride."

Kwon clasped his hands behind his back. "You mistake burden for innocence."

"No," Ina said. "I stopped mistaking your burden for necessity."

By the time lunch recess was called, phones across the room were vibrating nonstop. Reporters pushed into the hall to file updates. Outside, the noise from the plaza had changed pitch. It had lost chant rhythm and become the jagged roar people made when they were about to start throwing things.

In the corridor, Ji-eun waited with her witness badge clipped crooked to her coat. She had dyed her hair back toward black, but blonde still showed at the roots, old bleach refusing to die. Her left hand trembled against her thigh when she was still, so she kept moving, folding and unfolding a gum wrapper into tiny squares.

"How bad was it?" she asked.

Ina gave her the condensed version. "He got the theft admission. He wanted confession theater. He didn't get that."

Ji-eun gave a short laugh. "I can do confession theater. That's literally what my agency trained me for."

"Don't." Ina touched the folder in Ji-eun's hand, the copied rider pages. "Don't perform for them."

Ji-eun squared the folder edges against her palm. Her public smile clicked into place for exactly one second and vanished. "I know. I'm trying not to."

Then the doors at the far end of the corridor banged open and a marshal shouted for all witnesses to stay inside. Through the glass wall, Ina saw a camera tripod go down, then a spray of plastic shields moving forward in a wave.

She did not stay inside.

The plaza air smelled of cold rain, road salt, and pepper spray blooming sharp above it all. Barricades had buckled near the south stairs where loyalists in navy windbreakers had pushed into the families' section. Somebody had set off red smoke; it smeared the air into an open wound and made everyone look feverish.

A man with a megaphone stood on a planter and shouted that traitors were selling military secrets to foreign handlers. He wore polished loafers that kept slipping on wet stone and cursed each time he caught his balance. A woman from the missing-person network swung a candle cup at him, missed, and shattered it against the planter edge. People screamed more from adrenaline than injury, then pushed harder.

Police lines tried to split the mass into lanes and failed. Shields punched forward. Protesters shoved back with signs and elbows. Someone threw a battery pack. It hit a helmet and bounced into a puddle.

Ina kept her head down and moved toward the side gate where witness vans had parked. She lost sight of Ji-eun within ten steps. Her phone buzzed with three messages at once: stay inside, where are you, panel reconvenes in fifteen.

Near the bus stop she saw a girl in school uniform pinned against a railing, one shoe off, white sock black with mud. The sign in her hand had her brother's photo taped crooked, the corners peeling in the rain. She could not have been older than sixteen. She had a cartoon bandage printed with oranges around her thumb and was crying with furious concentration, trying to tie her hair back while a man in a navy windbreaker grabbed at her sign and shouted in her face.

"Go home," he yelled. "Children don't belong in state matters."

"My brother was seventeen when they took him," the girl yelled back. "Shut up."

The man raised his arm again, not a punch yet, but the shape of one.

Minjae came from the edge of the crowd fast and low, moving on pre-measured angles. He wore a soaked black hoodie under a borrowed police-issue rain shell with no insignia, and there was fresh tape on two fingers of his right hand where old skin had split. He shoved the man sideways with his forearm, pulled the girl behind him, and took the first baton strike across his shoulder.

"Move," he snapped at the girl. "Left side, now. Don't argue."

The girl stumbled, found her shoe with one foot, and bolted toward the families' medic tent clutching the sign to her chest.

Two officers grabbed Minjae from behind. One of them was young enough that his acne showed through the anti-fog smear on his visor. The other had an inhaler clipped to his vest and shouted through gasps as he twisted Minjae's arm higher than it needed to go.

"Assaulting an officer," the gasping one said. "Hands where I can see-"

"They're already on me," Minjae said, voice flat with pain. "You can stop pretending this is confusion."

He caught sight of Ina over the officer's shoulder. Rain ran from his hair into his eyes and he blinked hard, annoyed by it more than afraid.

"Ina," he shouted. "Get back in there. Don't waste this on me."

She stepped forward. A shield slammed between them, polycarbonate clouded with scratches. Somebody behind her shoved hard and she hit the shield edge with her hip. Pain flashed bright enough to make her vision white at the corners.

A fiberglass sign pole whipped out of the smoke and clipped the young officer's visor with a crack. He dropped to one knee, and the opening made three people lunge at once. A mounted horse cut across that gap so close Ina felt wet hide scrape her sleeve; its shoulder knocked a man into a barricade, and he screamed, clutching his wrist at a wrong angle.

From a command van, a riot captain barked through a loudspeaker, "Blue line, wedge left. Open a medical lane now." Shield teams obeyed, dragging two collapsed barricades into a diagonal funnel while mounted officers drove straight through the center.

The crowd's front ranks buckled under that move. Families with photo signs were pushed through the new lane toward the medic tent, and loyalists were peeled toward the curb. People retreated by meters, then by feet. The loudest agitators kept shouting, but the shape had changed from attack to stalemate. Sirens layered over chants. Drone cameras buzzed above the smoke.

Ina got to the side door with her coat torn at one pocket and red residue on her sleeve that might have been smoke dye or somebody else's blood. Inside the lobby, heat hit her face and made her eyes water harder than the pepper spray had. A clerk handed her a paper cup of water with shaking hands.

"You are Ms. Seo, right?" he asked.

He wore a mint-green tie with cartoon whales and had ink on his thumb from stamping witness badges too fast. His badge read KIM DAE-HO, Temporary Civil Service. He looked twenty-two and profoundly offended by how adulthood had introduced itself.

"Yes," Ina said.

"They want you in Chamber B. Ministry representatives are pushing a settlement discussion before the afternoon session."

"Where's Park Ji-eun?"

"Secure room with two marshals. She is furious."

"And Kang Minjae?"

Kim hesitated. "Detained at the south post. I heard they're booking him for obstruction and assault."

She closed her eyes once. Then she opened them. "Thank you."

Chamber B had no windows and reeked of reheated fish from somebody's lunchbox. Three ministry officials sat on one side of a long table with polished nameplates and tired eyes. The lead negotiator, Vice Minister Nam Gyu-tae, had a voice of soft sandpaper and a nervous habit of peeling labels off water bottles in one perfect spiral.

"Ms. Seo," Nam said, gesturing at the chair. "Sit. We can convert this into an administrative correction and keep your face off tonight's arrest crawl."

Ina stayed standing.

Nam slid a two-page form across the table. "Emergency closed-session compliance memo. You hand over unredacted source drives before reconvening, pause new mirror uploads for seventy-two hours, and permit in-camera authentication. I recommend supervised home release while review runs."

"Define supervised."

"No booking desk, no holding cell. You sleep at home. Counsel stays with you for follow-up interviews."

"And the mirrors go dark."

"They pause under preservation language," Nam said. "We call that restraint so nobody panics."

"Who writes the stay?"

"Ministry legal bureau drafts. Duty judge signs."

She read the bold line above the signature block: REFUSAL WILL VOID DEFERMENT OFFER AND TRIGGER IMMEDIATE REFERRAL FOR CHARGING REVIEW.

A younger official beside Nam, tie askew, leaned in and spoke to her, not the room. "Decline and I file the charging referral at 13:42 with detention recommendation attached. Marshal serves you before you reach the hallway."

Ina set the form down. "File it."

Nam's expression barely moved. He initialed the bottom himself and stamped the page with the red seal beside his phone: DEFERMENT WITHDRAWN - REFERRED 13:42. He passed the packet to the marshal at the door.

The marshal tore off the carbon copy and handed it to Ina. "Service acknowledged," he said. "Please keep this for your counsel."

The bruise over her hip throbbed where the shield had caught her. She tucked the stamped copy into her evidence case.

"The panel reconvenes in six minutes," Nam said. "You've made your position clear."

When Ina entered the hearing room again, Ji-eun was already in the witness seat. Two microphones pointed straight at her. She sat with her ankles crossed exactly as camera training had taught her, but each inhale hitched before she forced it smooth. She smoothed the tablecloth twice, lowered her shoulders, and held still as if stillness could pass for control.

Ina wanted to interrupt before Im got momentum. If she stepped in now, he would tag every answer as coached.

She gripped the edge of her chair and stayed silent.

Im started gentle. "Ms. Park, you're under significant professional strain at the moment, yes?"

"You could say that."

"Your recent projects were canceled."

"Most of them."

"So you have motive to blame institutions for personal career decline."

Ina felt him setting bait one narrow question at a time, trying to make rent terror sound like personal ambition.

Ji-eun smiled, bright and empty. "My career declined when your office leaked my private medical records to gossip channels. Are we doing motive or facts?"

A ripple went through the room. Im's jaw tightened.

Ina felt the gallery tilt toward spectacle. He wanted career envy on record so he could mop blood off the contract with one clean motive line.

"Let's do facts," he said. "Exhibit J-4, contract rider. Is that your signature?"

"Yes."

"Read clause twelve, line three."

Ji-eun read without stumbling. "'Artist shall comply with emergency performance directives, including script substitution, visual feed adjustment, and post-event wellness management as determined by Agency and associated public-interest partners.'"

"Public-interest partners meaning?"

"At the time, I was told it meant sponsors. Later I learned it meant people like Director Kwon's team and whoever they coordinated with in government."

Ina pressed her thumbnail into the binder edge until it hurt. If Ji-eun softened this part, Im would file it under rumor and move on.

"Speculation."

"No. I've got messages and call logs tied to that clause."

Im moved to another page. "Clause fifteen specifies debt recovery in breach. Amount?"

"Three hundred twenty million won."

Ina heard the number hit the room like dropped metal. Too many adults still heard that kind of debt and called it consent.

"You were aware of this when you signed."

"I was nineteen and my mother had signed too because our landlord was threatening to lock us out. I was aware the number had a lot of zeros."

Ina bit the inside of her cheek. Three hundred twenty million had always been where adults pretended coercion was just arithmetic.

A few people laughed and then stopped when Ji-eun kept staring at Im without smiling.

"Ms. Park," Commissioner Yoon asked, "what do you mean by post-event wellness management?"

The bruise over Ina's hip throbbed under her skirt. She caught herself preparing to nod Ji-eun toward safe euphemisms and killed the impulse.

She lowered her eyes to her notes and kept both hands visible on the table.

Ji-eun's public tone flickered. She dragged one thumbnail along the microphone seam until it squeaked, then let the brightness drop out of her voice.

"It means if you panic on camera they can sedate you and call it care. After Jamsil, agency counsel told me I had thirty minutes to accept an injection and read an apology written for me." Her jaw jumped once before she forced it still. "I did not take the injection because Ms. Seo got me out."

Im objected to narrative embellishment. Choi overruled him.

"Did Ms. Seo instruct you to make false statements today?" Im asked.

Ina kept her face blank and counted breaths, willing herself not to rescue the answer.

"No."

"Did she ask you to support her archive release?"

"She asked me to tell the truth and decide for myself whether I could survive it."

Ina braced for the graceful retreat agencies always wanted, the neat apology that converted coercion into misunderstanding.

"And can you?"

Ji-eun let the practiced smile return for half a breath, then dropped it. "Apparently not. My agency terminated me this morning for reputational harm."

Phones lit up across the gallery at once. A reporter whispered, "Confirmation push just hit." Another cursed and started typing with both thumbs.

Ina's own phone vibrated once against her notebook. She shoved it deeper under the paper stack and kept looking at Ji-eun.

"Who notified you?" Commissioner Ryu asked.

Ji-eun reached into her coat and held up her phone. The case had peeling stickers of old girl-group logos she had not bothered to scrape off. "My manager sent a heart emoji and a PDF. Very modern execution."

Even Choi's mouth twitched before he caught it.

"Submit the notice as exhibit," Yoon said.

Kwon requested permission to address the panel again. Choi denied it this time.

Public comments opened for fifteen minutes under strict limits and instantly ignored those limits. A former MiraeWave editor listed altered timestamps from memory, then started crying when she reached the names of two interns who vanished after raising flags.

A mother in a green parka walked to the microphone carrying a clear produce bag. Inside sat one school sneaker, white canvas gone brown at the toe, and a hospital wristband with her son's name half-smeared by disinfectant.
"He wore this on Mercury night," she said, lifting the bag toward the panel. "They returned one shoe and called it transfer."
"The emergency room took baton plastic out of his lip and logged minor abrasions while two men with ministry IDs stood behind the nurse and watched her type."
"You don't get to call this process while I still pick glass out of my kid's scalp."

Half the gallery stood to clap and the other half shouted her down as a liar. Choi hit the gavel twice. Yoon told the clerk to mark the bag for provisional exhibit intake. A veterans' association representative grabbed the next mic and yelled that dissolving the Council would hand propaganda lanes to hostile actors.

By six in the evening, winter light had gone from the high windows and the hall lights painted everybody sallow. The panel withdrew for deliberation while people stayed seated as if standing might break the thin thread holding the day together.

Ina found a corner near the vending machines. Her hip ached every time she shifted weight. Ji-eun sat beside her and opened a canned coffee with shaking fingers.

"I should've worn waterproof mascara," Ji-eun said. "If I'm going to get professionally erased, I could at least look expensive while it happens."

Ina snorted despite herself. "You looked terrifying."

"Good." Ji-eun took a drink and winced at the sweetness. "Did Minjae text?"

Ina checked. Nothing from him. One message from an unknown number: SUBJECT RELEASE PENDING CONDITIONS. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS TO FOLLOW.

Her stomach dropped. She showed Ji-eun, who swore softly.

"Pending conditions means tracker," Ji-eun said. "Or daily check-ins. Or both."

"I know."

"Are you going to fold if they indict you?"

Ina thought of Chamber B, of Nam's spiral of peeled plastic label, of how quickly compromise language turned into erasure.

"No," she said. "I already folded once in the flat. I'm done with that version of me."

At seven-twelve, the panel returned.

Choi read from a printed ruling with both hands on the page, voice steady and almost gentle. "This emergency civic panel finds credible evidence that the Restoration Council operated outside lawful mandate, used coercive contractual instruments against civilian participants, and coordinated manipulative broadcast interventions that contributed to public harm."

A sound moved through the room, dry and brittle as reeds rubbing in winter.

"Accordingly," Choi continued, "effective immediately: one, the Restoration Council is dissolved as a public-facing institution and its assets are frozen pending forensic audit. Two, an independent truth commission is authorized under emergency statute with subpoena power over participating agencies and contractors. Three, the panel refers associated criminal matters to the Prosecutor General's office, including allegations of coercion, unlawful surveillance, and operational negligence leading to loss of life."

Each clause should have felt like relief. Ina felt only the math of what would be traded to make those clauses stick.

Kwon did not flinch. He wrote something on his notepad and slid it to his lawyer. The lawyer read it and went pale.

Choi added, "This panel also recognizes that evidentiary releases in this matter may implicate secrecy and national-security statutes. Charging decisions remain with prosecutorial authorities and are not precluded by this ruling."

The words hit Ina like a second verdict folded inside the first. She could feel the wire narrowing around her throat in real time.

There it was, the hinge. The room felt it before the next speaker stood.

Senior prosecutor Im rose with a different folder, thinner and more dangerous.

"Pursuant to armistice-era secrecy law and unauthorized classified disclosure provisions," he said, "the office has filed an indictment against Seo Ina for unlawful dissemination of restricted state material and facilitation of prohibited cross-border communication channels."

Ina kept her face neutral for the cameras and tasted metal where she had bitten her tongue in the plaza.

There was no gasp this time, only a collective exhale, as if everyone had spent the last hour holding breath for exactly this line.

"Given her voluntary appearance and current public profile," Im continued, "the office does not seek immediate detention. Conditions include immediate surrender of passport, travel restricted to Seoul metropolitan jurisdiction without prior approval, and weekly reporting."

Before he sat down, a court marshal in a navy vest crossed to Ina with a yellow release packet and a portable scanner.

"Ms. Seo, travel document."

She pulled her passport from the evidence case. The marshal scanned its barcode, sealed it in a tamper envelope, then pressed her right thumb to an inkless pad and rolled the print onto a line that read REPORT EVERY MONDAY 09:00 / NO FOREIGN TRAVEL.

Her lawyer leaned close and whispered options, timelines, motions. Ina heard only half of it over the scratch of the marshal's pen as he signed receipt of custody.

Across the aisle, Ji-eun's phone started vibrating hard against the table. She answered and accidentally hit speaker.

"All active bookings are canceled effective now," her manager said, voice tinny through the microphone. "Finance is exercising setoff on your appearance escrow, and legal will proceed on debt recovery."

An alert stacked over the call: KRW 9,800,000 DEBIT - HANEUL RECOVERY HOLD.

"Blacklisted before dinner," Ji-eun said softly. "Efficient country."

"I'm sorry," Ina said.

Ji-eun shook her head. "Do not apologize for me telling the truth. Apologize if you disappear."

It was almost formal, that one uncontracted sentence, and it hit harder because of it.

Outside, the plaza had thinned to knots of stubborn people and press lights. Rain had stopped, leaving the pavement slick and black. Police buses idled at the curb with their heaters blasting, windows fogged by bodies in wet riot gear.

Minjae stood by the south gate under a floodlight with two plainclothes officers behind him pretending to check their phones. He had a fresh split at the corner of his mouth and a gray monitoring band strapped above his ankle where his pant leg rode up. He held two vending-machine coffees in one hand, a peace offering he did not believe in.

Ina crossed to him before the officers could object. He handed her one can. His knuckles were swollen.

"You're bleeding," she said.

"Mostly cosmetic. Makes me look dramatic." He glanced at the officers. "They gave me a choice: obstruction charge tonight or supervision release with tracker and daily report. I chose being useful."

"Useful to who?"

"Depends on the hour." He took a careful sip and grimaced. "You heard about the indictment?"

"Yes."

"You all right?"

She looked past him at the civic center facade, lit stone and reflective glass, all that institutional confidence built to survive scandal by converting it into paperwork.

"No," she said. "But the Council is gone."

"Gone on paper," Minjae said. "People are harder to dissolve."

"I know."

He shifted his weight and winced when his bruised shoulder caught. "Kid you saw out there? The one with the brother sign? She found me before they loaded me up. Said she had physics exam prep at six tomorrow and asked if she should still go."

Ina almost laughed, almost cried. "What did you tell her?"

"I told her yes. Bring an ice pack for your hand and go take your exam. Revolution can wait two hours."

One of the plainclothes officers stepped closer, impatient. Minjae held up a finger without looking at him.

"I can't stay long," he said to Ina. "They're logging every move. But I can still route messages through legal channels and community servers. Slower, cleaner."

"We might need slower," she said.

"You always hated slow."

"I learned."

He looked at her for a long second, searching for the version of her that had once trusted process more than people, maybe relieved not to find her.

"Weekly reporting starts tomorrow for me," he said. "For you too, sounds like."

"Lucky us."

"Call if they try to pull a surprise remand. I've got two lawyers who still answer because I fixed their cousin's heating system for free."

The ghost of a smile touched his mouth and vanished when he tasted blood from the split lip.

From the steps, Ji-eun shouted Ina's name. She had her coat collar up and her witness folder tucked under one arm, moving fast despite exhaustion. Mascara had dried in two salt tracks under her eyes, and the TV smile was gone.

"My manager just called again," she said when she reached them. "He offered to keep one ad contract alive if I post a statement saying I was emotionally unstable in testimony."

Minjae gave a humorless bark. "Classy."

"I told him he could sue me in public and practice saying the debt number in front of cameras until he got bored." Ji-eun looked at Ina. "If they freeze my account before rent, I might need your aunt's church floor again."

"You have it," Ina said. "No question."

A siren wailed down the avenue and faded. The two plainclothes officers finally approached for real.

"Mr. Kang," one said, "time."

He nodded, crushed the empty coffee can flat in his palm, and handed it to the officer instead of dropping it on the ground.

"See you at the next hearing," he said to Ina.

"Next hearing," she echoed.

He gave Ji-eun a short nod, then walked toward the waiting car with the slight uneven gait of someone pretending his shoulder did not hurt.

Ina and Ji-eun stood under the floodlight until the car pulled away. The plaza smelled of wet cardboard, extinguished smoke, and spilled instant coffee. Workers in orange vests were already gathering broken signs into trash bins, reducing the day to manageable pieces.

Ina opened her phone and read the new legal notice from the prosecution office line by line: case number, reporting desk, Monday at nine, no departure from Seoul metro without written permission. It looked absurdly clean, black text on white screen, no trace of red smoke or batons or the girl's muddy sock or Ji-eun's shaking hand pressed flat to hide itself.

When she tried to book a taxi to the hospital, the payment failed. A second notice explained why: PROSECUTORIAL PRESERVATION HOLD APPLIED. DAILY WITHDRAWAL CAP KRW 300,000. The app offered two buttons, ACCEPT or FILE OBJECTION.

She hit ACCEPT because her father still needed tomorrow's rehab deposit. Then she saved the legal notice to her archive folder, created a new subfolder labeled TRIAL, date-stamped it, and added a blank document titled CHAIN CONTINUES.
Before locking her phone, she set a predawn reminder: Seongdong print shop, scanner glass, donor calls.

The hearing had given her what she had asked for and what she had feared: institutional consequence and personal exposure in the same breath. The Council had been dissolved. The truth commission would begin. Kwon would not vanish into history cleanly.

Neither would she.
