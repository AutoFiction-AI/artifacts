# Chapter 4

Rain had soaked through Ina's blouse cuff by the time she reached MiraeWave's Building C, and the old burn scar on her left wrist tugged tight in the damp. Reheated coffee and monitor ozone clung to the hallway while the Han pushed its sweet-rot breath upriver each time the doors opened. She wiped rain from her glasses with her hem, saw the smear she made, and wiped again before she looked up at the production board. It already looked like a threat: 8:11 now, first story conference in nineteen minutes, second at ten with advertisers, noon for Standards, two for Director Kwon, four for Haneul Entertainment with Ji-eun's manager. Final script lock still had to happen by midnight if everyone lied about how long things took. She had read the same rundown in her kitchen at dawn, then walked into rain hard enough to pull fryer oil and wet cardboard into the curb.

The writers' room looked like a school classroom designed by people who hated windows. The walls were paper-thin, and marker fumes hung in the heat from a fan that turned without moving any air. Three whiteboards divided into columns: ROMANCE ARC, SPONSOR TOUCHPOINTS, SENSITIVE CONTENT. Under SENSITIVE CONTENT, somebody had written in red, all caps: NO FLAGS. NO BORDER MAPS. NO SPEECHES.

Executive producer Nam So-young was already there, barefoot under the table, shoes lined up neatly beside her chair because her left heel blistered if she sat in pumps for more than an hour. She rolled a dried persimmon between her fingers like a prayer bead, then bit it in half without looking up from the rating sheet.

"You're late."

"I'm two minutes early," Ina said, hanging her wet tote on the back of her chair.

"That counts as late in post-election quarter."

Nam slid a stack of paper toward her: last night's comparative ratings, ad buy forecasts, and a memo from the network's legal team with three pages highlighted in yellow. Every page used the same words: risk of polarization, advertiser flight, reputational volatility.

Head writer Lee Sang-ho leaned back and cracked his knuckles one at a time in escalating rhythm. It was how he thought. It was also how he warned the room he was about to pick a fight.

"We have a love story," he said. "A clean one. Two leads from opposite classes, one rooftop bar, one old neighborhood market, one misunderstanding, two kisses before episode four. We know how to sell that. We don't need national healing hidden inside the ramen placement."

"Nobody said national healing," Ina said.

Nam snorted. "Your last treatment did."

"It said family fracture."

"It said inherited fracture in a divided land. That is national healing with a fake mustache."

PPL coordinator Woo Hye-rin clicked her mechanical pencil nonstop while she talked, six clicks for each sentence, the rhythm of rounds loading into a magazine.

"Sponsors approved romance. They approved skincare close-ups, convenience-store dates, and a ride-share app integration in episode two. They did not approve ideological puzzles that get clipped into YouTube conspiracy channels before midnight."

She tapped the whiteboard with the pencil eraser. "Keep the line simple: they meet, they want each other, life gets in the way, they kiss anyway."

Ina sat. Her laptop fan started whirring before she opened a file. She had slept three hours and dreamed in subtitle timing bars: line in, line out, character count max forty-two. The green lyric notebook from her mother's apartment was in her tote under a legal pad. She had not intended to bring it. She had grabbed the wrong stack in the dark. Now she felt its hard corners through canvas every time she shifted her foot.

Lee tossed a scene card in front of her. "Episode one midpoint. Give me a cute misdirect at the river food stalls."

"I can give you that," Ina said. "But we need a second engine. If this is only flirtation, it dies after week two."

Nam folded her arms. "A second engine means conflict. Conflict means politics. Politics means angry uncle groups boycotting skincare serum. We're not doing that."

Ina looked at the whiteboard until the marker strokes blurred. She thought of the private rule she had written at two in the morning in a notes app she never synced: Do not let a story cover harm you would not name out loud. She had written it to sound legal, objective; she wanted grammar to brace it once money started leaning.

"Then we make the conflict domestic," she said. "Not policy. Not slogans. A sibling split. Female lead has spent twelve years looking for her younger brother after he's sent away by relatives during a debt collapse. She tells no one because shame. Male lead thinks she's avoidant. She's not. She's carrying a ghost who's still alive somewhere."

Lee stopped cracking his knuckles.

Woo clicked her pencil twice, then forgot to click again.

Nam chewed slowly, eyes narrowed. "Where was he sent away to?"

"To another city first. After that, no stable contact. The details stay ordinary: bad paperwork, predatory guardianship, a family that treats children like collateral. We don't say state, we don't say ideology, we don't say anything censors can underline in red."

Lee leaned forward. "That is melodrama."

"Romance is melodrama with better lighting," Ina said. "I'm giving you spine."

Nam put her persimmon down and reached for her shoes without putting them on. "How does this sell lipstick."

"The female lead has a private ritual," Ina said. "Every episode she hums a lullaby her brother hated when he was seven because he said it sounded old. Later we learn he still remembers it as an adult. The tune threads episodes without speeches. Viewers catch it even if they can't name why they're crying."

Woo grimaced. "Songs are rights issues."

"We commission four bars original," Ina said. "No lyric registration conflict."

Lee shook his head. "A humming motif can feel manipulative."

"So can slow-motion kissing in rain, and we still do it."

Nam laughed once, sharp. "She's right about that."

The room loosened by two degrees. It was not enough to relax, but it was enough to continue.

By ten-fifteen, the whiteboard held two stacks of cards instead of one. Romance beats on the left: convenience store, rooftop, mother-in-law fake-out, first date disaster. Sibling thread on the right: missing records office, lullaby recall, old photo with half a face burned off, anonymous message from a number saved as No Name 2.

At ten-thirty-six, the advertiser call started on speakerphone. Seven voices introduced themselves with perfect politeness and immediate demands.

"No unresolved social trauma in product scenes."

"Don't pair debt dialogue with brand logos."

"No crying in the dermal patch bathroom sequence."

"We need aspirational glow."

Nam managed the call with her producer smile voice, the one she used with men who held more budget than imagination. "Absolutely. We're keeping emotional stakes intimate and universal."

The word universal made Ina flinch. She wrote it on her legal pad and circled it until the paper nearly tore.

At noon, Standards sent preliminary notes before they had even read the full revision. That was how this phase worked: preemptive caution packaged as guidance. The PDF had comment bubbles every third paragraph.

Replace "lost country" with "lost family."
Delete "return" language in dialogue line 37.
Avoid "brother across the line"; line can imply unauthorized cross-regional rhetoric.
Lullaby motif acceptable if no historically coded vocabulary.

Ina read the notes twice. Her skin prickled in the airless room. The guidelines did not ban grief. They banned geography for grief.

At one-fifteen, Director Kwon arrived without warning, flanked by two council staffers who waited by the door and never sat. He wore a gray suit that looked pressed by someone else three minutes before he walked in. Silver hair combed straight back. Cuff links with no logo. The posture of a man who assumed chairs were optional.

Nam stood and bowed just enough to be polite but not enough to concede ownership of the room. "Director."

Kwon nodded. "Producer Nam."

He turned to Ina. "Writer Seo. I read your draft from last night."

She stood. "Which version did you read?"

"The one before legal edits."

Of course he had that version. Council servers mirrored everything she saved, including files she had deleted and replaced and deleted again.

Kwon held out a marked copy. "Your dialogue is technically clever. Your intention is timid."

Nam made a dry sound in her throat. "Timid works on television."

Kwon did not look at her. "Television has trained public reflexes for sixty years. If viewers feel nothing by minute twenty, advertisers buy out and the board strips the slot. Timid writing gets people fired."

Lee cleared his throat. "With respect, sir, weather that moves too fast kills ratings."

Kwon folded his hands behind his back. "Ratings without direction are market noise. We are not funding noise."

Nam crossed her ankles and slipped her shoes back on, one heel still bare where the blister tape had peeled. "Your stipend is meaningful, Director, but this show is still a broadcast product. If we trigger political review, the slot dies."

Kwon finally turned to her. "I am not asking for slogans. I am asking for one cue that survives Standards and still makes people call home after credits."

He looked back to Ina. "Where is that cue."

Ina felt every eye in the room settle on her, including the two council staffers at the door who pretended they were furniture. She could hear the fan rattling. She could smell dried fruit sugar and solvent from fresh marker ink.

"In the sibling plot," she said. "In the lullaby. In what is missing from paperwork and still present in muscle memory. It's not overt. It's persistent."

Kwon took one page from the stack and tapped a highlighted line. "This sentence: 'You cannot keep dividing a house and call it home.' Standards will kill it."

"I already replaced it."

"With what line?"

"'You keep moving walls and then wonder why no one sleeps.'"

Kwon held her gaze for a long time. "Better. Keep that level."

Nam rubbed her forehead. "Can we lock content decisions to script and stop improvising ideological audits in my room."

Kwon's expression did not change. "You will have lock authority on line edit and ad choreography. Writer Seo will have final language authority on symbolic content."

Nam's jaw tightened. "That is not how this production hierarchy works."

"It is now."

The room went silent in the way rooms do when money changes owners without paperwork.

Kwon turned toward the door, then paused. "One more point. Actor Park will carry these layers on her face. Have you prepared her."

Nam answered first. "Agency handles their talent."

Kwon ignored her. "Writer Seo?"

"I meet her at four."

"Do not fail there. If she plays confusion instead of recognition, the entire architecture collapses."

When he left, one council staffer stayed by the door a half second longer, eyes moving across whiteboards, script piles, faces. He was younger than Ina, maybe thirty, with a stitched repair on the elbow of an expensive jacket. As he followed Kwon out, he offered a small apologetic bow to no one in particular. It was human detail where she had expected only surveillance. It did not make him less surveillance.

Nam sat down hard enough to rattle pens in a cup. "Congratulations. You just got promoted into a knife fight."

Lee whistled low. "Final language authority. They're going to blame you from both sides."

"They already were," Ina said.

Nam stabbed the table with a finger. "Then hear this clearly. I won't lose this timeslot because men in suits want to stage a seminar through my lead actress. Keep your symbols below detection. I mean below mine too. If I can see them, the audience can see them, and we all get dragged."

"That includes me," Ina said.

"It includes you first."

At three-forty, Ina printed the latest draft and took a taxi to Haneul Entertainment's rehearsal complex in Gangnam. Rain had stopped. Steam rose off asphalt. Motorbike couriers cut between buses and cursed at everyone. Her phone filled with unread messages: two from Finance reminding her council stipend deposit timing depended on approved deliverables, one from hospital billing about her mother's balance adjustment failure, one from unknown number: You're writing this too soft. She deleted that one without opening details and felt the phantom vibration anyway.

Haneul's lobby lights were bright enough to bleach fatigue into a brand look. Citrus cleaner and hair spray sat in the air under a wall of framed album covers that watched her with twenty-four versions of joy. At check-in, the receptionist pointed at the camera-sticker tray and held out her palm without meeting Ina's eyes, smile fixed hard enough to look varnished.

Manager Choi Myeong-jin met her at the elevator, forty-something, navy suit, white sneakers with orthopedic inserts that made his gait slightly uneven. He carried two things at all times: a tablet full of schedules and a strip of peppermint antacids he snapped in half with his thumbnail between calls.

"We have fourteen minutes," he said before hello. "She has choreography at four-twenty, dialect coaching at four-forty, live commerce prep at five-ten, then vocal. No unscripted media interactions."

"I'm not media."

"You're a risk."

"I'm a writer."

"Same thing this quarter."

He swiped his tablet, checking boxes as they walked. "Also, we're not discussing contract terms with talent present."

"We have script lines that map to contract risk."

"We're not discussing contract terms with talent present," he repeated, slower, spacing each word like testimony.

They reached Studio B: mirrored walls, sprung floor, harsh overhead lights that flattened skin into polished fatigue. Ji-eun sat on the floor by a fan, legs folded under her, dark hair clipped up with a cheap plastic claw clip shaped like a cartoon fish. She had removed one false eyelash and forgotten to remove the other, giving her face a lopsided ferocity. Her left hand tremored lightly when she reached for her water bottle, then steadied when she wrapped two fingers around the neck and squeezed.

She saw Ina and smiled with her public mouth first. "Writer Seo. Thanks for saving me from dance cardio."

Choi checked his watch. "Twelve minutes now."

Ji-eun lifted her brows. "You're counting in front of me again."

"I'm counting for you."

She turned back to Ina, smile gone; she leaned in and swallowed once before speaking. "Come in before he starts timing our breathing."

Inside the practice room, dance tracks from next door bled through the wall: bass thuds and instructor counts. One-two-three-four, again.

Ina spread the printed pages on the floor. Scene 14. Rooftop argument between leads. Ji-eun's character, Sun-a, had to admit she had spent years searching for a missing brother while pretending she did not care about family at all.

Ji-eun skimmed quickly. Script literacy was not a bullet point in her profile. It was in her fingers: she tracked emotional pivot points with her thumbnail, scratching tiny half-moons into margins at each turn.

"This line works," she said, tapping page three. "'I knew his shoe size longer than I knew my own blood type.'" She glanced up. "That is disgusting. I like it."

"It should feel disgusting," Ina said. "Care work and resentment living in the same sentence."

Ji-eun hummed the four-bar lullaby phrase written in notation above a scene break. She was off key at first, then corrected on the second pass without asking for pitch reference. She frowned.

"It sounds like a church song that got lost on the subway."

"My aunt would call that a compliment."

Ji-eun flipped to the end of the scene packet where a new monologue sat in yellow highlight. "And this. You moved my close-up to after the humming."

"I needed your face on recognition, not mystery."

Ji-eun traced the last line with her finger and did not read it aloud. Outside, Choi's voice cut through the door speaking to someone about endorsement penalties and sleep medication compliance windows.

Ji-eun looked up. "Say it plain. Am I being used as a political instrument."

There it was, direct enough to leave no polite exits.

Ina could have answered with strategy. She could have answered with legal wording, with industry's favorite solvent words like messaging, resonance, contextual relevance. Ji-eun waited, left hand still shaking now that she had stopped touching the page.

"Yes," Ina said. "Partly. Also as a ratings machine. Also as debt collateral. None of those cancel the others."

Ji-eun gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Great. Multipurpose woman. Limited-time offer."

"I'm trying to keep the role survivable."

"Survivable for who."

"For you first."

Ji-eun leaned back on her palms. Her shoulder blades pressed into the mirror behind her and left two foggy half-moons in the condensation from her skin. "You know what they wrote into my rider last week? If the show enters political controversy index above threshold, I pay breach penalties for harm to brand partners. Me. Not the network. Not the men who invite retired ministers into writers' rooms."

Ina did know. She had seen the clause summary in a packet she was not supposed to have: callable debt, immediate liquidity seizure, training-cost acceleration. Numbers that ate years in one bite.

"How much is it?"

Ji-eun looked toward the door, lowered her voice further. "Three hundred twenty million won, plus campaign damages if sponsors bail. They call it recoupment. My mother calls it a second mortgage on a person."

Ina sat down across from her. The floor was still warm from dancers' bodies. "Your agency can negotiate if ratings hold."

"My agency sells my panic as professionalism. They negotiate lighting angles."

She lifted her left hand and watched it tremble. "This started last month, with that hand. They gave me magnesium. Then beta blockers. Then told makeup to hide it."

Ina took the pen from her own ear and placed it in Ji-eun's trembling hand without comment. Ji-eun squeezed until the tremor eased, anchored by pressure and stubbornness.

"I can push to keep the monologue coded," Ina said. "No overt terms. No line they can pin as intentional provocation."

Ji-eun's eyes sharpened. "And when everyone still reads it that way."

Ina thought of Nam's blistered heel, Kwon's cuff links, the council staffer's repaired elbow, the ad buyer voices on speaker telling grief where it could stand. Everybody in this machine had a way to pass cost down one level.

"I'll stand in front of it with you," she said.

Ji-eun held the pen so hard her knuckles blanched. "That sounds like a promise."

"It's a promise."

Ina knew a promise did nothing unless it left a trail. She opened her notes app and typed in front of Ji-eun:

Get option clause in writing.
If they trigger debt, file it where they can't delete it.

Choi knocked once and opened the door without waiting. "Time is up."

Ji-eun did not move.

Choi's eyes flicked to the script pages. "We're keeping this to performance notes."

Ji-eun rose in one fluid motion, stage-trained spine clicking into place. Public brightness snapped on like a switch.

"Manager-nim, Writer Seo just gave me excellent performance notes. I'm very inspired to suffer beautifully on schedule."

Choi popped half an antacid into his mouth. "That tone won't help you."

"It helps me."

He turned to Ina. "Final approval chain: agency legal, network standards, producer sign-off. Not council. Please remember jurisdiction."

"I remember everyone's jurisdiction," Ina said.

Choi nodded once, not agreement, just acknowledgment of a shared trap. As Ji-eun walked to the floor marks for choreography, she slipped the pen Ina had given her into the elastic of her practice pants. It was a tiny theft to keep one thing chosen.

By six, Ina was back at MiraeWave in Edit Bay 3, where the air conditioner ran too cold and still could not beat the heat from stacked render machines. The walls were lined with black acoustic foam that absorbed voices and made every cough sound guilty. Assistant editor Park Sun-woo sat at keyboard station two, rubbing tiger balm into his wrists between shortcuts. He had a newborn at home, and milk-powder dust clung to his cuffs over old coffee stains. The cold raised gooseflesh on her arms and kept her from mistaking adrenaline for clarity.

"We got Standards round two," he said without turning. "They started with fourteen removals. Nam fought to nine."

He pushed a printed note sheet toward her. Red circles around lines, timestamps, shot IDs.

00:17:42 delete "a house split in half."
00:19:08 replace lullaby lyric word "returning."
00:20:31 remove map-shaped scar makeup close-up, too symbolic.
00:22:54 trim brother photo insert by 1.5 seconds.

Nine removals before dinner. Ina felt each one as a small, bureaucratic bite.

Nam stormed in behind her, hair frizzing at the edges from humidity and stress.

"I bought you one survival gift," she said, dropping a takeout cup on Ina's desk. Iced Americano, no syrup. "Standards backed off deleting the whole rooftop monologue. But they want two line swaps and one shot trim."

"What is their rationale for the scar close-up," Ina asked.

"Too suggestive of territorial imagery."

"It's a burn mark on a prop arm."

"Everything's a map when they're scared."

The room's main monitor showed Ji-eun on rooftop set, city lights bokeh behind her. In raw take she hummed the lullaby before speaking, then swallowed hard, eyes bright but not wet. She looked controlled and human, dangerous exactly because she stayed controlled. Ina felt her jaw lock: this was the take that made denial look deliberate.

Nam tapped the screen. "Performance is strong. Maybe too strong. If this trends as political interpretation, advertisers panic. We need plausible deniability."

At seven-fifteen, Kwon called from a secure line patched through post supervisor desk. The line flattened him into tin and static, but the cadence still carried ceremony. Ina's stomach tightened at the ringtone; he never called this early unless he wanted obedience on record.

"I have reviewed the near-lock cut."

Nam mouthed, speaker? Ina nodded and put him on room monitor audio. The nod cinched something under her ribs; once the room heard him, no one could pretend to mishear.

Kwon continued. "The emotional spine holds. By minute twenty your cuts remove the brother's paper trail, and the episode reads as decorative pain."

The phrase decorative pain landed like a tailor's blade, and he always chose tailoring verbs for cuts.

Nam folded her arms. "That's why we've still got a slot. No slot, no signal."

"Then give me one line and one prop beat that survive distracted viewing and still drive next-day conversation."

Ina leaned toward the mic. "It will. The lullaby cue lands at twelve, nineteen, and thirty-two minutes. The sibling artifact lands in all three acts. Viewers will map it even if they never say it out loud."

Kwon paused. "You removed the phrase about shared roof."

Ina pictured the tracked-change strike through that line, red and surgical.

"Standards cut it. We fought it -- no, we burned two hours fighting it -- and kept broadcast."

"You replaced it with kitchen language."

"With kitchen speech," Ina said. She kept both hands flat on the desk to hide the tremor. "How people actually talk when rent's due and someone's missing."

Silence again, then: "Proceed. I expect stronger layering in episode two."

The line clicked dead.

Nam exhaled through her teeth. "He just marked your homework."

Ina stared at the timestamp sheet. There were nine removals, nine tiny amputations, and maybe enough body left to walk.

"Run scene fourteen from top," she said to Sun-woo.

They watched take after take. In one, Ji-eun overplayed rage. In another, she went too soft and turned the scene into apology. Take six held the balance: contempt, shame, and a flicker of hope she looked embarrassed to reveal. Ina marked it.

At eight-forty, agency legal sent their own notes, written in precise bullets with no adjectives.

- Delete ad-libbed "I was sold with the furniture" line.
- Remove shot of contract folder in frame (logo visibility + legal implication).
- Confirm no explicit references to state institutions.

Nam slammed her palm on the desk. "Who let her ad-lib that line."

Ina did not answer. She had.

Sun-woo rotated in his chair. "If we cut it, we lose transition to close-up."

"Then rebuild transition from reaction shot," Nam said.

"It will look patched."

"We're patching."

By ten, everyone in Edit Bay 3 had stopped pretending this was about art. The work had become plumbing under pressure: they moved this line so that this sponsor stayed, they trimmed this frame so that this committee could not flag intent, and they kept enough wound visible that the whole episode did not collapse into pretty faces ordering delivery.

Ina sat with headphones clamped over both ears, scrubbing waveforms. She ducked the music under Ji-eun's breathing at 19:03 so the inhale became audible. She left one quarter-second of room tone after the lullaby hum before dialogue started, an empty pocket where memory could land. She cut every phrase Standards demanded and smuggled rhythm back in through pacing, prop placement, and where eyes moved before mouths did.

At ten-thirty-two, Nam stepped out to take a call and came back white around the mouth.

"Advertiser consortium wants a verbal assurance from me," she said. "Tonight. They're asking whether this episode contains subliminal political coding."

Sun-woo swiveled back to his monitors with impressive neutrality.

Ina kept her gaze on waveform peaks. "What'd you tell them."

"That it's a family romance drama about ordinary people under economic stress."

"That's true."

"It's also a dodge."

"A dodge keeps payroll from vanishing."

Nam crouched beside Ina's chair until their knees nearly touched, forcing eye level.

"If this blows, they cut you first because you're not on network payroll. They cut me second because I hired you. If we survive week one, I might -- might -- keep one of us standing in week two."

"Then keep you," Ina said.

Nam stared at her for a beat, then stood. "Don't make me pick out loud."

At eleven-fifteen, final QC run started. Audio check, subtitle sync check, logo blur check, legal phrase check. Sun-woo's fingers moved over keys like someone playing piano in a house with sleeping children.

Ina's phone buzzed. Ji-eun.

Can you talk.

Ina stepped into hallway, where the vending machine hummed louder than distant traffic.

"How did rehearsal go."

"They moved call time to five a.m. and added media training with a former anchor who says I need warmer vowels when discussing family pain."

"That sounds unbearable."

"It sounds billable."

A pause. Then, in a lower voice: "Manager Choi told me once this airs, my option year activates automatically. More episodes, more control, bigger penalty if I walk."

Ina leaned against cinderblock wall painted a yellow that made everyone look sick. "Did they give you paperwork."

"He didn't need to. He waved the tablet and read the number like weather."

"You can send me screenshots."

"He's not that stupid."

Another pause. Bass from dance studio downstairs thudded faintly through concrete.

Ji-eun said, "You meant it, right, about standing in front."

Ina closed her eyes and pressed her thumb into the cinderblock seam until grit bit her skin. The vending machine's drone and the rain-damp nylon at her cuffs made the hallway feel narrower.

"Yeah. I meant it."

"You hesitated."

"I was walking. I wasn't -- I wasn't dodging you."

Ji-eun gave a breath that might have been a laugh. "You're terrible at lying to actresses."

"Good."

"Stay terrible. If I hear polished comfort from you, I know we're fucked."

After the call, Ina stood another minute in the hallway, watching a moth beat itself against fluorescent light. It kept hitting the hot tube and dropping, then climbing the wall to try again.

Back in bay, QC flagged one issue: at 20:31, the trimmed scar close-up still held one frame where the shape looked unmistakably like the peninsula if you wanted it to.

Nam pointed at the frame counter. "Remove it."

Ina zoomed in. It was one frame, 1/24th of a second.

"If we remove, eye-line jumps."

"Then fix eye-line."

Sun-woo said quietly, "I can interpolate."

"Do it," Nam said.

Ina watched the frame vanish. The map was erased faster than a blink.

At 11:57 p.m., they exported delivery master, 4K broadcast package plus backup. File name: MIRAEWAVE_PILOT_EP01_FINAL_LOCK_v13. Everyone in the room knew v13 was not final. It was only the version that made midnight.

Nam signed digital approval with her stylus, then shoved the tablet at Ina. "Writer sign-off."

Ina signed.

The upload progress bar crawled to 100%. Nobody applauded, and nobody felt relief. Bodies just stood up too quickly and felt their knees complain.

It aired Thursday at nine.

Ina watched from the back of a nearly empty convenience store two blocks from her apartment because she could not stand being in a room full of colleagues during first transmission. The owner kept volume low by habit, so she listened close, shoulder against the refrigerated drinks case, cold seeping through her blouse.

By the first ad break, comments were splitting faster than captions could refresh.

Policy speech in lipstick.
Finally a romance where money hurts.
Border propaganda for housewives.
Coward script pretending history is private.

At 9:47, Nam called.

"Stop hiding in retail. Get back to Bay 3. Containment call in six minutes."

Ina left a basket with kimbap and cheap beer on the counter, ran through rain, and reached the studio with wet cuffs and lungs burning.

Nam had already patched four lines into the bay monitor: herself, network legal counsel Yoon Hae-jin, council policy liaison Oh Jun-seok, and Manager Choi from Haneul. Choi stood in a corridor under rehearsal lights, tablet in one hand, peppermint wrapper in the other.

Yoon went first. "Let us keep this precise. One: network legal issues a neutrality clarification before 23:59. Two: no episode-two page leaves legal privilege chain before pre-clearance. Writer Seo, acknowledge."

Oh cut in before Ina could answer. "No. Policy review comes first. Legal protects the station; I protect mandate continuity. If episode two goes soft, council funding moves before dawn."

They were two demands aimed at the same choke point.

Choi lifted his tablet so the clause text filled his camera. "Agency notice. Clause 11.2 fired at 21:44 when pilot rating crossed threshold. Actor Park is now in mandatory option year. Next interpreted provocation bills her three hundred twenty million won plus sponsor clawbacks. I'm not debating intent. I'm pricing liability."

Ina leaned toward the mic. "Put Ji-eun on."

"She is in wash-off and not available for unscheduled comment."

"Do it."

Choi turned the camera. Ji-eun sat on a tile floor outside a rehearsal bathroom, stage makeup half removed, one false lash still clinging at the corner. Her left hand shook so hard the water bottle cap rattled against the plastic neck. Off-screen, someone pounded the bathroom door and told her call time had moved to 5:00 a.m.

"I threw up twice," Ji-eun said. The words scraped and then broke. "He read clause eleven-two while my mom held my hair."

Choi stepped back into frame. "She is dehydrated and panicking, and we still have a 05:00 camera call. Page delays cost six figures before breakfast."

Ina hit record on her phone and held it next to the speaker. Then she opened her encrypted backup thread and attached the live capture. "Listen carefully. I'm sending this call to Ji-eun, her mother, and outside labor counsel right now. No episode-two pages go to legal or policy until she gets an independent exam tonight."

Yoon's mouth tightened. "Do not transmit this recording outside approved counsel channels. If you do, I file emergency injunctive hold and suspend your portal access tonight."

Oh said, "And if you block policy sequencing, I file noncompliance with the council chair. Your stipend and script authority freeze before sunrise."

"Log both," Ina said. "I'm choosing her body over your process."

She tapped send.

Sun-woo's station pinged behind her. Two red banners snapped across her script portal:

OUTBOUND SHARING DISABLED: USER SEO_INA
WRITER ACCESS SUSPENDED: LEGAL HOLD ACTIVE

A third alert rolled over the edit queue:

EP02 DRAFT PIPELINE HALTED - AUTHORIZED WRITER TOKEN REVOKED

Nam muttered a curse. "You just shut down tomorrow's room."

Choi smiled without warmth. "Then stop interfering and let my actor work." He ended the feed.
