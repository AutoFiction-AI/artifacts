# Chapter 13

By the time Ina climbed down the paint-chipped stairs to Minjae's workshop, the rain had shifted from a hard autumn sheet to a stubborn mist that stuck to her coat and made the metal handrail sweat under her palm. The basement always smelled like hot dust and solder flux, plus the peach hair spray from the nail salon upstairs that leaked through the vents in sweet chemical breaths. Minjae had every lamp on, though it was only eight. Light hit the chipped concrete floor in white rectangles, and in each rectangle he had laid out cables by length, coiled like obedient snakes.

He did that when he was scared: sorted hardware like order could be welded into the world.

He looked up from a stripped router and took in her face in one sweep. "You didn't answer three calls."

"I was on Line Two all day. Kwon put me in a room with three policy people and a retired general who thought subtitles were a branch of artillery." She kicked off her wet shoes and held up the bakery bag. "I brought chestnut bread. It already tastes stale."

"Everything tastes stale this week."

He took the bag anyway, thumbed the paper open, tore one bun in half, and passed her the bigger piece before he ate. She watched his hand out of habit, watched how he turned his wrist to protect the old scar tissue across his shoulder even in a simple motion. In the first weeks she had mistaken that guarded movement for aloofness. Then she had seen him asleep once, not long, only twelve minutes between calls, and she had seen him cradle that shoulder against his chest like he was sheltering an animal.

She sat on the floor mat beside his low worktable. The table was crowded with burner phones, two portable SSDs, a coil of shrink tubing, and the cheap blue lighter he always stole from convenience-store counters and then never used. He lit nothing. He just clicked lighters open and closed when he needed to think.

"I listened to the cassette again," she said. "All of it. Slow speed, then normal, then with phase cleanup. There isn't any ambiguity left." 

He exhaled through his teeth. "You've been saying that for four days."

"Because it's true for four days." She wiped a drop of rainwater off her phone and put it face down. "He staged civilian deaths at the border and packaged it as patriotic defense footage. My mother knew enough to hide the chain, not enough to stop him, or she did stop him and still kept the fucking tape in a storage locker for me to find after she died. I can't keep waiting for a cleaner version of this story."

He chewed, swallowed, and looked at the routers instead of her. "So this is the speech before you ask me for something expensive."

"It's not a speech."

"Then say it plain."

She slid her messenger bag over, unzipped it, and took out three folded sheets of thermal paper from a convenience-store printer kiosk. The ink had already started to gray at the edges. She flattened the pages on the table and held each corner down with hardware: a screwdriver, a phone battery, a hex key, and one of his heavy brass nuts.

The first page was a map of release paths. The second was a timing grid. The third was a list of names in initials only, matched to payment amounts and account endpoints.

Minjae stopped chewing.

"Leak architecture," she said. "Not content yet. Infrastructure. If we do this wrong, the files die in ten minutes and we both disappear before sunrise."

He gave her a look that mixed irritation with reluctant interest. The look was one of the first things that had made her trust him: he never pretended not to care when he cared.

"Talk me through it," he said.

Ina traced the first route with her fingertip, nail catching at the cheap paper fibers. "Phase one is decoy pressure. We push redacted transcript fragments and timing anomalies to fan boards and church legal clinics in the same six-hour window. That gives public eyes but no operational signature."

He nodded once.

"Phase two is liquidity and witness protection. We move money out before freezes hit. Not huge sums. Enough to keep people in apartments and out of panic decisions. We split across auntie accounts, church kitchen books, and two fish-market vendors in Busan who already move cash in hand." She tapped the third sheet. "I need your route rails for that split."

"And phase three?"

"Full archive release with custody trail. Cassette digitization notes, the shadow ledger, contract riders, transfer logs, all of it. Mirror to civic servers, fandom mirrors, and one labor legal node that doesn't scare when keywords trip."

He stared at the timing grid, then at her. "You're planning this before Mercury goes live."

"Yes."

"You think Kwon won't smell it?"

"He'll smell something. I need him guessing wrong for thirty-six hours." She paused. "Forty-eight if we're lucky."

He let out a small laugh with no humor in it. "Luck. Right."

The pipes rattled overhead. The salon owner dropped something heavy on the tile, and both of them flinched before they could stop themselves. Then they both laughed, too loud, because fear loved an audience.

Minjae scratched the stubble on his jaw, eyes still on the page. "My sister's transfer is due Friday. If her handler misses one month, they cut her access and she disappears behind someone else's debt. You know that."

"I know." She swallowed. "I'm not touching that line."

"You can't promise that with your fingers crossed behind your back, Ina. If this blows open, every line gets touched."

"Then help me keep it tight enough that it doesn't blow open before we're ready."

He said nothing.

She heard the old refrigerator in the corner click on and hiss. She heard rain hit the basement window and run down the outside glass like fingers tapping to be let in. She also heard, more clearly than either, the question she had not wanted to ask aloud because she needed him to answer it without being cornered.

"Are you with me?"

He leaned back on his palms. "On what?"

"On the leak. On me. On not giving him one more season to package harm as strategy." She hated how pleading the sentence sounded. She hated that she did not retract it.

He closed the lighter in his hand. Click.

"I'm with you on ending him," he said. "I'm not with you on pretending this is clean."

She almost corrected his full form, then caught herself. He only went formal when he was trying to keep from yelling.

"I don't need clean," she said quietly. "I need possible."

Minjae stood and crossed to the sink in three steps. He rinsed his hands like he'd touched engine grease though he had only handled paper. Water rattled through old pipes. He stayed with his back to her long enough that she felt each second like an individual bruise, then he shut the tap, wiped his hands on a towel that had once been white, and turned.

"I can give you two route windows," he said. "One tomorrow night through Incheon cargo and one forty hours later through the fish crates from Busan. I can mask packet headers through a shell traffic dump, but if they run deep trace after freeze order, we're cooked."

Relief moved through her so fast it made her dizzy. She leaned forward, grabbed his wrist, and pressed her forehead against the heel of his hand. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet."

"Too late."

He tugged her up and kissed her hard enough that her lower lip caught his tooth. She tasted blood and chestnut sugar. His hand slid under her sweater and found her scar before anything else, thumb resting on the raised skin at her left wrist like he was checking she was still there. She had never told him the whole story of the burn, only the easy version about a kettle and an inattentive morning. He had never pretended to believe it.

The workshop floor was warmed by old heating pipes that ran beneath the concrete in uneven lines, and they learned those lines with their knees and hips as they moved, one patch warm, one patch cold. He pulled her shirt over her head and cursed when her earring snagged in the fabric. She laughed into his shoulder, then bit down because the walls were thin and the salon owner's teenage son already hated both of them for parking in his spot. Minjae's breath was rough by the time she got his belt undone. He paused once to look at her, really look, and she mistook the pause for surrender instead of calculation.

Afterward he lay flat on the mat, one forearm across his eyes, chest still jumping from effort. She straddled his hips, tracing the old shoulder scar with a fingertip damp from sweat. The scar had a ridge in the center like cheap rope under skin. He kept still while she traced it, though his jaw tightened.

"We'll make it through this," she said.

"That's a dangerous sentence from you."

"I know."

She got off him, found her shirt, and sat with her back against the table leg while he rolled the condom in tissue and tied it closed. He was always careful with trash that held DNA. Not paranoid, he called it. Experienced.

He handed her a bottle of barley tea gone warm. "Give me the route key requests tonight. Not over the app. Write them by hand."

"Done."

"And don't sleep at your apartment."

"I've got to pick up the printed ledger pages first."

"Then pick them up and go somewhere else."

She nodded, because agreeing cost less than arguing. Then she folded the thermal pages again, this time along new lines so the previous creases did not telegraph to a camera. He watched her fold and smiled in that quick, sideways way he used when he approved of a trick.

She took that smile as confirmation.

At the door she kissed him once, no urgency left, and he touched the back of her neck as if he could press a blessing through skin. "Call me when you're upstairs," he said.

"You'll get sick of hearing my voice by midnight."

"Not likely."

She climbed the stairs with the damp paper tucked inside her bra and the taste of him still in her mouth. On the landing, she looked back. He was already bent over the table, phone in one hand, thumb hovering above the screen while the other hand reached for the blue lighter and clicked it open, closed, open.

Outside, the rain had stopped. Ina stood under the salon awning and called him from the sidewalk like he asked. He picked up on the first ring.

"You good?" he said.

"I'm good."

"Get home and move fast."

"I will."

She ended the call smiling despite herself, turned toward the station, and did not see him step into the stairwell shadow to make another call before her train doors even closed.

At 1:37 a.m., while she slept in a borrowed room above a closed jjimjilbang in Hapjeong, the first freeze order hit her accounts.

She woke at 5:12 to the sound of her phone rebooting. Not ringing, rebooting: the startup chime too cheerful for the hour, followed by a language selection screen she had not seen since she bought the device. Korean, English, Japanese, Chinese. Factory state.

For half a second she thought she had grabbed the wrong phone from her bag. Then she saw the burn-marked sticker she used to hide a cracked corner of the screen and felt her stomach drop so hard she almost gagged.

"No," she said to no one. She tapped Korean. The setup wizard slid forward as if mocking urgency. She skipped everything she could skip and opened the banking app.

No app.

She installed from scratch with shaking fingers, logged in, and got a red banner: ACCESS RESTRICTED PENDING NATIONAL SECURITY REVIEW. CUSTOMER SUPPORT REF: 8902-A.

She tried her second bank. Same banner, different reference code. She tried the MiraeWave residual account she had kept for months as emergency oxygen. Suspended. She tried the church cooperative account where she parked small relief transfers in rotating amounts under invoice notes for rice, onions, and cleaning solvent. Frozen.

She checked her wallet: one transportation card, three 1,000-won bills, two coins, one receipt from last week for instant noodles and batteries. She dug in her bag, found an envelope she had forgotten with 74,000 won in mixed notes, and laughed out loud because she could hear exactly how long seventy-four thousand lasted in Seoul when landlords smelled fear.

By 6:10 she was in the line outside an ATM on a corner where the bakery opened before dawn. Two delivery men in padded vests stood behind her stomping their feet against the cold. The machine ate her card on the first try and spat out a polite message about institutional hold procedures. The man behind her muttered, "Always before election month," and she wanted to turn and ask him how many times this had happened to him, what flavor of panic he kept in his mouth while he waited, but the ATM beeped for the next customer and she stepped aside like a well-trained citizen.

She bought one Americano from the bakery with cash because she needed the cup heat in her hand more than she needed caffeine. The lid leaked down her fingers. Her thumbprint blurred on the paper sleeve.

At 6:42 she reached the Seongdong print-shop safehouse. The street shutters were still down; graffiti tags from a youth crew ran along the metal in acid green paint. She used the side key and went in through the alley door.

Inside, the air smelled wrong. Not stale coffee and ink like usual. Ozone and hot plastic.

Three tower units on the back desk were powered on but blank-screened. Their fans screamed at full speed, and every status LED pulsed red in the same rhythm like a synchronized heartbeat. A wipe routine, not a crash. Somebody had pushed a remote command and left no pretense.

She yanked a network cable and sat at terminal three. The login account field reset as soon as she typed. She opened local storage directly. Empty directories. Directory names still there, everything inside gone. She pulled the external backup drive from her bag, plugged it in, and watched the system report fresh format date: 03:03, this morning.

"You smug old bastard," she said, voice low and almost admiring despite herself. Kwon always believed in theater.

She paced the room once, then forced herself to stop because pacing did not restore data. She opened the metal cabinet where she had kept paper copies in a folder labeled TAX INVOICES 2024. The folder was gone. In its place sat a new folder, same label, filled with blank copier paper, edges still warm. Someone had taken the time to replace weight with weight.

She put both hands on the cabinet shelf until the vibration from the server fans traveled up her arms and into her shoulders. She could feel each screw head under the paint through her fingertips. She fixed on that texture until she could think again.

Not all copies were here.

Three weeks ago, after Kwon humiliated her in the strategy room for raising civilian cost, she had gone home and printed narrowed extracts of the shadow ledger in six-point type, then pasted strips of those pages between hymn lyrics in Aunt Hyejin's church binders. She had done it because she did not trust herself not to get sentimental and because Hyejin's choir women had terrible eyesight and never read the small print margins anyway.

If the binders were untouched, she still had fragments.

Her phone buzzed with an unknown number before she reached the door.

She answered on instinct. "Hello."

"Ms. Seo." Kwon's voice was unmistakable even through mobile compression, dry and measured, every syllable pressed flat as if he had ironed it before speaking. "I am told you attempted a series of irregular transfers before dawn."

She leaned against the doorframe and stared at the alley wall where rainwater had darkened old posters into illegible gray skin. "I tried to pay medical bills. That's irregular now?"

"In your current status, yes."

"What status is that?"

"Protective review. Your devices have been standardized and your liquidity temporarily constrained so no one mistakes panic for principle."

She shut her eyes. "You froze church food money."

"You conflated civic charity with covert disbursement. I corrected a category error."

"You mean you punished me for moving first."

A pause. She pictured him in his office with the antique clock behind him and the tea cup he never finished.

"I am giving you a chance to pause," he said. "You have not yet committed an irreversible act."

"You already did."

"You are tired. Sleep, then report to Line Two at ten."

"I'm not coming in."

"You are mistaken about your options, Ms. Seo."

He ended the call without goodbye.

Her hand had clenched around the paper coffee cup hard enough to crush it. Lukewarm coffee leaked down her wrist and into her sleeve. She let the cup drop in the alley and ran for the main road to catch a taxi because buses were too slow and she had exactly one place left to salvage what mattered.

Aunt Hyejin's church kitchen was already loud when Ina arrived. Metal bowls hit stainless counters. Someone had started chopping cabbage for kimchi and the room smelled of garlic, fish sauce, and wet newspaper. Hyejin stood at the center table in her apron and rubber gloves, reading glasses hanging from the chain at her neck, counting out egg trays with the concentration of a bookkeeper balancing a battlefield ledger.

She looked up once. "You look like a person who got bad news before breakfast." Then she squinted. "Worse. You look like a person who expected bad news and got it anyway."

Ina pulled off her shoes at the door and stepped onto the warm vinyl floor. "I need the old hymn binders. The blue ones with cracked spines."

Hyejin did not ask why. She nodded toward the pantry. "Top shelf, left side. And wash your hands first. You touch those books with city dirt, I'll haunt you myself while alive."

Ina washed, dried, and hauled down four binders that smelled of old paper and camphor. She spread them on a side table under the fluorescent light and flipped to pages she had marked weeks ago with tiny pen dots hidden inside punctuation marks. Between "grace" and "mercy," taped thin as skin, were strips of printed ledger entries: dates, transfer IDs, shell company abbreviations, and initials tied to payout amounts.

Not enough for a full release. Enough to prove a pattern.

Hyejin came over with a bowl of peeled radish, sat across from her, and started slicing with a short knife that had lost its handle finish years ago. "Cards not working?"

Ina kept peeling tape slowly so it would not tear the ink. "Everything froze. Accounts, devices, access keys."

"Your father's landlord called at dawn," Hyejin said. "He used his polite voice, which means he's preparing his rude voice for next week."

Ina shut her eyes for one breath. "How short are we?"

"For this month? Two million and change if we want to keep your father where he is and keep Saint Mary's from adding another late penalty to your mother's account." Hyejin flicked a radish slice into the bowl. "You can survive broke. You can't survive broke and surprised. Decide which one you're willing to be."

Ina looked at her aunt's arthritic fingers inside the gloves, how the knuckles bulged when she gripped the knife but the cuts stayed exact anyway, each radish piece the same thickness.

"I've got maybe seventy-four thousand cash," Ina said. "And a transit card."

Hyejin snorted. "Then you have seventy-four thousand more than last winter. We have had worse numbers." She pushed the bowl toward Ina. "Eat one. You're pale."

Ina took a slice. Salt and chili burned the cracked skin at the corner of her mouth where Minjae's tooth had caught her. She had forgotten the cut until then.

Hyejin noticed. Her eyes narrowed, then softened, then went sharp again. "Did he do this?"

Ina did not answer quickly enough.

Hyejin sighed. "I'm not asking if you love him. I'm asking if he's the hole in your boat."

Ina folded the ledger strips into a legal envelope and slid it inside her coat lining. "I'm going to find out tonight."

By afternoon she had borrowed a laptop from a freelance captioner she barely knew, paid in promises because cash was dead, and rebuilt enough of her comm stack to check one surviving secure log. The metadata told its own story. She had sent Minjae the first hand-written route request at 12:58 a.m. He had read at 1:03. At 1:11 his old line made a six-minute encrypted voice call to a number she did not have labeled, but the last four digits matched a contact she had seen once on Kwon's desk in a printed emergency chain.

Freeze order timestamp: 1:37.

There were many kinds of betrayal. Some arrived with shouting and broken glass. Some arrived as numbers in a clean column, impossible to argue with.

She texted Minjae one line: `Need to see you. No phones. Under Seongsu rail bridge, 22:00.`

He replied in thirteen seconds: `I'll be there.`

She spent the next six hours moving like someone carrying water in open hands, careful and furious. She copied the ledger strips by hand twice in block print, one set for herself, one set for a dead drop she had not yet chosen. She wrapped both sets in wax paper from the church kitchen so rain would not eat the ink. She sold her old camera body to a student filmmaker outside Hongdae for half its market value and left with a stack of damp bills that smelled like cigarette smoke. She paid a minimum on Saint Mary's debt in person through a cashier window because digital routes were gone, and the clerk stamped the receipt without looking at her face.

At 9:42 she stood under the Seongsu rail bridge with her hood up and counted each train by sound before she saw it. The bridge trembled every nine minutes. Water dripped through expansion joints and tapped the rusted guardrail in irregular rhythms. A fried-chicken truck had parked two blocks away, and the air held that hot oil smell that could make hunger feel like insult.

Minjae arrived on foot from the river side, hands in jacket pockets, cap low. He stopped three steps away and studied her the same way he did a suspicious drive: from edges inward.

"You look wrecked," he said.

"You look rested."

"I slept an hour."

"Luxury."

A train thundered overhead and made them both wait. When the noise passed, she spoke before fear could soften her.

"Did you call Kwon after I left last night?"

His jaw moved once. "Why would I do that?"

"Don't give me the first lie. We don't have time for the ceremonial one."

"Ina."

"At 1:11 your line called his emergency chain for six minutes. At 1:37 my accounts froze, my devices wiped, and my paper backups got replaced with blank weight. Did you call him?"

He looked past her toward the dark river for so long she heard every drip from the bridge. Then he met her eyes.

"Yes," he said.

The word did not crack anything audible. No glass. No shout. Just the small click of a structural bolt failing where she could not see it.

She laughed once, short and ugly. "You could've told me before I spent the day begging cash like a teenager who lost her wallet."

"If I'd told you then, you'd have blown every channel in one panic run."

"You don't get to say panic like it isn't a reasonable response to fucking treason."

"I called to buy time."

"For who?"

"For my sister." His voice rose, then dropped again. "For my sister, yes. For the people on my routes who aren't in your spreadsheets yet. For me, because I'm tired of choosing between prison and burial every week. Pick one."

She stepped closer until she could smell the machine oil in his jacket. "You chose him."

"I chose forty-eight hours where she stays alive."

"By giving him me."

"By giving him a warning that kept him from doing what he does when he's surprised."

"You mean disappearing people?" she snapped. "How generous. He only took my money and my tools today. Should I send him flowers?"

His face flinched at that, not from guilt but from rage he was trying to leash.

"You think I don't know what he is?" he said. "You think I haven't watched men lose fingers and names because someone in a clean office wanted a better headline curve? I called because your plan had no survivable lane for my family. You asked me to torch routes that feed them and trust your redactions would hold. Redactions don't stop knock-on sweeps."

"And Kwon does?"

"No. But he negotiates when he's got leverage. Dead heroes don't negotiate at all."

She shoved her hands into her coat pockets because if she did not she might hit him, and she did not trust what would happen after that first impact.

"What did you give him?"

"Architecture only. Not content. Not names."

"You gave him enough to preempt. That's the same result."

"It is not the same result."

"Tell that to my frozen accounts. Tell it to Saint Mary's billing office. Tell it to my father's landlord when he tapes an eviction notice over his mailbox."

He swore under his breath, then louder. "Fuck. I moved cash this afternoon. I brought it for you."

He pulled an envelope from inside his jacket and held it out. Thick. Maybe two million, maybe a little more.

She stared at it and did not take it.

"What is this? Hush money?"

"Emergency money. Route fees from last run."

"How many people did you underpay to make this stack look generous?"

"Don't do that." His hand shook now. "Don't turn everything into moral theater because you're hurt."

"I am hurt."

"I know."

"No," she said. "You know the strategic version. You don't know this version."

A truck backfired on the road above and echoed under the bridge like a small explosion. Neither of them ducked.

She took a breath and forced her voice flat. "Say it exactly. Why did you call him?"

He dropped his gaze to the wet concrete between them. "Because when you left, I pictured my sister waiting in that apartment in Pyongyang with the broken boiler and the blue gloves she wears indoors because her fingers crack in winter. I pictured what happens when her handler misses one transfer. I pictured your leak going loud before I could move her line. I called Kwon and told him you were building release rails. I told him if he froze you instead of grabbing you, I'd keep my routes quiet for one week and he could avoid public mess before Mercury." He looked up. "He agreed."

"He agreed to freeze me because you asked nicely."

"I asked with leverage."

"Your leverage was me."

"My leverage was the routes he needs."

"Routes you gave him back for free the second I trusted you."

He dragged both hands through his hair and laughed once, bitter. "Trusted me? Ina, you gave me architecture after three weeks of hiding half your intelligence deal and all your fallback plans. You don't trust people. You allocate them."

The sentence landed because it was not entirely wrong.

She felt suddenly, vividly, the weight of the envelope strips sewn into her coat lining, the scratch of paper edges against her ribs. She had built redundancies out of everyone she loved and called it prudence.

"Maybe I do allocate," she said. "But I did not hand your life to Kwon for forty-eight hours of comfort."

"Comfort?" He barked a laugh. "You think I'm comfortable? I haven't sat through a full meal in months. I sleep with one shoe on because calls come at any hour. I count men at every handoff and recalculate who is missing before I get home. Don't call this comfort."

"Then call it what it is."

"Triaging an unwinnable board."

"That sounds exactly like him."

He went still at that. When he spoke again, his voice was low and tired enough to break her if she let it.

"I learned from him because he paid everyone in my world long before you did. I hated that and I still learned it. You learned from him too. We both did. The difference is you still think your version is clean." 

"I never said clean."

"You said possible." He wiped rain from his mouth with the back of his hand. "Possible for who, Ina?"

She opened her mouth and closed it again. The bridge dripped. A train roared overhead. Somewhere beyond the pillars, a couple argued in slurred voices and then laughed at themselves.

When the noise thinned, she said, "I needed you beside me on this. Not in front of me with a warning flare."

"I was beside you until beside you meant my sister gets sacrificed first."

"So now what?"

"Now we stop pretending we're one team." He looked at the envelope still in his hand and shoved it back into his jacket. "I can still keep your name off some route chatter. I can still buy you small time."

"I don't want your small time."

"You will when they come."

"They are already here."

He took one step toward her. She stepped back.

That, more than any sentence, ended it.

His face folded inward for a second, then reset into the operational mask she had met in the first month of their lives together, before desire, before confession, before the bad habit of sleeping with hope.

"If you need to send anything physical now," he said, voice almost neutral, "don't use Seongdong lanes. They're burned. Use market cold-chain trucks only and rotate pickup points every thirty minutes. Do not trust repeat drivers."

The advice was good. She hated him for giving it.

"You sound like a subcontractor handing off a project," she said.

"Maybe that's what we were."

"No." She shook her head. "A subcontractor does not memorize where I keep burn cream or how many sugars I take in hospital coffee. A subcontractor does not ask me to call when I get upstairs." Her voice broke on the last word and she let it. "Do not reduce this so you can live with yourself."

He flinched and looked away.

For a moment she thought he might apologize, properly, without strategy. He did not.

Instead he said, "I am sorry." Formal, clipped, built for legal record, not repair.

She laughed with no humor left. "There it is."

She reached into her wallet and pulled out the small metal route key he had given her in summer, the one etched with a scratched MJ on one side and a cartoon squid on the other because he said all serious tools deserved one stupid mark. She held it between thumb and forefinger, then set it on the wet guardrail between them.

"Keep your infrastructure," she said.

He did not pick it up.

She turned and started walking toward the main road. After twelve steps she heard him call her name once. She kept walking.

At the bus stop she checked the borrowed phone. Two new messages waited.

The first was from Line Two scheduling: `MANDATORY APPEARANCE 08:00. NONCOMPLIANCE WILL BE DOCUMENTED.`

The second was from her aunt: `Landlord taped a paper on your father's door. Come by before midnight if you can.`

Ina looked up at the apartment towers on the hill, their windows lit in uneven grids. Somewhere in those rectangles, families were folding laundry, fighting over remote controls, paying bills they could barely afford, or pretending not to hear the neighbor downstairs crying. She felt the wax-wrapped ledger strips against her ribs and imagined every number on them turning into a hand reaching for her at once.

When her bus arrived, she did not board. She walked instead, because the fare would be cheaper tomorrow if she rationed the card and because movement was the only thing that made the anger stay in useful shape.

By the time she reached Mapo, her socks were wet through and the sky had gone the color of old metal. A dark van without logos idled across from her building entrance. The driver did not look at her.

He did not need to.
