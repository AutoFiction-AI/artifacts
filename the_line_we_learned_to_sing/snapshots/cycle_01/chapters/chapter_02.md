# Chapter 2

The alley behind the Seongdong print shop smelled like wet cardboard and frying oil. Rain had stopped twenty minutes earlier, but water still leaked from a cracked gutter and ticked against an empty paint can by the service door. Ina stood under the buzzing security light and read the same text from the hospital billing office for the third time.

`Minimum payment overdue. Please contact Seoul St. Mary's Financial Services.`

She had not deleted the message because deleting it felt like pretending she had options.

Rhee opened the metal door before Ina could knock. In daylight the woman would have looked like any office administrator in Seoul, but at midnight she looked like she belonged to a different utility service entirely: dark windbreaker zipped to the throat, old running shoes, hair pinned with two mismatched clips. She held a paper cup of vending-machine corn tea and a ring of keys heavy enough to bruise.

"You came alone," Rhee said.

"You asked me to." Ina tucked her phone into her pocket. "You keep checking like I'm a teenager sneaking out."

Rhee took a swallow of tea, grimaced, and shook sweetener from a packet into the cup. "I check because half our work is ruined by people who assume they're not being followed." She looked over Ina's shoulder into the alley, then stepped aside. "Come in. The presses are off. You can hear yourself think down there."

Inside, the print floor was dark except for one strip light over stacked pallets of pamphlet paper. The air held that dry-metal smell that old copiers gave off when they overheated, plus stale coffee and toner dust that tasted faintly bitter at the back of Ina's tongue. Rhee moved between dormant machines without turning on more lights. Her keys tapped against her thigh in a steady rhythm that made Ina think of a metronome.

At the freight elevator, Rhee dug in her windbreaker pocket and brought out a tiny blue inhaler. She used it quickly, her face turned away, then slipped it back before Ina could pretend not to notice.

"Basement sticks in my chest this time of year," she said, pressing the elevator call button. "Mold and paper. If you're lucky, your body never gets a vote in your politics."

The elevator opened with a groan. They descended one level.

The safehouse was not a bunker. It was a cramped office carved out of storage space and lit too brightly, with blocked windows, humming servers in a steel rack, a long map of the peninsula pinned across one wall, and a conference table scarred by decades of coffee rings. Someone had tried to civilize the room with a potted fern. The fern had lost.

Director Kwon stood when Ina entered.

He was in a charcoal suit that fit like armor, silver hair combed straight back, no tie pin, no visible insignia. If Ina had passed him in a hotel corridor, she would have guessed retired judge, maybe a corporate chair who still terrified vice presidents. He nodded once, then sat only after she sat. His posture did not move even when he reached for the ceramic cup at his elbow.

He had steeped the same tea bag twice. Ina saw him squeeze it against the spoon and fold it carefully on the saucer as if he had been trained during rationing years to waste nothing that could be used again.

"Ms. Seo." His voice was low, formal, and annoyingly calm. "Thank you for honoring the meeting."

Ina kept her bag on her lap. The green lyric notebook pressed against her knees through the canvas. "I didn't come to honor anything. I came to find out if this is a scam with patriotic branding."

Rhee shut the door and stayed by it, arms crossed, eyes on the hallway camera feed mounted above the filing cabinet.

Kwon gave the smallest hint of a smile. "Skepticism is a useful organ. Keep it."

"Then start with names." Ina looked from Kwon to the map and back. "Who are you, exactly? What is this group? How is any of this real?"

Kwon slid a cream-colored folder across the table. Her name was typed on the front in black serif font: `SEO INA - PROBATION REVIEW`.

Inside were copies of old letters, blurred photographs, and three pages of typed organizational history with footnotes and archive citations. On top sat one photo she had not seen in her mother's apartment: Eun-sook in a field jacket, cigarette between two fingers, standing beside Kwon thirty years younger in front of a broadcast van with patched antenna wiring. Her mother's handwriting ran along the white border.

`Signal held for 11m after sweep. K said hold anyway.`

Ina touched the corner of the print, then pulled her hand back.

"A photo can be faked," she said.

"Yes." Kwon nodded. "So can a constitution. So can election returns. Forgery is a universal language."

He opened a second folder, this one thinner. Hospital billing statements. Her father's lease arrears notice. A screenshot of her subtitle payroll account with the last three deposit dates. Every column was accurate down to the won.

Ina stared at him. "You went through my finances."

"We performed due diligence on a prospective contractor." Kwon folded his hands. "You may call it intrusion if that makes the ethics cleaner in your own mind."

"Do not tell me what makes my ethics cleaner."

Kwon did not flinch. "I do not. I observe your constraints so we can design around them."

She wanted to slam the folder shut and walk out. She also wanted to keep breathing in an apartment that still carried her mother's clothes in the closet and unpaid notices in the kitchen drawer.

"Say it plainly," Ina said. "No slogans. What do you do here?"

Kwon leaned back by exactly one inch.

"We preserve policy continuity for a state project that could not survive formal institutions," he said. "Military confrontation is catastrophic. Electoral rhetoric resets every cycle. Diplomatic channels narrow and widen according to the weather in Washington, Beijing, and domestic polling. Culture is the only instrument that crosses all those borders daily. Drama scripts cross by USB. Lyrics cross by memory. Fan edits cross by bluetooth in markets with no legal internet. You know this. Your mother knew this."

"Culture also gets people watched, arrested, disappeared." Ina heard her own voice sharpen. "You don't get to call that weather."

"I call it cost." He spoke the word as if he were reading a line item on a ledger. "The question is whether the cost purchases movement or only suffering."

"And who decides that in your office?"

"In this room, ultimately, I do."

Silence held for three breaths. Server fans whined. A train passed somewhere aboveground, the vibration traveling through concrete and chair legs.

Rhee pushed off the wall, crossed to the table, and placed a printed contract in front of Ina with a capped pen. Her nails were cut blunt and clean except for one thumb stained with blue copy ink. "Read all of it," she said. "Do not sign blind."

Ina let out one short laugh. "At least one decent person in the room."

Rhee gave her a hard look. "I'm not decent. I'm old enough to be accurate."

The contract was eleven pages, dense and hostile.

`Operational Secrecy and Narrative Services Agreement`.

Term: probationary appointment.

Compensation: KRW 7,000,000 monthly stipend paid via designated production affiliate.

Emergency support: authorized direct settlement support for immediate family medical arrears, contingent on compliance.

Key obligations:

- Permanent non-disclosure regarding council structure, personnel, assets, channels.
- Obedience to assigned narrative directives and revision deadlines.
- Participation in strategic message development under supervisor instruction.
- No unsanctioned contact with domestic or foreign security entities.

Breach clause:

- Referral under armistice-era security statutes.
- Immediate suspension of compensation and account review.

It was not employment. It was a leash with a salary attached.

Ina read each page slowly, her finger tracing paragraphs while Kwon waited without interruption. At the legal disclaimer on page eight she felt the burn scar on her left wrist catch against the paper edge. She lifted her hand, looked at the shallow white line, and remembered her mother telling her, when she was nine, that kitchen burns taught timing better than lectures.

"This says if I disclose anything, I can be prosecuted." Ina tapped page nine. "This says you can direct my output and revoke support if I don't comply."

"Correct," Kwon said.

"And this language about 'strategic message development' can mean anything from rewrites to psychological operations."

"It can." He held her gaze. "We are not a church choir, Ms. Seo."

She looked back down. "You invited the wrong person if you want blind discipline."

"I invited the precise person," he said. "You can write for mass feeling without sounding like a pamphlet. You can hide intent in ordinary scenes. And you currently cannot afford moral vanity."

The words landed like a slap she had already anticipated.

Ina set the contract down. "You don't know me."

Kwon turned the hospital statement toward her and pointed to the outstanding number without touching the paper.

`KRW 58,000,000`.

Then he turned the lease arrears notice.

`KRW 11,200,000`.

"I know the arithmetic currently governing your decisions," he said. "That is enough."

Rhee looked away toward the camera feed, jaw tight.

Ina's chest felt packed with dry paper. She heard herself over-explain, as if careful clauses might buy room to breathe.

"If I even consider this, and I'm not saying I do, then I need boundaries in writing. I do scripts and narrative architecture. I don't identify individuals. I don't draft content that calls for violence. I don't build fear campaigns."

Kwon listened without taking notes.

When she finished, he said, "Add an ethics addendum if you wish. It will guide your work. It will not govern events you do not control."

"Then what is the point of adding it?"

"To record that you were not asleep when you entered."

That answer pissed her off more than refusal would have.

"You make this sound noble," Ina said. "It sounds like blackmail with archival stationery."

Kwon's face did not harden. If anything, it softened by half a degree.

"Blackmail requires private shame," he said. "I am offering public purpose through private compromise. It is uglier than blackmail and often more durable."

Rhee snorted once, almost a laugh, then covered it with a cough.

Ina read the contract again, this time as a map of punishments. Every page had one promise and two traps. Salary relief if obedient. Debt support if silent. Institutional protection implied but never guaranteed. She felt the familiar editor's impulse to mark up weak phrasing, tighten loopholes, insert precise verbs. It would have been easier if the text were just bad. It was competent, which made it dangerous.

"What is probation here?" she asked.

"Mutual testing period," Kwon said. "You assess our seriousness. We assess your discipline."

"Can I walk away?"

"You may decline further assignments after probation. Secrecy remains perpetual."

"So no." Ina looked up. "Not really."

"Not fully," Kwon said.

He did not rush her. That was another tactic: let the room and the numbers do the pressure work.

Ina took the pen Rhee had set down. It was a cheap office ballpoint with a cracked clip. She signed her full name on page eleven, then initialed every margin where flagged tabs stuck out.

Her handwriting looked like her mother's when she was tired.

Rhee collected the copies, stamped each one with a small red seal, and handed Ina her contractor packet in a manila envelope.

Kwon folded his reused tea bag again, precise as surgery.

"Welcome," he said. "Your first assignment starts tonight."

Ina laughed once, short and ugly. "Of course it does."

Kwon stood, smoothed his suit cuffs, and moved to the map wall. "MiraeWave Studio B has a pilot table read at two-thirty. A romance project with high projected share among women forty and older. Our target cohort. You will adjust one scene so it carries reunification grief without triggering broadcaster censors."

"You want propaganda inside a melodrama."

"I want memory inside entertainment."

"Same difference if people get hurt."

"No." Kwon turned back to her. "Difference is everything. Sequence matters. Form matters. Tone matters. You know this better than anyone in this room."

Ina hated that he was right about her skills.

Rhee grabbed her keys. "Car is out front. We should move before traffic dies and police decide to be curious." She paused by Ina's chair. "Eat something in the car. You look like static."

"I'm static," Ina muttered.

Rhee reached into her bag and dropped a convenience-store kimbap triangle into Ina's hand. "Then conduct it in the useful direction."


Studio B sat on the third floor of the MiraeWave complex, behind a lobby wall plastered with glossy posters where everyone looked less tired than real people had any right to look. The security gate accepted Rhee's temporary badge without a beep. No one at reception asked Ina for identification. That scared her more than if they had.

The writer room smelled of stale marker ink and instant ramen broth. Script pages were spread across two pushed-together tables. Three assistants typed on laptops with the speed and panic of people paid to vanish errors before sunrise.

Head writer Cho Seungho did not stand when Ina entered. He sat sideways in his chair, ankle over knee, turning an unlit cigarette between his fingers like a coin trick. A nicotine patch showed above his collarbone where his T-shirt had stretched out. He looked at her once and then at Rhee.

"You brought me another consultant," he said. "I already have two consultants and one censor pretending not to be a censor."

"She's not a consultant," Rhee said. "She's script support under Director Kwon."

Cho's mouth twitched. "That title means she's a consultant with handcuffs."

"Call it whatever keeps you working," Rhee replied.

Ina set her envelope on an empty chair and opened the pilot script. The cover page read `RIVER LIGHT, EPISODE 1`.

A young actress sat at the far end of the table, posture perfect even in exhaustion, dyed dark hair tied low at the nape. Park Ji-eun. Ina recognized her from bus-stop ads and subway screens, but in person Ji-eun looked smaller, with a faint tremor in her left hand every time she reached for her water bottle. She wore a black hoodie over rehearsal clothes and had a strip of neon tape wrapped around her index finger where the pen rubbed.

Next to her sat an agency manager in a cream blazer, forty maybe, phone on speaker beside her notebook. The manager smiled like a tax auditor.

"We are only here for line rhythm," the manager said. "No political references, no historical slogans, no region-sensitive terms, no implied cross-border messaging. Legal has already cleared version six."

Cho waved the unlit cigarette at the ceiling. "And here comes version seven anyway."

Ina took the only open seat. She could feel Kwon's contract packet pressing against her leg through the chair mesh like a second spine.

"I'm not here to blow up your script," she said. "I'm here for one scene pass."

Cho gave a dry laugh. "That is what everyone says before they blow up my script."

They read from page forty-two, a kitchen argument between Ji-eun's character and her estranged older brother. In Cho's draft, the scene was competent and dead. He had them fight over a restaurant lease and then reconcile with an expensive bottle of soju placed for obvious sponsor visibility.

Ina listened once without interrupting. She marked three lines and looked at Ji-eun.

"Can we try a version where the argument is not about lease money," Ina said, "but about who kept setting an extra bowl at dinner after their youngest sibling went missing? No names, no geography, no institutions. Just family habit turning into grief."

The agency manager leaned forward. "Missing sibling is trauma framing. Trauma framing triggers advocacy interpretation. We do not authorize that."

"People have siblings," Ina said. "People lose them. That is not advocacy. That is Wednesday."

Cho scratched his patch absently through his shirt. "Network will ask what happened to the sibling."

"Let them ask," Ina said. "The scene does not answer. It lets audience answer from their own history."

Rhee, standing by the whiteboard, said nothing. She watched Ji-eun.

Ji-eun cleared her throat and tapped the pen twice against her taped finger.

"Can I try it once?" she asked.

The manager started, "Ji-eun-ssi, we discussed improvisation parameters-"

"I heard you." Ji-eun did not raise her voice. "I'm asking for one read."

Cho shrugged. "One read won't kill us."

"You keep saying that," the manager muttered.

They reset. Cho read the brother lines with more bite this time. Ji-eun listened, eyes on the page, then looked up when her cue came.

"You keep asking why I set three bowls," she said in character. Her voice stayed soft but landed clean at the back of the room. "I set three because my hand still remembers him. Your hand forgot faster."

Nobody moved for a second.

Ina felt the room recalibrate. The line was better than what she had suggested, less explanatory, sharper at the cut.

Cho lowered his script. "That passes censors if we don't add context," he said. "It reads domestic."

The manager was already typing into her phone. "I need legal review before confirmation."

Ji-eun looked at Ina, not smiling.

"Is this the part where you tell me it's art, not politics?" she asked.

Ina could have lied smoothly. She had built a career on making hard material sound harmless enough for subtitles and broadcast notes. Tonight she did not have that muscle left.

"It is both," she said.

Ji-eun nodded once, as if she had expected exactly that answer.

"Then don't feed me lines you can't defend later," she said. "My contract debt doesn't disappear because a scene feels honest."

That was the first truly personal sentence anyone had spoken in the room.

The manager snapped, "We are not discussing contract debt in a script session."

Ji-eun kept her eyes on Ina. "I am."

Cho broke the tension by slapping his script closed.

"Fine," he said. "We lock this version for table read and see if Standards screams tomorrow. If they scream, we trim the edge and keep the bone."

Rhee checked her watch and moved to the door. "Ten minutes. Then we go."

As assistants redistributed pages, Ina sat very still and listened to the room noise: laptop keys, plastic chairs dragging, someone tearing open another ramen lid. Normal production sounds. Ordinary labor. She had spent years in rooms like this, where meaning got tuned by fatigue and deadlines and whoever still had enough power at 3 a.m. to say no.

Now she knew another layer had been soldered under the table.

Ji-eun stood and stretched her shoulders like a dancer between takes. Up close, Ina saw two small crescent scars near Ji-eun's left thumb, old cuts from opening package tape with bare hands. Ji-eun followed Ina's gaze and flexed her fingers.

"Box cutters are always missing when you need one," she said. "So you improvise and bleed."

Ina almost smiled. "That sounds like this industry."

"That sounds like this country," Ji-eun said.

The manager called her name sharply. Ji-eun rolled her eyes so quickly only Ina caught it, then walked back to her chair with stage-perfect posture snapping into place before she sat.

Rhee touched Ina's elbow. "Car."

In the elevator down, neither of them spoke. Rhee unwrapped a butterscotch candy and offered one. Ina shook her head.

Outside, dawn had started to gray the sky above the river bridges. Delivery scooters cut through early traffic, headlights flashing over wet asphalt. Ina got into the back seat of a black sedan that smelled like vinyl and old pine air freshener.

Her phone buzzed.

Unknown number. Secure message app she did not remember installing.

`Welcome to Restoration Council probation.`

`Stipend transfer pending via Haneul Cultural Foundation affiliate.`

`Medical arrears partial payment initiated.`

A second message arrived from Seoul St. Mary's billing office two minutes later.

`Payment posted. Updated balance available in portal.`

Relief hit first, hard and physical, like dropping a crate she had been carrying for weeks without admitting how heavy it was. Shame followed so fast it felt rehearsed.

She opened the contract packet again in the car's thin morning light. Page eleven held her signature, slightly slanted because the conference table had been uneven. At the bottom, below the legal boilerplate, she had written one extra sentence before signing. Rhee had not stopped her.

`Contractor reserves refusal of directives intended to produce direct civilian harm.`

Kwon had initialed beside it in neat blue ink.

The addendum was weak. It could be interpreted into dust by any lawyer with patience and motive. She knew that. She also knew she had needed to write it anyway, if only to leave a breadcrumb for the person she wanted to believe she still was.

The sedan turned onto the bridge toward Mapo. Water below was flat pewter under dawn cloud. Ina watched her reflection in the window drift over the river and superimpose on cranes and apartment blocks and construction hoardings promising luxury towers to people who did not need to count coins before buying transit fare.

She pressed the burn scar on her left wrist with her thumb until the old skin whitened.

I can do this clean, she told herself.

Not clean like innocent. Clean like controlled.

She could write subtext without naming targets. She could shape longing without asking anyone to bleed for it. She could take their money, buy time, stabilize her father's rent, keep her mother's apartment from being eaten by debt, and still keep one part of herself out of their machinery.

That was the story she built before sunrise.

By the time the car stopped outside her building, she had revised it three times and believed none of the revisions fully. She carried the contract upstairs anyway.
