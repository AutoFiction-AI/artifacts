# Chapter 15

Cardboard rasped against church kitchen glass as Ina sealed each window against the alley light.

She had burned two route changes to lose the black sedan and arrived with just enough steadiness left for work her hands could finish.

Onion and old freezer burn clung to the cardboard. Hyejin had pulled it from behind the dry-goods shelf, where she kept broken-down produce boxes tied with twine. She stood beside Ina in her apron, reading glasses knocking softly against the buttons as she moved, and boiled water she forgot to pour three times.

"Drink," Hyejin said at last, shoving a mug into Ina's hand. "You look like you got ironed flat."

Ina drank because her aunt was watching. The tea was too hot and too sweet and tasted faintly of dish soap because the church never replaced its cracked mugs. A stripe of fluorescent light buzzed overhead. The strip had been buzzing for months and Hyejin kept saying she would ask Mr. Paik to change it, and she never did, because there was always another emergency first.

On the stainless prep table, Ina had arranged three burners, one old laptop with a missing F key, and a legal pad she had torn into columns: `contacts`, `status`, `last signal`, `location confidence`. Her handwriting started neat and turned jagged by the second page.

She had not slept properly since the windowless flat. She still felt that room in her wrists: the pressure mark of plastic cuffs, the heat of server fans, the rhythm of a steel chair leg scraping every time someone entered to ask the same question with different threat language. She had given them one backup address. She could still hear herself giving it, voice steady like she was reading subtitles in a sound booth.

The last surviving path sat in her coat pocket now, folded into the back pages of Hyejin's hymnbook code, soft at the corners from being touched too often. She checked for it every ten minutes like an old woman checking for house keys.

Minjae still had not answered by 20:11.

Two minutes later Ji-eun sent a voice note from Busan with harbor horns under her breathing. "There are people at the terminal checking IDs. Are they ours? Are they theirs? Just tell me if I run."

Ina typed: `Don't move. Indoors. No livestream. Keep curtains closed.`

The message sat with a spinning clock icon and then failed to send.

Every screen in the kitchen glitched at 20:17.

The church's old TV, tuned low to KBN news, froze on a weather graphic. The laptop's browser window went gray. One burner, the one Minjae had modified with a bent antenna and electrical tape, flashed a system alert in red: `Emergency Civil Defense Broadcast`.

Then the same face appeared on all three devices.

Lieutenant General Park Seong-hoon, vice chair of the Joint Chiefs, appeared in full dress uniform under the blue-and-gold emblem backdrop from the Defense Ministry briefing room. Ina had subtitled him before. He spoke in clipped press language, with bureaucratic care that irritated viewers and reassured them at the same time.

This version of him breathed too loudly and blinked too little.

"At 20:05," the deepfake Park said, "North Korean forward artillery units crossed demarcation markers near Yeoncheon under weather concealment. Civilian citizens in districts north of the river should evacuate to designated shelters immediately. Reservists will report through the National Defense app within one hour. This is not an exercise."

The lower-third graphics looked authentic at first glance. The date stamp used wrong kerning. The subtitle line break on "designated shelters" was wrong. The emergency code sequence had a typo. Tiny machine mistakes hid inside official confidence.

Ina felt the bottom of her mug tilt against her palm and realized her hand was shaking hard enough to spill.

"It's live everywhere," she said.

Hyejin leaned over her shoulder and squinted. "He looks like he got skin from somebody younger."

Ina almost laughed. She did not. "It's Mercury. They synchronized the feeds."

As if to confirm it, phones began screaming up and down the alley, one after another, then all at once, emergency tones stacked in ugly harmonics. A bus braked outside with a metallic scream that made the kitchen windows rattle. Somewhere nearby, a child started wailing and did not stop.

Ina switched to the reserve network panel. Channel after channel had identical clip injection points within seven seconds. Public broadcaster, two cable news stations, transit signage API, the military reserve app, and at least one major portal homepage. Whoever launched had rehearsal logs. They had timing maps down to frame intervals.

She opened her contacts sheet and started calling in order.

The two route couriers in Eunpyeong rang out to carrier hold music and then dropped.

The volunteer nurse on the Bukhansan shelter route sent her straight to a full voicemail box.

Only one person answered: a high school teacher she used to pass package drops through, Mr. Yoon, whose hobby was building radios from surplus parts and whose first question in any crisis was always about battery life.

"Mr. Yoon, stay inside," Ina said. "Don't move students unless there's direct local instruction from district office."

"I was welding a wire when this came through," he said, voice crackling. "The app says artillery risk. The TV says chemical warning now. Which lie are we buying?"

"Neither. Wait for district confirmation." She looked at the panel again. New injected text was rolling under the fake general: `Secondary contamination risk near water plants.` "Keep your masks ready anyway. And fill containers while you still have pressure."

"You sound like your mother when she did election coverage," he said. "That's not a compliment."

The line died.

Outside, engines revved and doors slammed. The alley was too narrow for two cars abreast, but people were trying anyway, headlights shoving against each other while neighbors shouted over hoods. A delivery rider clipped a side mirror and went down, scooter skidding sparks.

Ina called Minjae again.

A stranger grabbed the call over sirens, shouted that she had the wrong number, and cut the line.

She called an NIS number she hated having memorized.

Agent Choi picked up on the fourth ring. He never said hello. He spoke with a permanent sinus blockage and a collector's obsession with expensive fountain pens; in interrogation he had clicked a burgundy Sailor cap on and off until Ina wanted to bite him.

"You're seeing it," he said.

"It's your network," Ina snapped. "Shut it down."

"If it were ours, it would be cleaner."

"People are evacuating based on a fake mobilization order."

"People were primed to evacuate before the clip. Election week did that work for free." Papers rustled on his end. "Telecom is preparing a throttle in ten minutes."

Ina stared at the signal map. "A throttle will make this worse."

"Everything is worse." He lowered his voice. "Don't go public tonight. You're already in several draft packets."

"What packets?"

"Unverified foreign-collusion narratives. You don't want to know the details until we can—"

The call cut out mid-sentence.

There were no signal bars, and there was no fallback route.

She pushed back from the table and grabbed her coat. "I'm going out."

Hyejin caught her sleeve. Arthritic fingers, stronger than they looked, dug into wool. "You're not running into traffic because a screen told everybody to panic."

"People are going to get crushed in subway entrances."

"Then we open downstairs and we keep them where we can see them."

Hyejin was already moving. Fear made her practical. She shoved stockpots off the side stove, dumped two bags of rice into boiling water, and yelled up the stairwell for Deacon Kim. Her voice carried like a market vendor's despite her age, rolling over the noise outside in command sentences.

Within twenty minutes, the church basement had thirty-two people in it.

A grandmother with a perm flattening under sweat kept counting rosary beads though she said she had quit church years ago. A middle-school boy in soccer shorts sat on a folded table hugging a rabbit backpack and trying to hide his crying from his younger brother. A taxi driver with a cracked wedding ring and one shoe still wet from stepping into an open gutter handed out convenience-store kimbap like communion wafers, saying he could not eat when his stomach turned and somebody should.

Ina moved between them with a marker and an old attendance sheet, taking names, medication needs, missing relatives.

When the basement reached forty-one people, someone banged on the side door hard enough to shake dust from the ceiling pipes.

Constable Bae came in backward, hauling a woman by both forearms. Bae was younger than Ina had expected, with a cartoon bee patch sewn over a tear in his left boot and pepper spray crust drying on his collar. He kept glancing at his phone screen between dispatch updates; a wallpaper photo of a preschool kid in dinosaur pajamas flashed each time it lit.

"She fell on the station steps," he said, breathless. "Her ankle bent wrong. We got no ambulance."

The woman, fish blood on her pants from a market crate that had split over her, spat at him. "I didn't fall. You people shoved everybody into one staircase."

"Yes," Bae said, not arguing. "And now we need a chair."

Hyejin produced one. The woman sat and cried without sound while a volunteer wrapped her ankle with a torn dish towel and tape.

Above ground, the sirens changed. Ina recognized the interval pattern from riot control deployment drills. She hated that she recognized it.

She climbed to the sanctuary level to get a signal through the stained-glass window gap and looked down toward the arterial road. Traffic had locked into a metal knot. Two buses stood jackknifed across lanes, and between them a man in a puffer vest pounded on bus doors and screamed for someone named Hana. A scooter burned near the curb with the tight, oily flame of plastic parts.

Her burner vibrated once.

`MJ: WHERE`

She typed with numb thumbs: `Mapo church basement. You?`

The sent checkmark never arrived; the message sat gray with `pending` until the burner timed out.

A second message came from an unknown number: `Civic notice: suspected foreign-linked media operatives active. Report unfamiliar neighbors. Cooperation protects families.`

The message included the Seoul Metropolitan logo, slightly pixelated.

Hyejin came up carrying a stack of blankets, fabric stiff from cedar storage and old laundry soap. "Basement is full. We can use choir room next."

"You should lock the side entrance," Ina said.

"If we lock it, somebody freezes out there."

"If we leave it open, somebody follows the panic packets and drags people out by rumor."

Hyejin held Ina's gaze for a long second, then nodded once. "Half lock. Chain on, latch off."

That was how Hyejin made most decisions in crisis: split the risk and keep moving.

Before any hotline could reduce it to a number, the side chain rattled at 22:04 and Constable Bae stumbled in with a man over his shoulder.

"Dialysis patient from Seodaemun queue," Bae said, bent double and gasping. "Clinic locked us out."

The man was seventy-four, Im Byung-ho. Fistula tape still clung to his forearm. His fist was balled around a queue ticket softened by sweat. His lips were already going blue.

Ina hit her knees on the tile and started compressions. His coat stank of diesel and winter rain. On the fourth push she felt cartilage shift under her palms.

"No pulse," the volunteer nurse said. "Ambulance says forty minutes minimum."

Hyejin's van sat behind the parish hall, hidden for leak transport. If Ina sent it now, she burned their clean plate and their fastest exit. If she did not, Mr. Im died under the church lights while they protected evidence.

"Take the van," Ina said. "Now."

Bae and the nurse hauled him up in a folding chair. Ina ran with them to the alley, fingers pressed to the side of his throat all the way to the curb and feeling nothing.

While she wiped his blood from her hands with freezer paper at 22:19, her burner buzzed: a warehouse worker in Guro had his chest crushed when crowd pressure snapped a temporary shelter gate inward.

Before she could cap the marker, another call came at 22:31: a twelve-year-old asthmatic in Mangwon went into respiratory arrest after his inhaler ran out and his mother could not cross three blocked intersections to reach a pharmacy.

Each death was preventable. Each one.

Ina wrote each line on the legal pad with the same black marker she had used for ration counts. The marker kept slipping where Mr. Im's blood had dried across her thumb. She kept writing.

Telecom throttling had started by 23:00. Messages took minutes or never arrived. Calls connected and dissolved into robotic echoes. The city shrank to whatever block your voice could cross.

Around midnight, the deepfake clip mutated.

Edited versions appeared with new subtitles, accusing "internal sympathizers" of aiding border infiltration. One cut inserted a still image of a bus explosion from ten years earlier, color-corrected to look current. Another overlaid a fake geotag near the Han River and told civilians to avoid bridges, which drove even more cars into smaller streets and emergency lanes.

Ina watched one version loop on a shelter volunteer's phone while two elderly men argued over whether to walk home before curfew and ended up shoving each other hard enough that one hit a pew and split his eyebrow.

Wet wool steamed under coats, instant ramyeon broth filmed the air, and fear sweat slicked every sleeve.

People had stopped asking if the clip was real by 01:14. They asked only which rumor would kill them slower.

The basement lights flickered twice, steadied, then flickered again. Somebody started praying out loud. Somebody else told her to shut up. Hyejin banged a soup ladle against a stockpot and assigned chores in escalating volume until everyone had something to do with their hands.

Ina went back upstairs and tried Minjae again.

His phone did not ring this time; the relay returned a three-note error and dropped her back into silence.

The door chain rattled in three sharp jerks at 02:07, a pattern Ina recognized from their old route meetings.

She ran.

Minjae stood in the entry hall with rain in his hair and pepper-spray tears dried white on his cheeks. His jacket was unzipped, shirt half out, and there was blood on his cuff that was not his own. The scar on his shoulder, the one Ina had traced with her thumb months ago, showed through a ripped seam.

He did not hug her. He did not ask permission.

"Where were you?" he said.

"Here. Trying to keep people from stampeding to nowhere." Ina looked past him into the alley. "What happened?"

"Sweep happened." His laugh broke on the second syllable. "You remember sweeps, right? Door kicks, zip ties, floor face down, everybody's a traitor until paperwork clears."

Hyejin appeared with a towel and froze when she saw his face. "Minjae-ya, come sit."

"No." He wiped his nose with the back of his hand. "My sister's gone."

The words landed clean and hard, no room for mishearing.

Ina stepped closer. "Tell me from the beginning."

"Beginning is they hit Mullae at midnight. Third and fourth blocks first, then the row houses by the machine shops. They had addresses printed. They had apartment numbers." He shoved a crumpled paper at her. It was a photocopied intake sheet with blurred headers and a handwritten district seal. "They called it a protective relocation sweep."

The paper had gone soft with rainwater, and copier toner dusted her fingertips. On the back was a list of resident IDs, some crossed out in red.

"She texted me at 00:42," Minjae said. "She sent a photo of our stove because she forgot if she turned it off. You know how she gets. She worries about stupid domestic shit in the middle of hell." His voice cracked and then sharpened. "At 01:03 she called. I heard boots in the hallway and one of her kids screaming because somebody stepped on a drawing board. Then dead air."

His sister's kids came back to Ina in details instead of faces: the cartoon-peach sleep mask one of them wore when alley neon leaked through the curtains, the zip pouch full of bus transfer cards sorted by color because the world felt less random that way.

"Where are the kids?" Ina asked.

"Neighbor grabbed them. They're at his cousin's floor. They keep asking why their mother left without shoes." Minjae swallowed hard enough she saw his throat work. "She didn't leave without shoes. They took her while she was boiling anchovy broth. Pot burned to black."

Hyejin set the towel on a chair and took over without asking. "Eat first, then we move."

"I can't eat."

"Then chew and spit. You need salt."

He stared at her, then took the rice ball she pushed into his hand and bit once like it was punishment.

Ina spread the photocopy on the prep table under the buzzing light. Some names had check marks. Some had arrows to handwritten codes: `S-4`, `screening`, `transfer pending`. The header said `Joint Stability Taskforce`.

"This isn't standard police format," Ina said.

"I know what police paperwork looks like."

"These codes are intelligence intake tags."

Minjae's eyes snapped up. "How do you know that?"

The leverage shifted, swift and ugly.

She met his stare. She could lie. She did not. "They used similar tags in Hongje. During detention."

"So they used your server and your lessons both."

The sentence hit like a slap because it was close to true.

Ina forced herself to keep her voice level. "I gave up one backup location under threat. I didn't give them family addresses."

"Your one location connected route logs. Route logs have family addresses when people are stupid and tired and trying to deliver medicine before curfew."

He slammed his palm on the table. The burner phones jumped.

Hyejin stepped between them, not dramatic, just present. "You can fight after we find her. Right now you need a place that still has lists."

Minjae looked away first. "District gym in Yeongdeungpo had intake tables. They moved people out before dawn. Nobody says where."

"Then we go there and make somebody say it," Ina said.

They were in Hyejin's van by 03:05, the one with a dented rear door, a fresh scrape down the passenger side from the Seodaemun run, and a rosary hanging from the mirror that hit the windshield on potholes with a soft tick-tick sound.

Every tap of the rosary against glass made Ina count without meaning to. Four taps, inhale. Four taps, exhale. If she let panic choose her timing, she would start making the same category errors that had already filled half her pad.

Roadblocks turned the city into a maze of impatient headlights and uniforms. At one intersection, riot police redirected traffic away from a bridge while a loudspeaker repeated, "Remain calm and follow official instructions," in the same synthetic female voice that did subway etiquette announcements. Nobody sounded calm. People shouted district names through cracked windows like passwords.

Near Mullae, they passed Minjae's block. The machine-shop shutters were down. Every third door had red tape cross-sealed with municipal stickers. A burned pot sat on one windowsill, black ring up the side, exactly as he had said.

The red tape and handwritten unit numbers flashed her back to Hongje for one bad second: gloves, clipboards, names read out like inventory. She dug her thumbnail into her palm until the memory snapped and the windshield came back into focus.

They reached Yeongdeungpo District Auxiliary Gym at 03:42.

Inside, fluorescent lights burned at full brightness over folding tables and plastic chairs. Bleach stung the back of Ina's throat, and exhaustion dragged at every movement in the room. Volunteers in orange vests sorted paper stacks with frantic precision.

Ina scanned exits, camera domes, and badge colors before she reached Table C. If this desk froze, she needed a second point of entry ready in under a minute.

At Table C, a municipal clerk with glitter nail polish chipped to the quick and a hand brace on her right wrist took Minjae's resident numbers without looking up.

"Family name?" she asked.

"Kang," he said. "Household from Mullae three-block row houses. Adult female, dependent minors already separated."

Her fingers paused over the keyboard.

"I need given name," she said.

Minjae gave it. Ina heard it and held it privately.

The clerk typed, frowned, typed again. "No release log under that combination."

"Try screening backlog," Ina said.

"I did." The clerk rubbed her brace with her left thumb. "There were two transfers from that block around 02:10. Destination code blank."

"Blank how?"

"Blank like above my pay grade." She lowered her voice. "If code is blank it means agency handoff, not hospital."

Minjae leaned in. "Which agency?"

"Sir, don't do this here. There are cameras."

"My sister got pulled in a fake-war sweep and you have a blank box where her life should be."

The clerk finally looked up. She had mascara smudged under both eyes and the exhausted focus of someone who had been useful too long. "I've got a brother on reserve call tonight. I'm not your enemy. I'm telling you what I can tell you: this list won't save her. Get a lawyer with security clearance or someone inside."

Minjae laughed once, raw. "Inside is how we got here."

He turned away before security volunteers could notice his volume.

Outside the gym, dawn had not arrived yet, but the sky had gone from black to bruised violet. A coffee cart at the curb was doing business like a war economy. Ina bought three cans of hot sweet coffee from the vendor, a woman in a padded vest who kept clicking a metal tally counter each time she sold one, as if counting gave shape to chaos.

Minjae took the can and did not drink.

"You said assessment," he said after a long silence. "Assess this: last ping 01:03, sweep vans out of Mullae by 01:27, intake field blank by 02:10. That's not missing. That's routed."

"Maybe she's in secondary hold—no. Blank code means agency handoff. I know."

"Yeah, you know." He crushed the can in his fist, metal cracking. "Don't give me subtitle optimism. After Hongje your lane was simple: stay dark, protect the dead route, wait for leak window. Instead you built triage in Mapo while they cleared my block stairwell by stairwell."

"I was keeping people alive."

"You were stabilizing whoever was in front of you." He stepped closer, voice low and shaking. "My sister was two bus transfers away and inside a database you already burned."

That was another near-truth. Worse than accusation, because it fit.

Ina looked at the gym entrance where families queued with paper cups and stiff shoulders. "If I apologize now, does it bring her back?"

"No."

"Then use me." She forced each word to stay steady. "You want inside access? I've got one dead route left and names for people who traded intake lists. We work, or we bury people separately."

He stared at her long enough that she counted four siren passes on the main road.

"I don't trust you," he said.

"Don't trust me. Trust the logs."

He gave a short, wrecked laugh and wiped his face with both hands. "At least that sounds honest."

He reached into his jacket and pulled out a USB drive wrapped in chewing-gum foil. "I copied this from a taskforce terminal while they were yelling at each other about bridge closures. Partial pickup logs, maybe one hour window. If you burn this, I bury you."

"Fair," Ina said, and took it.

On the drive, back in the van with laptops balanced on their knees, they found forty-seven intake entries from three districts and eight destination codes. Two codes mapped to known municipal shelters. Three mapped to temporary police holding centers. Three were alphanumeric strings with no public directory match.

Minjae's sister was under one of the unknown codes.

Morning broadcasts had shifted tone by 06:20.

The emergency clip stopped replaying. Official stations now ran calm male anchors in navy ties, promising stability and condemning "malicious synthetic media." A red banner crawled under every channel: `Foreign Influence Investigation Underway`.

Then Ina's face appeared.

The first image was a screenshot of her leaving MiraeWave during the chapter-six raid, eyes narrowed against camera flash.

The second image was an old passport photo, cropped too tight.

The third image was a graphic linking her name to shell companies and fabricated remittance routes, with a headline that used the phrase `foreign narrative asset` in quotation marks designed to look like evidence.

The packet propagated faster than correction channels could breathe. By 06:34, neighborhood chat groups were sharing her image with warnings. By 06:41, one major portal had a liveblog citing anonymous intelligence sources. By 06:52, a conservative radio host called her "the woman who wrote panic into our bloodstream for foreign pay."

Ina stared at her own face on the van screen until features became geometry: eyebrow line, scar at wrist in one grainy still, mouth half open like she was guilty and surprised to be caught.

"They seeded this before midnight," she said. "Choi warned me about draft packets."

"So warn back," Minjae said. "Go live."

"If I go live now, I confirm I still have active channels. I burn the only leak path we have left."

"People already think you're a spy."

"People can think whatever they need to survive breakfast. If I answer in real time, they trace the reply to Hyejin's network, then to you, then to her kids." She swallowed the next sentence and said it anyway. "And to your sister, if she's still in a transport queue and not off-grid yet."

He slammed his hand against the dashboard once, then gripped the steering wheel and breathed through his teeth.

"Kwon will go on air formal as a funeral and call for unity," he said. "He'll pretend restraint while he feeds them your bones."

He was right.

Director Kwon appeared at a podium at 07:10 with the national flag and a civic-seal backdrop. His silver hair was perfect. His suit was untouched by overnight panic.

"Our nation has endured orchestrated psychological aggression," he said in measured cadence. "We will respond within law and with composure. Individuals who collaborated with foreign information organs will be identified through due process. Citizens should refrain from rumor and trust institutions."

He did not say Ina's name. He did not need to.

Hyejin called while Kwon was still speaking.

"Come back," she said. Her voice was rough from hours of shouting over frightened people. "Basement families are leaving and fighting in the alley over taxi priority. We got one old man with chest pain and no ambulance for forty minutes. Also somebody pasted your photo on our gate."

"We're coming," Ina said.

They drove in silence.

At a red light near Hapjeong that never turned green because officers were manually waving lanes, Minjae spoke without looking at her.

"Her kids keep asking if their mother forgot them."

Ina watched rain bead on the windshield and get smeared by overworked wipers. "What did you tell them?"

"I told them she's stubborn and she bites people who touch her stove." His mouth twitched once. "That made the little one stop crying for a minute."

Back at the church, somebody had spray-painted `TRAITOR` across the side wall in black enamel that dripped like motor oil.

Inside, the basement had thinned to twenty people. Those who stayed looked wrung out, less panicked, more ashamed. Crisis had moved from adrenaline to accounting.

Ina sat at the prep table and opened a new page on the legal pad.

`DEAD`

`MISSING`

`DETAINED`

`DISPLACED`

She built the list from calls, from scribbled notes passed by volunteers, from Minjae's copied logs, from one nurse's photo of a triage board, from rumor she could half-verify and rumor she refused to write down yet. Her palms still stung in the shape of Mr. Im's chest.

Confirmed dead stood at eleven by 08:05, including Im Byung-ho, whom one municipal sheet still listed as `relocated` until Bae shoved a handwritten correction across her table.

She crossed out the count at 09:12 and rewrote it as nineteen.

A fish-market vendor arrived at 09:26 in rubber boots slick with thaw water, dragging a boy whose school blazer was buttoned wrong and streaked with dried broth. She slapped a damp resident card and a convenience-store receipt onto Ina's table.

"Seodaemun stair crush," the woman said. "Dispatch told me to check online. I don't have internet. I have this and his lunch thermos. Tell me if my husband is alive."

The boy hugged a stainless thermos hard enough to leave finger dents in the metal. Anchovy broth dripped from the lid and ran over his knuckles.

Ina checked Bae's folded list against the triage photo and found the same name twice, once misspelled, both entries ending at the same time stamp.

For one weak second she wanted the entries not to match. A mismatch would have meant delay, ambiguity, another hour before she had to break someone open.

"Ms. Lee," Ina said, and hated the clean shape of her own voice, "he was pulled from the station stairs at 08:41. They tried CPR. He didn't make it."

The woman folded at the waist like somebody had kicked out a hinge. The thermos hit tile with a steel crack and rolled under the sink, leaving a hot fish-salt trail.

Ina drew a new column on the ledger: `FAMILY NOTIFIED`.

The marker shook once in her grip before she forced the nib down and held it there until the line stopped wavering.

She looked at Hyejin. "No one gets sent back to portal rumors. We confirm names here, then route them straight to body-ID desks before records get laundered."

A courier in a reflective jacket overheard and slammed his palm on the prep table. "My mother is missing and you want forms?"

Ina stayed between him and the stairwell door. "I want you at the right desk before they move her again. Give me her resident number."

If she let him blow past her now, he would spend the day screaming at the wrong counters while the right ones scrubbed his mother's trail.

He swore at her, then dug through his wallet with shaking fingers and handed over an ID card.

Hyejin was already tearing church bulletin paper into referral slips. "Write fast."

Ina heard the command as relief, not pressure. A task with edges was easier than another face collapsing in front of her.

By noon, every number came with two versions: official and observed. The official count was always lower.

Hyejin set down a plate of cold jeon nobody ate and pointed at the `MISSING` column. "Put ages. Families hear age first."

Ina added ages where she had them.

Each age made the page heavier in her hands, as if the paper itself had absorbed water and could no longer hold a clean fold.

Constable Bae returned around midday with his bee-patched boot and no voice left. He handed Ina a folded note from station triage.

"Your false-evacuation crush at Seodaemun is now four dead," he rasped. "I shouldn't give you this."

"Why are you giving it?"

He looked down at his phone wallpaper kid and then back at her. "Because my wife is six months pregnant and spent the night under a desk at daycare because an app told her artillery was coming. I would like one true document in this city."

Ina nodded once and copied the names.

Agent Choi finally reconnected at 13:20 on an encrypted voice relay that stuttered every few seconds.

"You're trending under hostile labels," he said. "Don't issue statement."

"I didn't plan to."

"Good. We can maybe contain prosecutorial timeline if you remain nonreactive."

"Contain for whom?"

A pause. Paper shuffling again. He always handled paper when lying.

"For everyone still breathing," he said.

"Minjae's sister is missing under agency handoff code. Can you trace?"

"I can't on this line. Send code through dead protocol."

"Dead protocol was compromised in Hongje."

"Then pray your church cipher still works." His voice flattened. "And Ina? If you post denial now, the packet team wins twice. They want you desperate and traceable."

The line cut.

Ina sat with the silent phone against her ear until Hyejin took it from her hand and set it down gently.

"You can't out-shout a loudspeaker with one throat," Hyejin said.

"I know."

"Do it later when it hurts them, not when it soothes you."

That was Hyejin's ethics in one sentence: utility first, absolution optional.

Minjae spent the afternoon on the floor against the pantry door, knees up, laptop open, trying route code variations with bloodshot concentration. Every twenty minutes he called his sister's kids from a different number so location tracing would have to work harder. He spoke softly on those calls, voice almost unrecognizable.

"Did you eat?" he asked the younger kid once.

A small voice crackled back through speaker. Ina heard only two words clearly: "peach mask."

"Yes, wear it," Minjae said. "Wear two if you want."

By evening, rain turned to sleet.

People stopped arriving at the church. People started knocking to ask if the rumor was true that foreign agents had poisoned shelter water. Hyejin sent each one away with a cup from the tap and made them drink in front of her.

Ji-eun's message from the night before finally delivered at 18:03, followed by a new one.

`I saw your face on TV in a noodle shop. Lady next to me said she knew you looked expensive. I laughed and then I threw up in the sink. Tell me if I should cut my hair and disappear again.`

Ina typed: `Stay put. Eat. Charge everything. No public posts.`

Three dots appeared. Disappeared. Returned.

`You always sound calm when everything is burning.`

Ina did not answer that line.

Three Joint Stability Taskforce officers came through the side door at 18:27 in wet black jackets with emergency tags clipped to their collars. The lead officer, Captain Ryu Jin-seok, carried a printed seizure form already signed.

"We're collecting unverified casualty ledgers and source devices," Ryu said. "Panic control order."

Minjae stood before she could answer. "This is a church kitchen, not your data center."

Ryu ignored him and tapped Ina's taped-up board with a gloved finger. "You hand over records now, or we clear this building and screen every person in it."

Ina felt the hymnbook pages in her coat pocket like a hot blade against her ribs. If they searched everyone, they could pull the dead-drop path and the kids' contact trail in one sweep. If she surrendered the board, she gave up twelve hours of chain-of-custody work.

She made the smaller disaster.

"You can take the board and one phone," she said. "You leave families out of it."

Ryu held out an evidence pouch. Ina handed over the burner with Mr. Yoon's call log and Bae's triage photos still inside. Minjae swore under his breath, and the muscle in his jaw jumped hard enough to see.

The officers were gone with the pages by 19:10. The prep tables looked flayed.

The official casualty bulletin published at 19:40: seven dead, thirty-two injured, ninety-one relocated for security screening, with Im Byung-ho still coded as `relocated`.

Ina rebuilt the seized sheet from memory, from damp referral slips, and from the marker notes she had written on her own palm before she handed the board over. By 20:22, the replacement, now taped across two prep tables, held twenty-six confirmed dead, at least fifty-nine injured, one hundred forty-three screening detentions, and thirty-four unresolved missing entries.

Twelve minutes later, Sunhee from the next block came back through the basement door carrying a clear belongings bag from Seodaemun overflow intake: cracked glasses, one house key, and a wallet with water-warped photos still inside. Her brother had been listed as `relocated for screening` on the noon bulletin.

"Then why did the morgue clerk make me sign for this?" she asked, voice scraped hollow.

Ina took the bag. The plastic was warm from Sunhee's grip, tacky with antiseptic residue and old cigarette ash. She matched the ID number to Minjae's copied logs and found the noon status line still unchanged.

She circled the entry so hard the pen tore paper, then flipped to a clean sheet and rewrote the board in a new order: `NAME`, `STATUS`, `SOURCE`, `LAST VERIFIED HUMAN CONTACT`.

"No more total-first updates," she said to Minjae. "We go name by name until their neat bulletin runs out of places to hide."

He looked at the blank wall where the officers had peeled their first board. "You traded a day of proof for one hour of quiet."

"I traded it so they didn't search coats and minors," Ina said. Her throat felt scraped raw, but she kept her voice level. "Now we rebuild faster than they can erase."

He nodded once and pulled his laptop closer. "Then we stay up."

Minjae's sister remained unresolved.

When the fluorescent strip finally gave out and the kitchen dropped to amber emergency lighting, Ina lit two candles from the church supply drawer and set them in metal bowls so wax would not drip on records.

She looked at the page where her own name now sat under a new heading she had not meant to write:

`FRAMED NARRATIVE IMPACT`

`- doxxed to neighborhood chats`

`- tagged as foreign asset on three major portals`

`- church gate vandalized`

`- witness reluctance increasing after image circulation`

`- can't rebut without exposing active evidence path`

The last line she underlined twice.

Minjae came to stand across from her, hands braced on the table. He looked older than he had that morning, as if panic had burned through a year's worth of pretense.

"Blame doesn't clear just because we crossed seventy-two names," he said.

"I wasn't trying to clear myself—no, that's not true. Part of me was. Not with you."

"Fine. Keep that part quiet and keep your hands moving. I need your indexing, not your absolution."

"Then keep feeding me codes."

He nodded, once, like signing an ugly contract.

"Then we work tonight," he said. "We map unknown transfers to real doors before dawn."

"No speeches. Just doors."

Hyejin turned off the stairwell light to save power and sat down with them, glasses chain clicking softly in the dim.

They worked with paper because paper could not be throttled, could not be geolocated unless someone physically stole it, could not be overwritten by an elegant lie in a server room.

They had cross-indexed seventy-two names against three source lists by 23:00.

By midnight, they had a probable match between one unknown transfer code and an old civil-defense warehouse used for undocumented labor screenings years earlier.

It was a thin lead. It was not nothing.

Ina tucked the hymnbook pages deeper into her coat and wrote the date at the top of a clean sheet.

Mercury Night had started as a synchronization problem, code and timing and trusted channels. It ended, in her hands, as handwriting and wet coats and a list that kept growing while official numbers stayed neat.

Outside, somebody in the alley cursed at a frozen scooter engine. A train crossed the river in the distance, a low iron sound through sleet.

Ina did not go online to defend herself.

She kept counting the dead, the missing, and the living who might still be pulled back if she did not spend the last evidence path on tonight's fire.

One clean ring broke through the sleet from Seoul St. Mary's emergency desk at 04:37. Ina copied the number in the margin, finished the line she was on, and reached for her coat.
