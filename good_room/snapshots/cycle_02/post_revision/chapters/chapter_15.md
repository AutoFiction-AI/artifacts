# Chapter 15

On the last Wednesday in May, Simone ordered a bourbon neat while Doug counted fourteen beers on the chalkboard behind the counter. He picked one of the fourteen that sounded like a beer and not a statement, and the bartender, who had dark red nails and a silver ring on her thumb, poured it from a tap with a wooden handle. The bar had high ceilings and the kind of exposed brick that meant it had been something else first.

Nine dollars. Doug did the math on what that would be annually if he came here twice a week, which was a thing he did with any purchase over six dollars. It was not a useful calculation. It was just what his brain did.

They sat at a high-top near the windows on tall stools that didn't have backs, which meant Doug had to decide what to do with his posture. He chose upright. Simone settled onto hers as if she'd already assessed the structural integrity and found it adequate.

She didn't have her notebook.

Doug had been watching Simone write in that notebook for almost three months. She wrote in pencil, quickly, in handwriting that tilted so far left the words looked as if they were bracing against the margin. She wrote things Doug said when he didn't know he was saying anything worth writing. She underlined things twice. She crossed things out with single precise lines. The notebook had been on his kitchen table at every session, and now it wasn't here, and the bare stretch of table between them made the dinner feel different before either of them had said a word.

The light through the tall windows was long and warm and the sidewalk outside was full of people who didn't seem to be going anywhere in particular, which Doug supposed was what people did in the Short North on a warm evening. He'd been to this neighborhood four times in his life, counting tonight. Two of those times were just driving through it to somewhere else.

"You look like someone who's never been to a bar," Simone said.

"I've been to bars."

"You look like you're appraising the property."

Doug looked at the windows, the tin ceiling, the long counter with its row of stools. "The ductwork's been painted but not insulated," he said. "You'd get condensation issues in winter."

Simone drank her bourbon. "That's exactly what I mean."

They ordered food. Simone got a salad with something on it Doug couldn't identify from the menu description — it said "shaved fennel" and he took this on faith. Doug got a burger. The waiter was a tall kid, early twenties, with the careful posture of someone who'd recently been told to stand up straighter, and he said "awesome" when Doug ordered, which was not a word Doug associated with a hamburger.

"How's your week been?" Simone said. It came out flat, more statement than question, what conversation sounded like after enough time together had worn the formal structures smooth.

"Fine," Doug said. "Processed a hail claim. A woman in Reynoldsburg — her roof's twenty-three years old and she wants full replacement cost. The policy caps it at actual cash value for roofs over twenty."

"How does that work?"

Doug explained. He explained the depreciation schedule, the inspection requirements, the specific way you calculated the remaining useful life of a twenty-three-year-old asphalt shingle roof. Simone listened with the expression she wore when she was listening professionally, except this wasn't professional, which made Doug aware that he was talking about roof depreciation on what was either a dinner between colleagues or a dinner between two people who no longer had a reason to be colleagues.

He stopped. "Sorry."

"Don't apologize. That's the first interesting thing anyone's said to me today."

"You need to meet more people."

"I've met plenty of people. Most of them are funnier than you on purpose and less interesting."

Their food arrived. The burger was fine. Simone's salad had the fennel on it, which looked like translucent strips of something that didn't want to be eaten. She ate it with the steady, unbothered pace of a person who ate salads at bars and didn't consider this remarkable.

For a while they didn't talk. The bar was filling in around them — a midweek crowd, which in the Short North meant young people in clothes that appeared to have been chosen with intention, and a few older couples who had the comfortable silence of people who'd been married long enough that dinner was a proximity arrangement, not an event. Doug could hear the music now, something with a guitar and a woman's voice that was probably famous and that he would never be able to identify.

"I keep thinking about Detroit," Doug said.

Simone looked up from her salad. "Don't turn it into a postmortem."

"I'm not." He watched the foam ring slide down inside his glass. "I'm saying eight accidental minutes isn't an hour."

"No," Simone said. "It isn't."

She swirled the bourbon in her glass and watched the liquid settle. Doug watched her. For almost three months he'd been noticing small things: how she held a pencil, how her whole body tipped slightly forward when she listened, how she used her ring finger to push her hair behind her ear. At some point the noticing had shifted from professional attention to something else. He didn't know when. It had happened one Tuesday at a time, until he was carrying around useless information about her body: which finger moved her hair, how long she held a glass near her mouth before she drank.

"Can I ask you something?" Doug said.

"Sure."

"Why'd you stop performing?"

Simone set her glass down. She didn't answer right away, which was unusual. Simone answered things quickly, a habit from two decades in writers' rooms where silence meant you agreed with whatever had just been said and your joke was going in the trash.

"I didn't know you knew I performed," she said.

"Layla mentioned it. When she was telling me about you. She said you'd done the circuit."

"Layla said that?"

"Those were her words."

Simone picked up her bourbon again but didn't drink it. She held it near her chin, and Doug could smell the whiskey from across the table, sweet and sharp, and he thought about how his own relationship with alcohol had become a negotiation since the toast — one beer, always one, always counted, always the awareness of what happened the last time he'd stopped counting.

"I did stand-up for eleven years," Simone said. "Improv for three of those. Some sketch. Chicago first, then New York."

"Were you good?"

"Technically, yeah. I could write a set. I could work a room. I understood structure — where to put the turn, how to build a callback, how to use silence. All the craft stuff."

"But."

"I was miserable." She pushed a piece of fennel to the edge of her plate with her fork. "And I don't mean nervous. Nervous is fine. Nervous burns off. I mean there was a room over a sports bar in Chicago where the curtain always smelled like fryer oil, and the stage lights were so hot I'd feel sweat start under my bra before I got to the second joke, and I still knew exactly where the laugh was coming. There was a landlord bit that hit every Thursday. Same inhale. Same guy in front slapping the table on the tag. By the end I could feel the laugh arrive in my body before I opened my mouth, and that should've been useful information, but mostly it made me want to peel my skin off."

Doug said nothing. He sat motionless on his stool, the way he did when somebody stopped circling and said the thing they had meant from the start.

"The laughs were the worst part," Simone said. "Because they were real. I'd come offstage with my thumb sticky from the tape on the mic and go sit in the bathroom until enough people had left that I didn't have to hear good set on the way out. One night I got in my car and drove up Lake Shore for forty minutes because if I'd gone straight home I would've had to admit I was choosing that on purpose. After a while I started cancelling spots. Then I started taking writing jobs. Then I stopped calling people back. Writing for other people felt cleaner. I could hear what was funny about someone else and put it in their mouth, and if it worked it was theirs. Not mine. I didn't have to stand there and own it." She said this looking at her glass, not at Doug. There was a small crease between her eyebrows that he'd seen before — she got it when she was concentrating on a note she was writing — but he'd never seen it aimed at her own speech. She was concentrating on being honest, and the effort was visible.

"How long have you been writing?"

"Twenty years, give or take. Three late-night shows. A pilot that never aired. A bunch of corporate stuff I'd rather not get into. Two podcasts." She paused. "Your material."

"My material doesn't work."

"It doesn't, no." She set her fork down. "Your voice doesn't go onto a page. I haven't figured out why."

They sat with that for a moment. Outside, the light was finally softening, the sun dropping behind the buildings on the west side of High Street. The bar had gotten louder — someone had turned the music up or the people had gotten drunker or both. The bartender's silver ring tapped against a glass as she poured.

"I can build it for somebody else," Simone said. "You mutter one true thing and a room goes with you. I'm still a little sore about that."

Doug thought about this. He pictured Simone in her twenties in a club in Chicago, saying lines she knew would work while some part of her stood off to the side and waited for it to end. Then he pictured himself in Pittsburgh after the pool story, the microphone heavy in his hand, the room going flat in front of him. The two failures were different, but they felt bad in the same place.

"Probably the same amount of worse," he said.

Simone laughed. It was short, more breath than voice, and she covered her mouth with her hand and then took her hand away.

"What?" Doug said.

She shook her head. "Nothing."

The burger was gone and Simone was on her second bourbon and Doug was still on his one beer, now warm, which he drank anyway because leaving a beer unfinished was wasteful in a way he couldn't articulate but felt strongly about.

Simone was talking about a comedian she'd written for years ago, a woman with timing so exact Simone could feel the laugh coming before the line was finished. "Third joke, first tag, little pause, then the word," she said. "Every night. Same place. Same laugh." She tore a piece of bread from the basket that had come with Doug's burger and that neither of them had touched until now. "Then the lights would come up and everybody'd put on their coats and leave exactly the same as they arrived. Dead."

"Dead how?"

"Like the room knew its part," Simone said. She rolled the bread between her fingers. "Laugh there. Then there. Everybody safe."

"Safe from what?"

"From saying what the laugh was actually about." She looked at him over the rim of her glass. "A real laugh isn't approval. It's what comes out when your body knows something before your manners do."

"At the wedding nobody knew where it was going," Doug said.

"No."

"Brendan did his speech. Everybody cried in the right spots. Then I got up there and started talking about the centerpieces and Megan's mother and that table and whatever else came out." He could feel the sequence as he said it, the heat in his face from that night, the looseness in his mouth. "I wasn't steering it. I was already saying it before I'd decided to say it."

"Right," Simone said. "You got there before the room could start behaving again."

"So they laughed because I was rude."

"Partly. Mostly they laughed because they knew. About the mother. About the table. About marriage, probably. You said the thing they were all busy not saying."

Doug rubbed the wet ring his beer had left on the table with his thumb. "When I do a set, they can hear me coming," he said. "Even if the joke's decent. They know I'm about to ask for something."

Simone nodded once.

"Yeah. The toast didn't ask. It showed up drunk and said it anyway."

Doug looked down at the table. The wet ring from his glass had already started drying at the edges. "So performance is the warning label."

"Something like that."

He waited for the part where she'd tell him what to do instead. She picked at the bread.

"You don't have a fix," he said.

"No."

"That's what my mother would do," Doug said. "Just stare at me. Then ask if I was eating enough."

Simone laughed, low and from the chest, and covered her mouth with her hand before letting it drop.

"Your mother," Simone said, and shook her head.

"She said I'd been polite for forty years and that was the first time I sounded like myself. She was eating a dinner roll when she said it."

"That's a good detail. The dinner roll."

"It's not a detail. She was eating a dinner roll."

"I know. That's why it's good."

They sat there. The bar was loud now, the midweek crowd settled in, and Doug could hear someone at a table behind them talking about a television show with the passionate specificity that people reserved for things that didn't matter, and he could hear the bartender's ring against glass again, and he could feel the high-top's edge against his forearm and the stool's absence of a back and the warm air that came in every time someone opened the door to the sidewalk.

"The taping's a week from Friday," he said.

"I know."

"I don't know what to do with that."

Simone didn't say anything for a while. She looked at the bread she'd torn and not eaten. Then she said, "I don't either. Not exactly." She finished her bourbon. "I know you can't go out there and do the version of you that's already smiling before he says the line. I know that much."

"So what, I walk out there and hope I accidentally become myself?"

"Maybe. Maybe you stop trying to make the room feel handled." She rubbed her thumb against the side of the glass. "Or maybe that's too clean. I don't know. I don't know how to write the part where you quit asking permission."

"That sounds useful."

"It does. That's how you know it's dangerous."

"Detroit wasn't a method," Simone said. "You were tired, you were pissed off, and there was nothing left to protect. That's all."

"That's not reproducible."

"No," she said. "But it's more precise than 'be truthful,' which is what idiots say when they don't have a note."

"In Cleveland I was wearing a costume."

"You were. You were doing Mulaney-meets-insurance-adjuster. It was terrible."

"Thanks."

"You're welcome."

Doug signaled for the check. It came to forty-seven dollars. He put his card down. Simone reached for her wallet and he said, "I've got it," and she said, "You sure?" and he said, "You came from New York to teach me to be funny and I'm not funny. The least I can do is buy you dinner."

"You're doing it again," she said.

"Doing what?"

"The thing you do. Where you say something true and it comes out funny and you've got no idea."

"I'm not being funny. I'm telling you the truth."

"I know."

They stepped outside. The air was warm enough that his shoulders dropped without permission. High Street was lit up with storefronts and streetlights and the headlights of cars moving slowly through foot traffic, people lining up outside bars and arguing over where to eat.

They walked south. Simone had parked two blocks down, on a side street off High. Doug had parked on a meter around the corner from that. The walk was short and mostly silent. Their shoulders almost touched once at a curb and didn't.

Doug could smell something blooming. He didn't know what it was. He wasn't a plant person. But it was there, sweet and sudden, one of those late-spring Ohio smells that only showed up after the temperature finally committed.

At Simone's car — a gray Subaru with New York plates and a coffee cup visible on the passenger seat — they stopped. Simone got her keys out. She held them in her hand without unlocking the door.

"Thank you for dinner," she said.

"Yeah."

"And for the roof depreciation lecture. That was genuinely interesting."

"It's a twenty-three-year-old roof. There's a conversation to be had."

Simone stood there. Doug stood there. The distance between them was about the width of the car's side mirror. He heard a car door slam up the block and felt his right hand come off his thigh as if it had somewhere to go. Simone still hadn't unlocked the door. Her keys were pressed into her palm, and her eyes dropped to his mouth once, quick, then came back up.

If he stepped forward, he would have to do it on purpose. He didn't know whether he was supposed to touch her arm first or just go in. He didn't know where his hands were supposed to go if she turned away.

Neither of them moved. The moment held a beat longer, enough to make restraint and refusal look identical, and then Simone said, "Goodnight, Doug."

"Goodnight," Doug said.

She unlocked the car. The lights flashed once, orange in the dim of the side street. She got in. She moved the coffee cup from the passenger seat to somewhere he couldn't see. She started the engine, and Doug stepped back onto the curb, and she pulled out and turned left on High and the Subaru's taillights joined the others heading north and then were gone.

Doug walked the half block to his Civic. He sat in the driver's seat and didn't start the car. The meter had expired — he'd fed it two hours and dinner had been closer to two and a half — but when he checked, there was no ticket. The windshield was clean. Small mercies, or Columbus parking enforcement, which was inconsistent on weeknights.

He sat with his hands on the steering wheel until the dome light went out. The taping was a week from Friday. It was not just an hour. It was walking onto a stage on purpose and trying not to look like a man arriving with his hands already up.

He started the car. The engine turned over with the reliable, unbeautiful sound it always made. He drove home to Clintonville, eleven minutes, the route he could do without thinking. He parked in his usual spot. He went inside. He stood in his kitchen.

The kitchen table where he and Simone had worked for three months — her notebook, his voice, the recorder she set between them like a referee — was bare. He'd wiped it down after their last session. It was just a table now, a flat surface in a quiet apartment in a building where nobody knew about the taping or the tour or the two feet of sidewalk outside a gray Subaru that neither of them had crossed.

He took the dinner receipt out of his pocket and flattened it on the table. The total was forty-seven dollars, plus tip. On the blank back he wrote, Good evening, Columbus, because apparently every bad idea started with a greeting. He looked at it for a full ten seconds. The words sat there like advance notice. He crossed them out.

Under that he wrote, The drive to Detroit was five hours and change if traffic held through Toledo. That died too. Once it was written down it became material, and material was the thing the room could hear coming.

He left the receipt on the table beside the pen. The ink shone wet under the kitchen light, two dead openings on cheap white paper. Then he opened a beer and drank it standing at the counter. He thought about Simone's keys, the quick drop of her eyes to his mouth, how she'd said his name, and the way she'd looked at him when she said permission like it was a real object, something a person could set down and still somehow be holding. Then he rinsed the glass, put it on the rack, and went to bed. A week from Friday, he would walk onto the Lincoln Theatre stage and try not to warn the room.
