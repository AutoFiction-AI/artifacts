# Chapter 5

Doug got to Worthington twenty minutes early because he always got anywhere twenty minutes early. It was a claims habit, left over from years of showing up first to assess the site, note the conditions before anyone else's presence altered them. Kelsey had picked the diner on High Street — neutral ground, she'd said, which implied that Doug's apartment and her apartment and every restaurant they'd ever been to together were charged territory now, places where the toast might follow them in. He'd agreed because he agreed with most things Kelsey suggested, and because the last time he'd disagreed with her about a restaurant she'd been fourteen and the argument had been about Applebee's and he'd lost.

The conditions here were a half-full diner with vinyl booths, a specials board in faded chalk that listed a turkey melt and a soup that was either cream of mushroom or cream of chicken depending on how you read the handwriting, and a draft coming under the front door that carried the smell of road salt and wet asphalt. February in Columbus. Not brutal, just relentless — a cold that didn't argue with you, just waited.

He chose a booth near the back, away from the window, which turned out poorly because the kitchen door swung open every thirty seconds and he could hear the fryer pop and plates being stacked and a cook saying something muffled and laughing at it alone. He ordered coffee. The waitress was a woman around his age, heavyset, with a brace on her left wrist and a pen tucked behind each ear — both blue, like she'd been burned once by running out of ink and had decided it wasn't going to happen again. She brought the coffee in a mug that said OHIO IS FOR LOVERS, which was wrong about Ohio in at least two ways Doug could think of.

He wrapped his hands around the mug. The heat was good. He sat there for eleven minutes watching the front door and thinking about what he was going to say and not arriving at anything that worked, which was how most of his important conversations began.

Kelsey came in at seven past noon. She was wearing a coat he didn't recognize — dark green, longer than what she usually wore, the collar up high on her neck — and her hair was pulled back tight, the no-nonsense configuration she wore at work, a woman who spent her days leaning over open mouths. She spotted him and didn't wave. She walked to the booth, sat down across from him, put her phone face-down on the table, and said, "I left at nine. The drive was shit."

"Road work?"

"No, the road was fine. The drive was shit because I spent two hours thinking about what I'm supposed to say to you and I still don't have it."

Doug nodded. He'd spent the morning doing roughly the same thing, except his commute from Clintonville was twelve minutes, and he'd filled the remaining time rearranging the salt and pepper shakers on his kitchen counter as if their alignment might produce a script for this conversation.

The waitress came back. Kelsey ordered a club sandwich and a Diet Coke without looking at the menu. Doug ordered a BLT because he'd been staring at the specials for eleven minutes and the BLT was the only item that didn't require a decision about sides or bread type. The waitress wrote it down left-handed, the brace adjusting her grip slightly off-angle, and Doug noticed that the brace looked new and clean, which meant the injury was recent, which meant she was still figuring out the small recalibrations — the grip and reach and angle — that came with working around a part of your body that had stopped doing what it used to do.

"I'm sorry," Doug said, when they were alone.

"Okay."

"I mean it."

"I know you mean it, Dad. You've been sorry since the day after. Sorry isn't really the question."

She picked up a sugar packet from the caddy and turned it over between her fingers — front, back, front — not opening it, just giving her hands something to do. He recognized this. She'd done it with erasers as a kid, her phone case as a teenager. Kelsey's anger always went to her hands first.

"What's the question?" he said.

"I don't know yet. That's what I drove two hours to figure out." She dropped the sugar packet. "Can I just tell you what it's been like? The last two weeks. Can I just do that?"

"Yeah."

"Twenty-three hundred," she said. "Tags. Not mentions — tags. People tagging me in posts about your video. People I went to high school with. People I've never met. My coworker Jen — you've met Jen, short, has the bangs — she screenshot a TikTok reaction video, some guy doing commentary over the toast, and she put it in the work group chat. With a heart emoji. She thought she was being supportive."

"That's —"

"I'm not done." Her voice didn't rise. It dropped, and the drop was worse. "A patient. I had a patient in the chair, bib clipped on, I'm in there with the scaler doing a subgingival, and she goes, 'Oh my God, are you Wedding Toast Girl?' My hand is in this woman's mouth. She's trying to talk around a scaler and she's telling me she cried, she thought it was so moving, she's actually tearing up while I've got calculus on my instrument and I'm just standing there with my hand in a stranger's mouth listening to her cry about my dad."

Doug looked at the table. There was a ring of dried coffee near his placemat, a faint circle, residue you'd document in a claims photograph if the surface were insured.

"Tyler's mother," Kelsey said. She'd gone quiet, and with Kelsey the quiet was always where the real pressure lived. Not the volume — the precision. "She sent me a text. Three sentences. 'We saw your father's speech. We understand.' She italicized 'understand.'"

"You can italicize in a —"

"Yes, Dad, you can italicize in a text message. That's not the point." She looked at him across the table with the particular steadiness she deployed when he did this — when he retreated into the small technical question because the larger one was sitting right there and he didn't know how to pick it up. "Tyler's mother. Who I sat next to at the rehearsal dinner for forty minutes while she told me about her bridge club and her hip replacement and her opinions on the asparagus. Who I've met four times. And she understands now. She understands our family. Because you got drunk and talked into a microphone about things you've never said to any of us sober."

The food arrived. The waitress set down the plates with her braced left hand, compensating slightly, the BLT landing a half-inch off the placemat. Doug thanked her. Kelsey didn't look up.

They ate. Or they went through the motions. Doug picked up a triangle of the BLT. The bacon was cold and rigid, one strip hanging past the bread like it was trying to leave the sandwich. He ate it anyway. The lettuce was brown at the edges. The tomato was the pale pink of February tomatoes, shipped from somewhere warmer and emptied of whatever they'd been by the trip.

Kelsey dismantled the club sandwich. She ate the turkey layer first. She'd been doing this since she was twelve, when she'd decided that sandwiches made more sense as components — disassembled and consumed in order of preference. Turkey first, then bacon, then bread and lettuce last, which she liked least. It was a detail Doug knew about his daughter, part of the private archive he'd accumulated over twenty-eight years: turkey first, her too-tight grip on a pen, the scar on her left knee from a bike pedal in fourth grade, the fact that she always parked facing out because she'd read somewhere it was safer. He catalogued these things thoroughly and precisely and never quite understood what they added up to.

"How's work otherwise?" he said, because the silence between bites was getting heavy and he didn't know how to hold it yet.

"It's fine. It's teeth."

"Right."

She chewed. He chewed. The kitchen door swung. Somewhere behind it a plate broke and someone said "shit" and someone else laughed and the normal operations of a diner on a Wednesday in February continued without regard for what was happening in the back booth.

"Can I ask you something?" Kelsey said.

"Yeah."

"The thing you said about the table — was that real?"

Doug's hands went still on the sandwich. The table. She was asking about the table — the table in the kitchen of the house on Briarwood, the maple table they'd bought when Kelsey was four from a furniture store that no longer existed, a table with a gouge near one leg where the delivery men had caught the doorframe and a long pale scratch across the top from Kelsey's protractor in seventh grade. She'd been horrified about the scratch. He'd told her it was fine, it gave the table character. And in the toast, eight whiskeys deep and still naming things exactly, he'd said something about that scratch — he couldn't remember the exact words, the eight whiskeys had turned the evening into a landscape seen through rain — but he remembered the word "protractor," because even then he was the kind of man who named the instrument, and he remembered something about the scratch being the only lasting mark he'd made alongside anybody. Or close to that. Close enough that it was on the internet and his daughter was sitting across from him asking if it was real.

"I was pretty far gone," he said.

"That's not what I asked."

"I know."

"Was it real? What you said about the scratch. The thing about — you know what you said."

Doug looked at the salt shaker. It was glass with a chrome lid, and the salt inside had clumped at the bottom from humidity, a solid mass where the granules had fused into something denser than they were meant to be. He focused on the salt and not on the table and not on the scratch and not on the house on Briarwood that Megan had sold fourteen months after she left, to a couple from Westerville who didn't know about the protractor, who would sand the mark smooth or cover it with a placemat or not notice it at all.

"I don't remember exactly what I said," Doug told her. This was a lie. It was the specific lie of a man who remembered precisely what he'd said and could not say it again sober because saying it sober would mean he'd chosen to, and choosing was different from the words walking out of you at a wedding reception while the room was still recovering from your ex-wife's husband's perfect toast. Choosing meant it was yours. Drunk, it was an accident. He could live with an accident.

Kelsey watched him. Her eyes were her mother's — dark, level, capable of holding a gaze longer than was comfortable as a form of pressure all its own. She knew he was deflecting. She had spent a lifetime watching him deflect. His retreats into literalness had a pattern. The technical answer arrived first, and the real answer sat beside it untouched.

She set down the piece of bread she was holding.

She folded her hands on the table.

And then neither of them spoke.

The diner went on around them. The kitchen door swung and the fryer crackled. Two booths over, a couple in their sixties argued about a nephew — the word "irresponsible" recurring at intervals like a metronome. The waitress passed with a pot of coffee and refilled Doug's mug without asking, which he registered as both a kindness and an intrusion but couldn't manage to say anything about either way. At the counter, a man was eating soup and reading his phone, and the sound of his spoon against the bowl was steady and small and regular, measuring time in cream of mushroom. The front door opened and cold air rushed in and someone said "Jesus, it's cold" and someone else answered "February" as if that were an explanation, which in Columbus it was.

A song played on the diner's radio — something country, a truck and a dirt road, a woman who left. Then Fleetwood Mac, "Landslide," which was so on-the-nose it had to be coincidence. Then a commercial for a dealership in Delaware, Ohio, zero percent financing, which Doug happened to know meant the interest was folded into the sticker price — useful information for no one in this booth at this moment.

Five minutes. Maybe six. He didn't look at his phone and neither did she.

The refill sat untouched long enough to skin over. Kelsey kept her hands folded until the ice in her Diet Coke gave up and settled. When she picked her sandwich back up, she started with the bread, the part she liked least.

"Tyler says hi," she said.

"Tell him hi."

"He liked the toast. He thought it was funny."

"Tyler did?"

"He laughed. I don't know at which part. I didn't take a survey." She shrugged. She was coming back to the surface, reassembling the normal shapes of conversation around herself. "Maybe it's funny if you're not in it."

"Maybe."

"Anyway." She wadded her napkin and dropped it on her plate. "I should go. I told Ty I'd be home by five."

"Drive safe."

"I always drive safe."

"I know you do."

She stood. She pulled on the green coat, zipped it to her chin. She was shorter than he expected — she was always shorter than he expected, because he carried in his mind a Kelsey who had kept growing past five-four, which she hadn't, same height as Megan, same height as Megan's mother, a genetic line that had nothing to do with anything and that he thought about now because thinking about his daughter's height was easier than thinking about the silence that was still sitting in the booth between them.

"The BLT was pretty good," he said.

Kelsey looked at him. And then she laughed — short, barely a sound, escaping before she'd decided whether to allow it. "God, Dad."

"What?"

"Nothing. The BLT was pretty good." She shook her head. She put a twenty on the table and when he reached for his wallet she said "Don't" in a voice that closed the discussion. Then she walked out of the diner without looking back.

He watched her through the window, crossing the parking lot in the dark green coat. She got into a silver Civic — she drove a Civic too, a newer model than his, because she'd replaced the one he'd given her at graduation with the same make and he didn't know whether that was loyalty or habit or the simple fact that Civics held their resale value, and he preferred to think about resale values rather than about the silence and the table and the five minutes during which his daughter had sat across from him knowing everything he couldn't say and letting the not-saying be enough.

The waitress brought the check. $23.40. He left the twenty Kelsey had put down and added twelve of his own, because the waitress had the brace and the two pens and had refilled his coffee without asking and he didn't know what else he could do for anyone today, so he could at least do this.

---

He called Megan at 4:30. He was sitting on the couch in the dark. He hadn't turned on the lights when he got home, and the February afternoon had already given out — the transition from gray to black so gradual it didn't register as a change, more like the gray just thickening until it was opaque. His phone was at twenty-two percent. He should have charged it first, but the charger was in the kitchen and the cord was too short to reach anywhere comfortable, and he'd spent the two hours since Worthington sitting on this couch building up to this call, and if he moved he might lose whatever fragile momentum he'd managed.

On the drive back from Worthington he'd passed the turn for Briarwood and kept going. The house had belonged to other people for twelve years, but he could still place the table in the old kitchen exactly: maple top, one leg gouged by the delivery men, the pale line Kelsey had cut into it in seventh grade by pressing too hard with a protractor and dragging. He'd covered the scratch with a placemat for a week because she'd been so upset, then stopped because the mark was there whether you acknowledged it or not. The toast had taken that line in the wood and put it in front of people who had no business knowing it existed.

She picked up on the second ring. "Doug."

"Hey."

"Hi." Behind her, a cabinet closed, then something touched the counter with a careful ceramic click. She was in her kitchen. Brendan's kitchen. The one with the island and the six-burner range and the herb pots on the windowsill in labeled ceramic containers, because in Brendan's house even the basil had a system. "I had a feeling you'd call."

"Yeah. I wanted to apologize. For the toast. For talking about the table. About Briarwood."

"I appreciate you saying that." Her voice was measured, each word given room. "But it wasn't vague, Doug. You said the maple table in the Briarwood kitchen still had the scratch Kelsey made with a protractor. You said you spent the last year of the marriage looking at that line because at least it was a visible thing. A mark you could point to. Then you said the scratch lasted longer than we did. People heard that."

Doug pressed his thumbnail into the arm of the couch. The upholstery had pilled in the places where his hands rested — eight years of the same position on the same cushion producing small hard knots of polyester fiber. He focused on one, about the size of a lentil.

"I don't remember it that clean."

"I do. Or close enough that the difference doesn't help either of us." Another soft cabinet close. "This isn't about me being furious. I've had two weeks to sit with it, and I do not think that speech invented anything. You'd been drinking. Brendan had just spoken. The room was already emotional. Then you said the thing neither of us had ever said in public."

"You're saying his toast set me off."

"I'm saying the night was already open, and then you stopped containing yourself."

"I wasn't trying to make you look bad."

"I know." Something small met the counter on her end of the line. "If you'd stood up and blamed me, this would actually be simpler. What was hard about it was that you didn't. You stood there and told a room full of people you could keep track of damage in a piece of furniture better than you could admit your marriage was going bad."

"How's Brendan?" he heard himself ask, which was not what he'd intended to say and not a question he wanted answered.

"He's good. He's at the hospital tonight." He heard a drawer slide shut. "He watched the video, Doug. More than once. He had a reaction to it, and I'd rather say it than let it come in sideways."

"I know."

Then she said, "He kept calling it brave."

The word arrived in Doug's dark apartment and just sat there.

Brave.

Brendan Coyle — orthopedic surgeon, weekend triathlete, a man whose own toast at the same wedding had been polished and warm and had brought the room to tears at precisely the right moment because Brendan understood pacing and Doug understood depreciation schedules — thought that Doug's drunken seven-minute catastrophe was brave.

Doug pressed the fabric pill flat with his thumbnail. It sprang back.

"He said most people never let their own family hear that much," Megan continued. "That was the part he respected."

Doug tried to locate what he felt. It wasn't one thing — it was a stack, multiple items on the same claim, each requiring separate assessment. Gratitude, because Brendan was being kind and the kindness was real. Humiliation, because the kindness came from the man who had his wife and his house and his composure and his herb pots. And something else underneath those, harder to file — the specific weight of being admired by the person whose life was the clearest evidence of everything you'd lost. Brave implied he'd chosen to do it. Brave implied he'd stood up at that podium and decided, sober and deliberate, to tell the truth. He hadn't chosen anything. He'd been eight whiskeys past the last checkpoint and the truth had walked out on its own, and calling that brave was like complimenting a burst pipe for the water pressure.

"It wasn't brave," Doug said.

"No?"

"It was bad timing. Whiskey. A microphone."

"All right," Megan said. "Those things are also true."

He could have let that be the end of it. Instead he said, "I signed with a manager."

There was a beat on the line before Megan said, "Layla Banks?"

"Yeah."

"For the toast."

"For whatever this is now. There are meetings. A Netflix development thing. Some speaking offers."

Another cabinet, this one opened and shut. The small domestic sounds in that kitchen irritated him on principle. "All right," Megan said again. "Then I need to say something plainly."

Doug waited.

"If you decide to do this publicly, our marriage cannot become the part you use when the room gets quiet. And Kelsey cannot keep being the person who pays for it after."

"I wasn't planning to use Kelsey."

"I know that." The line sat between them for a second. "Intent is not really the relevant issue anymore."

He looked across the dark apartment toward the kitchen counter, the charger coiled there like a short white snake. "You think I'd do that."

"I think you do not always know what you'll say once people are looking at you."

The sentence landed with the same dull force as Kelsey's patient in the dental chair, Tyler's mother understanding the family now, the waitress's brace, the twenty on the table, every small piece of the day that had turned into evidence against him. He pressed the fabric pill flat again. It sprang back again.

"Was it true?" he said.

The line stayed quiet long enough that he looked at the screen before Megan said, "What part?"

"That it lasted longer than we did. That we were already done and I was the only one still sitting there."

"No," she said. "Not the only one. I was sitting there too."

He closed his eyes. The couch cushion shifted under his weight, old foam compressing where it had compressed a thousand times before.

"That was the worst part of hearing it," Megan said. "Not that it was false. That it was public."

"If Kelsey asks you again what that line meant," Megan said, "I think you probably owe her a sober answer."

"Take care of yourself," she said.

"Yeah. You too."

He hung up. The apartment was fully dark. His phone said 4:53, the screen glowing in the room like something left on by mistake. He got up and plugged it into the kitchen charger and stood there at the counter while the little lightning bolt appeared on the screen, and the apartment had its usual quiet — not peaceful, not hostile, just the sound of a space with one person in it and room for more.

He stood there another minute with his hand still on the phone, trying to picture the wedding from Kelsey's table instead of the podium. His jacket was off. His tie was uneven. Tyler's mother and Brendan's colleagues and whatever cousins had filmed him all sitting there while he told the story of the scratch as if a piece of damaged wood could explain eighteen years of marriage. The shame in it was not that the story was false. It was that he'd made the two people most trapped inside it hear it with an audience, and maybe laugh too.

He opened the refrigerator. Milk. Condiments. Half a sleeve of saltines from a stomach bug in January. One egg that had rolled to the far back corner of the shelf and sat there under the light, small and solitary.

He closed the refrigerator and picked up the phone again. He opened his text thread with Kelsey. Her last message was from that morning, saying she'd be there a little after noon. He typed, "It was real." The three words sat in the box under her name. He looked at them until the screen dimmed, then deleted them and set the phone back by the charger.

Doug walked to the bedroom. He took off his shoes and placed them by the door — left, right, aligned with the baseboard, because he was a man who put things in order even when nothing else was — and lay down on the bed in his clothes. The ceiling had a water stain directly above him, a ring of discoloration from the tenant upstairs, something he'd noticed when he moved in twelve years ago and had never reported. He knew what caused it — a slow leak, probably a supply line fitting, a failure that cost maybe eighty dollars to fix and three hundred in drywall if you waited. He'd waited twelve years. The stain was roughly the shape of Ohio, which was either fitting or coincidence, and he went with coincidence, because the alternative — that his ceiling had developed opinions about his life — was a thing that happened in movies, not in two-bedroom apartments in Clintonville.

From the kitchen came the small buzz of the phone against the counter, then another a minute later. He let it buzz. He lay there looking at the stain until he could no longer make out Ohio in it, only the darker ring at the edge where the water kept finding the same path.
