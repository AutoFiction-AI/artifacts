# Chapter 14

The rental car was a gray Hyundai Elantra that smelled like the previous renter's choices: a pine air freshener clipped to the vent, dried coffee in the cupholder, the stale salt-and-grease trace of a fast food meal that had happened somewhere between here and wherever that person had been going. Layla had booked the tour earlier that week in a single phone call while Doug was on his lunch break. Cleveland Thursday, Pittsburgh Friday, Detroit Saturday. "Warm-ups," she called them. "Before the big show." The Netflix taping was in two weeks.

She had emailed him a PDF itinerary with club addresses, hotel confirmations, and a note in all caps that said DO NOT OVERTHINK THIS. Doug had printed it because he did not trust phone batteries. The pages sat on the passenger seat beside a yellow legal pad with his set list divided into blocks: OPENING, INSURANCE, CEREAL, DIVORCE, CLOSER. Under OPENING he had written two possible first lines and circled the one he disliked less. Under CLOSER he had written LAND HERE, as if landing were a matter of instruction.

He pulled onto I-71 north toward Cleveland and did not feel warm.

He had driven this route to visit Kelsey when she first moved there, before the visits became less frequent, then occasional, then something they planned over text three weeks in advance. Delaware. Mansfield. Ashland. He knew where the highway widened and where it narrowed, where the rest stop coffee started tasting less like Columbus and more like trucks. Kelsey lived twenty minutes from the club. He had not told her he was coming. That felt less cruel than telling her and making her decide whether to see him bomb in person.

The Holiday Inn was off an exit lined with chain restaurants that had all decided on different versions of beige. The lobby smelled like lemon cleaner and the coffee by the elevator had been sitting long enough to acquire a skin. In his room the bedspread was maroon and the art over the desk showed a sailboat on a lake nobody in Ohio had ever seen. Doug set the legal pad on the desk, took off his glasses, put them back on, and said his opener to the mirror.

"Good to be here in Cleveland. My hotel is technically in Independence, which feels like a strong word for a place behind an Olive Garden."

In the mirror he saw a man delivering geography with hope.

He tried the second opener.

"I work in insurance, which means when your basement floods I'm the guy who comes over and says, yes, that's definitely water."

He had written PAUSE after that line. He held the pause for the hotel mirror. The mirror did what mirrors do.

He showered, changed into jeans and an untucked button-down, and drove to Wiley's with the legal pad on the passenger seat as if it were a person whose opinion mattered. The club sat in a strip mall between a Great Clips and a vape shop. The parking lot had fresh pothole patches the color of healing bruises. Inside, the room smelled like fryer oil, beer taps, and carpet that had absorbed a decade of apologies. A TV over the bar showed the Guardians game with the sound off. Doug stood near the back wall while the MC did ten minutes about dating apps and one guy in a Browns hoodie laughed so hard he slapped his own thigh like it had betrayed him.

When the MC brought him up, a small wave of recognition moved through the room before he even reached the microphone. Not recognition of him exactly. Recognition of the category. Viral man. Toast man. The person from the phone.

He smiled because he had practiced smiling. "Good to be here in Cleveland," he said. "My hotel is technically in Independence, which feels like a strong word for a place behind an Olive Garden."

Nothing happened for a second. Then the bartender smiled politely at the register, which was the kind of reaction a person had to hearing the address on a package read out loud.

Doug nodded as if that had counted. "I work in insurance, which means when your basement floods I'm the guy who comes over and says, yes, that's definitely water."

A woman in the second row took a sip of vodka soda. A man near the aisle looked toward the bar menu as if checking whether something better might be printed there.

"We use a phrase called actual cash value," Doug said. "That's when the company looks at your ruined couch and says, after depreciation, your sadness is worth about twenty-four dollars."

He waited. He had seen comedians wait after a line like that, smiling a little as if the room were catching up. The room did not look like it was catching up. It looked like it had received information.

"Anybody here own a home?" he said. "Rent? Anybody successfully avoiding both?"

No one answered. A couple at a high-top looked at each other, the way married people do when they are silently negotiating who has to participate in public. Doug had planned a follow-up if someone said homeowners. He had planned a different follow-up if someone said renters. He had planned a third if someone yelled neither. Planning turned out not to help.

"Tough room," he said, because he had heard comedians say that too. "That's okay. Denial is actually most of my job."

A short laugh came from somewhere near the kitchen, not enough to locate. Doug moved on too quickly, which made the quickness visible. He went into the cereal material he and Simone had been shaping and somehow made it sound like an onboarding seminar.

"I live alone, so dinner and breakfast have started sharing infrastructure," he said. "If a bowl's involved, I stop asking which meal it is."

A woman near the back nodded once in a way that could have been agreement, sympathy, or neck pain.

"Cheerios is an honest cereal. It isn't promising excitement. It's saying, here's a beige circle, let's all be adults."

That line had sounded passable in the hotel room. Onstage it sounded like an accountant trying to open for a dentist.

He tried crowd work with a man in a Cavaliers hat because the man had been making attentive eye contact and Doug mistook attention for an invitation.

"You eat cereal for dinner?" Doug asked.

The man blinked. "Sometimes."

"What kind?"

"Whatever's there."

Doug had no idea what to do with whatever's there. He said, "Right," into the microphone and felt the room watch him fail to convert a stranger's pantry into an event.

He moved to the divorce material early because that was what performers did when one section died. They pivoted. They adjusted. He had written a transition on the legal pad and now heard himself use it exactly as written.

"Speaking of long-term disappointment," he said, "I got divorced twelve years ago, which is enough time for a person to be fine and also still have opinions about towels."

This got less than nothing. A few people shifted in their seats in the specific way people shift when they suspect the topic itself might become work.

Doug could feel himself imitating several men at once, none of whom were helping him. He widened his stance because he had seen comics do that. He took a sip of water because he had seen comics do that. He tried a callback to the Olive Garden line because he had written CALLBACK? in the margin and had mistaken the note for destiny.

"At least the breadsticks there have replacement cost," he said.

A glass set down on a table in front. Somebody near the back said "Hmm" with the neutral tone of a person testing soup.

Eighteen minutes in, a woman near the back yelled, "Do the toast!"

Doug said, "I can't. I'm sober."

That got him the only real laugh of the night. It came fast and clean and spread farther than anything else had. He felt it hit the walls. He felt it leave. Then he made the mistake of trying to stand on it.

"Right," he said. "Anyway, actual cash value."

The room fell away from him again so quickly it almost had dignity. He did nine more minutes because ending early would have required a different kind of nerve. He thanked them. They applauded with the mercy reserved for public failure and little children performing instruments.

Outside, the air smelled like damp pavement and fryer grease pushed out the back door. Doug stood beside the Elantra and called Simone before he could decide not to.

"How bad?" she said.

"I asked if anybody had read a homeowners policy."

"Why."

"I thought that was audience interaction."

"Did anybody answer?"

"No."

"Why would they?"

Doug looked at the club's side door, where a cook in a black apron was smoking under a yellow security light. "I opened with a line about my hotel technically being in Independence."

Silence.

"Simone."

"I'm here."

"I tried to do it like a comedian."

"Don't," she said.

He laughed once through his nose, not because anything was funny. "That feels like advice I could've used earlier."

"You'll need it again. Get some sleep."

He drove back to the Holiday Inn with the radio off. In the room the maroon bedspread had the pattern hotels used when they expected it to survive everything. He sat on top of it in his jeans and dress shoes and opened his phone. If he texted Kelsey, she could answer or not answer, and either result would sit there in writing. He typed, In Cleveland for work. He deleted for work because it was not work. He typed, Twenty minutes away. He deleted that too because it sounded like a threat or an apology. Eventually he turned the phone face down on the nightstand beside the Gideon Bible and did not call her.

At six the next morning he went down to the breakfast room because staying in the room felt too much like agreeing with it. Two youth hockey families had taken over the waffle maker. A man in a polo from an HVAC convention was reading USA Today with his mouth slightly open. Doug poured cereal from one of those miniature boxes that held one serving if you were pretending not to be hungry and two if you were telling the truth. He carried it to a table by the window and watched the parking lot while he rewrote his opener in block letters on a fresh page.

GOOD EVENING PITTSBURGH.

He stared at it until the cereal went soft. Then he crossed out GOOD.

---

The drive to Pittsburgh was two and a half hours through northeastern Ohio and across the western edge of Pennsylvania, where the flat farmland wrinkled into hills and the highway started asking for more attention. Doug stopped for gas at a Sheetz outside Youngstown. The coffee cost a dollar eighty-nine and tasted how truck stops smelled: burnt, industrial, somehow fine if you did not examine it too closely. He stood by the condiment station stirring powdered creamer into it and listened to a woman in scrubs tell a man in a Steelers hoodie that if he bought one more breakfast sandwich she was leaving him there. The man bought the sandwich.

Back in the Elantra he put the lid on the coffee, took one sip, and said his new opener to the windshield.

"Good evening, Pittsburgh."

That was not an opener. That was a fact with manners.

He tried the insurance material again, trimming words as he drove.

"I work in insurance, and one thing about insurance is we use optimistic language for depressing situations."

He stopped there because his exit was coming and because the sentence sounded like the start of a keynote.

Pittsburgh was more vertical than Cleveland, which was more vertical than Columbus. His hotel was another Holiday Inn near the airport. It had the same floor plan as Cleveland, but with a blue bedspread and a water stain on the ceiling that looked like it had almost made up its mind. The room smelled faintly of pool chemicals from an indoor pool he could not see. Doug lay on the bed for fifteen minutes with his forearm over his eyes and listened to the air conditioner knock itself on and off. At four-thirty he sat up, pulled the legal pad into his lap, and wrote pool? in the margin beside INSURANCE.

The question mark irritated him. The pool story was not a maybe. It was a file from March, complete with photos, receipts, and one handwritten note from a woman who believed seventeen rosebushes represented both horticulture and moral worth. The problem was that it was not a joke. Simone laughed when he told claim stories because she laughed at reality's paperwork, but a club audience expected a person with a microphone to know where he was taking them.

He kept replaying the Cleveland laugh. I can't. I'm sober. Simone had said it came because he told the truth. Doug believed her and still had no idea how to use that without announcing in advance that now he was about to tell the truth.

The Comic Attic sat beneath a bar called O'Reilly's, down a narrow staircase that smelled like spilled Yuengling and industrial cleaner. Maybe seventy-five people fit down there. The ceiling was low enough that the laugh from the room before his seemed trapped in it. The MC, a young woman named Diane with a sleeve tattoo and a laugh that carried through walls, introduced him as "the man who broke the internet at his daughter's wedding, Doug Bridge."

The applause was genuine. Doug took the mic, looked at the room, opened his mouth, and nothing came. One second passed, then three, then five. The audience shifted. At seven seconds a man in the front row uncrossed his arms. At nine, a woman by the wall looked at the person beside her. At eleven, someone coughed.

"I wrote it down," Doug said. "I said it in the car. In the hotel. In a Holiday Inn bathroom. It was there an hour ago."

A few people laughed out of pity, but pity made sound too, and Doug took it. He started with the first line he could reach.

"I work in insurance," he said, "and one thing about insurance is we use optimistic words for depressing situations. Restoration. Betterment. Replacement cost. Nobody in my field has ever walked into a burned kitchen and thought, this could be a nice opportunity."

A man in the second row smiled with only the upper half of his face. It was not nothing, but it was not momentum either.

"Actual cash value," Doug said. "That's when the company looks at your destroyed couch and says, we need to remember the wear and tear. Which feels rude on the couch question and worse on the human level."

He had intended to move from there into divorce, then cereal, then back to insurance, but the sections had come loose from each other somewhere between Cleveland and the Fort Pitt Tunnel. He heard the gap before his next line and tried to cross it anyway.

"Anyway," he said, "I've been thinking about cereal."

This got a real laugh, but it was the laugh people gave when the person onstage had exposed himself by accident. Doug felt relief and shame arrive together and mistook both for encouragement.

"I eat a lot of Cheerios now," he said. "Not Honey Nut. Plain. Plain says you're participating in breakfast for structural reasons."

One woman laughed. Another took a sip of beer and looked down into the glass as if checking whether he might be in there.

"You can tell if a cereal is for adults by whether the box looks disappointed in you."

That line had killed at no stage other than his hotel chair. Down here it landed like a note left on a windshield.

Doug lost the next transition entirely. He started a sentence about the grocery store, abandoned it, said "Right" into the mic, and saw Diane by the back wall tilt her head the way people do when they want to help but have accepted that they cannot. Twelve minutes in, with the cards in his head no longer in any order he could trust, he told the pool story instead.

"I had a claim in Westerville," he said. "Above-ground pool. Side wall split at two in the morning. Nine thousand gallons of water went sideways through three yards and took out the neighbor's rose garden."

That got attention. Doug kept going before he could remember to be funny.

"Seventeen hybrid tea roses," he said. "The neighbor had receipts. Separate receipts. I know that because she put them in a Ziploc bag and handed it to me like evidence in a murder case."

The room laughed.

"Forty-two hundred dollars in roses. I remember the number because no one should ever have to say forty-two hundred dollars in roses more than once, but my job required repetition."

More of them now. Chairs stopped squeaking. Someone at the bar turned fully toward him.

"The policy covered the pool owner's fence, the patio furniture, and part of the landscaping, but not the roses at replacement value because there was a sublimit on ornamental plantings that made everybody furious for different reasons."

He said the word sublimit and the room laughed harder, which told him they were with him in a place he had not expected anyone to go.

"I had two adults standing in wet mulch at eight-thirty in the morning arguing over whether a rose counts as a shrub. I had to ask follow-up questions."

For about ninety seconds Doug felt the room come toward him. Not because he had built a joke. Because he had stopped building.

Then he felt the legal pad in his head again, the neat labeled tabs. He reached for the bit Simone had helped him shape, and the reaching was enough.

"So that's the thing about replacement cost," he said. "The form says we'll return you to pre-loss condition. Which sounds comforting until you've met adults."

Nothing. Not even courtesy.

He kept going because the line was on the page and therefore, in some humiliating administrative sense, active.

"There isn't really a pre-loss condition for a person," he said. "There's just the current version with documentation."

Glasses touched tabletops. A waitress squeezed behind the back row and the room's attention went with her for a moment and did not return in full.

Doug tried to pull them back with the divorce tag he had written in the hotel.

"My divorce paperwork had numbers all over it," he said. "None of them offered replacement cost."

A man near the wall took out his phone and looked at it without lowering the brightness. Whatever had moved toward Doug during the pool story went back where it had been. He finished to applause that was better than Cleveland and still wrong.

He called Simone from the hotel parking lot before he went upstairs. A plane went over low enough to roughen the air.

"I stood there for eleven seconds," he said.

"That's a long time."

"The pool story worked."

"How long?"

"Ninety seconds."

"And then?"

"Then I said, 'So that's the thing about replacement cost.'"

Simone was quiet for a beat. "Yeah."

"That 'yeah' sounds bad."

"It's not good."

"They liked the roses."

"People like a thing that happened."

Doug leaned against the car door. "What were you thinking about during the pool story?"

"I'm asking you," she said.

"Nothing. I wasn't, I don't know. I was just talking."

"Okay," she said. "Call me after Detroit."

He bought a Diet Coke from the vending machine because the machine did not require interpretation. In the room he took off one shoe, then the other, and sat on the blue bedspread listening to the freeway through the window unit. He thought about going over the set again. He thought about not. On the legal pad he drew a line through the replacement-cost paragraph so hard the next two pages dented under it.

In the morning the breakfast room had the same eggs as Cleveland in a different hotel pan. Doug ate one piece of toast and drank bad coffee from a paper cup with a plastic lid that did not quite fit. At the next table a family in matching Detroit Tigers shirts argued about whether they needed to leave early for the Henry Ford Museum. Doug looked at the shirts and thought about spending the evening inside a room full of people from Michigan who might want the man from the video and might get a man talking about ornamental plantings instead.

He folded the legal pad closed and went to the car.

---

The drive from Pittsburgh to Detroit was the longest leg, four and a half hours west on the turnpike and across northern Ohio to Toledo, where I-75 ran the last stretch north into Detroit. Doug drove it Saturday morning. The sun hit the windshield at an angle the visor could not fix. He stopped for gas outside Akron. The coffee tasted the same as the Youngstown coffee. He bought a gas station sandwich, unwrapped it, looked at it, and rewrapped it. He ate it twenty miles later because hunger was a simpler problem.

Outside Toledo he pulled into a rest area just to stand still for a minute. The building had vending machines, a rack of Ohio brochures, and a men's room that smelled like bleach and old paper towels. Doug stood at the sink washing hands that were not dirty and looked at himself in the mirror over the row of basins. Same button-down. Same face from Pittsburgh. Less available conviction. He said, "Good evening, Detroit," to the mirror and hated the civility of it.

Back on the highway Layla called at noon.

"So how's it going? I heard Cleveland was a little, you know. Finding your legs."

"It was bad, Layla."

"It wasn't bad. Gerry said you had good energy."

"Gerry lies to comedians after they bomb. That's his whole job."

"Okay, so Cleveland was a learning experience. What about Pittsburgh?"

"Ninety seconds worked."

"That's great. Ninety seconds is a real start. We can build on ninety seconds. We can literally market ninety seconds if we have to. We could clip ninety seconds."

Doug looked at the highway, then at a truck hauling drywall sheets strapped in blue nylon. "I need to focus on tonight."

"Totally. Detroit's gonna love you. The venue's great, babe. It's a real room. This is exactly what you need."

"I don't know what I need."

"You need reps," Layla said, moving so fast the phrase almost counted as certainty. "You need stage time and muscle memory and then the taping's gonna feel normal. Or not normal, but handleable. That's a word now. Handleable."

Doug said nothing.

"Hey," she said, quieter for one beat than she usually allowed herself to be. "Don't spiral in the car, okay? That's dead time. Use the drive."

He almost said, It is all drive. Instead he said, "Okay."

The Mark was a real comedy club, not a strip mall or a bar basement but a dedicated room with proper lighting, a real sound system, and tiered seating that climbed toward the back. It held about a hundred and fifty, and it was close to full. The lobby walls had signed headshots in black frames and a drink minimum printed in cheerful font. Doug checked into the attached hotel first. His room was on the fifth floor. The bedspread was green. Somebody had placed the TV remote in a paper sleeve that said SANITIZED FOR YOUR COMFORT, which Doug found less comforting than the alternative.

He put his garment bag on the luggage rack and tried the first three minutes of material one more time, sitting on the bed because standing had not helped in Cleveland. He did not rehearse the heckler scenario because that would have required admitting it was likely. He did not rehearse the toast line because it belonged to a version of himself he had not chosen to manufacture sober.

Downstairs, the showroom had the temperature of money spent correctly. A server in black walked by carrying six cocktails at once without looking at any of them. The MC, a local comic named Terrence, did five minutes on Detroit traffic that got the kind of laughs Doug had started to think of as physical evidence. Doug watched from behind the curtain and felt how separate Terrence's ease was from anything Doug knew how to do. Terrence looked like a person with a microphone. Doug looked like a man waiting to contest an appraisal.

When Terrence brought him up, the applause started large and stayed large long enough that Doug understood people were not cautiously curious anymore. They were arriving with an expectation. A recognizable person from a recognizable failure. A hundred and fifty people who wanted either the toast or a version of the toast refined into product.

Doug walked onstage. The lights were warm enough to turn the back half of the room into shapes. He took a breath and started with the material he and Simone had built.

"I work in property claims," he said. "We have actual cash value and replacement cost. Actual cash value is what the insurer thinks your couch is worth after six years, one dog, and whatever you did to it during football season."

A few people smiled. One man near the front laughed alone and then stopped when no one joined him.

"Replacement cost is the number they tell you before paperwork begins," Doug said. "It's the optimistic number. The brochure number."

That got a couple of courtesy sounds. The room was not dead. It was evaluating.

"I live alone, so dinner and breakfast have started sharing infrastructure," he said. "If a bowl's involved, I stop asking which meal it is."

More polite smiles. A woman in the third row took a sip of wine and looked at him with the encouraging expression people use on first-time presenters in conference rooms.

"Cheerios is an honest cereal," Doug said. "It isn't promising a good time. It's saying, here's a beige circle. Let's all conduct ourselves appropriately."

Three minutes in, a man in the second row lifted both beers and yelled, "Toast!"

He was maybe forty-five, heavy, wearing a Tigers cap and a polo shirt tucked into jeans. He was grinning already. Doug could see he was not angry. He was having the best time in the room, which was the problem.

"Toast! Toast! Toast!"

Doug looked at him and thought about every YouTube clip of a comedian destroying a heckler. "Hey, man," he said. "I appreciate the enthusiasm."

It was wrong. He heard it as soon as it left his mouth. It was supervisor language, claims-department language. The guy brightened, as if Doug had opened a door for him.

"Come on, man. Do the toast!"

"I don't do the toast," Doug said. "That's not, I've got different material tonight."

"We don't want different material!"

"Sir," Doug said, and then knew he had made it worse.

"Sir? He called me sir." The man turned to the woman beside him, who had already started looking embarrassed on his behalf and everyone else's. "DO THE TOAST, DOUG."

Half the crowd looked embarrassed for him. The other half wanted to see how bad it could get. Doug could feel the set list leave him. It was not the whole stage or his voice, just the labeled tabs he had planned to move through in order. He stood there with a hundred and forty people looking at him and a drunk man demanding a wedding toast and nothing in his head but the last two days of interstate.

"Okay," Doug said. "Here's what I actually have. I drove here from Pittsburgh this morning and the coffee at a gas station outside Akron tasted exactly like the coffee I had outside Youngstown yesterday. I think it's the same coffee. I don't know how that would work logistically, but it tasted the same."

A woman near the bar laughed into her beer. The man's date stopped tugging on his sleeve.

"I've been in three Holiday Inns in three nights. Cleveland was maroon. Pittsburgh was blue. Detroit is green. I think it's green. It might be teal, I don't — anyway, somebody in an office decided those are three different experiences."

That got more of them. The heckler lowered one of his beers.

"Every room has that chair by the window. The one nobody sits in. I sat in it in Pittsburgh and you can see the parking lot and part of a Denny's sign, so I don't know what the chair is for."

The laugh widened. Terrence, by the bar, uncrossed his arms.

"And rest stops. I stopped at — I think four rest stops in two days, which is a lot. Everybody in there is holding food they didn't want an hour earlier and looking at the highway like it owes them an apology."

Doug heard the room with him now and kept going because stopping would have meant thinking.

"I-80 through Pennsylvania is actually beautiful. I wasn't expecting that. It seems unfair, because you're just paying tolls to drive through these hills and trees and nobody's stopping."

Someone said, "Jesus," softly, and laughed right after.

"And every Holiday Inn smells the same. Cleaner and carpet and something. Other people's sleep, maybe. I don't know what that smells like, but it's in there. It's supposed to be comforting but really it just means you could be in any city and nothing about the room would tell you which one."

The heckler went quiet. His date put her phone down. What Doug got from them then was not laughter exactly. It was the attention people give something true.

"That chair's still by the window when you get back. The parking lot's still there. Cleveland or Pittsburgh or Detroit, after midnight it's the same room. Ice bucket. Gideon Bible. Key card that stops working at some point, and they never tell you when."

He talked that way for eight minutes, about toll roads and hotel carpets and how every fluorescent hallway made him feel temporarily misfiled, about waking up in a room that already had the air conditioner on as if someone had guessed the shape of his comfort and missed it by half an inch, about the breakfast bars where the cereal came out of wall dispensers with little chrome handles like a ration system for disappointment. He said he trusted road signs more than motivational language because a road sign would at least admit how many miles were left. He said all hotel art looked like it had lost a lawsuit. Each line came out before he had time to build a better version of it, and that was why the room stayed with him.

Then he felt himself reach for the cereal bit waiting on the set list, and the reaching killed it.

"Anyway," he said. "The other thing I've been thinking about this week is depreciation."

The temperature in the room changed. Not dramatically. Enough.

"Depreciation is a document's way of saying time happened to your recliner and now nobody wants to pay for it."

Ice touched glass. A server stopped beside a table to collect empties and nobody minded the interruption.

"My couch has an actual cash value of maybe forty dollars," Doug said. "Which feels rude, because I'm still using the couch."

A woman who had been leaning forward settled back. The heckler picked up one of his beers again, not to yell now, just to drink.

"Cereal's like that too," Doug said, trying to get back to the bit exactly as written. "The photo on the box is replacement cost. The bowl in your apartment is actual cash value."

Nothing. That was the problem. Not boos. Not offense. The little intimate electricity of the last eight minutes dissolved into a normal club room where a man was talking into a microphone and the audience was deciding whether to stay with him out of courtesy. The last four minutes were polite again. When he said, "Thank you, that's my time," the applause was generous and confused.

Backstage, Terrence held out a fist the way comics did. Doug looked at it for half a second too long before understanding and touching it with his own knuckles.

"Long drive?" Terrence said.

"Three cities."

"Shit." Terrence nodded as if that answered several questions at once. "Get home safe."

That was all. No diagnosis. No consolation. Doug appreciated him for it.

---

He left Detroit at ten-forty, still in his stage clothes, the parking lot damp from a rain that had come and gone while he was inside. The Elantra's GPS said three hours and twenty-two minutes to Columbus. By the time he reached the highway, the wipers were dry-clicking across glass that no longer needed them. He turned them off and drove through the outer ring of Detroit with neon reflected in leftover water on the pavement.

Thirty miles outside the city, on I-75 south, he called Simone.

"How bad?" she said.

"A guy spent half the set yelling for the toast."

"And?"

"I called him sir."

Simone made a sound through her nose that might have been a laugh. "Jesus."

"Then I talked about hotel coffee and parking lots and they listened."

"How long?"

Doug passed a sign for Findlay and watched his headlights catch the metal bolts on the guardrail. "Eight minutes. Maybe a little more. Then I went back to depreciation and lost them."

The line went quiet except for highway noise under it.

"What we built doesn't work," Doug said.

"I know."

"I can't hand Layla eight minutes about rest stops and hotel carpets and call it a set. I wrote versions of that in Cleveland, in Pittsburgh, in the car. It never sounded like that onstage."

Doug drove past a darkened billboard and an exit ramp lit by one orange lamp. "I can hear the point where it changes. I just can't stop before it changes."

Simone did not answer right away. When she spoke, her voice was flatter than comfort and kinder for that reason. "The taping's in two weeks."

He waited for the part where she would tell him what to do next. She did not offer it. The silence asked more of him than advice would have.

"I don't know how to do that on purpose," he said.

"I know."

"That's not helpful."

"Helpful isn't available right now."

He almost laughed. He almost got angry. What came instead was fatigue, complete and procedural. "Okay."

"Get home safe," she said. "Tuesday we'll look at what was alive."

He drove the rest of the way with the radio off. At a rest area south of Toledo he bought a bottle of water and a pack of peanut butter crackers from a vending machine because the machine asked only for money. In the men's room he looked at himself under fluorescent light and saw stage makeup he had not applied, just the shine from a room that wanted more from him than he had. Back in the car he tried saying some of the road material out loud to see whether it had shape away from people.

"Every room has that chair by the window."

In the empty car it sounded like a sentence from a man talking to himself at night, which it was. He tried the gas station coffee line. He tried the line about the breakfast dispensers. Without the room, he could hear the sadness more clearly than the comedy, and because he could hear it, he started arranging it again, tidying it, placing it back into labeled sections. By the time he reached Delaware it already sounded worse.

He got to Clintonville a little after two. The apartment was dark. He let himself in, set his keys beside Simone's pen cap on the kitchen counter, and stood still until the refrigerator motor kicked on. There was milk in the fridge, half a block of cheddar wrapped in wax paper, mustard, two eggs, and nothing that counted as a plan. He closed the door and looked at the yellow Cheerios box on top of the refrigerator without opening it.

Tuesday was on the calendar. In two weeks he would stand at the Lincoln Theatre in front of 580 people and cameras. Tonight had proved something that felt both useful and impossible. The only part of him audiences wanted was the part he could not prepare without killing. He stood in the kitchen with one hand on the counter and listened to the apartment not containing anybody else. Then he turned off the kitchen light and left the cereal where it was.
