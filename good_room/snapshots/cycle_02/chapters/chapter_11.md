# Chapter 11

They'd been at it for three weeks, four days a week, at Doug's kitchen table. Simone had given up on jokes somewhere in the first week, and what they did now didn't have a name, or at least not one Doug recognized from any category of professional work he'd ever participated in. She asked him questions. He answered them. She decided which answers were something and which were nothing, and the criteria for that decision remained, to Doug, as opaque as the underwriting guidelines Mutual Fidelity revised every January without telling anyone what had changed.

The recorder sat between them — a small silver thing she'd carried from New York, scratched along one edge, a strip of masking tape on the back where she'd written "WORK" in black marker, though Doug didn't know what other kind of recording she might need to distinguish it from. She turned it on every morning when she arrived at nine and off again somewhere around one or two, and in between she listened in a way that made Doug hear his own voice in pieces — the pauses, the filler words he hadn't known he used, the places where a sentence died and he had to start a new one from scratch.

The table was the same one he'd bought from the IKEA in West Chester twelve years ago when he'd moved into the apartment. Birch veneer, four legs, no distinguishing features. It had a water ring from a mug he'd set down sometime in 2016 and never dealt with. Simone put her coffee on the same spot every morning, directly on the ring. Doug noticed and said nothing. Saying something would mean admitting he noticed where she put her coffee, and that felt too intimate for the arrangement.

"Tell me about the smell," Simone said. This was a Tuesday, the third week. She had her hair up, held with a pen she sometimes pulled out to write something and then stuck back, so that by noon her hair listed slightly to the left. She was wearing a flannel shirt with a button missing at the cuff, and she'd rolled the sleeves past her elbows. There was an ink stain on the inside of her left forearm that had been there since at least Thursday.

"What smell?"

"You said water damage has a smell. Yesterday, when you were talking about the house in Gahanna. You said you can tell how long the water's been standing by the smell."

"That's not — it's not really a smell thing," Doug said. "It's more of a timeline. You walk in and there's this —" He paused. He was looking at the table. "Okay. So within the first twenty-four hours, it smells like wet. Just wet. Like a basement, or a towel you left in the washer too long. And people are still in their houses at that point, still walking around, still thinking they can fix it with fans and a dehumidifier from Home Depot. The one that costs a hundred and forty dollars. Everyone buys that one."

"Okay."

"By forty-eight hours, if the water's been standing — in the walls, under the floorboards, in the insulation — it starts to turn. It goes from wet to something organic. Not rot yet. Pre-rot. It smells like earth, kind of. Like potting soil that's been sitting in a bag in the garage all winter."

"Pre-rot," Simone said.

"And then by day four or five, it's actual rot. You don't need to test for mold at that point. Your nose knows. The homeowner's been sleeping in that air for days and they've adjusted to it, the way you adjust to anything if you're in it long enough, but you walk in from outside and it hits you. It's sweet and musty and underneath there's something sharp, like wet cardboard that's been in the sun. And the homeowner is standing there asking if their floors can be saved, and you're breathing through your mouth trying to figure out the scope of the remediation, and the answer about the floors is almost always no, but you don't say that in the first five minutes."

Simone was writing something in her notebook. She didn't look up. "Why not?"

"Because they're standing on the floors. The floors they picked out. They're standing there in the house that smells wrong and the floors are soft under their feet and they already know. They just need you to not say it yet."

Simone stopped writing. She looked at Doug for a long moment. This was the face she made when deciding whether something belonged in the notebook or could be left in the room. Doug never understood the rules.

"That's good," she said. "Keep going."

"There's nowhere to go. That's what happens."

"Tell me about the dehumidifier. The hundred-and-forty-dollar one."

"It doesn't work. Not for real water damage. It's fine for a damp basement, but if you've got standing water in your walls, it's like trying to bail out a pool with a coffee cup. But people buy it because it's something to do. They need to be responding. They need to be the person who went to Home Depot and came back with equipment."

"Mm."

"I had a family in Reynoldsburg last year — March, pipe burst, five inches of standing water in the finished basement. They'd had it finished two years before. New carpet, new drywall, the whole thing. Twenty-two thousand dollars. I have the number because I looked at the original receipt. And they'd gone out and bought two of those dehumidifiers and a box fan and they had them all running in the basement and the husband was down there with a Shop-Vac and the wife was upstairs on the phone with her sister and the kids were at school and it was —" Doug stopped. He picked at the edge of the table's veneer where it had started to lift. "It was just a Tuesday. Just a regular day where everything they'd spent two years building was wrecked and they were trying to fix it with equipment that cost three hundred dollars total."

Simone sat back. She tapped her pen on the notebook twice. Doug had started keeping track of these things: the pen in her hair, the coffee on the water ring, the two taps when something mattered. He was an adjuster. He noticed patterns.

"What," Doug said.

"Nothing. That's material."

"It's a claim. I'm describing a claim."

"I know what you're describing." She crossed something out in her notebook and wrote something else. "What else do you know about that costs a hundred and forty dollars and doesn't work?"

Doug thought about this. "My reading glasses."

Simone's mouth moved but she didn't laugh. She wrote it down.


The material that worked came out sideways, always. Doug couldn't produce it on command. Simone had stopped asking him to be funny after the first week, when he'd sat through forty-five minutes of silence and then offered two observations she'd received with the expression of a physician hearing symptoms that didn't match the chart.

What worked was Doug talking about what he actually knew: insurance, the office, Roy, the mechanics of ordinary life. He'd spent fifty-two years paying attention to ordinary things without considering that attention a quality.

On Wednesday, Simone asked about the cereal.

"What kind?"

"Raisin Bran. Store brand, not Kellogg's. Kellogg's is $4.89 and the store brand is $3.19 and they're the same cereal. Same raisins, same flakes, same sugar content. I've done the comparison."

"You've compared them."

"The Kellogg's has six more raisins per serving. I counted once."

Simone looked at him. "You counted the raisins."

"It was a Sunday."

There was a silence. Outside, the sound of a car door on the street, and then nothing.

"And this is dinner," Simone said.

"Most nights. It's fast, it doesn't generate dishes. One bowl, one spoon. You rinse them and you're done. A real dinner — a cooked dinner — is a plate and a pan and a cutting board and a spatula and by the time you've cleaned up, you've spent more time on the aftermath than on the meal. The ROI on cooking for one person is terrible."

"The return on investment of dinner."

"If you factored in your time at even minimum wage, every meal you cook alone costs about thirty dollars."

"You've done that math."

"Yes."

"Why is cereal better at night?" Simone asked. "That's the part I want. Not the economics. Why do you like it better as dinner than as breakfast?"

Doug looked at the water ring on the table. "Because in the morning it's what you're supposed to eat. You eat cereal for breakfast and it's just — it's breakfast. It doesn't mean anything. But at night, it's a decision. You're choosing not to cook. You're choosing not to order something. You're choosing the simplest possible version of a meal because the day is over and you don't need to perform anything for anyone, including yourself. Cereal at night is — it's honest. It's what dinner looks like when nobody's watching."

Simone didn't say anything for a long time. She was looking at her notebook but she wasn't writing.

"Yeah," she said. "That's something."

She asked about Roy's sandwich.

"Turkey on wheat. Mustard. No tomato. He's been ordering the same thing for nine years. One time they put tomato on it by accident and he peeled it off and set it on the edge of his tray and didn't mention it. He just removed it and ate the sandwich. The next time we went in, his order was correct. I don't know if he said something between visits or if the guy behind the counter noticed. It was never discussed."

"What does Roy think about all of this?"

"He didn't watch the video."

"I know. But what does he think about you doing this — the sessions, the Netflix thing, the whole trajectory?"

Doug considered this. "Roy thinks what he thinks. He doesn't — he's not going to have an opinion and then share the opinion with me so I feel something about his opinion. If I told Roy I was quitting to do comedy, he'd say 'Okay.' If I told him I was staying at Mutual Fidelity forever, he'd say 'Okay.' The okay would be the same both times."

"And that's comforting."

"I didn't say that."

"I'm saying it."

Doug looked at the water ring. "Yeah. It is."

This kept happening. Doug would say something about Roy, or a claim, or the unit price of store-brand raisins, and the room changed. Simone would look at him and he'd know he'd handed her more than he meant to leave on the table.


The material that failed was simpler to diagnose but harder to stop producing.

On that same Wednesday afternoon, Simone asked Doug to tell her what it was like being famous.

"I'm not famous."

"You're on the internet. Eighty million views. People recognize you."

Doug tried. "It's — I mean, people come up to me. At the grocery store, or at the gas station. They want to take pictures. I don't mind it, it's fine, they're being nice. One guy at the Kroger in Clintonville asked me to do the toast for him, just right there in the produce section, and I said I didn't remember it, and he said 'Come on, man,' like I was holding out on him. I was buying bananas."

Simone watched him with the expression she got when he drifted into the public version of himself. Doug could hear it too. The sentences straightened out. He was describing fame from above instead of talking from inside it.

"That's —" Simone started.

"That's nothing. I know."

"It's not nothing. It's just not you. That's a story about being famous. It's not your story. Your story is the bananas."

"I don't have a story about bananas."

"You have a story about everything. You just told me you counted raisins on a Sunday. But when I ask you about fame, you give me 'people come up to me.' That's not Doug. That's what anyone would say."

The toast attempt was worse. Thursday of the third week, Simone asked him to talk about the toast directly.

"What do you want to know?"

"Anything. What you remember, how it felt, what you think about it now."

Doug took a breath. "It was at Kelsey's wedding. I'd had — well, the video. Brendan gave this toast that was —" He stopped. He could feel the shift happening. Simone was always listening for it: the moment he left the kitchen table and started talking from some safer, more arranged place.

"He gave a toast that was really good," Doug said, and the air went out of it. Even he could hear it. The sentence sat there on the table between them like something left too long in the sun.

"Okay," Simone said. "And then."

"And then I got up and I —" Doug looked at his hands. "I don't really remember getting up. I remember the microphone was heavier than I expected. And I remember —"

"That's dead," Simone said.

Doug stopped.

"You hear it, right?"

"Yeah."

"The microphone, Brendan's toast, all that setup. You're backing away from it and giving me the arranged version. It goes flat."

Doug sat with this. Outside, a car passed with its windows down and something with bass coming from the speakers, the low pulse of it pressing against the kitchen window and then fading. April in Clintonville. The trees along the street had buds that looked swollen and irritated, like they hadn't decided whether spring was worth the effort.

"So what am I supposed to do with it?"

"Nothing. Stop trying to make it presentable." Simone uncapped her pen. "The insurance stuff, the cereal, the dehumidifier, Roy's sandwich — you know those cold. You stay inside them. The toast, the Kroger stuff, any of this fame shit — you step outside yourself and start explaining Doug to me. I can't use that."

"That's a terrible note."

"It's a useful one," Simone said.

Doug felt the note settle with the ugly certainty of policy language. The parts Simone kept were the parts he hadn't dressed up: insurance, Roy, cereal, prices, habits. The toast and the fame collapsed the minute he tried to manage how they sounded. That terrified him because the first category was his actual life. If he did the material that worked in front of strangers, he wouldn't be giving them jokes. He'd be handing over the inventory: a fifty-two-year-old man who counted raisins on a Sunday because nothing else in the apartment needed counting.

They broke for lunch. Simone went to the sandwich place around the corner — she'd been going there since her second week in Columbus and ordered a different sandwich every time, which Doug found inefficient. He ate a granola bar and drank water and stood in his kitchen looking at the Ikea bookshelf and the art print Megan had left behind, a watercolor of something that might have been a bridge or a pier. It occurred to him Megan had probably left it because she didn't want it. The thought arrived twelve years late and stung anyway.

Simone came back with a Cuban sandwich and a coffee. She set the coffee on the water ring.

"More insurance," she said.

Doug talked about depreciation. Thursday, or maybe still Wednesday; the sessions had started to bleed together. Same table, same recorder, same strip of light moving across the kitchen. He talked about what happens to the value of a couch over time — how a couch you bought for twelve hundred dollars is worth, after ten years, maybe two hundred, and the difference between what you paid and what it's worth now is depreciation. It was the most honest math he knew. It accounted for wear. It admitted use diminishes value.

"And that's how it works with everything," Doug said. "Not just couches. Appliances, roofing, carpeting, your water heater, your HVAC system. Everything has a schedule. Everything depreciates at a predictable rate. You can look it up. There are tables."

"And marriages?" Simone said. She said it casually, like it was the next item on a list, but her pen was still.

Doug looked at the recorder. "There's no table for that."

"But."

"But if there were." He paused. "If there were, you'd have your replacement cost and your actual cash value. Replacement cost is what it would take to get the same thing new. Actual cash value is what it's worth now, after everything. After the years and the arguments and the Tuesday nights and the way someone loads the dishwasher and the sound they make when they're sleeping and all of it. The actual cash value is what's left when you subtract the depreciation."

"And nobody puts that in the paperwork."

"Nobody puts that in the paperwork."

Simone wrote something. She didn't look up.


The second scene happened on a Friday. They'd been at it since nine. By four the light had gone amber and moved across the table from the recorder to Simone's notebook to the space between their hands.

They were both tired. Simone had stopped taking notes an hour earlier. The recorder kept running. After long enough they both forgot it.

She was asking about the apartment itself. She'd already taken what she wanted from it as material — the cereal, the bookshelf, the art print, the second bedroom with the boxes — and now she wanted the life around those things.

"How long have you been here?"

"Twelve years. Since the divorce."

"And you moved here right after."

"Within a month. Megan — we sold the house. Split it. I found this place online. I didn't see it in person before I signed the lease. The pictures were accurate."

"That's a very insurance-adjuster way to pick an apartment."

"The pictures were accurate."

Simone smiled. It wasn't the diagnostic smile. She was tired, and tired took the edge off her face. She'd told him once that Columbus was messing with her sleep because her body stayed on New York time. By late afternoon it showed. She sat deeper in the chair and stopped looking ready to spring out of it.

"How did you know it was over?" Simone said. "Your marriage."

Doug looked at her. This wasn't a material question. Simone wasn't looking at her notebook. She was looking at him.

"The conversation, or —"

"Whatever it was. The moment that made it real."

Doug didn't answer right away. He could feel the pull toward telling it neatly, the way Megan would have told it. Simone sat very still.

"We'd been — Megan had been saying things for a while. About how she felt. About the marriage. She said it in that organized Megan voice. She'd say, 'I think we need to acknowledge that this isn't working,' and I'd say, 'What isn't working?' and she'd look at me and I genuinely didn't know what she meant. Or — I knew. I could also truthfully say I didn't know, because knowing and understanding are different coverage levels and I was insured for one but not the other."

Simone didn't react to the metaphor. She waited.

"So she left. One day I came home from work and she'd taken things. Not everything. Her clothes, her toiletries, some books. She took the KitchenAid mixer — four hundred and twenty dollars, I never used it — and she left the art print." He gestured at the wall without looking at it. "We had different stuff by then. Separate accounts. She'd already been moving into the new place incrementally, and the day she took the last of it was just — it was a Wednesday. I came home and I could see what was gone. I walked through the rooms and I could inventory it."

"But."

"But I was fine. I was processing. I knew it was coming. I'd known for months. The paperwork was in progress. I was managing it."

He heard the word after he said it and wished he'd chosen another.

"And then I took a shower. End of the day, I always shower when I get home, I still do. And I got in and her shampoo was gone from the ledge."

Simone did not move.

"I don't remember the brand. Something with green tea in the name. It came in a tall green bottle and she'd been using the same shampoo the entire marriage. Eighteen years. The bottle was always on the ledge. Every morning, every night. Even after she started talking about leaving, even after the conversations and the lawyers and the listing agreement — the bottle was there. It was the last thing of hers in the bathroom."

The apartment was quiet. Outside, late-afternoon sounds — a dog somewhere, a lawn mower whining once before it caught, the seasonal noise of Columbus deciding it was spring. The stripe of light on the table had reached Doug's hand.

"Everything else I could process. The mixer, the books, the clothes — those were items. I could inventory them, I could assess the depreciation, I could arrive at a number and the number would be accurate and I'd be fine. But the shampoo —" He looked at his hand in the light. "You can't depreciate shampoo. It's a consumable. It has no residual value. It's just gone, and the ledge where it sat is empty, and every time you take a shower you see the empty space on the ledge where it was. And after a while you don't see it anymore, because you adjust, because that's what you do, but for the first — I don't know. Six months. A year. Every shower."

He stopped. The recorder hummed between them, so quiet it was nearly the sound of the room itself.

Simone picked up her pen. She did not put it in her hair. She opened her notebook to a clean page and wrote. She wrote for a while, longer than she'd written down any single thing Doug had said in three weeks.

She didn't laugh. She didn't give him one of her work words. She just kept writing.

Doug watched her hand move across the page. She had water damage and cereal and Roy's sandwich and the art print and the boxes and now the empty ledge in his shower. It was all the same pile. If this ever went onstage, people wouldn't just be hearing jokes. They'd be seeing how he lived.

He was going to be known.

The pen stopped. Simone looked up. Her eyes were red at the edges. She closed the notebook and set the pen on top of it.

"We should stop for today," she said.

"Okay."

Neither of them moved. The light had shifted past Doug and was climbing the wall behind him, up over the watercolor Megan hadn't wanted.

"I've never told anyone that," Doug said. "The shampoo."

Simone reached forward and clicked the recorder off. The click barely disturbed the room.

"I know," she said.

They sat until the light thinned. Simone finished her cold coffee. Then she packed the notebook and recorder into her canvas bag.

"Same time Monday," she said.

"Yeah."

At the door she half-turned back to him.

"The shampoo isn't for the set."

Doug looked at her.

"I'm writing it down because you didn't dress it up," she said. "That's different."

He nodded. "Okay."

She left. He heard her on the stairs, then the outside door.

The apartment went quiet. Her coffee cup was in the sink. Her pen cap had rolled onto the table while she was packing up. He picked it up and set it on the counter.

He went to the bathroom. He didn't turn on the shower. He looked at the ledge. His own shampoo sat there, blue cap, half empty.

Back in the kitchen, he poured a bowl of store-brand Raisin Bran and ate it standing at the counter. The milk was two days from expiration and still fine. He rinsed the bowl and spoon. He washed Simone's coffee cup and set it in the rack.

The pen cap stayed on the counter.
