# Chapter 2

The video was at fourteen million views when Doug checked it at 6:47 a.m., standing in his bathroom in boxers and a t-shirt that said MUTUAL FIDELITY CLAIMS DIVISION SOFTBALL 2019. They had never had a softball division. Someone in marketing had ordered the shirts anyway, and Doug had kept his because it was soft and because he had no one to be embarrassed in front of.

Fourteen million. He said the number to the mirror, to his own face, which looked approximately the way a face looks after four hours of sleep, three of which had been spent reading what strangers thought of it. The number had no meaning. It was the population of a moderately sized country. Doug had looked this up at 1 a.m., when the count was still at eleven million and he'd needed some frame of reference — Belgium, or close to it. More people had watched him say things he couldn't remember deciding to say than lived in a nation with its own parliament and a functioning rail system.

He drove to work in the Civic with the heat on high because February in Columbus was the color of wet concrete and roughly as warm. His phone sat in the cup holder, buzzing at intervals that had stopped corresponding to individual events and had become ambient, like sitting on a washing machine. He'd stopped looking at each notification around midnight. The 310 number — Los Angeles, he'd figured out — had called six more times since Saturday. He hadn't answered. He didn't know anyone in Los Angeles. He didn't want to know anyone in Los Angeles.

The internet had been busy overnight. Doug knew this because he'd watched it happen before giving up on sleep, lying on his couch in the dark with the phone held above his face, reading the terms of his own situation.

There were memes now. Someone had captured the moment — three seconds into the toast, roughly — where Doug's left hand was raised in a gesture that, in context, was him reaching for words and, out of context, looked like a man delivering a papal blessing over his daughter's open bar. This image existed now with white block text: YOUR MARRIAGE HAS BEEN ADJUSTED. There was another one, his face mid-sentence, mouth open, eyes half-closed in what was clearly the alcohol but read on screen as beatific certainty: WHEN THE WHISKEY KICKS IN AND THE TRUTH COMES OUT. There were dozens more. Doug's face with the caption PROOF OF LOSS, which he recognized as insurance terminology someone had pulled from his job title, and which was more accurate than whoever made it probably intended.

Someone had auto-tuned the toast into a song. Doug had listened to thirty seconds. His voice — his actual voice, the one that came out of his body — had been pitch-corrected and layered over a beat that sounded like it belonged in a car driven by someone thirty years younger than him. The words were still his words. The cadence was still roughly his cadence. But the musicality made every sentence sound like it was building toward a chorus that never arrived, which was, Doug thought, not a bad description of his marriage.

He'd closed the app.

A Twitter thread by someone whose profile identified them as a "body language analyst and certified life coach" had broken down the toast frame by frame. Doug's posture (defeated). His hand gestures (subconsciously reaching). His eye contact with Kelsey (avoidant but searching). His single glance toward Megan, which the analyst called "the micro-expression of a man who has rehearsed saying nothing for twelve years and just forgot the script." The thread had four thousand retweets. Doug had watched the original video twice and could not identify any of the things this person was identifying, which either meant the analyst was making it up or that Doug's body had been doing things he hadn't authorized, which was, given the toast itself, not surprising.

A Reddit post titled "This man just eulogized his own marriage and I can't stop watching" had, when he'd last checked, forty thousand upvotes and a comment section that scrolled for what appeared to be geological ages. Doug had started reading the comments. This had been a mistake he intended to repeat.

The Mutual Fidelity parking lot looked the same as it always did. Doug pulled into his spot — not assigned, but his through the accumulation of years that turns habit into tenure — and sat in the Civic for a minute with the engine running. The building was three stories of beige brick. It processed insurance claims. It was the most uninteresting structure in a city that had several strong contenders, and Doug had never been so grateful to see it.

He walked in. The lobby had the same carpet, the same security desk where Greg sat — a large man who read Louis L'Amour paperbacks during the slow hours, which were most hours, the spines cracked and face-down on the counter like small collapsed tents. Greg waved Doug through every morning with a two-finger salute he'd been giving for nine years. This morning he held the salute a beat too long, then let his hand fall to the paperback and rubbed the bent corner of the cover with his thumb, looking down at the desk instead of at Doug.

"Morning, Greg."

"Morning, Doug." A pause. "Cold one today."

"Yeah."

Doug took the elevator to the second floor. The doors opened onto the open plan: thirty desks in rows, fluorescent lighting that made everyone look like they were recovering from something, the hum of phones and keyboards and the break-room coffee maker that produced a liquid Doug had accepted as coffee the way he accepted most things — without enthusiasm, without complaint, as a fact of the environment.

He could feel them looking. Not everyone — some people were working, or performing work, or genuinely hadn't seen the video, which was still possible on a Monday morning, though the window was closing fast. But enough. Karen from auto claims, who sat three rows over and who Doug knew primarily as someone who ate a hard-boiled egg at her desk every morning at 9:15, the smell arriving with the regularity and gentleness of a small chemical event — Karen looked up when he walked past and then looked down so fast she nearly pulled something. Dave, who handled commercial property and who kept a signed photo of Woody Hayes on his desk next to a jar of mixed nuts he ate from continuously with the unconscious rhythm of a man feeding a parking meter — Dave opened his mouth, closed it, opened it again, and said "Hey, Doug" in the careful voice people use when they're pretending this is a normal interaction and the pretending is the loudest thing in the room.

"Hey, Dave."

Doug sat at his desk. His desk was the same desk. His computer was the same computer. His work inbox — the Mutual Fidelity one — had seventeen new messages, which was about right for a Monday, though two turned out to be from people he barely knew in other departments who'd found pretexts to email him. One was from a woman in accounting named Sherry who needed "clarification on a claim number" and who had never contacted Doug directly in six years of working in the same building. The claim number she referenced was real, but the question about it was so simple that anyone with system access could have answered it themselves, and Doug understood this in the way he understood most things about Sherry — distantly, without opinion, and with the recognition that the email was about the video the way Janet was about to be about the Hoffmeyer claim.

Janet appeared at 9:40. She stood at the edge of his cubicle in her usual posture — one hand on the fabric partition, the other holding her coffee mug, which was ceramic and white and said WORLD'S OKAY-EST MOM, a gift from her teenage son that she'd been drinking from for two years with an expression that suggested she was still deciding whether it was affectionate or an insult. Janet kept a dish of butterscotch hard candies on her desk, the kind in gold cellophane wrappers, and she'd been offering them before difficult conversations for so long that the appearance of a butterscotch had become, around the office, a kind of early warning system. She wasn't holding a candy now, which was either a good sign or evidence that she was trying to approach this casually and couldn't quite get there.

"Doug. Do you have a minute?"

"Sure."

She sat in the chair next to his desk — technically Roy's overflow chair, but it served as the conference seat for conversations that didn't merit the actual conference room, which had been double-booked since January and retained the faint memory of a tuna someone had microwaved in there during the holidays.

"The Hoffmeyer claim," Janet said. She opened the folder. "The contractor's estimate came in, and it's within range, but I want to make sure we're documenting the square footage correctly. We had that issue last month with the Patterson file where the adjuster — not you — used the wrong calculation for the roof pitch, and we ended up overpaying by eleven hundred dollars, which isn't catastrophic but it's not nothing."

"I can double-check the measurements."

"Good. Thank you."

They looked at each other. Janet closed the Hoffmeyer folder. She didn't leave. She held the folder in her lap and directed her gaze at a spot slightly left of Doug's face — the spot on the partition where he'd pinned a Columbus Blue Jackets schedule that was two seasons out of date and that he kept meaning to take down and hadn't, because taking it down would require deciding what, if anything, to replace it with, and that question, over time, had become harder rather than easier.

"So," Janet said.

"Yeah," Doug said.

Janet opened her mouth, closed it, and stood up. "Let me know about the Hoffmeyer measurements."

"I will."

She walked back to her office. Doug watched her go. She paused at her desk, picked up a butterscotch, unwrapped it, and put it in her mouth. She stood there with the cellophane twisted in her fist, looking at her monitor without touching the keyboard, the candy shifting once in her cheek.

---

Roy arrived at 10:15, which was late. Roy was never late. Roy was early or precisely on time because his relationship with time was the same as his relationship with everything else — he'd arrived at an accommodation and didn't see the point in revisiting it. But this morning Roy had been to the dentist, and the right side of his jaw was swollen in a way that made him look like he was keeping a small, uncomfortable piece of information in his cheek. He sat at his desk, which was adjacent to Doug's in the way it had been adjacent for nine years, and opened his computer with the patient deliberation of a man logging into a system he didn't particularly want to use but had accepted as part of the arrangement.

"Morning," Roy said. The word came out slightly sideways because of the jaw.

"Morning. You okay?"

"Root canal." Roy said this the way he said everything — as fact, without commentary or expectation.

"Sorry."

"It's fine. It's a tooth."

Doug's phone buzzed on the desk. He turned it face-down. His work email dinged — Janet, forwarding something from HR titled "Social Media Guidelines: A Reminder for All Employees," which had been sent to the full office but which Doug understood, with the practiced clarity of a man trained to read the subtext of policy language, was for him.

"Roy."

"Yeah."

"Did you—" Doug stopped. He wasn't sure how to phrase it. "Have you been on the internet this weekend?"

Roy blinked once. The swollen side of his jaw made the pause look slower than it probably was. He kept his eyes on Doug long enough for the fluorescent lights to fill the silence. "No," he said. "I check the weather. That's about it."

"Okay."

"Why?"

Doug looked at his phone, which was buzzing against the desk surface with a sound like something small and trapped. "No reason."

Roy considered this. He opened a file — paper, because Roy still preferred paper files even though the system had been digital for three years and Janet had stopped fighting this preference approximately eighteen months ago.

"I heard something," Roy said, without looking up.

"Yeah?"

"Something happened at the wedding. A couple people mentioned it."

"Yeah."

"I don't need to know what it was."

"Okay."

"I'm just saying that. In case you were going to tell me."

"I wasn't."

"Good." Roy turned a page. He read it. He turned another page. Doug watched him read the way you watch a person do something simple and ordinary when the rest of the world has become complicated and strange — with a gratitude so large he could feel it in his hands, which had been gripping the armrests of his chair without his noticing, and which he now made himself release.

The desk phone rang. The landline, which almost never rang because most business came through email and the only people who called the desk line were older policyholders who didn't trust computers and Kelsey, who sometimes tried the office when Doug's cell went to voicemail. He picked it up.

"Doug Bridge, Mutual Fidelity."

"Mr. Bridge, this is Tammy Hess from the *Today* show. I'm calling to see if—"

"I'm sorry, this is my work line."

"I understand, and I apologize. We got your office number from—"

"I'm sorry," Doug said, and hung up.

He looked at the phone. The *Today* show had his work number. The *Today* show had found the general number for Mutual Fidelity Insurance, navigated the automated directory, and selected Doug Bridge, Claims Division. He tried to imagine the person — Tammy Hess — sitting at a desk somewhere, probably in New York, probably in a building that was not beige brick, calling an insurance adjuster in Columbus because of something he'd said drunk at a wedding.

"Who was that?" Roy asked.

"Nobody. Wrong number."

Roy accepted this the way he accepted everything — completely, without investigation. He turned another page.

Doug's personal phone had accumulated, during the Roy conversation, eleven more emails to the Gmail account he used for nothing except password resets and the occasional communication from Kelsey's high school, which she'd graduated from ten years ago and which still sent him fundraising appeals he'd never unsubscribed from because the process of unsubscribing felt, somehow, like a more decisive severing than the divorce had been.

The emails were from people and organizations he'd heard of and many he hadn't. A production company. A podcast called *Dads Being Real*. Two literary agents. A woman who identified herself as a "brand partnership consultant." Someone from a morning radio show in Cincinnati. The subject lines varied — "Quick question," "Loved your speech," "Opportunity," "Collaboration inquiry," "YOU'RE AMAZING" — and each one was a small door Doug had not opened and did not want to open and could not stop looking at.

He put the phone in his desk drawer. He closed the drawer. The phone continued buzzing inside the drawer, muffled now, the sound of a wasp under a glass.

He opened the Hoffmeyer file. He measured the roof pitch calculation. He documented the square footage. The numbers were straightforward and the numbers asked nothing of him and he read them with a care that anyone watching might have mistaken for dedication but was actually something closer to need.

By noon the video was at twenty-two million. Doug knew this because he checked in the bathroom, standing in a stall with his pants on and the seat down, reading the screen with the focused attention of a man studying the results of a test he hadn't known he was taking. He refreshed the page. Twenty-two million and climbing. He put the phone away and went back to his desk and ate the turkey sandwich he'd brought from home and didn't taste any of it.

By two o'clock it was at twenty-six million. By the time he left the office at 5:30, walking across the parking lot in the dark — because February in Columbus meant darkness at 5:30, and the parking lot lights made the slush look orange, and the cold was the kind that found the gap between his collar and his neck and settled there — the count had reached thirty-one million.

---

He drove home. The apartment was the same as he'd left it — the couch cushion still dented from where he'd sat reading comments until two the night before, the suit from the wedding draped over the kitchen chair where he'd finally taken it off Sunday afternoon, the one dress shoe by the front door and the other one still unaccounted for. He'd been wearing one shoe when he woke up Sunday morning. The other was somewhere between the reception hall and his apartment, and the fact that he'd been home for two days and hadn't looked for it or thought about it until now was the kind of thing that, in a different person's life, might have been funny.

He stood at the kitchen counter and ate a bowl of cereal. Raisin Bran, the store brand, not because he preferred it but because it was $2.19 and the name brand was $4.49 and the taste difference wasn't enough to justify the markup. This was the kind of calculation Megan had watched him make for eighteen years — the per-unit comparison, the coupon logic, the small satisfactions of rational purchasing — and the look on her face during the last three years of their marriage, when he'd hold up two boxes of something in the cereal aisle and explain the math, had been the look of a woman who had realized that the man she'd married would comparison-shop his way through every experience, including the ones where the point was not the savings but the standing next to someone in a grocery store because you wanted to be there.

The cereal was fine. The apartment was quiet. Outside, a car drove past with its bass turned up so loud the glasses in the cabinet above the sink rattled against each other, a sound Doug heard every night at roughly this time and had stopped noticing and now noticed again because noticing was all he'd been doing for two days and he couldn't turn it off.

He checked the phone. Forty-four million.

The number had more than tripled since the morning. Doug tried to visualize forty-four million people. He could not. He tried to think of it in insurance terms — forty-four million policyholders, each one a file, each file a name and an address and a specific thing that had gone wrong with their house. He couldn't process that either. The number existed outside any frame he had for understanding the world. He assessed claims for houses in central Ohio. He understood square footage, depreciation schedules, the rate at which a water-damaged hardwood floor loses value. He did not understand forty-four million strangers forming opinions about seven minutes of his life that he himself could barely remember living.

He sat on the couch. He opened the comments.

He knew this was a mistake. He'd known it was a mistake the first time, Sunday morning, and had done it anyway, the way he sometimes couldn't stop re-reading a claim file that didn't add up — the compulsion of looking for the one detail that would make the whole thing cohere.

The comments didn't cohere. They weren't supposed to. They were the sound of a fraction of forty-four million people processing Doug Bridge into something that fit their needs.

**This guy is my hero.** That was the most common type. Doug as folk hero, Doug as truth-teller, Doug as the man who said what everyone thinks but nobody says. These came with exclamation marks and crying-laughing emoji and the phrase "I felt that" repeated so many times it had stopped being a feeling and become a category.

**This is the saddest thing I've ever seen.** The second type. Doug as cautionary tale, Doug as the embodiment of male loneliness, Doug as what happens when you don't talk to someone for twelve years and then talk to everyone at once. A woman with a profile picture of a sunset had written a three-paragraph comment about her own father that was longer and more emotionally detailed than anything Doug's own daughter had said to him about the toast, and he read it twice and then felt guilty for reading it, which was a strange thing to feel about a stranger's confession about her own life that had nothing and everything to do with his.

**This is clearly staged.** A persistent minority. Doug was an actor. The whole thing was a marketing stunt. The "wedding" was fake. One commenter had produced a frame-by-frame analysis arguing that the lighting was "too professional for a phone camera" and that Doug's stumble at the four-minute mark was "choreographed." Doug had been drunk enough that he couldn't remember the stumble at all, but he could guarantee it hadn't been choreographed, because he had never choreographed anything in his life, including the one dance at his own wedding, which Megan had led.

**What an asshole.** The angry ones. Doug had ruined his daughter's wedding. Doug had humiliated his ex-wife in front of everyone she knew. Doug was a narcissist, a drunk, a man who had made his child's day about his own failures. These were the ones that came from people who seemed to be yelling — sentences short and emphatic and absolutely certain — and Doug read them with the numb attention of a man reviewing a claims denial he'd already anticipated. They weren't wrong. Some of the things he'd said — he still hadn't watched the full video a third time, but the pieces he remembered were enough — some of those things should not have been said. Not because they weren't true but because a wedding reception at the Columbus Athenaeum with eighty guests and an open bar and his daughter in a white dress was not the venue for truth, and knowing this didn't help, because knowing things after the fact was Doug's particular skill, and it had never once in his life made any difference.

**I can't stop watching this.** The addicts. People who'd watched it three times, five times, twelve times. People who'd memorized lines. People who quoted his sentences back with timestamps and wrote "THIS PART" as though the words were a shared text they'd all studied. Doug did not understand how something he'd said once, drunk, to a room of eighty people at the Athenaeum, had become a thing strangers annotated.

There were others. A man who'd written, simply, "I'm going to call my daughter." A woman who said, "The part about the table made me leave my husband," and Doug stared at this for a long time because a person he had never met was claiming that something he'd said, drunk, about a table — a table; he could barely remember the table part — had ended her marriage, and either she was exaggerating or she wasn't, and he didn't know which was worse. A teenager, or someone who typed like one, had written: "ok boomer but also i'm crying." A commenter named InsuranceBro614, whose username alone made Doug's stomach tighten because it could have been someone from the office, had written: "I work in claims and this is the most accurate description of a marriage I've ever heard from an adjuster. 10/10 would recommend the whiskey." Doug did not want to figure out who InsuranceBro614 was. He also could not stop trying to figure out who InsuranceBro614 was.

He read comments until 1:45 a.m. The apartment was dark except for the phone's glow. His cereal bowl was in the sink, unwashed. The suit was still on the chair. The missing shoe was still missing.

At 1:47 he put the phone down and picked up the Gahanna file.

The Gahanna claim was a water heater. A standard forty-gallon residential unit in a house on Agler Road — the homeowner had submitted it Friday, before the wedding, and it had been sitting in Doug's take-home stack since then, untouched, while Doug was getting drunk and saying true things and becoming a person forty-four million strangers had opinions about. The water heater had burst at the base seam. The basement had taken four inches of standing water before the homeowner found it. He'd documented everything: photographs of the water line on the basement walls, the receipt for the original unit purchased in 2016, a contractor's estimate for remediation and replacement. The documentation was clean. The coverage was clear. The depreciation on a 2016 unit was calculable — ten years of expected life, six years in service, the math was straightforward and the math didn't care what Doug had done at a wedding.

He read the file. He read it again. He read the contractor's estimate line by line — demolition of damaged drywall, removal of saturated carpet padding, installation of dehumidification equipment for a minimum of seventy-two hours, replacement of the forty-gallon unit with a comparable model, labor at $85 per hour for an estimated fourteen hours. The numbers were real. They referred to actual things — water that had actually damaged a floor, carpet that was actually ruined, a heater that had actually failed at a seam the way heaters do when they're six years old and the anode rod hasn't been replaced and nobody checks the anode rod because nobody thinks about the thing that keeps the tank from corroding until the tank corrodes. The numbers didn't have opinions about Doug. They didn't think he was brave or pathetic or a genius. They were a claim, and he was an adjuster, and the distance between the problem and the resolution was a thing he could measure and close.

He made notes on the file. He flagged a question about whether the homeowner's policy covered mold remediation or whether that would require a separate rider — the standing water had been there for several hours before discovery, and four inches for several hours in a basement with limited ventilation was enough to start a mold issue that the homeowner might not see for weeks. He paper-clipped the estimate to the photograph of the water line. He put the file on the kitchen table, aligned with the edge, and set his pen on top of it.

It was 2:20 a.m. The phone sat face-down on the couch where he'd left it. The screen was dark. Somewhere — on the internet, in Belgium, in all the places people look at their phones in the middle of the night — millions of people were watching Doug Bridge say the truest things he'd ever said, and none of them knew that the man in the video was sitting in a two-bedroom apartment in Clintonville, in his underwear, at a kitchen table, at 2:20 in the morning, processing a water heater claim from Gahanna because it was the only thing left that made sense.
