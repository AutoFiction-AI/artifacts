# Chapter 16

The Lincoln Theatre was on East Long Street, in a part of Columbus that Doug had driven past for twenty-six years without ever registering as a place where people went on purpose. He'd processed a claim once for a storefront two blocks east — water intrusion, failed flashing, four inches of standing water in a basement that had been a jazz bar in the 1940s — but the theater itself had never entered his consciousness until Layla sent him a one-page fact sheet listing its seating capacity (582 on her sheet; 580 in the Netflix contract, so her sheet was wrong and Doug had not raised it), its Art Deco heritage, and three bullet points under the heading "Why This Venue Works For Your Brand."

He had never been inside it. He was inside it now.

The lobby smelled like old carpet and fresh paint. Black cables ran from the front doors to the theater entrance under strips of gaffer tape. Doug had nearly tripped on the first strip and learned the name from a crew member. Gray adhesive residue clung to the edge of his shoe. He noticed things carefully and for no useful purpose.

The stage was bigger than he'd imagined. He'd pictured something the size of the comedy clubs — Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Detroit — where the stages were platforms, barely elevated, close enough to the front row that you could see the drinks sweating. This was different. The stage at the Lincoln Theatre was wide and deep, with a proscenium arch above it and wings on either side where things disappeared into darkness, and when Doug walked out onto it for the first time, at 10:15 a.m. on a Wednesday in early June, his footsteps echoed in a way that sounded like someone else walking behind him.

The seats were empty. Five hundred and eighty of them. Dark red upholstery. The work lights were on. A balcony ran along the upper level, more seats up there, and Doug could see Simone already sitting in the front row of the balcony, legs crossed, notebook on her knee, watching the stage the way she watched everything — like a doctor reading a chart she'd already diagnosed.

The crew was setting up. Nineteen people, Layla had told him, as if the number were a good sign. Doug counted them himself because counting gave him something to do while people moved around him carrying equipment he didn't understand. Four cameras on wheeled platforms. Lights hung from metal rigs above the stage and stood along the sides, aimed at the stool. At the back of the orchestra section a woman worked the sound board with headphones around her neck and a faded pink travel mug that said KAUAI. A rubber band snapped against her left wrist between tasks. Cables crossed the stage floor. A stool sat at center stage. Next to it, a small table with a glass of water and a monitor angled down toward him. Nobody had explained the monitor. Doug hadn't asked.

Charlie Falk was in the third row, center section, wearing sneakers brighter than any of the stage lights. He had a notebook, his phone, and a disposable coffee cup with a palm tree on it. Columbus apparently had a coffee place with a palm tree logo and Doug had missed it for twenty-six years. Charlie was talking to a woman in her thirties with a headset, a clipboard, and a pen behind her ear. She kept touching the pen as if checking it was still there.

"Okay!" Charlie called up to the stage. "Whenever you're ready, Doug. Just run it through. We're not rolling, this is just a feel thing. Getting the energy of the room."

The room's energy, as far as Doug could assess, was the energy of a room that was waiting for something to happen and was professionally prepared to wait as long as necessary. The crew moved efficiently and quietly. Someone adjusted a light. Someone else checked a cable connection at the base of a camera. The sound board woman sipped from her Kauai mug. The room was cool — the air conditioning had been running since before Doug arrived, and the vents were audible, a low hum that Doug tried not to think of as the sound the theater was making while it waited for him.

He looked at the stool. He looked at the glass of water. He looked at the monitor, now clearly a countdown timer set to sixty minutes and not yet started. He looked at the five hundred and eighty empty seats and the nineteen crew members and the four cameras and Charlie Falk's palm-tree coffee and Layla, who was in the back row of the orchestra section, sitting in the aisle seat, doing something with her phone that could have been texting or scrolling or praying.

He picked up the microphone from the stool. It was heavier than the ones in the clubs. Or it was the same weight and his hand was different. He couldn't tell.

"Good morning," he said. The microphone turned his voice into something that filled the room and bounced off the back wall and came back to him a half-second later, so that he heard himself greeting an empty theater twice, which felt about right for how the last six months had gone.

He started the material.

The first three minutes were fine. Fine in the way that the first three minutes of the corporate keynote in Indianapolis had been fine, and the first two minutes in Cleveland had been fine, and the first minute in Pittsburgh before he froze had been fine — fine in the way that falling is fine for the first several floors, before the ground arrives. Doug delivered the opening section about insurance and risk and the way people assess their lives without knowing they're doing it, and the words came out in the right order and at roughly the right pace and they had the right pauses and the right emphasis, but they were dead on arrival. They left his mouth, crossed maybe seven rows, and hit the floor.

He kept going. The cereal bit. The bit about Roy's sandwich order that never changes. The water damage section that Simone had called "the core of the set" and that had worked, in flashes, in Detroit, for about ninety seconds before he'd lost it. He delivered all of it and it sat in the theater like furniture in a model home — arranged correctly, signifying a life no one lived in.

Charlie was taking notes on his phone. His thumb moved fast, then stopped. He'd look up over the screen at the stage as if waiting for it to explain itself, then start typing again. Doug could almost hear the language that would get wrapped around this later — energy, shape, conversation, room. The clipboard woman had stopped nodding. A camera operator near the left wing was adjusting a lens that did not need adjusting, his hands finding something to do while the stage produced nothing worth recording.

Doug got to the twelve-minute mark and stopped. Not because he'd run out of material but because the silence between his sentences had become the dominant sound in the room, louder than the air conditioning, louder than the crew's patient not-watching, louder than his own amplified voice saying things that had no weight.

"Can I start over?" he said.

"Absolutely," Charlie said. "Reset, take a breath, do your thing."

Doug took a breath. He did his thing. His thing, it turned out, was the same thing — the same words in the same order with the same cadence producing the same nothing. Simone had spent weeks building the material from the insurance stories and the cereal monologue and Roy's sandwich order and the other pieces of Doug's life she'd identified as the source of his voice. On paper it held together. Onstage it was a photograph of a fire: all image, no heat.

He made it twenty-one minutes the second time. That was worse. The extra nine minutes meant nine more minutes of words landing on empty air and the crew's patience becoming something Doug could feel against his skin, like humidity. The water damage section came out as a monologue about rot and dehumidifiers and Home Depot and people standing on their ruined floors, and Doug could hear, as he said it, the exact version of these words that had worked in his kitchen when he wasn't trying to say them — and this version, the performed version, the version with the mic and the lights and the five hundred and eighty seats, was a photocopy of a photocopy, and every generation lost something he couldn't identify or replace.

He stopped. He set the microphone on the stool.

From the balcony, Simone gave one small, unsurprised shake of the head, the look Doug had seen on homeowners still trying to save floors that had already buckled. Her notebook stayed closed on her knee. Doug had been watching Simone watch him for weeks and knew her silences the way he knew water damage timelines — by stage. This one was day four.

---

Layla took Doug to dinner at a restaurant three blocks from the theater. It was Italian, or the menu was in Italian and the bread came with olive oil, and the tables had white paper over the tablecloths that you could draw on with crayons from a cup, which suggested the restaurant had not decided what it was. Layla ordered a glass of red wine and then a second glass before the entrees arrived, which Doug had not seen her do before. She also ordered a bruschetta she didn't eat — it sat between them on a small plate, accumulating olive oil around its edges, and Doug tracked its gradual saturation with more attention than the conversation warranted because the conversation was the thing he did not want to be tracking.

Charlie was there too. He'd changed his sneakers since the rehearsal. Doug noticed this because the sneakers were now white instead of orange, which meant Charlie had brought two pairs of sneakers to Columbus for a two-day trip, which meant Charlie had a system for footwear that Doug could not access and did not want to.

"Okay," Layla said. "Here's what I've got." She was leaning forward, elbows on the white paper, and she'd drawn a small circle with one of the crayons without seeming to realize it. Red crayon. Tight circle.

"What if we restructured the format," she said. "Not the content — the content is strong, the content's really strong — but what if the delivery was more conversational? Like, what if Charlie moderated? What if it was more of a sit-down, interview-style — "

"A conversation," Charlie said. He was already nodding, already building. "Yes. Like, Doug, you sit, I sit, we just — it's like a hangout. Like a late-night vibe but without the bits. Just two guys talking and the audience is in on it."

"I'm not a talk show host," Doug said.

"No, and that's why it works. You're not hosting, you're just there. You're being Doug. I'm the conduit. I'm the thing that lets the Doug happen."

Doug picked up a piece of bread and looked at it. The bread was good. It was the best part of the evening so far, and the evening was twenty minutes old.

"Or," Layla said, pivoting without taking a breath. "Okay, other idea. What if we showed the original toast? Like, on a screen behind you, and you watch it and react."

"React to my own video."

"Yes! And you can comment on it, you can fill in the blanks, you can tell the audience what you were actually thinking at each — because that's what people want to know. They've seen the toast. They want the inside."

Doug chewed the bread. He swallowed. He set the remaining piece on his plate.

"That's not comedy. That's a DVD commentary."

The table went quiet. The first two ideas died there. Layla's crayon circle had been joined by a second circle, tighter than the first, drawn inside it. Charlie was looking at his phone but not reading it. His eyes were parked a few inches above the screen.

"What about a Q and A," Charlie said after a while. "From the audience. Totally unscripted, totally — the audience drives it, Doug responds, and we just capture what happens."

"A Q and A about what."

"About everything. About you, about the toast, about your life, about — "

"That's what the profile was. That's what the whole internet is. People asking me questions and me not having the answers they want."

"But that's the bit," Charlie said. "That IS the bit. You not having the answers — that's what people — "

"That's not a bit. That's just what happens when you ask me questions."

Layla reached for her wine glass, stopped, and drew a third circle inside the second one. The crayon made a faint sound against the paper, a dry scratching that was the loudest thing at the table. She put the crayon down. She picked it up again. She put it down.

Then Layla did something Doug had never witnessed: she stopped talking. She sat there with both hands flat on the paper tablecloth.

"What if we..." Charlie said. He lifted a finger as if the rest of the sentence were on its way, then let his hand fall and looked past the bruschetta at nothing Doug could use.

Doug looked at them both. Layla with her circles and her untouched bruschetta and her two glasses of wine. Charlie with his white sneakers and his phone by the plate, screen dark now. Nobody had another idea.

"Can I get the check?" Doug said to the waiter. Nobody argued.

---

He went back to the theater at a quarter to eleven. The front doors were locked but a side entrance was propped open with a rubber wedge — a man in a black T-shirt carrying a coil of cable over his shoulder nodded at Doug as he passed without asking what he was doing there. People had been nodding at Doug without questioning his presence for six months. It was one of the consistent features of being briefly famous — the assumption that you belonged wherever you showed up, even when you had no idea why you were there yourself.

The theater was almost dark. The production lights were off, the work lights were off, but a single bare bulb on a tall stand glowed at center stage — a ghost light, one of the crew had called it that morning during setup, explaining to a colleague that you always leave one burning. Tradition. Also safety, the orchestra pit being three feet down and uncovered in places. Also, the same crew member had added without smiling, ghosts. Doug hadn't been able to tell if he meant it. He'd had a tattoo of a compass on his forearm and the habit of testing cable connections by tugging them twice, always twice, with the precision of someone who had learned the cost of a loose connection.

The ghost light threw a circle of yellow on the stage floor, maybe eight feet across, and beyond it the theater was darkness and shape — the curve of the balcony, the rows of seats visible only as repeating shadows, the exit signs glowing red at the ends of the aisles like the indicator lights on smoke detectors. The HVAC was still running, quieter now than it had been during the day, a vibration Doug felt through the stage floor more than he heard through the air.

He sat on the edge of the stage, legs hanging over the front, feet dangling above the orchestra pit. The pit was shallow — three feet, maybe, with a plywood cover over most of it and a gap near the left wing where cables had been run through. Doug looked at the gap and estimated the fall onto plywood from stage height at roughly the same as stepping off a kitchen counter: survivable, stupid, enough to break a wrist if you landed wrong. He chose not to think about what it meant that his first action in the dark empty theater was to assess the fall risk. He was an adjuster. He assessed things. It was the only response to the world he'd ever had that worked reliably.

The seats stretched out in front of him, five hundred and eighty of them, visible only as shapes in the red exit-sign glow. In two days they would hold people. Kelsey would be in the fifth row. Tyler would be next to her, saying something agreeable about the venue, because Tyler said agreeable things about everything and meant them all equally and none of them specifically. Megan and Brendan would be in the back section, which Doug knew because Layla had mentioned the seat assignments and Doug had absorbed them the way he absorbed claim file details — completely, involuntarily, and with the understanding that knowing a fact and knowing what to do with it were entirely different competencies.

Gloria would be there. Fourth row center. She'd called two days ago to confirm she was coming and to tell him that Ruth had wanted to come too and Gloria had said no because "Ruth claps at the wrong times and I won't have it." Doug had not argued. Gloria's management of Ruth was not his jurisdiction.

Roy would not be there. That was Roy.

Doug sat in the quiet and thought about his mother at the Maple Glen dining room, eating what the retirement community called lasagna, telling him that the thing about the table was real in a way the rest of his speech usually wasn't. She'd said it the way she said everything — as a fact about the world that happened to involve her son — and Doug had taken it in and moved on because Gloria's assessments did not come with instructions for what to do after you'd heard them. But the sentence had stayed. It had attached itself to him the way the water ring had attached itself to his kitchen table — present, visible, part of the surface now.

He thought about Kelsey at the Thai place. Not the conversation itself but the moment before it, when she was looking at the menu she didn't need to read because she'd been ordering the same thing for fourteen years, and her face had the particular expression of someone who was about to say something that would change the dimensions of the room. She hadn't said it yet. She was still deciding. And Doug had watched her decide, the way he watched homeowners stand on their ruined floors before the assessment — knowing the number was coming, knowing what the number would mean, knowing that his job was to not say it yet because they were standing on the thing that was gone and they needed another minute with it.

She'd said it. The spreadsheet. The Excel file. The ledger of small grievances Doug had built during the divorce, tracking Megan's late pickups and forgotten permission slips with the meticulous specificity of a man who did not know how to grieve except through documentation. And Doug had sat across from his daughter with his curry cooling and thought about all the rows and columns and timestamps, all the evidence he'd accumulated against his ex-wife while his sixteen-year-old daughter found the file and understood, for the first time, that her father saw her life as a dataset. He'd told Kelsey he kept score because he didn't know how to miss her any other way, which was either the truest thing he'd ever said to his daughter or the most pathetic, and Doug's experience was that the truest and most pathetic things were usually the same thing, and neither one made the spreadsheet okay.

He sat on the stage. The ghost light hummed above him. The theater breathed.

Tomorrow was a day off. Layla had scheduled it as a "mental health day," which Doug understood to mean a day where nobody had to watch him not work and everybody could pretend the problem might fix itself by Thursday night. The taping was Friday. Five hundred and eighty people would be in these seats, whether they had paid for tickets or been given them — Doug didn't know which, and the not-knowing felt like one more small thing in a pile of small things he should probably understand but didn't.

He pulled out his phone. The screen lit up his face and the first four rows of seats in a blue-white wash. It was 11:22 p.m.

He called Roy.

Roy answered on the second ring. He was still awake, unusual because Roy went to bed at 9:45. Roy had mentioned that once, years ago, not as a confession or a philosophy but as a fact, and the fact had settled into Doug's understanding of Roy the way all facts about Roy settled — simply, completely, and without requiring further discussion.

"It's late," Roy said, the way he stated any other fact.

"I know. Sorry."

"I was reading." A pause. "The library had a book about bridges. Civil engineering. It's not as interesting as it sounds."

"I'm at the theater," Doug said.

"The one for the show."

"Yeah."

"At eleven o'clock at night."

"I'm sitting on the stage."

Roy didn't say anything for a moment. Doug could hear, through the phone, what he was fairly certain was the sound of Roy turning a page. Roy was still reading while talking on the phone because Roy did not subordinate one activity to another when both could be conducted at the same time. In Roy's system efficiency covered most of what other people called politeness.

"I don't think I can do this," Doug said.

There was a silence, only a few seconds. Roy's silences were measured that way, not in the theatrical pauses other people used to signal they were composing something important. Roy's silences meant he was constructing a sentence, and the sentence, when it arrived, would contain exactly the information he intended and nothing more.

"Then cancel it," Roy said.

Doug sat with this. The ghost light flickered once — a power fluctuation, or a loose connection, or ghosts.

"I signed a contract," Doug said.

"Then it becomes clerical," Roy said.

"What does that mean?"

"It means people email each other."

Doug laughed. It came out of him the way the toast had come out of him at Kelsey's wedding — without permission, without preparation, without the part of his brain that had been managing the situation for twelve hours getting a vote. The sound was small and involuntary. It was what happened when something true arrived without warning and the body recognized it before the mind could intervene.

He laughed and the theater caught the sound and sent it back to him from the balcony and the back wall and the five hundred and eighty empty seats. For a moment the Lincoln Theatre was full of Doug Bridge laughing at Roy turning disaster into office work. It was the first real thing that had happened on this stage all day.

"Yeah," Doug said. "People email each other."

"Go home," Roy said. "It's Wednesday."

"I know what day it is."

"Go home. Sleep. Eat something. Not cereal."

"I'll probably eat cereal."

"Then eat cereal." A pause. The sound of a page. "Friday's going to come whether you're ready or not. That's how days work."

"That's very helpful, Roy."

"It wasn't meant to be helpful. It was meant to be accurate."

Doug sat on the stage for another ten minutes after he hung up. The theater was quiet except for the HVAC and the ghost light's low electrical buzz and, faintly, from the alley, the sound of someone closing a truck door. He looked at the empty seats. He looked at the ghost light. He looked at the monitor by the stool. The countdown still showed sixty minutes.

Doug stood up. His knees cracked — a sound the theater faithfully amplified and returned to him, because that was what this building did: it took whatever you gave it and made it bigger, whether you wanted it to or not.

He walked off the stage into the wing, past the ghost light, through the side door and into the alley. The air outside was warm — early June, the season when Columbus stopped apologizing for its weather and committed, briefly, to being a place where you could stand outside at midnight without a coat. Doug's car was parked on East Long Street, under a streetlight that buzzed at a frequency similar to the ghost light's, as if the city and the theater were running on the same current.

He got in the car and sat for a moment with his hands on the wheel and the engine off. The streetlight buzzed above the windshield. Roy would already be back in his bridge book. Friday would still arrive without asking what he had ready for it.

He started the Civic and drove home.
