# Chapter 11

They'd been at it for three weeks, four days a week, at Doug's kitchen table. Simone had given up on jokes somewhere in the first week, and what they did now didn't have a name, or at least not one Doug recognized from any category of professional work he'd ever participated in. She asked him questions. He answered them. She decided which answers were something and which were nothing, and the criteria for that decision remained, to Doug, as opaque as the underwriting guidelines Mutual Fidelity revised every January without telling anyone what had changed.

The recorder sat between them — a small silver thing she'd carried from New York, scratched along one edge, a strip of masking tape on the back where she'd written "WORK" in black marker, though Doug didn't know what other kind of recording she might need to distinguish it from. She turned it on every morning when she arrived at nine and off again somewhere around one or two, and in between she listened in a way that made Doug aware of the specific sounds his own voice made — the pauses, the filler words he hadn't known he used, the places where a sentence died and he had to start a new one from scratch.

The table was the same one he'd bought from the IKEA in West Chester twelve years ago when he'd moved into the apartment. Birch veneer, four legs, no distinguishing features. It had a water ring from a mug he'd set down sometime in 2016 and never dealt with. Simone put her coffee on the same spot every morning, directly on the ring, which Doug noticed and did not mention because mentioning it would require acknowledging that he noticed where she put her coffee, which felt like more than the situation called for.

"Tell me about the smell," Simone said. This was a Tuesday, the third week. She had her hair up, held with a pen she sometimes pulled out to write something and then stuck back, so that by noon her hair listed slightly to the left. She was wearing a flannel shirt with a button missing at the cuff, and she'd rolled the sleeves past her elbows. There was an ink stain on the inside of her left forearm that had been there since at least Thursday.

"What smell?"

"You said water damage has a smell. Yesterday, when you were talking about the house in Gahanna. You said you can tell how long the water's been standing by the smell."

"That's not — it's not really a smell thing," Doug said. "It's more of a timeline. You walk in and there's this —" He paused. He was looking at the table. "Okay. So within the first twenty-four hours, it smells like wet. Just wet. Like a basement, or a towel you left in the washer too long. And people are still in their houses at that point, still walking around, still thinking they can fix it with fans and a dehumidifier from Home Depot. The one that costs a hundred and forty dollars. Everyone buys that one."

"Okay."

"By forty-eight hours, if the water's been standing — in the walls, under the floorboards, in the insulation — it starts to turn. It goes from wet to something organic. Not rot yet. Pre-rot. It smells like earth, kind of. Like potting soil that's been sitting in a bag in the garage all winter."

"Pre-rot," Simone said.

"And then by day four or five, it's actual rot. You don't need to test for mold at that point. Your nose knows. The homeowner's been sleeping in that air for days and they've adjusted to it, the way you adjust to anything if you're in it long enough, but you walk in from outside and it hits you. It's sweet and musty and underneath there's something sharp, like wet cardboard that's been in the sun. And the homeowner is standing there asking if their floors can be saved, and you're breathing through your mouth trying to figure out the scope of the remediation, and the answer about the floors is almost always no, but you don't say that in the first five minutes."

Simone was writing something in her notebook. She didn't look up. "Why not?"

"Because they're standing on the floors. The floors they picked out. They're standing there in the house that smells wrong and the floors are soft under their feet and they already know. They just need you to not say it yet."

Simone stopped writing. She looked at Doug for a long moment. Her expression was the one she wore when she was deciding whether something was material or just a thing that happened. The distinction mattered to her in a way Doug couldn't fully follow — it seemed to involve some internal algorithm that processed what he said through filters he had no access to.

"That's good," she said. "Keep going."

"There's nowhere to go. That's what happens."

"Tell me about the dehumidifier. The hundred-and-forty-dollar one."

"It doesn't work. Not for real water damage. It's fine for a damp basement, but if you've got standing water in your walls, it's like trying to bail out a pool with a coffee cup. But people buy it because it's something to do. They need to be responding. They need to be the person who went to Home Depot and came back with equipment."

"Mm."

"I had a family in Reynoldsburg last year — March, pipe burst, five inches of standing water in the finished basement. They'd had it finished two years before. New carpet, new drywall, the whole thing. Twenty-two thousand dollars. I have the number because I looked at the original receipt. And they'd gone out and bought two of those dehumidifiers and a box fan and they had them all running in the basement and the husband was down there with a Shop-Vac and the wife was upstairs on the phone with her sister and the kids were at school and it was —" Doug stopped. He picked at the edge of the table's veneer where it had started to lift. "It was just a Tuesday. Just a regular day where everything they'd spent two years building was wrecked and they were trying to fix it with equipment that cost three hundred dollars total."

Simone sat back. She tapped her pen on the notebook twice — a habit Doug had catalogued along with the pen-in-hair thing and the coffee-on-the-water-ring thing. He was an adjuster. He noticed things. It was the only way he'd ever processed a room.

"What," Doug said.

"Nothing. That's material."

"It's a claim. I'm describing a claim."

"I know what you're describing." She crossed something out in her notebook and wrote something else. "What else do you know about that costs a hundred and forty dollars and doesn't work?"

Doug thought about this. "My reading glasses."

Simone's mouth moved but she didn't laugh. She wrote it down.


The material that worked came out sideways, always. Doug couldn't produce it on command — Simone had stopped asking him to be funny after the first week, when he'd sat through forty-five minutes of silence punctuated by two attempts at observational humor that she'd received with the expression of a physician hearing symptoms that didn't match the chart.

What worked was Doug talking about the things he actually knew. Insurance. The office. Roy. The specific mechanics of ordinary life as experienced by a man who'd been paying close attention to ordinary things for fifty-two years without realizing that attention was itself a quality.

On Wednesday, Simone asked about the cereal.

"What kind?"

"Raisin Bran. Store brand, not Kellogg's. Kellogg's is $4.89 and the store brand is $3.19 and they're the same cereal. Same raisins, same flakes, same sugar content. I've done the comparison."

"You've compared them."

"The Kellogg's has six more raisins per serving. I counted once."

Simone looked at him. "You counted the raisins."

"It was a Sunday."

There was a silence. Outside, the sound of a car door on the street, and then nothing.

"And this is dinner," Simone said.

"Most nights. It's fast, it doesn't generate dishes. One bowl, one spoon. You rinse them and you're done. A real dinner — a cooked dinner — is a plate and a pan and a cutting board and a spatula and by the time you've cleaned up, you've spent more time on the aftermath than on the meal. The ROI on cooking for one person is terrible."

"The return on investment of dinner."

"If you factored in your time at even minimum wage, every meal you cook alone costs about thirty dollars."

"You've done that math."

"Yes."

"Why is cereal better at night?" Simone asked. "That's the part I want. Not the economics. Why do you like it better as dinner than as breakfast?"

Doug looked at the water ring on the table. "Because in the morning it's what you're supposed to eat. You eat cereal for breakfast and it's just — it's breakfast. It doesn't mean anything. But at night, it's a decision. You're choosing not to cook. You're choosing not to order something. You're choosing the simplest possible version of a meal because the day is over and you don't need to perform anything for anyone, including yourself. Cereal at night is — it's honest. It's what dinner looks like when nobody's watching."

Simone didn't say anything for a long time. She was looking at her notebook but she wasn't writing.

"Yeah," she said. "That's something."

She asked about Roy's sandwich.

"Turkey on wheat. Mustard. No tomato. He's been ordering the same thing for nine years. One time they put tomato on it by accident and he peeled it off and set it on the edge of his tray and didn't mention it. He just removed it and ate the sandwich. The next time we went in, his order was correct. I don't know if he said something between visits or if the guy behind the counter noticed. It was never discussed."

"What does Roy think about all of this?"

"He didn't watch the video."

"I know. But what does he think about you doing this — the sessions, the Netflix thing, the whole trajectory?"

Doug considered this. "Roy thinks what he thinks. He doesn't — he's not going to have an opinion and then share the opinion with me so I feel something about his opinion. If I told Roy I was quitting to do comedy, he'd say 'Okay.' If I told him I was staying at Mutual Fidelity forever, he'd say 'Okay.' The okay would be the same both times."

"And that's comforting."

"I didn't say that."

"I'm saying it."

Doug looked at the water ring. "Yeah. It is."

This was the kind of moment that kept happening. Doug would say something about Roy, or about a claim, or about the unit price of store-brand raisins, and the room would shift slightly, like the air pressure had changed. Simone would be looking at him in a way that made him aware he'd given away more than he'd intended. Not more than he'd meant — he meant all of it. But more than he'd intended to put into the space between them.


The material that failed was simpler to diagnose but harder to stop producing.

On that same Wednesday afternoon, Simone asked Doug to tell her what it was like being famous.

"I'm not famous."

"You're on the internet. Eighty million views. People recognize you."

Doug tried. "It's — I mean, people come up to me. At the grocery store, or at the gas station. They want to take pictures. I don't mind it, it's fine, they're being nice. One guy at the Kroger in Clintonville asked me to do the toast for him, just right there in the produce section, and I said I didn't remember it, and he said 'Come on, man,' like I was holding out on him. I was buying bananas."

Simone was watching him the way she watched him when the real Doug was retreating and the narrating Doug was arriving. Doug could feel it happening — could feel the sentences becoming organized, could feel himself describing the experience of fame rather than being in the experience of fame, and the difference was total and deadly.

"That's —" Simone started.

"That's nothing. I know."

"It's not nothing. It's just not you. That's a story about being famous. It's not your story. Your story is the bananas."

"I don't have a story about bananas."

"You have a story about everything. You just told me you counted raisins on a Sunday. But when I ask you about fame, you give me 'people come up to me.' That's not Doug. That's what anyone would say."

The toast attempt was worse. Thursday of the third week, Simone asked him to talk about the toast directly.

"What do you want to know?"

"Anything. What you remember, how it felt, what you think about it now."

Doug took a breath. "It was at Kelsey's wedding. I'd had — well, the video. Brendan gave this toast that was —" He stopped. He could feel the shift happening, the thing Simone was always listening for: the moment when Doug stopped talking and started narrating. When his voice moved from the kitchen table to some middle distance where he was composing rather than speaking.

"He gave a toast that was really good," Doug said, and the air went out of it. Even he could hear it. The sentence sat there on the table between them like something left too long in the sun.

"Okay," Simone said. "And then."

"And then I got up and I —" Doug looked at his hands. "I don't really remember getting up. I remember the microphone was heavier than I expected. And I remember —"

"You're being boring," Simone said.

Doug stopped.

"You know you're being boring, right? You can feel it."

"I can feel it."

"What you just did — the part about the microphone, about Brendan's toast — that's you trying to tell the story of the toast. You're narrating. And when you narrate, you die."

Doug sat with this. Outside, a car passed with its windows down and something with bass coming from the speakers, the low pulse of it pressing against the kitchen window and then fading. April in Clintonville. The trees along the street had buds that looked swollen and irritated, like they hadn't decided whether spring was worth the effort.

"So what am I supposed to do with it?"

"Nothing. You're boring when you try to be interesting. That's the whole diagnosis."

"That's a terrible diagnosis."

"It's the most useful thing I've ever told anyone I've worked with." Simone uncapped her pen. "The insurance stuff, the cereal, the dehumidifier, Roy's sandwich — that's you. You're not trying to be anything. You're telling me what you know. And the gap between how seriously you take it and how strange it actually is — that's where the comedy lives. But the minute you try to talk about the toast, or about being recognized at the Kroger, or about what any of this means, you become a man with opinions about himself, and that man is —"

"Boring."

"Worse. Boring is generous. Self-commentary is where comedy goes to die. Everyone who tries to talk about their own fame becomes the same person. Doesn't matter who you are."

Doug felt something give — not in his chest, not in his throat, somewhere less anatomical than that. It was the feeling of a claim he'd been working for weeks that suddenly resolved. The documentation lined up. The coverage was clear. He was boring when he tried to be interesting. He was interesting when he wasn't trying. These were facts, as solid and specific as a depreciation schedule, and they were terrifying because the material that worked — the insurance stories, the cereal arithmetic, the Roy details — was his actual life. Performing that material in front of strangers meant letting them see the life. Not the viral-toast version, not the internet's Doug, but the real inventory: a fifty-two-year-old man who counted raisins on a Sunday because there was nothing else in the apartment that needed counting.

They broke for lunch. Simone went to the sandwich place around the corner — she'd been going there since her second week in Columbus and ordered a different sandwich every time, which Doug found inefficient. He ate a granola bar and drank water and stood in his kitchen looking at the Ikea bookshelf and the art print Megan had left behind, a watercolor of something that might have been a bridge or might have been a pier, the kind of art that committed to nothing, and it occurred to him that Megan had probably left it because she didn't want it, not because she was leaving him a piece of herself, and this thought arrived twelve years late and landed with the small, precise weight of something he'd always known.

Simone came back with a Cuban sandwich and a coffee. She set the coffee on the water ring.

"More insurance," she said.

Doug talked about depreciation. This was Thursday, or maybe still Wednesday — the sessions had started to bleed together, one afternoon into the next, the same table, the same recorder, the same quality of light shifting across the kitchen as the hours passed. He talked about what happens to the value of a couch over time — how a couch you bought for twelve hundred dollars is worth, after ten years, maybe two hundred, and the difference between what you paid and what it's worth now is called depreciation, and it's the most honest math in the world because it accounts for the fact that things wear out, that use diminishes value, that the couch you're sitting on is not the couch you bought.

"And that's how it works with everything," Doug said. "Not just couches. Appliances, roofing, carpeting, your water heater, your HVAC system. Everything has a schedule. Everything depreciates at a predictable rate. You can look it up. There are tables."

"And marriages?" Simone said. She said it casually, like it was the next item on a list, but her pen was still.

Doug looked at the recorder. "There's no table for that."

"But."

"But if there were." He paused. "If there were, you'd have your replacement cost and your actual cash value. Replacement cost is what it would take to get the same thing new. Actual cash value is what it's worth now, after everything. After the years and the arguments and the Tuesday nights and the way someone loads the dishwasher and the sound they make when they're sleeping and all of it. The actual cash value is what's left when you subtract the depreciation."

"And nobody puts that in the paperwork."

"Nobody puts that in the paperwork."

Simone wrote something. She didn't look up.


The second scene happened on a Friday. They'd been at it since nine and it was after four and the light in the apartment had changed — the flat white of morning had given way to the amber of late afternoon, angling through the kitchen window and laying a stripe across the table that had moved, over the hours, from the recorder to Simone's notebook to the empty space between their hands.

They were both tired. Simone had stopped taking notes an hour ago. The recorder was still running but neither of them was performing for it anymore — the thing about recording everything, Doug had learned, was that eventually you forgot the machine was there, and forgetting was the point. The machine was patient. The machine didn't need you to know it was listening.

Simone was asking about the apartment. Not the apartment as material — she'd already harvested the cereal, the bookshelf, the art print, the second bedroom with the boxes — but the apartment as a place a person lived.

"How long have you been here?"

"Twelve years. Since the divorce."

"And you moved here right after."

"Within a month. Megan — we sold the house. Split it. I found this place online. I didn't see it in person before I signed the lease. The pictures were accurate."

"That's a very insurance-adjuster way to pick an apartment."

"The pictures were accurate."

Simone smiled. Not the diagnostic smile she used when something was material. Just a smile. She was tired, and when she was tired her face relaxed out of its professional attention into something less deployed. She'd mentioned once, in a moment that wasn't part of any session, that she hadn't been sleeping well in Columbus because her body was still on New York time. It was only an hour's difference, but the hour mattered, she said. She'd been carrying that sleeplessness for three weeks and it showed in how she sat in the afternoons — less perched, more settled, as if fatigue had finally convinced her the chair was adequate.

"How did you know it was over?" Simone said. "Your marriage."

Doug looked at her. This wasn't a material question. The recorder was on but Simone wasn't glancing at her notebook. She was looking at him, and the quality of the looking was different from the diagnostic attention she'd been bringing to the insurance stories and the cereal and Roy's sandwich order for three weeks. This was a person asking another person a question because she wanted to know.

"The conversation, or —"

"Whatever it was. The moment that made it real."

Doug didn't answer right away. He could feel the pull toward narrating — toward telling the divorce story in the organized way Megan would tell it, with contextualized feelings and measured transitions. He could feel Simone watching him feel it. She was very still. She had a habit, when she sensed something real approaching, of going so quiet that the room seemed to pull inward around her silence.

"We'd been — Megan had been saying things for a while. About how she felt. About the marriage. She said it the way Megan says things, which is organized. She'd say, 'I think we need to acknowledge that this isn't working,' and I'd say, 'What isn't working?' and she'd look at me and I genuinely didn't know what she meant. Or — I knew. I could also truthfully say I didn't know, because knowing and understanding are different coverage levels and I was insured for one but not the other."

Simone didn't react to the metaphor. She waited.

"So she left. One day I came home from work and she'd taken things. Not everything. Her clothes, her toiletries, some books. She took the KitchenAid mixer — four hundred and twenty dollars, I never used it — and she left the art print." He gestured at the wall without looking at it. "We had different stuff by then. Separate accounts. She'd already been moving into the new place incrementally, and the day she took the last of it was just — it was a Wednesday. I came home and I could see what was gone. I walked through the rooms and I could inventory it."

"But."

"But I was fine. I was processing. I knew it was coming. I'd known for months. The paperwork was in progress. I was managing it."

The word sat between them. Managing.

"And then I took a shower. End of the day, I always shower when I get home, I still do. And I got in and her shampoo was gone from the ledge."

Simone did not move.

"I don't remember the brand. Something with green tea in the name. It came in a tall green bottle and she'd been using the same shampoo the entire marriage. Eighteen years. The bottle was always on the ledge. Every morning, every night. Even after she started talking about leaving, even after the conversations and the lawyers and the listing agreement — the bottle was there. It was the last thing of hers in the bathroom."

The apartment was quiet. Outside, late-afternoon sounds — a dog somewhere, the specific pitch of a lawn mower starting up and catching, the seasonal noise of Columbus deciding it was spring. The stripe of light on the table had reached Doug's hand.

"Everything else I could process. The mixer, the books, the clothes — those were items. I could inventory them, I could assess the depreciation, I could arrive at a number and the number would be accurate and I'd be fine. But the shampoo —" He looked at his hand in the light. "You can't depreciate shampoo. It's a consumable. It has no residual value. It's just gone, and the ledge where it sat is empty, and every time you take a shower you see the empty space on the ledge where it was. And after a while you don't see it anymore, because you adjust, because that's what you do, but for the first — I don't know. Six months. A year. Every shower."

He stopped. The recorder hummed between them, so quiet it was nearly the sound of the room itself.

Simone picked up her pen. She did not put it in her hair. She opened her notebook to a clean page and she wrote something. She wrote for a while — longer than she'd written for any single thing Doug had said in three weeks of sessions. She wrote in small careful letters that Doug couldn't read from across the table.

She didn't laugh. She didn't say "That's material" or "Keep going" or "That's something" or any of the phrases she'd been using for three weeks to sort his life into piles.

She wrote it down.

Doug watched her write and felt the thing he'd been circling since the first session: the understanding that there was no wall between the material and the man. The insurance stories were his loneliness. The cereal was his loneliness. Roy's unchanging sandwich was his loneliness. And now the shampoo was his loneliness too, written down in Simone's notebook in her small letters, and everything in the notebook was material, and material was what went on stage, and what went on stage was what strangers would see, and the thing he'd spent twelve years sealing behind the measured, documented, depreciated details of his ordinary life was going to be visible to anyone who bought a ticket or pressed play.

He was going to be known.

The pen stopped. Simone looked up. Her eyes were red at the edges — fatigue, or something adjacent to fatigue, the way tired sometimes has other things packed inside it. She closed the notebook. She placed the pen on top of it. She did not put it back in her hair.

"We should stop for today," she said.

"Okay."

Neither of them moved for a while. The light had shifted past Doug and was climbing the wall behind him where the art print hung — the watercolor bridge or pier, the thing Megan hadn't wanted.

"I've never told anyone that," Doug said. "The shampoo."

Simone nodded. She reached forward and clicked the recorder off. The sound was small and precise.

"I know," she said.

They sat in the kitchen as the light went. Simone finished her cold coffee. Doug did not offer to make more because more coffee at five in the afternoon was not something he would think to offer, and Simone knew this about him by now, had absorbed enough of Doug's rhythms over three weeks of shared table to know that his hospitality had limits that were not about generosity but about the fact that certain things did not occur to him.

Outside, the lawn mower had stopped. The neighborhood had settled into that particular quiet of a spring Friday evening — not silence, but the soft negotiations of people arriving home, car doors and mailboxes and the specific distant television sounds of houses where other people lived with other people.

Simone gathered her notebook and her recorder and put them in the canvas bag she carried. The bag had a coffee stain on the bottom and a pin on the strap that said "WRITE DRUNK EDIT SOBER" — a Hemingway misquote she'd told Doug she hated but hadn't removed because a friend gave it to her and the friend died three years ago and the pin was the only thing of his she'd kept. She'd mentioned this once, in the second week, casually, the way she released personal details — in small pieces, unprompted, and then she moved on before the weight could settle.

"Same time Monday," she said.

"Yeah."

She stopped at the door. She didn't turn all the way around — half-turned, hand on the knob, bag over one shoulder.

"Doug."

"Yeah."

"The shampoo. I'm not going to shape it into a bit or slot it into a set. That's yours. But I want you to know that it's the most precise thing you've said, and precision is everything you've got. That's your whole mechanism — you notice the exact thing, not the general thing, and when you say it people recognize it as true, and recognition is what makes it work. That's the engine."

Doug stood by the kitchen table. He didn't say anything, because there was nothing to say that wouldn't diminish what she'd said. He'd learned this from years of standing in people's damaged living rooms — sometimes the only correct response to someone telling you something important was to let the importance stay in the air between you until it either dissolved or became permanent.

"See you Monday," he said.

The door closed. Doug listened to her footsteps on the stairs, the outside door, then nothing. The apartment was quiet the way it was always quiet — had been for twelve years — but the quality had changed. The recorder was gone. The notebook was gone. Simone's coffee cup was in the sink, which he'd wash later. Her pen cap — the cap, not the pen — sat on the table where it had rolled when she was packing up. He picked it up and put it on the counter and left it there.

He went to the bathroom. He didn't turn on the shower. He looked at the ledge. His own shampoo sat there — a drugstore brand, nothing with green tea in the name, $3.79 per bottle, roughly two and a half months per bottle at his rate of use. He'd calculated this once. The math was the only way he'd ever found to hold the things he couldn't hold any other way, to make them small enough and specific enough to fit inside the precise, measured architecture of his understanding, where everything had a value and every value had a schedule and the schedule told you what things were worth and you could live inside those numbers and the numbers would not leave.

He went back to the kitchen. He took out the box of store-brand Raisin Bran and poured a bowl and stood at the counter and ate it. The milk was two days from expiration but still good — he checked, he always checked — and the raisins were adequate and the bowl was exactly what dinner looked like on a Friday night when you'd just told someone the truest thing about your life and she'd written it down and not laughed, and you were going to have to decide what that meant, but not tonight. Tonight was cereal and a clean bowl and a rinsed spoon and the pen cap on the counter that you weren't going to move.

He washed the bowl. He washed the spoon. He washed Simone's coffee cup and set it in the drying rack next to his own.

He left the pen cap where it was.
