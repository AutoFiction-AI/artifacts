# Chapter 3

Doug got to the office at ten to eight. He was always early, not from ambition but from the same instinct that made him check his mirrors twice before changing lanes — a sense that arriving on time meant arriving late, and arriving late meant something had gone wrong that he should have prevented. The parking lot was a third full. Roy's car, a 2011 Camry with a dent in the rear quarter panel that Roy had decided years ago was not worth fixing, was already in its usual spot near the loading dock, where nobody else parked because it was farthest from the entrance and because nobody else wanted to walk, which was exactly why Roy parked there.

Mutual Fidelity occupied the second floor of a building on Sawyer Road that also housed a tax preparation service and an orthodontist whose patients could sometimes be heard through the floor, a low hum of suction and distress that Doug had long ago stopped noticing. The elevator was slow enough that Doug took the stairs, which he did not consider exercise. The office was open-plan, thirty desks arranged in rows of five, separated by low partitions that provided the illusion of privacy without any of its function. The carpet was the color of weak tea. The ceiling tiles were acoustic and stained in two places from the building's intermittent plumbing issues, which Doug understood better than the building management did — he'd assessed the office's own water damage claim two years ago, and the repair had not addressed the root cause, which was a compression fitting in the third-floor bathroom that would fail again within eighteen months. He'd noted this in his report. No one had followed up.

His desk was fourth in the second row, next to the window. Roy's desk was across the partition. Both desks had the same equipment — a monitor, a keyboard, a phone, a wire inbox that held actual paper because claims documentation still arrived by mail more often than people assumed. Doug's desk had a coffee mug from the 2019 office Secret Santa that said WORLD'S MOST ADEQUATE on it, a gift from Roy that was either a joke or a description, and Doug had never asked which because he suspected the answer was both.

He hung his coat on the back of his chair and turned on his monitor. The inbox had eleven new emails, eight of them internal. Two were from claimants. One was from his supervisor Janet, forwarded without comment, subject line: "FW: Social Media Use and Company Representation — Revised Guidelines." Doug opened it. The email was from HR and used the phrase "professional conduct in digital spaces" three times in four paragraphs. It did not mention Doug by name. It didn't need to. Mutual Fidelity had existed for forty-one years and none of its employees had ever gone viral for anything, a record that Doug had broken by accident on a Saturday night with eight whiskeys in him and a microphone someone should have taken away.

He closed the email and opened the first claim file.

The morning had its own architecture. Doug liked mornings at the office — the specific quiet of thirty people doing the same kind of work, the heating system pushing warm air through the ceiling vents with a sound like slow breathing, the occasional phone conversation conducted in the moderate tone everyone used, as if the entire floor had agreed on a volume and stuck to it. He'd been here fifteen years. The work suited him in a way he'd never had to justify because no one had ever asked. You processed claims. You read the documentation, assessed the damage, compared it to the policy, determined what was covered and what wasn't, and issued a finding. The work was precise, specific, and consequential to the people whose houses had flooded or burned or collapsed, and it required a kind of attention that Doug gave without effort — the attention of someone who noticed what was actually there rather than what should be there.

His specialty since 2014 was residential water damage. He'd ended up in it the way people end up in most specialties — a staffing need, a reassignment, a gradual accumulation of expertise that became identity. He knew what happened when a pipe burst in a wall at 2 a.m. in January — the sound first, which homeowners always described as a pop or a crack but was really more of a give, something structural surrendering. He knew the smell of a house three days after a sump pump failure — the sweet, vegetal rot that got into the drywall and stayed there, behind the paint, in the fiberglass insulation, months after the standing water was gone. He knew the look on people's faces when they stood in their own ruined living rooms holding the documentation they'd been told to gather, which was always either too much or not enough and never exactly what the form asked for.

The first file on his desk was a garage roof in Reynoldsburg. A family named Pardee had woken up on a Thursday to find their garage ceiling bowed inward, the weight of ice accumulation having finally exceeded the structural capacity of a roof that was, according to the building permit, twenty-three years old. The claim was straightforward — ice damage, covered under the homeowner's policy, minus the deductible. Doug reviewed the adjuster's field photos on his monitor: the sagging drywall, the water stain spreading across it like a bruise, the ice dam visible on the exterior shot where the gutter had separated from the fascia board. He compared the roof age to the depreciation schedule and prepared his assessment. The Pardees were going to get less than they expected, because the roof's actual cash value was not what it would cost to replace it — it was what a twenty-three-year-old roof was worth with twenty-three years of weather on it. This was the part of the job that required a specific kind of honesty: telling people that the thing they'd lost was worth less than they thought, by the numbers, even though the numbers had nothing to do with what it felt like to wake up to a ceiling caving in over your car.

He finished the Pardee file at nine-fifteen and opened the next one. A grease fire in a duplex on East Main. The tenant had left a pan of oil on the stove and gone to check on a child in the other room, which was a sentence Doug had written in various forms hundreds of times — the particular American catastrophe of unattended cooking oil, which caused more residential fires than any other single source, and which almost always started with someone leaving the kitchen to do something that seemed like it would take ten seconds. The fire itself was contained to the kitchen, but the smoke had traveled through the shared wall into the other unit, and the question of how far smoke damage extended was always a negotiation between what the nose could smell and what the policy defined as "damaged." The neighbor in unit B claimed she could still smell it in her bedroom closet. The insurer's position was that odor without visible residue did not constitute damage. Doug was going to have to go out there with a thermal imaging camera and settle it. He made a note.

He was halfway through the grease fire documentation when Kevin Beech from IT stopped at his desk. Kevin was twenty-six, tall, with the kind of beard that was either intentional or a decision he'd forgotten to revisit. He carried a laptop under one arm and held his phone in the other hand in a way that suggested he was about to use it. He'd been wearing the same pair of New Balance sneakers for as long as Doug had known him, gray with a orange stripe, and they were holding up in a way that seemed to bother Kevin, like he was waiting for permission to replace them.

"Hey, Doug." Kevin shifted the laptop to his other arm. "So I know this is kind of random, but — could I get a picture? My girlfriend's been sharing your video all week and she's watched it like fifteen times."

Doug looked at Kevin. Kevin had fixed his email three months ago when the Outlook sync failed, and they had discussed nothing personal on that occasion or any other.

"A picture," Doug said.

"Yeah, like a selfie? If that's cool. She'd just — yeah."

It was not cool, in a way Doug couldn't have explained. It was a small thing, a phone held up, a moment. But something about it felt like being reclassified — moved from one category to another without filling out the paperwork. He'd been a coworker Kevin nodded at in the hallway. Now he was something Kevin's girlfriend wanted proof of.

"Sure," Doug said, and Kevin leaned in and held up his phone and Doug made a face that he could feel, from the inside, was the face of a man being identified in a lineup.

"Awesome. Thanks, man." Kevin looked at the photo. "She's gonna lose it. You're, like, her favorite person on the internet right now."

Doug wanted to say that being someone's favorite person on the internet was a category he had not known existed until a week ago and would have preferred to go on not knowing about. He said, "Tell her I said thanks."

Kevin left. Roy, on the other side of the partition, had not looked up from his monitor.

"That'll happen," Roy said.

"What will."

"The picture thing. Once people know you're a person who gets pictures taken, they want to take pictures." Roy typed something, finished it, and leaned back. "My nephew is on YouTube. He reviews flashlights. People send him flashlights. He has over three hundred flashlights now."

"He reviews flashlights."

"He reviews flashlights. He's very thorough about it. Tests the lumens, the battery life, the water resistance. I don't understand it but he seems to enjoy himself. He's got a whole room that's just flashlights."

Doug couldn't tell if this was offered as comfort, comparison, or simply information. With Roy, it was usually the third.

He turned back to his monitor and opened the next file.

---

The pool claim had been sitting in his queue for two days, and Doug had been saving it the way some people save the good part of a meal — not consciously, just pushing it down the stack. The file was labeled BURRIS/COMMONWEALTH — two parties, two policies, a jurisdictional tangle that the intake notes described as "complex coverage determination required."

Carl Burris, of Westerville, owned a twenty-four-foot above-ground pool. Round, steel-walled, installed in 2019 by a company that had since gone out of business, which was not relevant to the claim but was noted in the file and struck Doug as the kind of detail that attached itself to bad luck. On the night of February 8th, the wall of the pool had split along a manufacturing seam. The manufacturer's warranty had expired nineteen months prior. Approximately six thousand gallons of water — Doug did the math: a pool that size held roughly eight thousand gallons when full, and February in central Ohio meant it was at three-quarter capacity from snowmelt and winter runoff — had poured through the gap in the wall, crossed eight feet of frozen yard, gone under the chain-link fence that separated Burris's property from his neighbor's, and flooded the rear garden of one Margaret Commonwealth.

Margaret Commonwealth grew roses. Specifically, she grew hybrid tea roses, which Doug learned from the documentation were a particular category of rose bred for large blooms on long stems, the kind you saw in florist shops and at funerals. She had seventeen of them. She had receipts for all seventeen, dating back over six years of purchases from a nursery in Delaware, Ohio, that specialized in rose stock. The total documented value of the seventeen hybrid teas was $4,218.47, which included the plants themselves, the soil amendments, a drip irrigation system she'd installed herself, and a cedar trellis structure that the floodwater had knocked sideways and cracked at the base.

The roses were dead. Six thousand gallons of chlorinated pool water in February had killed them comprehensively — the chlorine burned the root systems and the cold prevented any chance of recovery. Margaret Commonwealth had photographs. She had taken them, Doug noted, in the specific way of someone who understood that documentation mattered: each plant labeled, each receipt matched to a purchase date, the trellis photographed from three angles. She had done this better than most professional claimants he worked with. Her file was immaculate. The roses were still dead.

She had filed the claim. And here was where it got interesting.

Carl Burris's homeowner's policy covered the structural failure of the pool — the replacement cost of the wall, the labor to repair it. But his liability coverage, Section II of the standard homeowner's form, covered bodily injury and property damage to third parties only under specific conditions, and the definition of "occurrence" in Burris's policy required that the damage result from either an "accident" or "negligence." The pool wall splitting was arguably an accident. But the insurer could also argue negligence — that Burris should have inspected the wall before winter, should have maintained the seam, should have known that a steel wall installed by a company that no longer existed might not last forever. The adjuster's field notes indicated that Burris had not inspected the wall. Burris had not, as far as Doug could determine from the documentation, thought about the wall at all between the day the pool was installed and the night it failed. Doug understood this completely. Most people did not think about the structural integrity of the things that held their lives in place until those things gave way.

Margaret Commonwealth's policy was a different problem entirely. Her homeowner's coverage included a rider for her garden — she'd had the foresight to schedule the rose collection individually, which meant the plants were listed and valued on the policy. This was unusual and, Doug thought, genuinely impressive. But the coverage was for named perils: wind, hail, fire, theft, vandalism, and a handful of other events that the actuarial tables considered worth insuring against. Not flood. Not "structural failure of adjacent property." Not "your neighbor's above-ground pool catastrophically discharging six thousand gallons of chlorinated water onto your prize hybrid teas in the middle of February," which was not a named peril in any homeowner's policy Doug had ever read, because the actuarial tables had not, apparently, accounted for this possibility.

Doug read the file twice. He pulled up the policy language for both Burris and Commonwealth on side-by-side windows. He cross-referenced the liability endorsement on Burris's policy with the Ohio Revised Code standards for negligence in property maintenance. He looked up the replacement cost of hybrid tea roses from the Delaware nursery's website, which listed prices by cultivar — the cultivar names alone were worth the search: Mister Lincoln, Double Delight, Peace, Chrysler Imperial. Margaret Commonwealth had a Pink Peace that the nursery listed at ninety-two dollars.

He was, he realized, enjoying himself. Not the loss itself — the loss was real, the roses were dead, Margaret Commonwealth's six years of cultivation were gone and no policy was going to bring back a mature Pink Peace in bloom. But the coverage question was genuine. Two policies, neither designed for this scenario, both containing language that could be read in multiple directions, and the resolution would determine who paid for seventeen dead roses and a cracked cedar trellis. It was the kind of problem that had a right answer buried somewhere inside it, and finding that answer required exactly the kind of attention Doug was built to give.

---

The sandwich place was called Sal's, or had been — Sal had sold it four years ago to a couple named Park who had kept the name because changing the sign would have cost more than the goodwill it might generate. It was a block and a half from the office, a narrow storefront with a counter, a deli case, and six tables with chairs that didn't quite match. Doug and Roy had been eating here every workday for nine years. Roy ordered the same thing every time: turkey on wheat, mustard, no tomato. Doug varied his order within a narrow range — ham and swiss, or roast beef, or occasionally the Italian, which was good but more bread than he wanted by the end.

Today Doug got the roast beef. Roy got the turkey. They sat at the table by the window, which was their table in the way that it was simply the table they always sat at, not because they'd claimed it or would have been bothered if someone else were sitting there. No one ever was. The table wobbled slightly and had wobbled for the entire nine years, and neither Doug nor Roy had ever mentioned it or attempted to fix it, which Doug supposed was a form of acceptance.

"So the pool wall splits," Doug said. He was holding his sandwich but hadn't bitten into it yet. "Six thousand gallons. February. It goes under the fence and into the neighbor's garden."

Roy ate. He ate with the efficiency of a man who considered lunch a task to be completed rather than an experience to be prolonged. He chewed, swallowed, and listened with the same expression he wore for everything, which was no particular expression at all.

"The neighbor has roses," Doug said. "Seventeen hybrid teas. She's got receipts for all of them going back six years. She scheduled them on her policy individually. And they're all dead."

"Chlorine," Roy said.

"Chlorine and cold. Either one probably would've done it. Both together, there's no question."

"So she files."

"She files. But here's the thing — whose policy covers it? Burris, the pool owner, he's got liability coverage for third-party damage, but only if it qualifies as an occurrence under the definition, which means either accident or negligence. Did the wall splitting count as negligence? He never inspected it. Nobody inspects their pool wall. But the insurer could argue he should've."

Roy took another bite. He was already halfway through the sandwich. "What about her policy."

"Named perils only. She's got the garden rider, which is smart — most people don't schedule their plants — but the perils are wind, hail, fire, theft. Not flood. And not 'my neighbor's pool blew out.'"

"Nobody's policy covers that."

"Nobody's policy covers that. That's the gap. Both policies get close but neither one closes the distance. And in the middle you've got seventeen dead roses and a woman who did everything right — she documented, she insured, she scheduled. And it still might not be enough."

Roy considered this. He folded the paper his sandwich had come in into a neat square and set it on the tray. He'd done this every day for nine years. The square was always the same size.

"How much," he said.

"The roses? Four thousand two hundred eighteen dollars and forty-seven cents. That's plants, soil, irrigation, and a cedar trellis."

"That's a lot of roses."

"Hybrid teas. Some of those cultivars run sixty, seventy dollars a plant. She had a Pink Peace listed at ninety-two."

Roy looked out the window. The street was gray and wet, the kind of late-winter Columbus day where the sky and the pavement were the same color and the temperature sat right at thirty-four degrees, which was too warm for snow and too cold for anything to feel like it was going anywhere. A woman walked past with a stroller that had a plastic rain cover over it, the kind that made the stroller look like a small greenhouse.

"You'll figure it out," Roy said.

"I don't know that I will. There might not be a clean answer."

"There's always an answer. Sometimes the answer's just that nobody pays."

Doug bit into his sandwich. The roast beef was fine. It had been fine for nine years. He had not ever needed it to be more than fine, which was either a statement about the sandwich or about Doug, and he chose not to determine which.

They ate. The lunch crowd at Sal's was small and consistent — two women from the physical therapy clinic downstairs who came in most days, a man in a reflective vest who ordered three subs to go and left without sitting down. The Parks' daughter, who was maybe ten, sat behind the counter doing homework with headphones on, her pencil moving in the unhurried rhythm of a kid who'd been doing homework in this exact spot for years. She had a juice box balanced on a stack of napkins.

Roy finished and set his hands flat on the table, which was how Roy indicated he was done eating but not ready to leave. He sat like that, looking out the window.

Doug watched him. Roy's life fit him the way certain clothes fit certain people — not tailored, not stylish, but the right size and worn in. He owned a house in Gahanna that was, by Roy's own description, "a house." Three bedrooms, one of which he used, one of which was empty, one of which held a treadmill he'd bought in 2017 and used regularly until he decided that walking outside achieved the same result without the monthly cost of electricity, so the treadmill sat there now, unplugged and draped with a bath towel he'd never retrieved. He drove the dented Camry. He watched no streaming services and did not own a subscription to anything except the newspaper, which arrived in physical form and which he read at the kitchen table each morning. He read one book a month from the Columbus Metropolitan Library, choosing the next one by walking to the new arrivals shelf and picking up whatever was at eye level — a system he'd described once, without irony, and which Doug had found both unreasonable and difficult to argue with. He went to bed at nine forty-five every night.

Roy was not performing anything. He was not making a statement about simplicity or consumption or the way other people lived. He was a man who had arrived at a set of preferences through experience and saw no reason to revise them. He was content — not happy, which implied a condition that could be lost, but content, which implied one that had been decided on. Doug found this comforting and also, in a way he couldn't quite name, vaguely threatening, like sitting next to someone who'd already finished an exam you were still working on.

"You hear from the phone people?" Roy said. He meant the calls Doug had been getting — the interview requests, the media inquiries, the 310 area code that kept ringing. Doug had mentioned them at the end of yesterday, briefly, in the tone of someone reporting a weather event that had happened nearby.

"A few more," Doug said. "I haven't been answering."

"Probably wise."

"I don't know what I'd say."

"You'd say whatever you'd say. Then they'd put it somewhere and other people would have opinions about it. That's how it works."

"That's how it works," Doug repeated. Not as agreement. Because hearing Roy say it made it sound both obvious and slightly terrifying.

Roy looked at Doug's plate. "You going to eat that pickle?"

Doug looked at his plate. There was a pickle spear he hadn't touched. He didn't like pickles, which Roy knew, and Roy liked pickles, which Doug knew, and the transfer of the pickle had been happening for so long that asking about it was itself the point — not necessary, but repeated, and the repetition had made it into something.

"All yours," Doug said.

Roy ate the pickle. They walked back to the office. The air was cold and smelled like car exhaust and the particular mineral flatness that Columbus air carried in February — no flowers, no cut grass, nothing decomposing or growing, just the neutral smell of a city in winter waiting for a reason to change. Doug's phone was in his coat pocket. It had not buzzed during lunch, which was a relief and also, in some small way he didn't want to look at directly, a disappointment.

---

He spent the afternoon on the Commonwealth file. He called the Burris insurer's claims department and spent fourteen minutes on hold listening to a piano arrangement of a song he almost recognized before a representative named Theresa confirmed that the liability endorsement on Burris's policy did include a negligence provision but that the company's position was that pool wall failure due to manufacturing defect did not constitute owner negligence. Doug disagreed but said he'd review the language again. Theresa sounded tired. She probably had forty open files on her desk. Doug thanked her and hung up.

He called Margaret Commonwealth's insurer next. The representative there, whose name Doug didn't catch because the man spoke as though the phone were a formality he'd rather not be participating in, confirmed that the garden rider covered named perils only and that water damage from a neighbor's structural failure was not among them. Doug asked about the policy's "additional coverage" section, which sometimes included a catch-all provision for unusual events. The representative said he'd check. He did not sound like a man who intended to check.

At four-thirty Janet stopped by his desk. Janet was fifty-three, had managed the residential team for eleven years, and wore reading glasses on a beaded chain that she adjusted so often the motion had become unconscious — she'd reach up and touch the chain even when the glasses were already in place, the way some people touch a ring they've been wearing for years. She had a pen behind one ear that Doug was fairly sure she'd forgotten was there.

"Did you get a chance to look at the email I forwarded?" she said.

"I did."

"HR wants to make sure everyone's aware of the updated guidelines." She said this with the expression of a person delivering a message she had not written and did not entirely endorse. "It's not about you specifically."

"I know."

"It's a general policy refresh." She paused. The pen shifted behind her ear as she tilted her head. "How's the Commonwealth file looking?"

"Complicated. Two policies, coverage gap between them. Neither one was written for what actually happened."

Janet nodded. This was the language she understood. "Keep me posted. If it goes to subrogation, I'll need the full timeline."

She walked away. Doug appreciated Janet for the same reason he appreciated the office itself: she operated within a system that had consistent rules, and those rules held even when the outcomes were unsatisfying. She had not asked about the video. She had not asked how he was doing or whether he was okay or what he planned to do about any of it. She had forwarded the HR email without comment and then asked about a file, and both actions were exactly what they should have been.

At five-fifteen Doug shut down his monitor. Roy was already putting on his coat — Roy left at five-fifteen every day, not from rigidity but because he saw no benefit in staying past the time he was paid to stay. They walked to the parking lot together without talking, which was not silence so much as the absence of anything that needed to be said, and the difference between those two things was one of the reasons the friendship worked.

Doug got into his Civic. The seat was cold through his pants. He started the engine and sat for a moment while the heat worked its way through the vents, watching the lot empty in the ordinary rhythm of a workday ending — cars pulling out one by one, headlights in the gray dusk, everyone going somewhere that was not here.

His phone buzzed in his coat pocket. A text from a number he didn't recognize: *Hi Doug, this is Layla Banks — we spoke briefly last week. I'd really love to set up a time to chat about some opportunities. I think there's something real here.*

Doug read it. He didn't respond. He put the phone on the passenger seat, put the car in reverse, and drove home. The route was twelve minutes. He'd timed it once, four or five years ago, and it had been twelve minutes every day since, give or take whatever the light at Indianola and North Broadway decided to do, which was either a coincidence or the kind of consistency that a city offered when you weren't asking it for anything unusual.

The apartment was dark when he walked in. He turned on the kitchen light. The cereal box was on the counter where he'd left it that morning, the top still open, and he poured a bowl and ate standing up. The Cheerios tasted the way they always tasted. The milk was getting close to its sell-by date but was still fine. He ate and thought about the roses — all seventeen of them, and the woman who had documentation for every one, and the gap between what the policies said and what had actually happened, and how sometimes the most interesting problem was the one that didn't have a clean answer, just two incomplete answers sitting next to each other.

He rinsed the bowl and set it in the sink next to the bowl from yesterday. The apartment was quiet in the way it was always quiet — not peaceful, just empty, the specific silence of a space that had room for more than one person and contained only one. The second bedroom door was closed, the way it was always closed. Behind it were the boxes he hadn't unpacked when he'd moved in twelve years ago, and behind the boxes was a room that was listed on his lease as an office but contained no desk and no computer and served no function except to store the evidence that he had once lived somewhere else.

He didn't turn on the television. He sat on the couch and opened the Burris file he'd brought home and read the policy language again from the beginning, Section I through the endorsements, and the sentences were precise and the logic was clear and for an hour, sitting in his apartment with the cereal bowl in the sink and the phone on the counter and the day behind him like a door he'd walked through and closed, the work was the steadiest thing he had. It was not everything. But it was enough.
