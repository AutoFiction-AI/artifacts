# Chapter 12

Alex Chen arrived at Doug's apartment on a Tuesday morning in May carrying a canvas messenger bag with a fraying strap, a digital recorder, and a thesis. The thesis was that Doug Bridge represented something about contemporary masculinity — its failures, its accidental honesty, its unwillingness to perform. Alex had explained this over email in language that Doug read twice and understood about forty percent of. He'd agreed to the profile because Layla told him to and because saying no would have required a reason he didn't have.

Alex was thirty-one and had the specific intensity of someone who had chosen journalism when journalism was already dying and had committed to it anyway. He wore a denim jacket that was either vintage or just old — Doug couldn't tell — and he had a pen clipped to the breast pocket that he kept removing, chewing the cap, and replacing. The cap was destroyed, soft plastic cratered by months of thought. He also had allergies. He'd blown his nose three times before Doug got the coffee made, and he kept rubbing his eyes with the heel of his hand in a way that made him look like he'd been crying, which he hadn't. Columbus in May was a pollen event. Doug had processed a claim once involving mold remediation triggered by spring pollen accumulating in an HVAC system, which was a fringe case but not unprecedented.

"So," Alex said. He pressed record on the digital recorder and set it on the kitchen counter between them, next to a box of Grape-Nuts that Doug had not put away. "I want to start with something maybe a little abstract."

"Okay," Doug said.

"What drives you?"

Doug looked at the recorder. The red light was on. "I drive a Civic," he said. "2019. Bought it used with thirty-two thousand miles."

Alex smiled. He had a good smile — the smile of someone who believed his subjects were going to reveal themselves if he waited long enough. "No, I mean — what motivates you? What gets you up in the morning?"

Doug thought about this. His alarm was set for 6:40. He got up because it went off. Before the alarm, he'd been getting up because his bladder woke him around 6:15, but he'd adjusted his water intake after 8 p.m. and that had mostly resolved. He didn't think this was what Alex meant.

"I have a job," Doug said. "The job starts at eight. So I get up in time for the job."

Alex wrote something in his notebook. The notebook was a Moleskine, the kind you buy at bookstores, and its pages were dense with small handwriting that tilted left. Doug noticed the tilt because he'd processed a claim once involving handwriting analysis — a forged receipt, a guy in Reynoldsburg, nine hundred dollars on a pressure washer that didn't exist. The analyst had said left-tilting handwriting indicated introversion. Doug didn't know if that was true for Alex or for the Reynoldsburg guy.

"Tell me about the toast," Alex said.

"Which part?"

"How it felt. That night. Standing up there."

"I was drunk," Doug said.

"Right, but — "

"I'd had eight whiskeys. I don't normally have eight whiskeys. I don't normally have four. The bartender was pouring them tall because the venue was all-inclusive and he'd stopped measuring."

"But what made you stand up? What made you give the toast?"

"Brendan had just given one," Doug said. "My ex-wife's husband. His was very good. People cried."

"And that motivated you to — "

"I don't know if 'motivated' is the right word. I was already standing. Or I was sitting and then I was standing. I don't remember the transition."

Alex wrote something else. Doug could see, upside down, the word "transition" with a box around it. He wondered what Alex thought that word was going to mean in the profile. He had a feeling it was going to mean something different than what Doug meant, which was that he physically did not remember going from chair to upright.

They talked for another hour. Alex asked about Doug's childhood, his marriage, his divorce, his relationship with Kelsey, his job, his apartment, his daily routine, his feelings about fame, his feelings about comedy, his feelings about his feelings. Doug answered each question the way he answered coverage inquiries at work — with the specific information requested and nothing additional. Alex kept waiting for the additional part. Doug kept not providing it.

At one point Alex asked, "Do you think of yourself as funny?"

"No," Doug said.

"But eighty million people watched that video and laughed."

"Some of them were probably just watching. You don't know everybody laughed."

"The comments — "

"I've read the comments. Some of them think I'm an asshole. One guy said I should be arrested for public intoxication. He got twelve hundred upvotes."

Alex paused. He rubbed his eye again. His allergies were getting worse — the morning light through Doug's kitchen window had a visible quality to it, a fine yellow-green dusting on the sill that Doug wiped down every other day and which returned every other day, because that was what May in central Ohio did. Alex had stopped wiping his nose and had moved to a strategy of sustained sniffling that he seemed to think was less noticeable. It was not less noticeable.

"Can I use your bathroom?" Alex asked.

"Down the hall," Doug said. "Door on the left. The door on the right is the office."

"You have a home office?"

"It doesn't have a computer in it," Doug said. "It's got boxes."

Alex hesitated, clearly wanting to ask about the boxes, and then went to the bathroom. Doug heard him blow his nose four times, a sequence that built in intensity like a man trying to clear a drain. Doug sat at the kitchen counter and looked at the recorder. The red light was still on. It was recording the silence, the sound of the refrigerator humming, the muffled honking from the bathroom. Somewhere in a digital file, this would exist: Alex Chen's sinuses and Doug Bridge's Frigidaire, captured for journalism.

---

The plan was that Alex would shadow Doug for two days. The first day was the Tuesday, which was a work day, which meant Alex would see the office, and lunch with Roy, and a session with Simone in the late afternoon. Layla had choreographed this. She'd called it "giving him the full Doug experience," and Doug had not asked what she thought the full Doug experience was because he suspected the answer would involve the word "authentic" and he'd heard that word enough times from enough people who didn't mean the same thing by it.

They took Doug's Civic to Mutual Fidelity. Alex sat in the passenger seat and looked at the interior the way Doug had seen adjusters look at damaged property — assessing, cataloging, forming a judgment that would eventually become a number, except Alex's number was word count.

"How long have you had this car?"

"Three years. Well, three years and five months. I bought it in December."

"Do you like it?"

"It runs," Doug said. "The heat works. The AC works when it wants to. The passenger window sticks in cold weather but we're past that now."

Alex wrote something on his phone. Doug drove.

The Mutual Fidelity parking lot looked the way it always looked — gray, flat, half-full at 7:52 a.m. The building was a three-story rectangle that communicated nothing about what happened inside it, which was the processing of other people's disasters into financial outcomes. Doug had worked there for fifteen years and had never once thought the building was interesting, and it bothered him slightly that Alex was photographing it with his phone as if it signified something.

Inside, Doug introduced Alex to Janet, who shook Alex's hand and said, "We're very proud of Doug," which was the first time in fifteen years that Janet had expressed pride in anything other than the department's quarterly throughput numbers. Doug absorbed this the way he absorbed most unexpected information — as data that didn't fit the existing file, which meant he needed a new file, which meant more work.

Alex sat in a chair Doug borrowed from the break room and watched Doug work. Doug processed claims. A kitchen fire in Grove City — grease, not electrical, which mattered because the homeowner's policy had an exclusion for "appliance failure" and the insured was trying to argue that the stove's ignition system had malfunctioned, when the adjuster's site report clearly showed unattended cooking oil. Doug reviewed the photographs, noted the pour pattern on the stovetop, wrote his assessment. It took forty minutes. Alex watched the entire time. He sat with his notebook on his knee and his pen in his mouth and his eyes slightly red from whatever Columbus was doing to his sinuses, and Doug could feel Alex watching him the way he sometimes felt a claim file watching him — waiting for him to decide something.

"Is this typical?" Alex asked when Doug closed the file.

"It's a Tuesday," Doug said.

"I mean, is this what your days are usually like?"

"They're usually like this. Sometimes there's a site visit. Today there isn't."

A coworker named Diane Posner walked past Doug's desk, paused, and said, "My daughter showed me a TikTok of you. She's fifteen. She said you're iconic." Diane pronounced "iconic" the way someone might pronounce a medical diagnosis they'd just received — carefully, uncertainly, as if it might require follow-up. She was holding a yogurt cup and a plastic spoon, and she had the posture of a woman who had been eating yogurt at her desk so consistently and for so long that the yogurt had become structural — load-bearing, somehow, to the architecture of her workday.

"Tell her thanks," Doug said.

"She wants to know if you're on TikTok."

"I'm not."

"She says you should be."

"I don't know how it works," Doug said, and Diane nodded in a way that suggested this was both an acceptable answer and a missed opportunity, and she continued to her desk with her yogurt.

Alex wrote something in his notebook. Doug didn't look at what it was. He was fairly sure the word "iconic" was going to appear in the profile attributed to a context that made it sound more meaningful than Diane Posner's fifteen-year-old daughter and a TikTok he'd never seen.

Lunch was at the sandwich place. It had been the sandwich place for nine years, and it was called Sal's, though there was no evidence that a person named Sal was or had ever been involved in its operation. The menu hadn't changed since Doug started going there. The booth by the window hadn't changed. Roy hadn't changed. Roy ordered turkey on wheat, mustard, no tomato. Doug ordered ham and Swiss. They sat.

"Roy, this is Alex. He's writing a profile."

Roy looked at Alex the way he looked at most new information — with the measured disinterest of a man who had determined long ago that the vast majority of new information was not going to improve his day. "About what?"

"About me," Doug said.

"Huh," Roy said. He unwrapped his sandwich.

Alex had his recorder on the table. "Roy, can I ask — what was your reaction when you saw the toast video?"

"Didn't see it," Roy said.

"You never watched it?"

"No."

"Are you — can I ask why not?"

Roy took a bite of his sandwich. He chewed. He swallowed. The interval between the question and his answer was long enough that Alex shifted in his seat, which was something Doug had learned not to do years ago. You waited for Roy. Roy did not speed up.

"I don't watch videos," Roy said. "I barely watch television."

"But your best friend went viral. Eighty million people — "

"Doug's my coworker," Roy said. "He's also my friend. But he was those things before the video and he'll be those things after. I don't need to watch a video of him to know what he's like. I've been eating lunch with him for nine years."

"That's — actually, that's a really interesting perspective. Can I quote you on that?"

Roy looked at Doug. Doug knew the look. It was the look Roy gave when someone treated a plain fact as if it were a philosophical position.

"It's your article," Roy said.

They ate. Alex asked more questions. Roy answered them in the fewest possible words, which created a rhythm that Alex seemed to find charming and Doug found normal because it was normal — it was every Tuesday and every Thursday and most Mondays and Wednesdays too. At one point Alex asked Roy what he thought about Doug's newfound fame and Roy said, "I don't think about it," and Alex wrote this down as if it were a revelation instead of a description of the way Roy processed the world, which was by not processing most of it.

Alex asked Doug if his friendship with Roy had changed since the video. Doug looked at Roy. Roy was eating his sandwich. Nothing about Roy had changed since the video. Nothing about Roy had changed since Doug had met him. Roy was a fixed point.

"No," Doug said.

"Not at all? Even — "

"It hasn't changed," Doug said.

Alex wrote something down. Doug ate his sandwich.

The session with Simone was at Doug's apartment at four o'clock. They'd been meeting four days a week for over a month now, and the apartment had accumulated evidence of the work — index cards on the kitchen table with phrases scribbled on them, a digital recorder that belonged to Simone, a legal pad with notes in her handwriting that Doug couldn't read because it was worse than his, which he had not previously thought possible.

Simone was sitting at the table when they arrived. She had a coffee from somewhere and she was reading her phone with the expression of someone who had seen enough of the internet for one lifetime and was seeing more anyway. She looked up at Alex and then at Doug.

"He's the journalist," Simone said. Not a question.

"Alex Chen," Alex said, extending his hand.

Simone shook it. She had rings on three fingers — not jewelry, working rings, the kind that stayed on because taking them off was more effort than wearing them. Her hair was up, held in place by a clip that was losing its structural battle. "Layla told me you were coming. What's the angle?"

"I'm interested in Doug as a kind of — "

"A symbol?"

"I was going to say a case study in — "

"He's not a case study," Simone said. "He's a guy. We're trying to write material." She turned to Doug. "Sit down. Tell me about the thing from yesterday, the one about the deductible."

Doug sat. He was aware of Alex's recorder on the table next to Simone's recorder, two red lights blinking at slightly different intervals, like mismatched turn signals. He felt like a claim being documented from two angles.

He told the deductible story. It was about a man in Hilliard whose roof leaked during a storm and who had filed a claim not realizing that his deductible was five thousand dollars, which meant the first five thousand of damage came out of his pocket, and the total damage was fifty-two hundred, and the insurance company's portion was two hundred dollars. The man had called Doug to ask if this was a mistake. Doug had explained that it wasn't. The man had said, "I've been paying premiums for twelve years." Doug had said, "The premiums purchased the policy. The policy has a deductible." The man had been quiet for a long time and then asked, "So what did I pay for?" Doug hadn't had a good answer because the man was technically right — he'd paid for the idea that something would be there when things went wrong, and something was there, but the gap between the idea and the two-hundred-dollar check was a gap that Doug had spent his career standing inside of, explaining to people why it was the size it was.

Simone listened. She didn't interrupt. When Doug finished she said, "The last part. 'The gap.' That's the bit."

"It's not a bit," Doug said. "It happened."

"That's why it's the bit."

Alex watched this exchange and Doug could see him watching it — the slight forward lean, the pen cap in his mouth, the notebook open on his lap. Doug wondered what Alex saw. Two people at a kitchen table, one of them talking about insurance deductibles, the other one finding comedy in it. He suspected Alex saw something more significant than that. Alex seemed like a person who found significance the way Doug's apartment found pollen — it drifted in through every opening and settled on every surface and could not be kept out regardless of effort.

They worked for another forty minutes. Doug talked about claims and Simone found the moments where the mundane turned specific enough to be strange, or sad, or accidentally precise about something that wasn't insurance at all. Alex took notes. At one point Simone looked at Alex and said, "You're going to make this sound like therapy, aren't you."

"I'm just observing," Alex said.

"Observing is what therapists say right before they ask how that makes you feel."

Alex didn't respond to this. He wrote something down. His pen cap was in his mouth again. He had the look of a man who was composing sentences about what he was seeing, which was different from actually seeing it, and Doug wondered if Alex knew the difference. Journalists, in Doug's limited experience, were like adjusters — they assessed what happened and wrote it into a format that turned mess into narrative. The difference was that adjusters knew the narrative was a reduction. They knew the form left things out. Doug wasn't sure journalists knew that, or if they did, whether it bothered them the way it sometimes bothered Doug when a claim file flattened something real into a number and a determination code.

After the session, Doug and Alex drove to the Kroger on High Street because Doug needed groceries and the profile apparently required footage of Doug buying groceries. "I need eggs," Doug said as they walked in. "And bread. And coffee. The coffee I buy is on sale every three weeks on a cycle, and this is a cycle week, so the timing's good."

"You track the sale cycle?"

"It's a seven-dollar difference. Over a year that's about a hundred and twenty dollars. That's not nothing."

"Right. No, that's — yeah."

Doug walked the aisles. Alex walked next to him. The fluorescent light was the same as always — flat, even, the kind of light that made everything look equally unimportant. Doug put eggs in the cart, then bread, then the coffee, which was in fact on sale, $8.99 instead of $15.99. He picked up a bag of oranges because they looked fine and he'd been eating an orange at his desk every afternoon, something he'd started doing after the Indianapolis trip without thinking about why and had continued doing without thinking about why.

A college kid in a Buckeyes hoodie recognized Doug near the cereal aisle. He stopped, pointed, and said, "Oh shit. Wedding toast guy."

"Hey," Doug said.

"Can I get a photo? My roommate's gonna lose his mind."

"Sure."

Doug stood next to the kid. The kid held up his phone. Doug looked at the camera the way he looked at anything pointed at him unexpectedly — with the expression of a man who had been asked to identify himself in a lineup and was not entirely certain he could.

"Dude," the kid said, looking at the photo. "You look like you're getting arrested."

"I don't know what to do with my face," Doug said. This was true. He had never learned the specific muscular arrangement that photographs required. In every photo Doug had ever appeared in — wedding photos, work ID, driver's license — he looked like a person who had just been told something mildly concerning and was still processing it.

The kid showed the photo to Alex. Alex looked at it and then at Doug and wrote something in his notebook.

They checked out. The cashier, a woman in her sixties with reading glasses on a beaded chain and a name tag that said BEVERLY, scanned his items without looking up and said, "You need bags?" and Doug said, "I brought my own," and this exchange — ordinary, unnarrated, free of subtext — was the most normal thing that had happened all day. Doug was grateful for it in a way that he could not have explained to Alex and did not try to.

In the parking lot, loading grocery bags into the Civic's trunk, Alex asked the question Doug had been waiting for since the morning.

"How has fame changed you?"

Doug put the eggs in last because eggs went on top. This was not a philosophy. It was structural.

"I bought better cereal," he said.

Alex waited. Doug closed the trunk.

"That's it?"

"I used to buy store brand. Now I buy Grape-Nuts. It's not even that much more expensive. A dollar forty, maybe."

"And that's the only change."

Doug thought about it. He leaned against the Civic. The parking lot was warm — the first sustained heat of the year, the asphalt giving it back, the kind of afternoon where the air above the blacktop shimmered if you looked at it right. He thought about the money, which was real. The attention, which was real. The contract with Layla, the sessions with Simone, the Netflix deal, the pharmaceutical conference in Indianapolis where four hundred people had watched him fail and then clapped. He thought about the fact that a college kid had just called him "wedding toast guy" in a grocery store and would post that photo and people he'd never met would see it and form opinions about his face.

He thought about the cereal. The cereal was the only change he could point to and say: that is different. Everything else was either too large to describe or too subtle to name, and Doug did not describe things he couldn't measure.

"The cereal's the main thing," Doug said.

---

The profile published nine days later. Layla texted Doug the link at 6:47 a.m. with three exclamation marks and a fire emoji. Doug was brushing his teeth. He read it standing at the bathroom sink, his mouth full of toothpaste, because he didn't want to wait the thirty seconds it would take to spit and rinse. This should have told him something about how much he cared about what a stranger had written about him.

The headline was: **"America's Most Reluctant Comedian."**

The article was long. It was well-written. Alex could write — the sentences were clean and the observations were specific and the structure moved from the toast to the aftermath to the profile visit with a momentum that carried you forward the way good journalism did. Doug recognized the quality because he read the *Columbus Dispatch* every morning and had a sense for when reporting worked and when it was just organized.

The Doug in the article was charming. He was self-deprecating in a way that suggested quiet wisdom rather than the absence of anything more interesting to say. He was a "midwestern Everyman" — the phrase appeared in the second paragraph — who had accidentally "spoken truth to power" by getting drunk at his daughter's wedding and saying what every man of his generation wanted to say but couldn't. The article called him "the last honest man in America" in a subheading, which was not a phrase Doug would have used to describe himself or anyone, because honesty was not a competitive sport and if it were, Doug would not have placed.

The article described Doug's apartment as "spartan but deliberate, the home of a man who has decided what he needs and discarded the rest." This was generous. Doug had not discarded anything. He had moved in twelve years ago with boxes he hadn't unpacked and had continued living around them the way water goes around rocks — not as a decision, as a path of least resistance. The deliberateness the article attributed to him was the absence of deliberation, which was different, but Alex had not seen it that way because Alex was thirty-one and still believed that a man who owned very little had made a philosophical choice about ownership rather than simply not going to the store.

The article described the lunch with Roy as "a portrait of male friendship in its most elemental form — two men who have sat across from each other long enough that silence carries the same weight as speech." Doug read this twice. He thought about the actual lunch, in which Roy had eaten a turkey sandwich and said almost nothing and Alex had tried repeatedly to make Roy's nothing into something and Roy had let him because Roy did not care what anyone wrote about anything. The article had turned Roy's indifference into philosophy. Roy was not a philosopher. Roy was a man who ate turkey sandwiches and did not have opinions about things that didn't require opinions.

The article described the session with Simone as "a creative partnership that operates on the frequency of recognition — two people who have found, in each other, a collaborator who understands that comedy is not performance but excavation." Doug did not know what frequency of recognition meant. He and Simone sat at his kitchen table and he talked about insurance claims and she told him which parts were funny. It was not excavation. It was closer to sorting mail — most of it was routine, and every once in a while something landed that neither of them expected, and Simone would say "that works" and write it down and Doug would say "that's not a joke" and Simone would say "I know."

The article quoted Doug seven times. Three of the quotes were accurate in the way Doug understood accuracy — the words matched what he'd said and the context matched what he'd meant. The other four were accurate in the way insurance documents were sometimes accurate, where every fact was correct and the total picture was wrong. Doug had said "I don't know how to be a public person" while trying to explain why he didn't have a TikTok account. In the article, the quote appeared beneath a paragraph about the crisis of modern masculinity. It looked like a confession. It was a tech-support complaint.

The section about the grocery store made Doug sound like a character in a movie about a lovably humble man who still shops for his own groceries despite being famous. Doug had been buying his own groceries for fifty-two years. There was no "despite." The fact that the article needed a "despite" — needed the groceries to mean something beyond eggs and bread and coffee at $8.99 — told Doug everything about the distance between the profile's version of him and the actual him. It was the same distance that existed between the internet's version and the actual him, and between the Netflix version and the actual him, and the layers were accumulating now like coats of paint on a wall nobody looked at closely, each one covering the one before, and somewhere underneath all of them was the original surface, and Doug was starting to forget what color it had been.

Layla called at 7:30. "It's incredible," she said. "The engagement's insane. It's already been shared like forty thousand times."

"Forty thousand," Doug said.

"I've had three calls this morning. A podcast wants you. The literary agent's back about the book deal. And — okay, don't freak out — the *Times* wants to do a follow-up."

"The Dispatch — "

"The New York Times, babe. The *Times* Times."

Doug said nothing. He was still standing in the bathroom. The toothpaste had dried on the corner of his mouth. He looked at himself in the mirror — the same face, the thinning hair, the reading glasses on the back of the toilet where he'd left them two days ago, the face of a man who adjusted insurance claims in Columbus, Ohio, and had once gotten very drunk at a wedding. The mirror didn't know about the article. The mirror showed what it showed.

"Doug? You there?"

"Yeah," he said. "I'm here."

At the office, three people mentioned the profile before Doug reached his desk. Janet stopped him in the hallway and said, "I read it. Very thoughtful," in a tone that suggested she was commenting on a eulogy. A woman from accounting whose name Doug could not remember said, "My husband loved the cereal part." Doug thanked her. He thanked Janet. He sat down. He turned on his computer. He opened the claims queue.

Roy was already at his desk. His coffee was half-drunk. His computer was on. He had the same posture he'd had every morning for the entire time Doug had known him — slightly forward, shoulders level, the posture of a man who had found the precise angle at which work was tolerable and had never adjusted.

"Morning," Roy said.

"Morning," Doug said.

"I read the headline."

"Just the headline?"

"It was enough." Roy took a sip of his coffee. He set the mug down. "Three too many words."

Doug waited. He had learned a long time ago that Roy's pauses were not invitations to speak. They were structural, load-bearing, part of the sentence even though they contained no words.

"You're not a comedian," Roy said. "And you're not reluctant. You just haven't figured out how to stop."

Doug looked at Roy. Roy was already looking at his screen. He had moved on. The assessment was complete — delivered in the same flat tone Roy used to flag a discrepancy in a claim file, factual and final and not available for appeal.

Doug turned to his own screen. The claims queue had fourteen items. A burst pipe in Westerville, straightforward. A chimney fire in Dublin. A woman in Upper Arlington whose sump pump had failed during last week's rain — she'd filed her first claim that morning, and her documentation was thorough in a way that suggested fear rather than experience. She'd photographed the basement from four angles. She'd saved the receipt for the sump pump. She'd written a description of events in neat handwriting that went slightly frantic in the margins where she'd added details she'd remembered after the fact.

Doug opened the file. He started reading.

The phone's red light blinked — Layla, probably, or a podcast, or someone who had read the article and wanted something from the man described in it, the charming reluctant comedian with the spartan apartment and the midwestern silences and the quiet wisdom. That man did not exist. But the article was well-written and the internet would share it and people would read it and believe, the way they'd believed the toast, and the version would harden into another layer, and Doug would carry it the way he carried all the others — not as weight exactly, but as accumulating distance between himself and whatever he actually was, a distance that grew every time someone else described him and he recognized nothing.

He processed the claim. The burst pipe in Westerville was PVC joint failure, covered under the standard homeowner's policy, no exclusions applicable. He wrote his assessment. He entered the determination code. He moved to the next file.

The phone kept blinking. Doug didn't answer it. He had fourteen claims and the morning was already half gone and the woman in Upper Arlington was waiting to find out if her ruined basement was covered, which it was, and somebody needed to tell her, and Doug was good at telling people things like that. He picked up the phone. It was Layla. He put her on hold. He dialed the woman in Upper Arlington.
