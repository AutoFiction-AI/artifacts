# Chapter 6

Doug had never been to Los Angeles. He had never had a reason to go. The farthest west he'd traveled was Denver, for a claims seminar in 2017, and Denver had been fine in the way that most places were fine — the hotel had a lobby, the conference room had a projector and a bowl of individually wrapped mints, and the airport was a building he left through. Los Angeles was not Denver.

The first thing he noticed was the light. Not that it was sunny — Columbus had sun, sometimes, and Doug was not opposed to sun — but that the light had a different quality, as if someone had adjusted the settings on the day itself. Stepping out of the terminal at LAX, his shadow on the concrete had edges. Clean lines where light ended and shade began. In Columbus, in March, shadows were suggestions. Gray smears that could have been a cloud, a building, a guess. Here his shadow was precise enough to trace.

Layla had arranged a car, which turned out to be a man named Dmitri in a black Escalade who held a sign with Doug's name spelled "Doug Brigge" and drove with one hand on the wheel and the other on his phone, texting in what looked like Russian at a speed that suggested either the conversation was urgent or Dmitri simply drove and texted at the same rate, which was fast. He had a small laminated photo of a girl, maybe five or six, clipped to his sun visor, and he drove like a man who wanted to get home to her while also not entirely believing this was achievable. He asked Doug where he was visiting from. Doug said Columbus. Dmitri said, "Ohio?" Doug said yes. Dmitri nodded once and did not ask a follow-up question, which Doug appreciated.

The freeway had seven lanes. Doug counted them. Seven lanes of traffic moving at what appeared to be both very fast and completely stationary, depending on which lane you looked at, and Dmitri wove between them with the indifference of a man who had calculated the odds of his own death and found them acceptable. Outside the windows, the city slid by in a blur of low buildings and strip malls that looked, briefly, like any city, until Doug started noticing the plants. Palm trees lined the boulevard like tall bare poles with mops at the top — they looked less like trees than like something a landscaper had installed as a concept. Along the concrete walls bordering the freeway, bougainvillea exploded in bright pink and purple, pouring over fences and climbing walls and blooming in March with a kind of aggression that felt like a violation of seasonal protocol. In Columbus, in March, nothing bloomed. Things were still thinking about it. Here the flowers had decided months ago and were already bored with being in bloom.

The hotel was in Silver Lake, which Layla had described as "totally your vibe" in a text, a phrase Doug had looked at for a long time without understanding. The building was two stories, painted a gray that someone had probably called "concrete" or "ash" on a paint chip, and had a sign that was just the hotel's name in lowercase letters without punctuation: the wilding. Not The Wilding. Just the wilding, as if capitalization was something that happened to other hotels.

The woman at the front desk had short hair and earrings made from what appeared to be tiny pieces of driftwood. She was maybe thirty, and she wore a linen shirt that was either wrinkled by design or had been ironed by someone who didn't believe in it. She called Doug "love" when she handed him his key card, which was not a plastic card but a matte black rectangle with no visible markings. "You're in room seven, love. Up the stairs and to the left. There's cold brew in the lobby fridge, and we've got a sound bath in the garden at six if you want to decompress from your travel."

Doug did not know what a sound bath was. He did not ask. He took the matte black rectangle and went up the stairs.

The room had exposed brick on one wall, which Doug understood as a design choice rather than a construction deficiency, though his instinct was to assess the mortar joints, which looked original and were, in his professional opinion, due for repointing within five years. The bed was low to the ground and made with white sheets that had the crisp authority of sheets that had never been slept in, or had been washed at a temperature that removed all evidence of prior use. There were no closet doors. There was a closet — a rectangular alcove built into the wall with a rod and four wooden hangers and a folded white robe — but where doors would normally be there was nothing. Just the opening. Doug's bag had his suit in it, the same suit he'd worn to the wedding, the one Layla had told him to bring because "you might need it, babe, you never know," and he hung it on one of the wooden hangers and watched it hang there, visible from the bed, from the desk, from the bathroom doorway. You could not not see the closet. The suit looked like a person standing in the alcove, waiting to be worn by someone who didn't live here. This was apparently the point.

The bathroom had a shower made entirely of glass — floor, walls, door, all of it, the kind of arrangement that meant the person showering had no barrier between their body and the rest of the room. In Doug's apartment in Clintonville, the shower had a vinyl curtain from Target that he replaced every eight months when it started to mildew, and the replacement cost for that curtain was $12.99, and nobody had ever been in a position to observe him showering, which was exactly how he preferred it.

On the nightstand, next to a small succulent in a clay pot: a book called *The Surrender Experiment*. Doug picked it up. The cover had a picture of a man who looked calm in a way that suggested either genuine inner peace or a significant financial investment in appearing calm. The subtitle was "My Journey into Life's Perfection." Doug read the first sentence, which was about the author sitting in meditation and realizing that his personal preferences were the source of all his problems. He put the book down. He had not considered that his personal preferences were a problem. He had considered them preferences.

He sat on the edge of the low bed and looked at the room. The exposed brick. The doorless closet with his suit hanging in it. The glass shower he could see from where he sat. The book about surrendering. The succulent, which was alive and seemed to be doing fine without anyone attending to it, which was maybe the point of succulents.

His phone buzzed. Layla: "How's the hotel?? Isn't it incredible??"

Doug typed: "There are no doors on the closet."

Layla: "OMG I know right?? It's so Silver Lake. Get some rest babe, big day tomorrow!! Meeting's at 2. I'll pick you up at 1."

Doug looked at the time. It was 4:30 in the afternoon, Pacific, which was 7:30 in Columbus, which was when he normally ate dinner, which was normally cereal. He was hungry. He went downstairs. The woman at the desk — her name was either Marin or Maren, the tag was in a cursive that blurred the difference — directed him to a restaurant three blocks away. "They do amazing small plates," she said.

He walked three blocks through a neighborhood where every storefront had a single word for its name — Provision, Botanica, Yield — and nothing in the windows that indicated what was sold inside, and where a vine with orange flowers he'd never seen before was growing out of a crack in a retaining wall as if the crack were the point. The restaurant was Provision, and the menu was written on a chalkboard in handwriting so deliberate it might have been a font.

He ordered a chicken dish that came with something called "charred broccolini" and a grain he couldn't identify. The waitress asked if he wanted "still or sparkling" and he said "tap" and she paused as if processing this. The bill was $34 for one plate and a water. In Columbus, $34 bought him and Roy lunch for three days including pickles. He paid it and walked back to the hotel in air that was sixty-eight degrees at seven o'clock in March, which in Columbus would have required July, and the wrongness of the temperature made everything feel slightly tilted, as if he'd walked onto a set where the season was incorrect and nobody had fixed it.

He went to bed at nine o'clock Pacific, which was midnight his time. He brushed his teeth in a sink made of concrete — actual concrete, the kind used for foundations, formed into a basin — and looked at his face in a mirror that was larger than the mirror in his apartment and somehow showed more of him than he wanted to see. He got into bed. The sheets were smooth and cool. Through the doorless closet opening, his suit hung in the dark, a familiar shape in an unfamiliar room, shoulders slightly off because the wooden hangers were wider than his actual shoulders. He closed his eyes. Somewhere outside, a dog barked twice and then stopped, and the silence that followed was warmer and softer than any silence in Columbus, where March silence had a metallic quality, the sound of cold air holding still.

---

The Netflix office was not what Doug expected, though he wasn't sure what he'd expected, since his exposure to the entertainment industry was limited to a documentary about the Fyre Festival that Roy had described to him in such detail that Doug felt he'd seen it, even though Roy didn't watch documentaries, which meant Roy had heard about it from someone who had, and the secondhand summary had arrived at Doug's desk at Mutual Fidelity fully intact.

The building was glass and pale wood and had a lobby that felt more like a hotel lobby than the hotel's lobby had. A woman at the front desk gave Doug a visitor badge on a lanyard and told him to wait, and while he waited he sat on a couch that was either very expensive or an extremely convincing reproduction of something very expensive, and next to the couch there was a small table with a bowl of tangerines on it, and Doug ate one because he hadn't had lunch. He hadn't had lunch because the meeting was at two — which was apparently when people in Los Angeles ate lunch, not met about television — and Layla had picked him up at twelve-thirty instead of one, talking on her phone in the back seat of another black car about someone named Marcus who was "literally on fire right now, not in a bad way, in a brand way." Doug had watched Silver Lake turn into a freeway turn into another neighborhood turn into the Netflix parking lot with his stomach empty and a tangerine peel in his hand and nowhere to put it. He folded it into his jacket pocket and the jacket smelled like citrus for the rest of the day.

The conference room was on the third floor. Glass walls, which Doug was beginning to think was a condition in Los Angeles — an allergy to opacity, a commitment to making everything visible, including himself, which was the part that tightened his grip on the tangerine peel in his pocket. There were succulents on the windowsill. The table was long and pale and had the grain of real wood, which Doug noticed because the table in the Mutual Fidelity break room was laminate with a wood-grain print, and the difference was the difference between a claim with documentation and a claim without.

Charlie Falk came in like weather. He was mid-forties but moved like he hadn't been informed. White sneakers that were geometric and angular and looked like they'd been designed by someone who also designed buildings. A gray T-shirt that fit in the way of T-shirts that cost more than Doug's dress shirts. He had a notebook — not a phone, an actual spiral-bound notebook, covered in handwriting — and he was flipping through it as he walked, talking before he sat down, which gave Doug the impression that the conversation had started in another room and Charlie had carried it in here like a tray he hadn't put down.

"Doug. Doug, Doug, Doug." He said the name like he was testing the weight of it. He sat down across from Doug and leaned forward on his elbows. "Okay. I'm just going to say it. I've watched the toast — Maya, how many times have I watched the toast?"

One of the two people who had followed Charlie into the room looked up from her laptop. She was younger than Kelsey, maybe twenty-five, and had her hair pulled back tight and a pen wedged behind her ear that she kept reaching up to touch, as if checking it was still there. She wore sneakers too, but hers looked like they'd actually been used for walking, and there was a thin leather cord around her wrist with a single bead on it that she turned between her fingers when she wasn't typing. "You said fourteen," she said.

"It's more than fourteen. It's probably — that doesn't matter. The number doesn't matter. What matters is, every time I watch it, I see something new. And that's the mark of — that's, like — that's the thing."

The other person was a man in his early thirties with a beard trimmed so precisely it looked like it had been done with a level and a tattoo on his forearm of what appeared to be a line graph, though of what data Doug could not determine. He'd been nodding since he entered the room. He had not yet stopped. He had a notebook too, but his was leather-bound and expensive-looking, and he wrote in it with a pen that had a cap, which he removed and replaced between notes with a soft clicking sound.

"So." Charlie put both hands flat on the table. "Here's what I'm seeing. And tell me if I'm off base, because I want this to be a conversation, not a pitch. I want this to be us, in a room, just — figuring out what this thing is."

He paused. Doug waited. Charlie appeared to be waiting too, as if "tell me if I'm off base" had been a genuine request for permission to continue.

"You can go ahead," Doug said.

"Right. So what I love about the toast — and I know what I'm about to say is, like, a word that gets thrown around in this building, I know that. But I mean it specifically. What you did in that toast was — you were *authentic*. You were *vulnerable*. And the comedy wasn't — it wasn't material. It wasn't jokes. It was a man standing in, like, total emotional — it was — okay." He restarted. "You were in this space of honesty, and the comedy was a byproduct. Not the point. The byproduct. Does that make sense?"

Doug considered this. He'd been standing at a microphone. He'd been drunk. "I think so," he said, which was not true.

"And what excites me," Charlie said, building on his own sentence before it had finished settling, "is what that means for the special. Because we're not looking for a comedy special. We're looking for a Doug Bridge special. We're looking for — and this is the frame, this is what I keep coming back to, Maya, what's the —"

"Dad-core?" Maya said, not looking up.

"Dad-core, yes, but the bigger one."

"The masculinity conversation."

"The masculinity conversation. That's the frame." Charlie turned back to Doug. "You are, whether you know it or not, at the center of the masculinity conversation right now. The internet didn't just make you viral, Doug. The internet looked at you and said, here's a different way to be a man. Not the polished guy, not the — no offense to, what's his name, your daughter's —"

"Brendan," Doug said.

"Brendan. Not the Brendan model. Not the guy who has the right thing to say at the right time. You're the guy who says the wrong thing at the wrong time and *means it*. That's the conversation. That's the whole thing."

Charlie sat back. He looked at Doug with the expression of a man who had placed a large bet and was watching the wheel spin. The nodding man — his name, Doug would learn later, was also Brendan, which was a coincidence that Doug did not have time to process — nodded harder. Maya touched her pen.

Layla, who had been on her phone in a chair against the wall since they'd sat down, looked up. "And the energy around this is insane, babe. Just — Charlie, tell him about the numbers."

"The numbers." Charlie waved this away with his hand. "Eighty million views, that's not a video, that's a cultural — but I don't even want to talk about numbers. I want to talk about Doug. I want to talk about your life."

"Okay," Doug said.

"So walk me through it. A day. What does your day look like?"

Doug thought about his actual days. He woke up at 6:40. He showered. He ate toast or a banana. He drove to Mutual Fidelity. He processed claims. He ate lunch with Roy at the sandwich place they'd been going to for nine years. He processed more claims. He drove home. He ate dinner. He watched something on television or he didn't. He went to bed.

"It's pretty normal," he said.

"What's normal? Give me specifics."

"I go to work. I process insurance claims. Residential, mostly. Water damage. I eat lunch. I come home."

"And dinner? What's dinner?"

"Cereal, most nights."

Charlie went still. It was a specific kind of stillness — the stillness of a man who has been digging through rubble and hit something solid, who understands he should not move too fast or it might shift. The nodding man stopped nodding. Maya's hand froze on the way to her pen.

"Cereal," Charlie said.

"Yeah."

"For dinner."

"It's faster than cooking. And I'm the only one eating, so it doesn't —" Doug didn't finish the sentence because he wasn't sure where it was going and because the sentence, wherever it was going, was going somewhere he didn't want to be in this conference room.

"What kind of cereal?"

"It depends. Raisin Bran, usually. Sometimes Cheerios if they're on sale."

"Raisin Bran." Charlie said the words the way a jeweler might say the name of a stone. "And you eat this — where? At a table? On the couch?"

"Standing at the kitchen counter."

Charlie put both hands on top of his head, which was the gesture of a man watching something leave the park. "Standing at the counter," he repeated, mostly to himself. He looked at Maya. She was already typing.

"Okay, so — here's where I'm going with this." Charlie leaned forward again. "And this is just an idea, I'm just riffing, but — what if part of the special is your actual life? Not recreated, not dramatized. Your *actual* apartment, your *actual* routine. We put a camera in your kitchen and we film you eating Raisin Bran standing at the counter. We film you at your desk at — where do you work?"

"Mutual Fidelity."

"At Mutual Fidelity, processing claims. We film you driving to work. We film the *real stuff*. And then the live portion, the performance, that's you being — you. Talking about being you. The way you did at the toast."

Doug thought about his apartment. He thought about the kitchen counter with its stain near the sink from a coffee mug he'd left too long, which he cleaned every Sunday with a sponge he replaced monthly. The Ikea bookshelf in the living room with the books Roy had lent him over the years, none returned, arranged not by author or title but by the order he'd received them, which was a filing system only he understood. The one art print on the wall above the couch — a lithograph from a gallery on High Street that Megan had bought during one of the phases of their marriage when she was trying to make the apartment feel like somewhere you'd choose to live, and which she'd left behind when she moved out, either because she didn't want it or because she'd forgotten it, and Doug had never asked which because both answers would have been bad in different ways. The second bedroom, the one he called the office even though there was no computer in it, just boxes from the move twelve years ago, still packed, stacked against the wall in a configuration that had settled into permanence, sealed with packing tape that had yellowed but not split.

"It's not very interesting," Doug said.

"That's the POINT, Doug." Charlie said this with both hands open, palms up, as if receiving something. "That's the whole thing. You're not interesting in the way that interesting people *try* to be interesting. You're interesting because you're *not trying*. The apartment, the cereal, the insurance job — that IS the show. We don't need to add anything. We just need to point a camera at it."

"You want to show my apartment on Netflix."

"I want to show your LIFE on Netflix."

"My life is pretty boring."

"Doug." Charlie put his hand on his chest. "I need you to hear me when I say this. Boring is the most exciting thing in entertainment right now. Boring is — Maya, the anti-aspiration thing, didn't we just —"

Maya looked up from her laptop. "The anti-aspiration conversation." She had a small scar on her chin, barely visible, and when she talked to Charlie her voice had the careful patience of someone who had been in many rooms like this one and had learned that the rooms ended faster if she supplied the words Charlie was reaching for.

"Exactly. People are tired of watching people live better lives. They're tired of aspirational. They want — they want *cereal*. They want standing at the counter in a normal apartment. They want a man who drives a — what do you drive?"

"A Civic."

"A Civic. Who drives a Civic and eats cereal for dinner and goes to work at an insurance company and has been doing this for — how long have you been there?"

"About fifteen years."

"Fifteen years. They want *that*, Doug. Because it's real. And we don't have enough of — there's not enough real right now."

Doug looked at the succulents on the windowsill. One of them had a brown spot on one leaf, and he had the urge to tell someone.

"I could show you the apartment," he said. "If you want to see it. I've got pictures on my phone from when I filed my own renter's claim a couple of years ago. Pipe burst in the unit above me. I documented everything — the water damage, the affected square footage, the baseboards. I've got before and afters."

Charlie looked at Layla. Layla looked at Charlie. The other Brendan — not that Brendan, a different Brendan, one who worked in content development and had a line graph tattooed on his arm and who said "totally" as connective tissue between other people's sentences — started nodding again, vigorously, pen cap clicking.

"He's incredible," Charlie said to Layla.

"I *told* you," Layla said, putting her phone down for the first time since the meeting started.

"We don't need to see pictures of your apartment, Doug." Charlie was shaking his head and smiling. "We're going to come to your apartment. We're going to bring a small crew to Columbus, Ohio, and we're going to film you living your actual life. That's the project."

"What would I do?"

"What you always do."

"I process claims."

"Yeah."

"You want to film me processing claims."

"I want to film you *being you*, and if being you means processing insurance claims, then yeah. We film you processing claims."

Doug thought about what processing claims actually looked like — him sitting at his desk reading documentation, making phone calls to policyholders, typing notes into the system, occasionally driving to a property to take photographs of water stains and mold patterns and warped flooring. He thought about explaining to a homeowner that their twenty-year-old carpeting had a pre-loss actual cash value of approximately $1.40 per square foot, and the specific expression people made when they heard this number, which was the expression of someone learning that the thing they'd walked on every day was worth almost nothing.

"The claims are confidential," he said.

"Sure, yeah. We can work around that. Obscure names, shoot from angles that don't show documents — that's production stuff, that's not your problem."

"The policies are proprietary. Mutual Fidelity probably wouldn't —"

"Doug." Charlie held up one hand. "We'll handle it. That's my side. Your side is being Doug."

"I'm always Doug."

"I know," Charlie said, and his voice dropped for a moment from its performance register into something quieter, almost tired, as if he'd been Charlie Falk, Development Executive, for the full meeting and was, for one second, just a man in a room who could hear what he'd been saying and knew what it sounded like from the outside. He looked down at his notebook, flipped a page, and the page was covered in his own handwriting but he didn't read it. Then he picked up his pen and the moment passed. "Okay, so — content test. Immediate next step. We'd want to shoot some preliminary footage. Nothing formal. We film you reacting to your own video. We film you at work. We film you eating dinner."

"Cereal."

"Cereal. Yes. We film the cereal." Charlie was back. Fully, completely back, and the enthusiasm was genuine, which was the part that Doug could not get his head around. Charlie was not being sarcastic. He was not mocking. He was sincerely excited about the prospect of filming a fifty-two-year-old insurance adjuster eating Raisin Bran alone in his kitchen in Columbus, Ohio, and this sincerity was more disorienting than mockery would have been, because mockery Doug could have understood. Mockery was the entertainment industry's version of depreciation — a reasonable adjustment for wear and use. This enthusiasm was something else entirely. It meant someone believed Doug's cereal was worth something, and Doug did not know how to assess worth without a depreciation schedule.

The meeting went on for another thirty-five minutes. Charlie outlined the content test timeline — a small crew would come to Columbus in two weeks, three people, "super low-key, you won't even notice them," which Doug doubted because he always noticed when people were in his apartment, which was part of why he lived alone. Maya asked about his schedule and whether he had any dietary restrictions, a question that confused him until he realized she was planning meals for the crew, not for him. The other Brendan took notes on his phone and said "love that" after nearly everything Charlie said, which was a phrase Doug had never heard used as a standalone response to a declarative sentence. He counted. Brendan said it nine times. He said "totally" eleven.

When the meeting ended, Layla and Charlie stood in the hallway talking while Doug waited near the elevator holding his visitor badge and looking at a framed poster of a show he'd never heard of. He could hear fragments — "momentum," "window," "the authentic thing is so big right now," "we've gotta move before the cycle turns" — and the language was the same as in the meeting but faster, stripped of the pauses and the looking-at-Doug moments, the way a claim looks different in the adjuster's notes than it does in the policyholder's living room. Doug realized that what he'd been sitting in was not a meeting but a presentation, and the presentation had been for him, designed to make him feel something specific, and it had worked. He felt something specific. He just didn't know what to call it.

---

The flight home left at 7:15 p.m. Doug was at the gate by 5:30 because he didn't know what else to do with the afternoon and because being early for a flight was the kind of behavior that made sense to him in a day where very little else had. Layla had offered to "grab a late lunch, or — oh, there's this incredible thing at LACMA, have you ever been?" and Doug had said no and then said he was fine and taken the black car back to the hotel and packed his bag and checked out, and Marin-or-Maren had said "Safe travels, love" and he'd left the matte black key card on the counter and walked out into the afternoon light that was still, even now, too sharp, too precise, too much like light that had been designed rather than had just happened.

He'd left *The Surrender Experiment* on the nightstand. He'd read two more pages while packing. The author had given up all personal preferences and let life guide his decisions, and this had apparently led him to build a successful software company, which seemed to Doug like a result that required at least some personal preferences about software, but he was not in a position to evaluate another man's surrender.

LAX was LAX. He ate a turkey sandwich at a place near the gate that charged $16 for turkey and Swiss on sourdough, which was roughly twice what the sandwich from his and Roy's place cost, and the sandwich from his and Roy's place came with a pickle. He sat at the gate and watched planes taxi on the tarmac and thought about what Charlie Falk had said. Not the specific words — those were already blurring the way witness testimony blurred in a disputed claim, the details smoothing into impressions — but the shape of it. They wanted to film his life. His apartment, his cereal, his Civic, his desk at Mutual Fidelity. They wanted the things he considered evidence of a life that had settled into its permanent dimensions, and they wanted them precisely because they were that evidence. The thing he would have depreciated was the thing they were appraising at full value.

He didn't know what to do with this. He bought a water and a pack of peanut M&M's from a kiosk where nothing cost less than $4.50, and he boarded the plane, and this time he had a window seat because Layla had rebooked his return flight, and he pressed his forehead against the cold plastic and watched Los Angeles shrink below him — the grid of streets, the low buildings, the freeways like rivers running cars instead of water — and then the mountains, which he hadn't known were there, which had been behind the city the whole time and which he had never once seen from the ground.

The plane crossed the desert, which was brown. Then something that was probably Nevada or Utah, also brown but differently — drier, more committed to its brownness, not apologizing the way the scrub near LA did, with its scattered bushes that looked like they were trying. Then the land turned green in patches, fields Doug recognized as agriculture even from thirty-seven thousand feet, and the patchwork became the Midwest, and the Midwest was flat, and the flatness was something he had never thought about as a quality. It had just been a fact. The way you didn't think about the temperature of the room you were in until you'd been in a room that was too hot.

Columbus appeared below him as the plane descended. He could see the grid of it, the low sprawl, the highways he drove every day reduced to gray lines on a surface of green and brown. The Scioto River was a dark thread. The neighborhoods were indistinguishable from this height — Clintonville, where he lived, and Worthington, where the diner was, and the Short North and German Village and Bexley and all the small named places that made up the geography of his daily life, all of them flattened into the same even surface, none reaching very high, none insisting on anything.

It looked right. He couldn't have said exactly why. It looked like a place that had enough room for what it was and didn't need more. It looked manageable. It looked, from thirty-seven thousand feet, like something a person might choose.

This was new. Doug had never thought of Columbus as a choice. He'd gone to Ohio State because it was affordable and close to Dayton. He'd stayed because he got the job at Mutual Fidelity and the job was fine. He'd lived in Clintonville because the apartment was available and the rent was $1,150 a month, which was reasonable for a two-bedroom, and the commute was eleven minutes in normal traffic. None of these were choices. They were the absence of choices, which was a different thing, or which he'd always assumed was different, until he sat in a glass room in Los Angeles and watched a man in expensive sneakers become ecstatic about his cereal. The absolute unreasonableness of that enthusiasm — caring about Doug's Raisin Bran, his kitchen counter, his Civic — had done something to the things themselves. It had made them visible. Not better, not special, just visible, the way the frame around a painting didn't change the painting but did tell you someone thought it was worth framing.

The plane landed. The wheels hit the runway and the cabin lurched and the passengers clapped, which Doug had never understood — you didn't clap for your bus driver, you didn't clap when you pulled into a parking space — but tonight the impulse made a kind of sense. He was back. Back felt different.

He got his bag from the overhead bin. He walked through the jet bridge and through the terminal, which was smaller than LAX in a way that felt proportional rather than insufficient. He walked to the parking garage where his Civic had been sitting for two days, and the car was gray and streaked with the salt residue of a Columbus March, and it started on the first try the way it always did, and the heater came on, and the seat was cold and then it wasn't.

He drove home on 670 and then north on High Street and then into Clintonville, and the trees were still bare, the way they'd been when he left, because two days was not enough for spring to arrive in Ohio. The streetlights made the familiar shapes of his neighborhood — the houses, the parked cars, the sidewalks he walked sometimes at night when he couldn't sleep. He pulled into his spot behind the building and turned off the engine and sat in the dark for a moment with his hands still on the wheel.

His apartment was exactly as he'd left it. The Ikea bookshelf. The art print above the couch. The kitchen counter with the stain by the sink. The second bedroom, door closed. He didn't open it. The boxes were in there, arranged in their permanent configuration, same as always, same as twelve years.

He opened the cabinet above the stove and took out the Raisin Bran. He poured a bowl. He stood at the counter and ate it, and the cereal tasted the same as it always tasted, which was fine, which was what cereal tasted like. He thought about Charlie Falk saying *That's the SHOW, Doug* and he looked at his kitchen and tried to see what Charlie saw. What he saw was a bowl, a spoon, a man, a counter, a stain, a clock that read 11:47 p.m. Eastern, an apartment in Clintonville, Ohio, with all its doors exactly where they should be.

He rinsed the bowl and set it in the drying rack, which held one bowl, one spoon, one coffee mug from this morning. He went to bed. The shower curtain in his bathroom was vinyl, translucent, and it pulled all the way closed on a curved rod, and he pulled it closed even though he wasn't showering, just because it was there and it did what it was supposed to do.
