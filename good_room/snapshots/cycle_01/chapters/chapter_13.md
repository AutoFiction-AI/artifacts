# Chapter 13

The Thai place was on a side street south of campus, a narrow storefront between a dry cleaner and a vacant lot that had been vacant for as long as Doug had been coming here, which was fourteen years. The awning had faded from red to something closer to the color of salmon left out overnight — a specific shade of defeated optimism that Doug noticed every time and never mentioned because there was no one to mention it to. The same ceramic cat sat in the window with one paw raised, though it had migrated from the left side of the sill to the right at some point in 2019, and Doug had registered the move without understanding it. A bell rang when he pushed the door open. It was Saturday, just past noon.

Kelsey wasn't there yet. Doug was twenty minutes early, the same way he'd been twenty minutes early at the diner in Worthington three months ago, and for every parent-teacher conference and dentist appointment and claims assessment in the decades before that. He showed up early the way other people showed up with opinions — automatically, reflexively, because the alternative was arriving to conditions you hadn't assessed.

The woman at the front — Doug had been seeing her for fourteen years and did not know her name, a failure he'd compounded annually until it became irreversible — pointed him toward the booth in the back without being asked. She was wearing the jade bracelet she always wore, and reading glasses that were new since the last time he'd been here, thin gold frames that she looked over rather than through as she walked him to the table. The booth's vinyl had cracked along the right side where it met the wall, a split that had started small and widened steadily, the way cracks did when nobody repaired them. Doug sat on the left side. He always sat on the left side. Kelsey sat facing the door, which she'd done since she was a teenager, and he'd never asked why.

He opened the menu out of habit, not need. He had been ordering the same thing here for fourteen years: the massaman curry, number thirty-seven, which came with a small red sticker on the menu indicating spice, though the menu's rating system stopped at three stickers and the massaman only had one, which Doug interpreted as manageable. It was not manageable. Every time he ordered it, the first bite was fine and the second was fine and by the fourth his sinuses opened and his eyes watered and a warmth moved through his chest that he attributed to the peppers and not to anything else, and he ate the entire bowl every time and drank three glasses of water and never switched to anything milder, because switching would mean acknowledging that he'd been ordering the wrong thing for fourteen years, and Doug did not acknowledge errors of that duration. He carried them forward.

The restaurant smelled the way it always smelled — fish sauce and hot oil and the lemongrass from the tom kha that someone at a nearby table was eating. The kitchen was visible through a narrow window in the back wall, and he could see hands moving, a wok flashing under the hood vent, steam rising. The music was the same instrumental loop it had always been — vaguely Southeast Asian, recorded on what sounded like a Casio keyboard, and it repeated every twelve minutes. He'd timed it once. Kelsey had been sixteen.

She came in at fourteen past noon. Not late — Kelsey wasn't late — but not early either, which for someone who'd driven two hours from Cleveland was a specific kind of punctuality that meant she'd sat in her car in the lot for a few minutes before coming inside. Doug knew this because he did it too. The Bridge approach to difficult encounters: arrive early, sit in the car, rehearse nothing because you don't know what to rehearse, then go in.

She was wearing a denim jacket over a white T-shirt, and her hair was down, which was unusual — at the diner in Worthington it had been pulled back tight, the work configuration, the she-was-there-to-do-something configuration. Down meant something different. Down meant Saturday. Down meant she'd thought about what to wear and decided on something that wasn't armor.

She slid into the booth across from him. She picked up the menu, looked at it for three seconds, and put it down. She had been ordering the pad thai, number fourteen, since she was fourteen, which she'd pointed out once as a coincidence and then corrected herself — she'd started ordering it at fourteen and now she was twenty-eight, so it was half her life, not a coincidence, just persistence.

"The drive was okay," she said, before he asked.

"Good."

"Seventy-one was fine. Construction past Medina but it wasn't bad."

"Medina. Yeah, they've been doing that stretch since —"

"I don't know. A while." She took a sip of the water the hostess had left. The glass had a hairline crack running from the rim to the midpoint, the kind of damage you'd document in a claim but that the restaurant had apparently decided was still within operational tolerance. "How's work?"

"Same. We're doing quarterly reviews next week."

"Exciting."

"It's not exciting. It's the same review I've had for eight years. 'Meets expectations.' Roy gets 'meets expectations' too. He considers it the correct rating."

Kelsey almost smiled. The almost was visible — the corner of her mouth moved and then decided against it, retreating to a neutral position like a quote that doesn't close.

"How's the practice?" Doug asked.

"It's teeth, Dad."

"Right."

The woman with the jade bracelet came to take their order. She wrote nothing down, which Doug found both impressive and faintly unsettling — he could not have processed a single claim without documentation, and the idea that this woman carried the details of multiple orders in her head with no backup suggested a confidence in her own memory that Doug did not share.

Kelsey ordered the pad thai. Doug ordered the massaman curry. The woman nodded and left.

"Tyler says hi," Kelsey said.

"Tell him hi."

"He wanted to come down. I told him this was a dad thing."

"Okay."

She turned the chopstick wrapper over in her hands, creasing it along one edge, then folding it in half, then half again. He watched her hands. The nail on her left ring finger was chipped, a crescent of bare nail showing through the dark red polish, and the wedding ring sat below it — a simple band, white gold, the one Tyler had proposed with. Doug had met the ring at dinner three days after the engagement and had assessed it automatically: white gold, approximately one carat center stone, four-prong setting, retail value probably thirty-eight hundred to forty-five hundred based on the cut grade, which he couldn't determine without magnification. He'd shaken Tyler's hand and said something adequate. He couldn't remember what.

"I read the profile," Kelsey said.

"The thing Alex wrote."

"'America's Most Reluctant Comedian.'" She said it flatly, without inflection, the way you'd read a claims number. "Roy was right about the headline."

"Roy's usually right about things he reduces to one sentence."

"It was a nice piece. It was wrong, but it was nice."

Doug took a sip of his water. "I know."

"You know it was nice or you know it was wrong?"

"Both."

The pad thai arrived first, which was normal — pad thai was faster than curry. The plate was the same oval ceramic it had always been, with a chip at the edge that Doug had noticed approximately forty visits ago and that had not grown, which meant the chip was stable, not structural, a detail that mattered in claims work and not at all in restaurants but that Doug noticed anyway. A wedge of lime sat on the edge. Kelsey squeezed the lime over the noodles without looking, the muscle memory of twenty-eight orders, and stirred it with her chopsticks.

"I keep thinking about the toast," she said. Then she stopped. She set her chopsticks down. "Actually, that's not true. I keep thinking about something else."

Doug's curry arrived. The waitress set it down and the steam carried the smell of coconut and chili and something sweet underneath, tamarind maybe. He picked up his spoon. He did not eat.

"Something from a long time ago," Kelsey said. "From the divorce."

The word landed on the table between them — *divorce* — and sat there next to the soy sauce and the chopstick wrapper she'd folded into a small tight rectangle. Doug felt the air in the booth change, the way it changed in a house when you opened the door to a room that had been closed and the air that came out was different from the air in the rest of the place. Staler. Older.

"Okay," he said.

Kelsey picked up the chopstick wrapper and unfolded it. Folded it again. She was looking at the table, not at him, and her jaw was set in the way it got when she was working something loose — the expression she wore, he imagined, when she was scaling below the gumline and the calculus wasn't giving.

"I was sixteen. You and Mom had been separated for about two months. I was going back and forth. Tuesday and Thursday nights at your place, weekends alternating. The schedule."

"I remember the schedule."

"I know you remember the schedule, Dad." She looked up. Her eyes were direct and dark, and he could see something in them that he'd been seeing since the diner in Worthington — not anger, not the specific furious precision she'd brought that day. Something underneath the anger, older and quieter. "I'm not asking if you remember the schedule. I'm telling you something."

Doug set his spoon down. "Go ahead."

"One of those Tuesdays. I was at your apartment — the first one, the one on Indianola, before you moved to Clintonville. I was doing homework on your laptop because mine was at Mom's. You were at work, or maybe you'd gone to the store. I don't remember. You weren't there."

The Indianola apartment. Doug had lived there for eight months between moving out of the Briarwood house and finding the Clintonville place. It had been a one-bedroom with carpet that smelled faintly of the previous tenant's dog, and the bathroom had a window that didn't lock, and he'd slept on a mattress on the floor for the first three weeks because the bed frame he'd ordered online had arrived with a cracked side rail and the replacement took nineteen days. He remembered all of this. He documented his own life with the same thoroughness he applied to claims.

"I opened the laptop to do my homework," Kelsey said. "And there was a spreadsheet open. An Excel file."

Doug's hands went still on the table.

"Columns," Kelsey said. Her voice had gone quiet — the Kelsey quiet, the one that was precision rather than volume, the one he'd been hearing since she was old enough to figure out that being loud didn't work on him but being exact did. "Date. Event. Description. You had entries going back months. Before the separation. You were tracking things about Mom."

Doug did not move. The curry was sending steam past his face, and he could feel the heat of it on his cheek, and the Casio music had cycled back to its beginning and was starting the twelve-minute loop again, and the couple at the table across from them was laughing about something — a bright, uncomplicated laugh that belonged to people having a different kind of Saturday.

"March fourteenth, Kelsey pickup thirty-five minutes late from school. March nineteenth, forgot to send permission slip for field trip — had to be faxed from office. April second, schedule conflict with dentist appointment, Megan booked during my Tuesday." Kelsey recited these without looking at anything, from memory. She'd been carrying them for twelve years. "You had maybe forty entries. Each one with a date and a description. Some of them had a column that said 'documented' — yes or no."

The restaurant continued around them. A waiter passed with a tray of spring rolls. The kitchen window showed hands plating something green. The bell on the door rang and someone came in and the woman with the jade bracelet led them to a table, and Doug heard all of it the way you hear sounds when you're underwater — present but distorted, arriving through a medium that changes everything.

He remembered the spreadsheet.

He remembered it precisely, the way he remembered every document he'd ever created — the cell formatting, the column widths, the specific shade of blue he'd used for the header row because Excel's default was too dark and he'd adjusted it to match the Mutual Fidelity formatting guide, which was not a relevant consideration for a personal spreadsheet tracking his ex-wife's failures as a co-parent, but he'd done it anyway because formatting was formatting and he couldn't not.

"I didn't read all of it," Kelsey said. "I read enough." She pushed her pad thai to the side, the noodles cooling, the lime wedge darkening at the edges. "I was sixteen. I was doing homework. And I found a spreadsheet where my dad was tracking every time my mom screwed up, and every single entry was about me. About picking me up. About forgetting my permission slips. About scheduling around me."

Doug opened his mouth.

"I'm not done." Not loud. Steady. "I didn't tell you. I didn't tell Mom. I closed the laptop and I did my homework on paper and I never mentioned it. Twelve years."

The folded chopstick wrapper was in her hand, crushed now, a small dense rectangle of paper that her fingers kept working as she talked.

"And what I thought — what I thought at sixteen, sitting in that apartment on Indianola with the carpet that smelled like someone else's dog — was that my dad saw me as evidence."

The word arrived in Doug's chest. Not metaphorically — he felt it in his sternum, a pressure that had nothing to do with the curry or the booth or the cracked vinyl under his left thigh. *Evidence.* That was the word. His daughter had been sixteen years old and she'd found a file on his laptop where he'd turned her into data points, into a record of failures that belonged to someone else but were measured in the unit of her — her late pickups, her missed permission slips, her Tuesday nights.

"Not a person," Kelsey said. She was looking at him now, and her eyes were full but she wasn't crying — she was holding it the way she held everything, with the tight grip and the jaw set and the gaze that was its own form of pressure. "A case file. I was a case file."

---

Neither of them ate for a while.

The pad thai sat on Kelsey's side of the table and the massaman curry sat on Doug's, and the steam from the curry had thinned and the coconut smell had faded to something less insistent, and the Casio music played its loop and started over and the restaurant went on being a restaurant on a Saturday in late April, indifferent to what was happening in the booth by the window.

Doug had not spoken. He was aware of this — aware of the seconds passing, aware of Kelsey sitting across from him with the ruined chopstick wrapper and her untouched noodles and twelve years of carrying something she'd found on a laptop in an apartment that no longer existed. He was aware that he needed to say something and that nothing he said would be right and that saying nothing was also wrong and that these two facts did not resolve into a single clear action. They just sat there side by side like competing coverage interpretations on the same claim.

He could not apologize. He understood this without examining it. An apology would be the wrong instrument — too smooth, too available, the thing you reached for when you wanted to close a file rather than understand what was in it. Kelsey hadn't driven two hours to receive an apology. She'd driven two hours to put the spreadsheet on the table between them, and the spreadsheet was there now, and an apology would be like filing it in a drawer.

"I remember making it," Doug said.

Kelsey was still. Her hands had stopped working the wrapper. She was watching him the way she'd watched him at the diner — the level gaze, the silence that was its own form of waiting.

"It was —" He stopped. He could feel the pull. The narrating Doug, the one who organized and contextualized, the one who would say *It was a difficult period* or *I was going through a lot* or some other framing that would make the spreadsheet smaller than it was. He could feel that version of himself assembling its sentences, and he could feel Kelsey watching him feel it, because she always knew — she always knew — when he was retreating into the orderly version.

He picked up his spoon. He stirred the curry once. He set the spoon down without eating.

"I didn't know what to do with what I was feeling," he said. "After your mom left. I don't mean the marriage. I'm not talking about the marriage. I'm talking about you. The schedule. Every other weekend, Tuesdays and Thursdays. I'd drive to the school to pick you up and you'd be standing there on the curb with your backpack, and then two days later I'd drive you back to your mom's, and the apartment was — the apartment on Indianola was a place I lived and you visited. That was the difference. You visited. And every time you left I didn't know what to do with the space you'd been in."

He looked at his hands on the table. They were the same hands that had typed the spreadsheet — the same fingers that had entered the dates and the descriptions and adjusted the header-row blue because even his grievance had a formatting preference.

"So I documented it. Because that's what I do. Something happens and I don't know what to feel about it, so I write it down. I put it in a column. I give it a date and a description and I file it, and once it's filed I can look at it instead of feeling it. The file is smaller than the feeling. The file fits."

The couple across the restaurant laughed again. A plate broke somewhere in the kitchen — a clean, undramatic sound — and someone said something in Thai and someone else responded, and the normal operations of the place continued without regard for what was being said in the booth by the window.

"I was keeping score because I did not know how to miss you any other way."

He said it, and the sentence sat in the air between them, and he heard himself use *did not* instead of *didn't* — the full form, the careful form, each word placed deliberately because the stakes were too high for shortcuts. He meant every word at its full weight. He had been keeping score. He had not known how. It was the only way he'd had.

Kelsey was quiet. She was looking at the chopstick wrapper in her hand — mangled, creased beyond use, a small ruin of paper that her fingers had been destroying for twenty minutes. She set it on the table next to her plate. She picked up her glass of water and drank. The hairline crack in the glass caught the overhead light.

"That's not an apology," she said.

"No."

"Okay." She put the glass down. "I'm not sure I wanted one."

"I'm not sure one would help."

"No. Probably not."

They sat with it. The music cycled. A family came in — two parents, two small kids, the smaller one in a booster seat that the hostess produced from behind the counter with the efficiency of someone who'd done this a thousand times. The kids were loud in the unconcerned way of children in restaurants, and one of them was asking for spring rolls and the other was asking for Sprite, and neither of them knew that in the booth by the window a father and daughter were sitting in the wreckage of something that had been buried for twelve years and had just been set on the table between the pad thai and the curry.

Doug picked up his spoon. He took a bite. The curry was warm, not hot — it had been sitting too long — and the spice hit differently when the food wasn't steaming, sharper, more direct, without the cushion of heat. His eyes watered. He didn't wipe them.

"Eat your noodles," he said.

Kelsey looked at him. Then she picked up her chopsticks and ate a bite of pad thai, and the noodles were room temperature by now and the lime had gone flat and neither of them commented on it.

"I told Tyler," she said, after a while. "About the spreadsheet. Last year."

"What did he say?"

"He said that was messed up." She twirled her chopsticks through the noodles without lifting any. "And then he said, 'But at least he was paying attention.' Which is such a Tyler thing to say that I almost threw something at him."

"He's not wrong."

"He's not wrong in a way that's not helpful." She ate another bite. "That's kind of Tyler's whole deal. He's never wrong in ways that aren't helpful."

Doug ate his curry. The second bite was worse — the spice was building, accumulating. He drank water. The water didn't help. It never did.

"How is Tyler?" he asked, and he meant it differently than he usually meant it. He usually meant it as a logistical check — Tyler fine, Kelsey fine, Cleveland fine, everything processed and filed. He meant it now as a real question, and Kelsey heard the difference because she looked up from her noodles and studied him for a moment before she answered.

"He's fine," she said. And then: "He's really fine. He's always fine. He's —" She set her chopsticks down. She pressed her hands flat on the table, fingers spread, the chipped nail and the wedding ring and the slight tension in her wrists that meant she was working something out in real time, not reciting something she'd prepared. "He's fine the way a hotel room is fine. Everything works. The bed is made. The towels are clean. The thermostat is set to seventy-two. You walk in and you think, yeah, this is fine. This is exactly what I asked for. And it is. It's exactly what I asked for."

She stopped. She picked up a noodle with her fingers — not her chopsticks, her fingers — and ate it, and the gesture was so stripped of pretense that Doug saw his daughter at nine years old, eating spaghetti with her hands because she hadn't felt like using a fork and he'd let her because Megan wasn't there and when Megan wasn't there the rules about utensils had been suggestions.

"I don't know if I married stability or boredom," Kelsey said. "Or if there's a difference. And I can't figure it out because every time I try to think about it I think about you and Mom, and then I think about the spreadsheet, and then I think maybe boring is fine. Maybe boring is the whole point." She looked at him. "Don't tell me what to do."

"I wasn't going to."

"You were thinking about it."

"I was thinking that I'm the last person who should give anyone advice about marriage."

Kelsey almost laughed. The almost was closer to actual laughter this time — it reached her eyes and her mouth moved and a sound came out that wasn't quite a laugh but was in the neighborhood. "Yeah. You're pretty disqualified."

"The data speaks for itself."

"God. Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"The insurance voice. The thing where you turn everything into a claims form."

Doug looked at her across the table — across the cooling curry and the flat pad thai and the mangled chopstick wrapper and twelve years of a secret she'd carried since she was sixteen and the long, specific history of two people who loved each other in a language neither of them spoke fluently. He thought about the spreadsheet. He thought about the woman at the front with her jade bracelet and her new glasses. He thought about the vinyl crack in the booth that nobody had repaired and the chip in the plate that had been stable for forty visits and the ceramic cat in the window that had migrated from one side to the other for reasons nobody would ever explain.

"I don't know how to turn it off," he said. "It's not a voice. It's just how I think."

"I know," Kelsey said. "That's the problem. And it's also —" She stopped. She pressed her lips together. She was doing the thing she did when something was about to come out that was too close to tender, too close to the thing she wasn't ready to put on the table alongside the spreadsheet. "It's also why the toast made sense. The way you talk. The exactness. It's why eighty million people watched you — because nobody talks like that when they're drunk. And you did."

Doug had nothing to say to this. He ate another bite of curry. The spice had reached the point where it was no longer a flavor but a condition, a state of being, and his nose was running and his eyes were wet and he was not going to switch to something milder because this was the fourteenth year of the massaman curry and he was not a man who changed his order.

"Do you want dessert?" he said.

"Mango sticky rice," Kelsey said, without pause.

Doug flagged the waitress. One order, two spoons. The waitress wrote nothing down.

The mango sticky rice came on a plate that was too large for it — a generous portion by the standards of the dish, which was always small: the mango sliced into a fan and the rice in a mound beside it, glistening with coconut cream, sesame seeds on top catching the light from the fixture above the booth. Kelsey pulled the plate to the center of the table. She took her spoon and cut into the mango. Doug took his spoon and scooped a piece of rice. The coconut cream was sweet and slightly warm, and the rice had the texture of something that had been tended to properly — cooked slow, rinsed, steamed, sweetened — the kind of food that required patience to make and no effort at all to eat.

They ate in silence. Not the loaded silence of the diner in Worthington — not the five minutes of honest nothing that had said everything they couldn't. This was a different silence, easier, the silence of two people who had said the hard thing and were sitting in the aftermath, and the aftermath tasted like coconut and mango and sesame, and neither of them needed it to be anything more than what it was.

Kelsey's spoon clinked against the plate as she reached for the last piece of mango. She got it. Doug didn't contest this. He had never contested the last piece of anything with his daughter. He didn't know if this was generosity or the specific passivity that had shaped most of his relationships, and he decided, sitting in the booth with the cracked vinyl and the faded awning outside and fourteen years of the same orders, that it didn't matter. She wanted the mango. He let her have it.

"I should probably head back before five," Kelsey said. "Tyler's making dinner."

"Tyler cooks?"

"He makes this chicken thing. It's fine."

"Fine like the hotel room?"

Kelsey looked at him. For a second he thought he'd gone too far — she'd handed him something fragile about her marriage and he'd turned it into a callback, which was a comedy mechanic, and this was not comedy. But then she snorted — an ugly, genuine sound, not a laugh, closer to the noise someone makes when they've been hit with something unexpectedly accurate.

"Yeah," she said. "Fine like the hotel room. The chicken is fine like the hotel room."

She put a twenty on the table. Doug reached for his wallet. "I've got it," she said.

"You drove two hours."

"And you listened. That's worth twenty bucks." She slid out of the booth. She stood and pulled on the denim jacket and zipped it halfway — always halfway, as if committing to the full zip would constitute an opinion about the weather she wasn't prepared to hold.

"Thanks for the curry," he said. This was not what he meant, but she would know what he meant, because she always knew what he meant, even when — especially when — he said something else.

"The curry you can't handle and won't stop ordering."

"It's fine."

"It's not fine. Your eyes were watering for twenty minutes."

"That was the curry."

Kelsey looked at him. She didn't argue. She didn't need to.

"I'll call you this week," she said.

"Okay."

"I mean it. I'll actually call."

"Okay."

She walked to the door. The bell rang. She was gone.

Doug sat in the booth. The mango sticky rice plate had one smear of coconut cream left on it, and two spoons, and the remains of a meal that had started as routine and ended somewhere he didn't have a category for. He picked up the check. Thirty-eight dollars. He left Kelsey's twenty and added twenty-two of his own, and the math was wrong — he was overtipping by more than he usually would — but the woman with the jade bracelet had been serving him for fourteen years and had never once asked about the toast or the video or the profile, and he didn't know if this was discretion or indifference, and either way he was grateful in the particular way you're grateful to someone who lets you be ordinary when the rest of the world has decided you're something else.

He walked to the lot. It was half-full, and Kelsey's Civic was already gone — she'd left quickly, the way she left things, cleanly, without lingering in the doorway. The sun was warm on his arms for the first time in months, late April finally asserting itself against the long memory of winter, and the sky was the washed-out blue that Columbus produced in spring — not dramatic, not worth photographing, just blue enough to count.

He got in the car. He did not start it. He sat with the windows up and the key in his lap and thought about the spreadsheet.

Column A: Date. Column B: Event. Column C: Description. Column D: Documented (Y/N).

He had made it in the apartment on Indianola, on the laptop that his sixteen-year-old daughter had opened to do her homework, on a weeknight when she was in the next room and he was at work or at the store or wherever he'd gone that he couldn't remember now, and the spreadsheet was open because he'd been adding to it that morning, because every morning during those months he'd sat at the kitchen counter with his coffee and entered the latest data the way he entered claim data at the office — date, event, description, status — and the routine of entering it had felt like control, and control had felt like the only alternative to the thing he could not name and could not file and could not depreciate on any schedule he knew.

He had turned his daughter into a line item. He had done this because he loved her and did not know how to hold the loving, and documentation was the only container he'd ever trusted.

She had known for twelve years. She had carried it for twelve years — through the rest of high school, through college, through the engagement and the wedding and the toast and the months since the toast — and she'd come to the Thai place on a Saturday in April and set it on the table between the pad thai and the curry, and he'd told her the truth. Not an apology, not a defense. The flat, exact truth that was the only thing he'd ever had to offer and the thing he almost never offered. And she'd heard it, and they'd split the mango sticky rice, and she'd gone home to Tyler and his fine chicken in their fine apartment, and neither of them had mentioned the toast.

He sat in the car for a long time. The parking lot was warm through the windshield and the ceramic cat in the Thai place window stared at the street with its one raised paw and the vacant lot next door was still vacant and the awning was still faded and everything was exactly where it had been when he'd pulled in an hour and a half ago, except that his daughter had driven two hours to tell him the worst thing she knew about him, and he'd sat there and taken it, and what she'd told him was true.

Doug started the car. He drove home to Clintonville. The route was familiar — right on High, left on Arcadia, past the library and the coffee shop and the intersection where a hydrant had burst last December and he'd assessed the resulting water damage to the duplex on the corner, four thousand in remediation, covered under the city's liability policy, closed and filed. He drove past it and the duplex looked fine. You couldn't tell anything had happened. The damage was repaired, the paperwork was complete, and the only evidence that fifteen hundred gallons of water had flooded the ground floor was in a claims file on a server somewhere, a record that documented everything that had been lost and everything it had cost to fix and said nothing at all about what it had felt like to stand in the water.

He parked. He went inside. He didn't eat cereal. He sat on the couch in the living room as the afternoon shifted into evening, and the apartment was quiet, and Simone's pen cap was still on the counter where he'd left it weeks ago, and the boxes were still in the second bedroom, and the water stain on the ceiling was still shaped like Ohio, and everything was where it had always been, and none of it was the same.
