# Chapter 9

Doug had practiced the set fourteen times. He knew this because he'd kept a log — not intentionally, not in a spreadsheet, just hash marks on the back of a Mutual Fidelity envelope that sat on his kitchen counter between the toaster and the jar of pens he never used. Fourteen times pacing between the bathroom and the kitchen, which was roughly eleven steps and approximately the distance Simone had said a small stage would give him. The first five times he'd read from the pages she'd printed out and annotated in her small, exact handwriting — *pause here*, *this is where they laugh*, *don't rush the transition*. The next five from memory, checking the pages only twice. The last four he hadn't needed the pages at all. Twenty-five minutes of material, organized into three sections with callbacks and what Simone called a "button" at the end, which was comedy terminology for a closing line that left the audience feeling finished.

Doug could deliver all twenty-five minutes to his refrigerator with confidence.

The refrigerator had never been to an afternoon session on pharmaceutical sales incentives. The refrigerator had never consumed four drinks at an open bar. The refrigerator had not come to the keynote expecting to see the guy from the wedding toast video, the one the internet had declared a comic genius, the one eighty million people had watched and decided was saying something important about fatherhood or marriage or American masculinity or whatever the latest thinkpiece had settled on. The refrigerator was a 2019 Kenmore with a dent on the lower left panel where Doug had hit it with a suitcase, and it had been an excellent audience.

Layla had called the night before. "Okay, so, quick rundown. It's Astelline Pharmaceuticals, they do like anti-inflammatories or something, I don't know, it doesn't matter. Regional sales conference, about four hundred people, they've been in sessions all day and they want to be entertained. You're the after-dinner keynote. They've seen the video, they love you, they're primed. Thirty minutes, in and out. Fifteen K. You're gonna be great."

"What if I'm not great," Doug had said.

"Then you're still getting paid fifteen thousand dollars, babe. Think about that."

Doug had thought about it. Fifteen thousand dollars for thirty minutes. He made sixty-one thousand a year before taxes at Mutual Fidelity, which came to $29.33 an hour if you divided by the standard two-thousand-and-eighty-hour work year, which Doug had once done on a slow Tuesday. Fifteen thousand dollars for thirty minutes was five hundred dollars a minute. It was $8.33 per second. Every second he stood on that stage, whether he was funny or silent or dying in front of four hundred strangers, was worth what his turkey club at the sandwich place cost. This did not make him less nervous. It made him nervous and aware that his nervousness was expensive.

He drove to Indianapolis on a Thursday afternoon in late April, two and a half hours on I-70 through landscape that transitioned from Ohio's version of flat to Indiana's version of the same flat with different highway signs. He'd left at three, telling Janet he had a personal appointment, which was technically true in the way that all insurance language was technically true — the statement was accurate and the picture it painted was incomplete. He listened to the radio for the first hour and turned it off because every song either had too much energy or reminded him of something and he couldn't afford either.

The Indianapolis Convention Center was a building that looked like it had been designed by a committee whose only instruction was "large." Doug parked in a garage that charged eighteen dollars, which was not unreasonable for downtown parking but which he noted because he noted the cost of things. It was how his mind organized the world. Some people noticed architecture or other human beings. Doug noticed that the parking garage had a rate structure that charged by the half-hour for the first three hours and a flat fee after, and that the flat fee was a worse deal if you were staying less than four and a half hours, which he intended to.

Inside, the convention center smelled like carpet cleaner and the particular staleness of air that had been circulated through the same building for three days of corporate programming. Banners for Astelline Pharmaceuticals hung from the ceiling — a teal logo, a tagline he couldn't read from this distance, and a photo of a woman smiling while doing yoga, which he assumed had something to do with the anti-inflammatory product line. People in lanyards moved through the hallways with the purposeful drift of the professionally conferenced, carrying tote bags filled with branded items they would never use.

A woman met him at the backstage entrance. Her name was Denise. She was the events coordinator, and she had a clipboard, a headset looped around her neck, and a blister on her left heel that she was trying not to favor. Doug could tell because she shifted her weight to the right every few seconds and winced slightly when she forgot about it. She walked him down a corridor with exposed pipe runs along the ceiling and gray industrial carpet that was a different, worse gray than the carpet in the public areas.

"So we've got you on at eight," Denise said. "Dinner wraps around seven-thirty, then there's a little bit of remarks from our VP of sales — Brian, he'll be quick, he's been told to be quick — and then he introduces you. Sound good?"

"Sure," Doug said.

"The video screens will have your name up. You want any slides or anything?"

"No."

"Great. There's water on the podium and we've got a handheld mic or a lavalier, whatever you prefer."

"Handheld," Doug said, because Simone had told him to use a handheld. She'd said it gave you something to do with one of your hands.

"Perfect." Denise checked her clipboard, flipping to a page she'd already looked at. "Is there anything you need? Green room's got some snacks, water, sodas. I think there's a fruit plate that's been sitting there since this morning, so maybe skip the fruit plate."

"I'm fine," Doug said.

"Okay great. I'll come get you about five minutes before." She paused. Her expression shifted into something less professional and more person-shaped. "My daughter watches your video like once a week. She's fourteen. She thinks you're hilarious."

Doug didn't know what to say to this. "That's nice of her."

"She made her stepdad watch it and he didn't think it was that funny, which she took as a personal attack on her judgment." Denise smiled in the way people smile when they're telling you about their family and don't have time to tell you about their family. "Anyway. Five minutes before. I'll come grab you."

Doug sat in the green room, which was not green but beige, and which was not a room so much as a partitioned section of backstage with a folding table, four chairs, a mirror ringed with bulbs of which three were dead, and the fruit plate Denise had warned him about. The grapes had moved through ripe and past resigned into something approaching geological. He didn't touch them.

He could hear the ballroom through the wall. Four hundred people, their collective sound arriving as a generalized hum — the same frequency as any crowd in any large room, many conversations producing a single indistinct noise. It was like standing next to a machine he couldn't see. He tried to pick out individual voices and couldn't. They were all one thing. In forty minutes he would stand in front of that thing and try to be funny, and it would either laugh or it would produce the other sound, the one that was patience disguised as silence.

He took the pages out of his jacket pocket. The material. Twenty-five minutes, printed on three sheets in twelve-point type because Simone had told him he shouldn't need to squint. He looked at the first line. He knew the first line. He knew every line. The problem was not the lines.

His phone buzzed. A text from Simone: *Don't rush the opening. Let the room come to you.*

He typed: *OK.*

A second text: *You know this material. Trust it.*

He put the phone facedown on the table. Through the wall, the crowd hummed. Someone laughed — a big laugh that carried through the partition and arrived in the green room as a muffled bark. It was the sound of someone being funny in front of those four hundred people, and that someone was Brian the VP of sales delivering his pre-dinner remarks, or someone at a table telling a story about their regional numbers, or a man doing an impression of his boss, or any of the thousands of small human interactions that produced laughter without effort or preparation or fourteen rehearsals between a bathroom and a kitchen.

Doug looked at the fruit plate. He picked up a grape and squeezed it between his thumb and forefinger. It did not give.

---

Brian, the VP of sales, turned out to be a large man with the energy of someone who had been told to keep it brief and resented the instruction. He wore a suit jacket over a polo shirt, which Doug noticed because it was a choice that committed fully to neither formality nor casualness and achieved a third thing that was worse than both. Brian did six minutes of remarks about regional performance metrics and market penetration trends and then pivoted to the introduction with the practiced ease of a man reading from a teleprompter while pretending he wasn't.

"So, uh, we've got a really special treat tonight," Brian said, and Doug, standing behind the stage-left partition, could hear the speech through a monitor speaker mounted on the wall. "I think most of you have probably seen the video — I mean, who hasn't, right? Eighty million views, the whole thing. And I just wanna say, as someone who gave a best man speech at my brother's wedding that, uh, did NOT go viral — I've got nothing but respect." Laughter. Brian was getting more laughs in his introduction than Doug was going to get in his entire set, and Doug knew this the way he knew a claim was going to be denied before he finished reading the documentation — the evidence was already organized, he just hadn't written it up yet.

"Please welcome, the man, the legend, the guy your wife has been showing you on her phone — Doug Bridge!"

Doug walked out.

The applause was genuine and warm, the sound of four hundred people who had already decided they liked him. The lights were brighter than the green room or his apartment or anywhere he had ever practiced, and they turned the audience into a dark, applauding mass with individual faces visible only in the first three rows. The mic was heavier than the one Simone had borrowed for their sessions at his kitchen table. It was warm from Brian's hand. Doug held it and walked to the center of the stage and the applause faded and the room waited.

"Thank you," Doug said. "Thanks for, uh. Thanks."

Good start.

He looked at the audience. Front row: a table of eight, conference badges still on, water glasses and wine glasses in various states of empty. A man with a red tie who was already smiling, committed to the evening. A woman with reading glasses pushed up on her forehead who looked ready to laugh. These were friendly faces. Doug focused on the man in the red tie and tried to speak to him, the way Simone had suggested — find one person and talk to them.

"So Layla — that's my manager, which is a sentence I still can't believe I'm saying — she told me this was a pharmaceutical sales conference." Pause. The room waited. "And I work in insurance. Which means we're both in the business of telling people why the thing they thought was covered isn't actually covered."

A laugh. Not a big laugh. A recognition laugh — the sound of people who got the reference and appreciated the effort without being surprised by any of it. It was fine. It was what Simone had said a first joke should do: establish his presence, signal to the room what kind of speaker he was going to be. The problem was that the room had already decided what kind of speaker he was going to be, and it wasn't this.

He moved to the second bit. This was about risk assessment — how insurance adjusters and salespeople both spend their careers calculating the odds that something will go wrong, except salespeople bet it won't and adjusters bet it will, and this fundamental difference in outlook was also a pretty good description of his first marriage.

Nothing.

Not hostile nothing. Not awkward nothing. Just the sound of four hundred people waiting for the thing they came for, which was not this. The man with the red tie was still smiling, but it was the same smile, held in place like a light fixture. The woman with the reading glasses had taken them off her forehead and put them on, which meant she was now looking at Doug with the clarity of corrected vision, and Doug was not improved by clarity.

He could feel it. The room deciding. It wasn't sudden — it was like watching a claim file accumulate documentation, each piece of evidence building toward the same conclusion. The audience was polite. They had been flown here, or driven, and their company had paid for the dinner and the drinks and the conference and the keynote entertainment. They were disposed to enjoy themselves. They wanted him to be good. Doug could feel them wanting it, and their wanting was worse than hostility because it meant they were going to keep trying, and he was going to keep failing, and both sides were going to pretend none of this was happening for as long as the social contract held.

He started the third section. This was the one about claims — specific claims he'd processed, the absurdity of the situations, the way people's small disasters revealed how they lived. Simone had told him this was his strongest material. Sitting across his kitchen table, she had laughed at these stories. She'd written notes in the margins. She had said, "That's good. That's the real thing."

Doug told the story about the man in Westerville whose above-ground pool wall had split open and flooded his neighbor's yard. He described the damage assessment, the coverage question, the way the neighbor's homeowner's policy covered weather events but not what the industry classified as "third-party structural failure." He was giving them the setup. The button — that was Simone's word for the payoff, the closing beat — was about how the pool owner and the neighbor had been friends for eleven years and the friendship ended not over the flood itself but over the question of whose insurance company should pay for the rosebushes.

He delivered the button. A few people laughed. A cluster at a table in the middle made a sound that was generous. But a woman in the third row — not the woman with the reading glasses, a different woman, younger, blazer, conference lanyard — looked down at her phone. She didn't pick it up. She just glanced at it, the way you glance at a door when you're thinking about leaving a room. It lasted one second. Doug saw it and it was louder than anything else happening.

He was rushing now. He could hear himself — the sentences coming faster, the pauses collapsing, the space Simone had built into the material caving in as Doug tried to get through it before the room finished deciding about him. He skipped the section about depreciation. It was his bridge from the claims material into the personal material, and without it the transition was abrupt, a hard turn from insurance stories into stuff about his apartment, and he heard the abruptness as the words came out but couldn't fix it because the words were already happening and they were the right words in the wrong order with no room to breathe.

A man near the back of the ballroom coughed. It was a real cough, a throat-clearing — the man probably had a tickle from the convention center's three-day accumulation of recycled air and needed to cough and coughed. But in the room, in the gap between Doug's sentences, the cough expanded to fill the space and became the loudest sound in the ballroom. Doug heard it the way he heard water damage behind drywall — the small specific signal that told you the problem was bigger than what was visible.

He tried the bit about his apartment. The cereal dinners. The Ikea bookshelf. He'd rehearsed this section and it had sounded fine in his kitchen, but "fine in rehearsal" was a phrase that meant nothing, like "pre-existing condition" or "adequate coverage" — technically descriptive and practically useless. The audience gave him something on the cereal line. Not a laugh. A sound. A collective half-exhalation that acknowledged what he'd said and signaled that they were still there, still trying, still willing to give him the benefit of whatever this was. It was generosity and it felt like charity and Doug could not tell the difference.

He repeated a line. He didn't intend to — he'd already said it in the claims section and now it came out again in the apartment section, the same phrase about "assessing the damage," and he heard it leave his mouth and recognized the repetition and the recognition sat in his chest like a piece of food that hadn't gone down properly. The audience didn't react. They may not have noticed. Or they had noticed and were being kind about it, which was functionally the same as not noticing except worse.

He was at nineteen minutes. He had six minutes of material left. He had the section about Roy, which Simone had liked, and the closer, which was structured to end on a coverage-gap metaphor that was supposed to land as both funny and genuine and give the audience the sense of completion that a performance was supposed to provide.

Doug looked at the room. The room looked back. The man in the red tie had stopped smiling. The woman with the reading glasses had her arms crossed, not in hostility but in the way people cross their arms when they've settled into waiting. The phone woman had put her phone away, which might have been encouraging except that putting it away was a commitment to sit through the rest, not a sign of engagement. The cougher had not coughed again. The ballroom was suspended in the particular stillness of an audience that wanted the thing to be over without wanting to be the one to end it.

"I think that's probably enough," Doug said.

Someone in the second row started clapping. The sound spread through the room the way all polite applause spreads — not a wave but a series of individual decisions to participate in the fiction that the performance had concluded on its own terms. Within three seconds it was a full room of applause. It was respectful. It was sustained for approximately the right duration. It was the exact volume and length appropriate for a keynote speaker who had done his best and fallen short, and every person in the room understood this and Doug understood they understood it and they knew he knew and the applause continued anyway because that was what you did. It was the social contract holding.

Doug said, "Thank you," and walked offstage and the lights came up and four hundred pharmaceutical sales representatives began the complicated process of deciding whether to feel sorry for him or irritated that the entertainment hadn't delivered or generous enough to pretend it had been fine.

Backstage, Denise was waiting with a water bottle. "Great job," she said, and the speed with which she said it — quickly, brightly, the verdict composed before the evidence was in — told Doug everything.

"Thanks," Doug said.

"Brian's gonna do a couple minutes of wrap-up and then we're done for the night. There's a shuttle to the hotel at nine-fifteen if you need it."

Doug did not need the shuttle. He needed to be in his car, alone, driving east on I-70 toward Columbus, but it was nearly nine and he had a room at the Holiday Inn Express on West Washington Street and a pizza he'd ordered on his phone during Brian's introduction, which meant the pizza would arrive before he did, which meant it would be sitting in a hallway getting cold, and he thought about this with the specific focus of a man who could not think about what had just happened and so thought about pizza instead.

---

The Holiday Inn Express on West Washington Street was four stories of beige siding with a parking lot that was three-quarters empty. Doug's room was on the third floor. The hallway smelled like the hallway of every hotel he'd ever been in, which was not many — he was not a person who stayed in hotels — but the smell was universal: cleaning solution, carpet adhesive, and the faint residual presence of every person who had ever walked this corridor with a keycard and a particular reason for not being home.

The pizza was outside his door, in a box on the carpet. He brought it in. It was from a place called Tony's Original that he'd found on his phone while Brian was talking about Q2 sales targets. The pizza was cold. Not room temperature — cold in the way that food gets when it sits on thin carpet in a hotel hallway in late April in Indiana. The cheese had congealed into a single continuous surface, and the crust had stiffened into something between bread and the box it came in. Doug ate two slices standing at the desk, which was bolted to the wall, because there was nowhere else to stand that wasn't the bed or the bathroom. The pizza was not good. It was adequate, in the way Roy used that word — a classification that contained neither complaint nor praise but simply placed the thing in its correct category and moved on.

The room had a king bed, a desk built into the wall, a television mounted at an angle that suggested someone had once tried to steal a previous one, and a bedspread with a pattern. Doug looked at the pattern. Overlapping diamonds in brown and a color that the manufacturer had probably called "sage" on the order form but which was the color of nothing, or the color of a decision made in a conference room by people whose job it was to select bedspread patterns for three hundred and seventy Holiday Inn Express locations and whose mandate was: give offense to no one. The pattern achieved this. It was inoffensive the way polite applause was inoffensive — it acknowledged the situation without responding to it. Doug sat on the bed and the mattress received his weight with the precise lack of resistance that institutional mattresses were engineered to provide. Not comfort. Not discomfort. The mattress equivalent of "fine."

He sat there. The bedspread's sage diamonds arranged themselves around his thighs. The clock on the nightstand said 9:47. There was a lamp bolted to the nightstand, bolted because someone at Holiday Inn corporate had determined that guests could not be trusted with portable lamps, and this struck Doug as a reasonable assessment of human behavior and also the kind of decision he would have made if he were in charge of assessing the risk profile of hotel guests, which, in a way, was not that different from what he did for a living.

In Columbus, Roy would be getting ready for bed. Roy went to bed at 9:45 every night, a fact Doug knew because Roy had mentioned it once, without elaboration or justification, the way Roy mentioned everything — as information, not as an opening for discussion.

Doug took out his phone and called Roy. It rang four times.

"Hello."

"It's Doug."

"I know. Your name comes up."

"Right." Doug paused. "Did I wake you up?"

"No. I was reading. There's a book about the Dust Bowl that's better than you'd expect."

"Huh."

"How'd it go?"

Doug looked at the bedspread. He looked at the pizza box on the desk. He looked at the lamp bolted to the nightstand and the television bolted to the wall and the desk bolted to the wall and the whole room, which was bolted to the earth and the earth was bolted to nothing and everything was, when you assessed it properly, attached to something that was itself barely holding.

"Bad," he said.

"Bad how?"

"Bad like everyone was polite about it after."

Roy was quiet for a moment. Doug could hear something in the background — a page turning, or maybe just Roy's house being the particular kind of quiet that Roy's house was, which was the quiet of a place where one person lived in a way that required very little of the space.

"Polite is worse than bad," Roy said.

"Yeah."

"You know where you are with bad."

Doug didn't say anything.

"I stopped at nineteen minutes," he said, after a while. "The set was twenty-five. I got to nineteen and told them that was probably enough."

"Was it?"

"Was it enough? I don't know. It was enough for me. I don't think they would've fought for six more minutes of what I was giving them."

"Then nineteen was the right number."

Doug lay back on the bed. The pillow was flat in the way hotel pillows are flat — compressed by the sequential weight of strangers into a shape that accommodated everyone and suited no one. He stared at the ceiling. It had one of those textured surfaces, tiny bumps that looked like a frozen sea viewed from altitude. A water stain in the far corner, brown at the edges, roughly circular. Doug assessed it reflexively: old damage, treated but not properly remediated. No active leak. Someone had painted over it and called it done. The most common mistake in residential damage repair — cosmetic treatment applied to structural failure. You couldn't see the problem anymore, so the problem was solved. Except it wasn't.

"I got paid fifteen thousand dollars tonight," Doug said.

"For nineteen minutes."

"The contract was for thirty. I did nineteen."

"That's still a lot of money for nineteen minutes."

"Seven hundred and eighty-nine dollars a minute. I did the math in the parking garage."

"Of course you did."

They were quiet for a few seconds. Doug could hear Roy breathing, steady and unhurried, the way Roy did everything.

"Are you driving back tonight?"

"No. I've got a hotel room. Holiday Inn."

"How is it?"

Doug looked at the room. The bolted lamp. The bolted television. The desk that was part of the wall. The pizza box with its grease-translucent lid. The bedspread pattern that had surrendered during its own design process.

"It's a Holiday Inn in Indianapolis," Doug said.

"Right."

They sat on the phone. The silence between them was the kind of silence they shared at their adjacent desks at Mutual Fidelity when neither of them had anything to say and neither felt the obligation to produce something — the silence of two people who had been in proximity long enough that the absence of conversation was a form of conversation. It was adequate.

"Roy."

"Yeah."

"The material doesn't work. Simone and I put together twenty-five minutes, and it's structured, and it's got callbacks and a closer that's supposed to land. And I can do it for my kitchen. I've done it fourteen times for my kitchen. But it's not — it isn't the thing."

"What thing?"

"The thing from the video. The thing they came for. They came to see the guy from the toast and I can't — " Doug stopped. He picked up the cold pizza crust from the nightstand where he'd set it and looked at it and put it down again. "I can't do it on purpose. The video was — I was drunk. I was angry about Brendan's toast being so goddamn perfect and I said things I never meant to say out loud. You can't rehearse that. You can't put it in a set list."

"No."

"Simone knows. She knows the material isn't the real thing. But she doesn't know what else to try, and I don't know what else to try, and tonight I stood up in front of four hundred people and did the material and it was like — " Doug looked at the ceiling. The water stain. The cosmetic repair. "It was like filing a claim you know is going to be denied. All the documentation is right, and the forms are filled out, and the loss is real, and you submit it and you know it's coming back rejected because the underlying thing isn't covered. The structure is sound and the answer is still no."

"You should get some sleep," Roy said.

"Yeah."

"Drive back in the morning. Don't drive tonight."

"I won't."

A pause. Then Roy said, "Doug."

"Yeah."

"Nineteen minutes is nineteen more than most people manage."

Doug wasn't sure if this was comfort or observation or just Roy's way of placing a fact in its correct location before closing the file. With Roy, the distinction was often academic.

"Good night, Roy."

"Night."

Doug hung up and set his phone on the nightstand, next to the bolted lamp. He took off his shoes and his dress shirt and lay on the bedspread in his undershirt and pants. He did not get under the covers. Getting under the covers would be a concession that this was where he was sleeping tonight — a Holiday Inn Express in Indianapolis with a cold pizza and a stain on the ceiling and the specific residue of having stood in front of four hundred people and been not enough — and he was not ready to concede it even though it was true and the evidence was documented and the claim, such as it was, had been filed.

He stared at the ceiling and thought about the performance. Not the whole thing — he couldn't hold the whole thing in his head at once, the way you couldn't look at all the damage in a flooded house simultaneously and instead had to move room to room, assessing each space on its own terms. He thought about the woman looking at her phone. One second. One glance downward. He thought about the cough that filled the silence like water finding the low point. He thought about the polite applause spreading through the ballroom — each person individually reaching the same conclusion and documenting it with their hands. He thought about Brian in his suit-jacket-and-polo saying *the man, the legend* to a room that wanted a legend and got a man.

He thought about the toast. He hadn't watched the video in weeks. He'd stopped because each viewing made it less like something that had happened to him and more like something that had happened to a different person who shared his face and his voice and his specific complaints about his ex-wife's new husband's toast. The drunk man in the video had no idea he was being watched. That was the whole thing. That was what made it work and what made it unrepeatable — the total absence of awareness, the complete vacancy of performance. The man in the video was saying true things because the whiskey had dissolved the membrane between what he thought and what he said, and there was no audience in his mind, just the room and the people and the things he'd carried for years that suddenly had nowhere to be except out loud. You couldn't get there sober. You couldn't get there by trying. You couldn't get there by standing between a bathroom and a kitchen fourteen times and making hash marks on an envelope.

Doug had tried to be the man in the video. For nineteen minutes, in front of four hundred people who wanted him to be that man, he had tried, and the trying was the thing that killed it. The toast had worked because it hadn't known it was a performance. The keynote had failed because it couldn't be anything else.

He lay on the bedspread and the sage diamonds pressed into the backs of his arms. The room was quiet. Outside the window, Indianapolis was doing whatever Indianapolis did at ten o'clock on a Thursday night in late April. Doug could hear traffic, faintly, and the air-conditioning unit cycling on with a rattle in its housing that suggested a maintenance issue someone had decided wasn't worth fixing. He knew the sound. He'd heard it a hundred times in claim inspections — the small mechanical complaint that would become a large mechanical complaint if ignored long enough, but which, for now, was just the sound of a system doing its job imperfectly, which was the sound of most systems, which was the sound of everything.

The fifteen thousand dollars would arrive as a direct deposit within fourteen business days, per the contract he'd read in full because he was a man who read contracts. It would land in his checking account alongside his Mutual Fidelity paychecks and look like any other money, because money was money, and fifteen thousand dollars earned standing in front of strangers in a ballroom being politely endured spent the same as fifteen thousand dollars earned sitting at a desk processing claims, which was both a comfort and not.

He closed his eyes. The television remote was under his hip, pressing its buttons into the meat of his thigh. He did not move it. The pizza box sat on the bolted desk. His dress shirt was draped over the bolted chair. His shoes were on the floor, placed neatly next to each other because Doug placed his shoes neatly even in rooms he would leave in the morning and never return to. This was not a virtue. It was a habit. It was who he was, and who he was had not been enough tonight, and the room held this fact the way rooms hold all the facts that happen inside them — without opinion, without comfort, without assessment.

Down the hall, the ice machine dispensed a load into its bin. The sound was mechanical and sudden — a clatter of cubes falling into a steel basin — and then the compressor hummed and the machine settled and the hallway was quiet again. No one had requested the ice. No one was coming for it. The machine produced it anyway, on a timer, through the night, whether anyone needed it or not.
