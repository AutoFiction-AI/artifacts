# Chapter 14

The rental car was a gray Hyundai Elantra that smelled like the previous renter's choices — a pine air freshener clipped to the vent, dried coffee in the cupholder, the residue of a fast food meal that had happened somewhere between here and wherever that person had been going. Doug adjusted the mirrors, connected his phone to the auxiliary cable even though he had nothing to play, and pulled onto I-71 north toward Cleveland.

Layla had booked the tour in a single phone call while Doug was on his lunch break. Three cities, three comedy clubs, three nights. "Warm-ups," she called them. "Before the big show." She said it the way his supervisor Janet said "touching base," which meant something was about to become his problem. The Netflix taping was in two weeks. Doug did not feel warm.

The highway was flat and gray and familiar — he'd driven this route to visit Kelsey when she first moved to Cleveland, before the visits became less frequent, then occasional, then something they planned over text three weeks in advance. She lived twenty minutes from the comedy club. He had not told her he was coming.

He checked into a Holiday Inn off I-77 at four-thirty. The room was what every Holiday Inn room was: a bed, a desk, a chair that swiveled, a television mounted at an angle that suggested someone had once tried to steal it, and a window that looked onto the parking lot. The bedspread was a shade of maroon that existed only in hotel rooms. Doug sat on it and opened his laptop.

For the past two weeks, he had been watching comedians on YouTube. He watched John Mulaney tell a story about a party and analyzed the rhythm: setup, misdirection, commitment to the bit, callback. He watched Hasan Minhaj shift from funny to political to personal and tracked the transitions. He watched old Carlin specials — Carlin was dead and therefore couldn't be disappointed in what Doug was about to do with his legacy. He took notes. Not comedy notes — structural notes. He wrote things like "pause after second beat — 1.5 sec" and "personal detail + absurd extension = laugh" as if comedy were a claims assessment with measurable outputs.

The club was called Wiley's. It sat in a strip mall between a Great Clips and a vape shop, and the marquee out front read THURSDAY NIGHT SHOWCASE and, beneath it, in smaller letters, FEATURING VIRAL SENSATION DOUG BRIDGE. Doug looked at the words and thought about how "sensation" was also what you felt right before you threw up.

Inside, the room held about a hundred and twenty people, though tonight it was closer to ninety. The stage was low — maybe ten inches off the floor — with a single mic stand and a stool. The lighting was overhead fluorescent, the kind that made everyone look slightly worse than they actually were. A hand-painted sign on the back wall said LAUGH.

The owner, a man named Gerry who had a gray ponytail and a Cavs T-shirt from at least the LeBron era, walked Doug to the back, which was a storage room with a sink and a mirror that had lost most of its silvering. "Good crowd tonight," Gerry said. "We sold forty-two tickets off your name alone, which is — yeah, that's good for a Thursday." Gerry had a bad knee he favored when he walked, and he talked about it the way people with chronic pain do — like the knee was a third person in the conversation with its own schedule and opinions. He'd been running comedy rooms for eighteen years. His ex-wife had been a comic, he said, "back when that meant something different." He didn't explain what different meant.

Doug thanked him and closed the door and looked at himself in the half-mirror. He was wearing a button-down, untucked, and jeans. He had considered what to wear for an embarrassing amount of time. He had settled on this because it was what a comedian would wear — casual, approachable, the costume of someone who just wandered up here. The costume was complete.

He walked onto the stage and took the microphone from the stand the way he'd seen Mulaney do it — a casual lift, as if the mic just happened to be there and he just happened to want it. He looked at the audience. They looked back.

"So," he said. And he paused. One beat. Two. The way Carlin paused. The way a comedian pauses when he's about to say something.

He had nothing to say after the pause. The pause was the entire plan.

He launched into the material, but not his material — a version of it filtered through the comedians he'd studied. He set up the insurance bit with a Mulaney-style digression about being a person who reads every contract. He tried a Minhaj-style shift from the funny to the personal, pivoting from a claims dispute to a brief aside about his divorce that he delivered with practiced casualness that was audible in every syllable. He tried crowd callbacks. He tried working the pauses. He tried the thing where you pretend to lose your thought and then find it, which Carlin did because he was George Carlin and which Doug did because he had watched a man do it on a screen in a Holiday Inn and thought it looked learnable.

None of it was his. It was technically complete — the jokes were there, the callbacks were there, the pauses were in the right places — and the room could feel it. The laughs were wrong. Not absent — wrong. People laughed at his setups instead of his punchlines. They laughed at his pauses, which were supposed to build tension but instead broadcast that he was waiting for something to happen. A man near the bar laughed once, loudly, and then stopped, as if he'd been caught.

Doug did the crowd work he'd rehearsed. "Where are you from?" he asked a couple in the front row. "Parma," the man said. He was wearing a Cleveland Guardians jersey with a mustard stain on the sleeve, and his wife or girlfriend had her hand on his knee in a way that suggested she was either comforting him or preventing him from leaving. Doug had prepared a response about suburbs. He forgot it. "Nice," he said. "Good area."

The room was not hostile. It was patient. It was waiting for the person they'd seen in the video, and instead they were getting a man in a costume, performing the movements of comedy the way someone who'd learned ballroom dancing from a diagram performs ballroom dancing. The feet were in the right places. The music was playing. Nothing was happening.

Eighteen minutes in, a woman near the back shouted, "Do the toast!"

She had curly red hair and a beer she'd been nursing since before Doug came on, and she'd been checking her phone with the resigned frequency of someone waiting for a text that was not going to arrive. She wasn't being mean. She was being honest, which was worse.

The room contracted. Doug could feel it — every person in the audience now unified in wanting the same thing. They hadn't come for bits. They'd come for seven minutes of a drunk man being too honest in front of his daughter's wedding guests, and what they were getting instead was a pantomime.

"I can't," Doug said. "I'm sober."

The room laughed. It was sudden, unguarded — the laugh that happens before anyone decides whether to laugh. Doug did not know it was funny. He had stated a fact. The toast had required eight whiskeys, a catastrophically good toast from Brendan, and twenty years of things he'd never said out loud. What he had tonight was a rental car and a Holiday Inn and a set list he'd annotated like a claims file. He could not do the toast for the same reason a house fire does not happen on schedule.

He did twelve more minutes. They were worse than the first eighteen. He ended by saying "Thank you" to a room that was already reaching for coats and phones, and he walked off past Gerry, who said, "Good energy up there," which was a lie delivered with the professional ease of a man who had told it several thousand times.

In the parking lot, the air was warm for late May. Doug sat in the Elantra and called Simone.

"How was it?"

"Bad."

"Bad how?"

"I tried to do it like a comedian."

A pause. He could hear her doing something — running water, maybe doing dishes. "Yeah," she said. "Don't do that."

"I know."

"What worked?"

"Nothing."

"Nothing at all?"

He thought about the woman and the laugh. "Someone asked me to do the toast and I said I couldn't because I was sober. People laughed."

"That's because it was true."

"It was just a fact."

"Yeah," Simone said. "That's the same thing."

Doug hung up and drove back to the Holiday Inn. He lay on the maroon bedspread and stared at the ceiling and thought about calling Kelsey, who was twenty minutes north of here, asleep next to Tyler in the apartment Doug had helped them move into nine months ago. He did not call her. He did not have anything to say that she would want to hear at eleven-thirty on a Thursday, and the things he might want to say — something about the spreadsheet, something about the toast, something about being in her city and failing at the thing the city thought he was good at — required a kind of honesty he could apparently only access drunk, and he was sober, and he was in a Holiday Inn room that smelled like industrial cleaner and someone else's sleep. He fell asleep with his shoes on, which was becoming a pattern.

---

The drive to Pittsburgh was two and a half hours through northeastern Ohio and across the western edge of Pennsylvania, where the flat farmland wrinkled into hills and the highway curved in ways that required attention. Doug stopped for gas at a Sheetz outside Youngstown. The coffee cost a dollar eighty-nine and tasted how truck stops smelled — burnt, industrial, somehow fine if you didn't think about it. He drank it in the parking lot and watched a man in a hi-vis vest fill up a pickup while arguing on the phone about a dog, or a fence, or both.

Pittsburgh was more vertical than Cleveland, which was more vertical than Columbus, which was essentially flat. The hotel was another Holiday Inn, this one near the airport, and the room was identical to the Cleveland room except that the bedspread was blue and the window faced a different parking lot. Doug hung his shirt in the closet and lay on the bed and stared at a water stain on the ceiling that had the shape of something that refused to be anything recognizable.

He thought about the Cleveland performance. He thought about what Simone had said — that the truth and the fact were the same thing, for him. He understood this and could not use it. Understanding that you were funny when you were just being accurate was different from being able to be accurate on stage. The stage installed an expectation, and the expectation sealed the space between Doug and whatever it was that had happened at the wedding.

The club was called The Comic Attic, which was a lie — it was in a basement, beneath a bar called O'Reilly's, down a narrow staircase that smelled like spilled Yuengling and industrial cleaner. The room held about ninety people. Tonight there were closer to seventy-five. The stage was slightly higher than Cleveland's — maybe eighteen inches — and the extra eight inches shouldn't have mattered, but they did. Doug felt exposed up there. The MC, a young woman named Diane who had a sleeve tattoo and a laugh that carried through walls, introduced him as "the man who broke the internet at his daughter's wedding — Doug Bridge!"

The applause was genuine. They wanted him. Some of them had paid specifically for this, had told someone on Thursday that they had plans on Friday night, had driven to this basement bar in Pittsburgh because an algorithm or a friend or their own curiosity had brought them here. Doug felt the weight of their expectation like the air before a storm.

He took the mic. He looked at the room. He opened his mouth.

Nothing came.

It was not nerves, and it was not a blank. His mind did not go empty — it went absent, as if the part of him that produced words had left through a door he hadn't known was there. He could see the audience. He could feel the microphone, which was warm from Diane's hand. He could hear the bar above them, muffled by the ceiling — the bass thump of a jukebox playing something he couldn't identify, the distant crash of someone doing something wrong with a pint glass. His body was on the stage. The rest of him was somewhere else.

One second. Three. Five. The audience shifted. Seven seconds. A man in the front row uncrossed his arms. Nine seconds. A woman near the wall looked at the person beside her. Eleven seconds. Someone coughed, and the cough was not a cough — it was the sound a room makes when it needs someone to do something.

"I had material," Doug said. His voice sounded like it was coming from the ceiling. "I practiced it in the car. In the hotel. I said the words out loud in a Holiday Inn bathroom and they were there. They were real words. I had them."

He paused. This pause was different from the Carlin pause in Cleveland. This was the space where the words should have been.

He started the set. It came out damaged — the bits were present but the connections between them were gone, and Doug moved through them the way you walk through a house after a break-in, checking what was still where you'd left it. The insurance bit was there. The callback about depreciation was there. The transition to the cereal section was missing, so he jumped, and the audience felt the gap and tightened.

Twelve minutes in, he told the pool story.

He didn't plan to. He'd reached the end of what he could remember and the pool was just there — the above-ground pool in Westerville, the wall that split, the water that went everywhere, the neighbor's seventeen hybrid tea roses documented with receipts at forty-two hundred dollars, the coverage gap that nobody's policy addressed. He told it the way he'd told it at his kitchen table when Simone first asked him to be funny — not as a bit, not as material, but as a problem. A genuinely fascinating problem. A problem where a man's pool destroyed another man's roses and the insurance system, which existed to assign responsibility and compensate loss, could not determine whose fault beauty was.

The room leaned in. He could feel it — not laughter exactly, but attention. Attention that was one step from laughter and one step from something else. He was in it. For ninety seconds he was in it, and the room was with him, and the words came not from the set list but from wherever they came from when he wasn't trying, and someone in the third row laughed and the laugh spread and for ninety seconds Doug was the man from the video.

Then he tried to transition. He reached for the next bit — the one about replacement cost that Simone had helped him shape — and the reaching was the mistake. The reaching was the performance reinstalling itself. He could feel the room pull back, a tide going out, and the next six minutes were the debris left behind. He finished. The applause was better than Cleveland — real in places, polite in others, confused throughout.

Backstage, which was a broom closet with a folding chair, Doug sat and pressed his palms against his knees. His hands were dry. His knees were vibrating, a fine tremor he could feel through the fabric of his khakis but that probably wasn't visible. He thought about the ninety seconds. He thought about the eleven seconds of nothing before that. He thought about how the pool story had worked because he'd forgotten he was performing, and how forgetting you were performing was not something you could write into a set list.

He called Simone from the hotel.

"Better," he said.

"Better than Cleveland?"

"The pool story worked. For about ninety seconds."

"And then?"

"Then I tried to do the next bit and it died."

Simone's silence had a quality to it — not absence but attention. She was thinking, and her thinking was audible the way a room is audible when someone is listening hard. "You know what happened," she said. It was not a question.

"I know what happened. I don't know how to make it happen on purpose."

"Yeah," she said. "That's the problem."

---

The drive from Pittsburgh to Detroit was the longest leg — four and a half hours through Pennsylvania and across the narrow top of West Virginia and up through Ohio to Toledo and then south to the city. Doug drove it Saturday morning. The day was warm and the sun hit the windshield at an angle the visor couldn't fix. He stopped for gas outside Akron. The coffee tasted the same as the Youngstown coffee. He bought a gas station sandwich, unwrapped it, looked at it, and rewrapped it. He ate it twenty miles later because hunger was a simpler problem than whatever else was happening to him.

The highway was its own kind of room. I-80 through Pennsylvania was trees and hills and trucks and the same rest stop appearing every forty miles with the same food court and the same families standing in the parking lot staring at their phones with the exhausted solidarity of people who had been in a car too long and were not yet close enough to where they were going. I-75 through Ohio was flat again, the return to flatness after the hills, and Doug drove through it without the radio on, listening to tire noise and the Elantra's air conditioning, which had a rattle that surfaced at exactly sixty-seven miles per hour and disappeared at sixty-eight.

Layla called at noon.

"So how's it going? I heard Cleveland was a little — you know. Finding your legs."

"It was bad, Layla."

"It wasn't bad. Gerry said you had good energy."

"Gerry lies to comedians after they bomb. That's his whole job."

"Okay so it was a learning experience. What about Pittsburgh?"

"The pool story worked for ninety seconds."

"That's great! Ninety seconds is — that's a solid chunk. We can build on ninety seconds."

Doug thought about what Simone would say about building on ninety seconds. He was fairly certain she would say nothing, which would be worse than disagreement.

"I need to focus on tonight," he said.

"Totally. You've got this. Detroit's gonna love you." A pause. "The venue's great, by the way. It's called The Mark. It's got this whole — anyway. You've got this, babe."

He arrived in Detroit at three-thirty and checked into a Holiday Inn that was neither better nor worse than the other two. The bedspread was green. The parking lot had a view of another parking lot. He stood in the shower for a long time, not washing, just letting the water hit the back of his neck where the driving had knotted something he could press his fingers into but not undo.

The Mark was a real comedy club — not a strip mall or a bar basement, but a dedicated room with proper lighting, a real stage, real sound, tiered seating that climbed toward the back. It held about a hundred and fifty, and it was close to full. The MC was a local comic named Terrence who did five minutes of solid material about Detroit traffic that got real laughs from people who had been sitting in that traffic three hours ago. Terrence moved across the stage naturally, worked the room, made it look like something a person could decide to do. Doug watched from behind the curtain and felt the gap between Terrence's ease and his own situation, which was not a gap so much as a different country.

Doug walked on stage. The lights were better here — warm, focused, turning the audience into shapes and outlines beyond the first three rows. He started the set. Not the comedian costume from Cleveland, not the damaged recall from Pittsburgh — something closer to what he and Simone had built. The insurance bits. The cereal bit. He delivered it cleanly. It was fine. The audience was with him in the way an audience is with someone they've decided to give a chance, which was different from the way they were with Terrence, who they'd been with because they couldn't help it.

Three minutes in, a man in the second row started.

"Toast!"

He was maybe forty-five, heavy, wearing a Tigers cap and a polo shirt tucked into jeans — a formality decision that had gone wrong in both directions. He had a beer in each hand, one mostly full and one mostly empty, and the posture of a man who had been drinking since whatever had happened before this and intended to keep going after. He wasn't mean. Doug could see that. He was a man who had paid money to see a specific thing and was not seeing it, and he had decided, with the clarity that four or five beers provide, to ask for it directly.

"Toast! Toast! Toast!"

Doug looked at him. The room tensed. Some people laughed — the nervous laugh of an audience watching something that could go either way. Doug thought about what real comedians did with hecklers. He had watched YouTube videos of this too — Bill Burr dismantling a drunk guy with surgical precision, comics he'd studied at three in the morning thinking, I can learn this.

He could not learn this.

"Hey, man," Doug said. "I appreciate the enthusiasm."

It was the wrong thing to say, and he knew it as the words left. "I appreciate the enthusiasm" was not a comedian's line — it was a supervisor addressing a difficult employee, the register of someone who had processed enough workplace conflicts to know how to manage disruption without resolving it. The heckler blinked and then doubled down: "TOAST! Come on, man, do the TOAST!"

"I don't do the toast," Doug said. "That's not — I'm doing different material tonight."

"We don't want different material!" The man was grinning now. He wasn't angry. He was having the best time of anyone in the room, which was the problem. His enthusiasm had made him the show, and Doug was the straight man in someone else's bit.

"Sir," Doug started, and the "sir" was the second mistake, because comedians don't call hecklers "sir" — adjusters call claimants "sir." The professional register landed in the room like a form letter at a party.

"Sir! He called me sir!" The heckler turned to the woman next to him — his wife or date, who had the expression of someone revising the cost-benefit analysis of this evening in real time. She put her hand on his arm. He shrugged it off, gently but completely. "DO THE TOAST, DOUG!"

The room split. Half the audience was embarrassed for Doug. Half was watching the spectacle with the helpless attention of people watching a fender bender — they didn't want it to happen, but now that it was happening they wanted to see how it ended. Terrence, standing near the bar with his arms crossed, had the look of a man watching something unfold that he'd seen before and could not help with.

Doug gave up on the heckler. He turned back to the room and tried to find the material. It was gone. Not absent like Pittsburgh had been — chased out by the confrontation, the way a burst pipe clears a room faster than a fire alarm. He had the set list memorized and could not reach a single line of it.

He stood on stage in front of a hundred and forty people with no material and a drunk man who wanted a wedding toast and nothing in his head but the last two days.

So he talked about the drive.

He didn't decide to. It was just there — the four and a half hours from Pittsburgh, the gas station coffee that tasted the same in Youngstown and Akron and probably tasted the same in every gas station between the Atlantic Ocean and the Mississippi River. The sandwich he'd been afraid to eat and then ate anyway because hunger was simpler. The Holiday Inns with their color-coded bedspreads — maroon in Cleveland, blue in Pittsburgh, green here — as if someone in a corporate office had decided that the illusion of variety was worth manufacturing three different fabric dyes. The GPS voice that said "recalculating" with the patience of someone who had accepted that humans would never follow directions correctly. The rest stops that all had the same Cinnabon and the same families in the parking lot staring at their phones with the exhausted solidarity of people who were neither where they'd been nor where they were going.

He talked about the highway. About how I-80 through Pennsylvania was beautiful in a way nobody mentioned, because it was just the road you took to get somewhere else, and the beauty was accidental — the hills rolling green because the trees had just come in, and the light hitting the asphalt at four in the afternoon in a way that made you think about how much of the country you were never going to see because you were always on your way to do something in the next city that you couldn't do in the last one.

He talked about the loneliness of rest stops. How everyone at a rest stop was between places, and the rest stop itself was not a place — it was a pause in being somewhere, and the people there were all pausing at the same time without knowing each other, and all of them were going to get back in their cars and continue being alone in the direction they were already going.

He was not trying to be funny. He was describing what had happened to him in the last two days with the same precision he'd use for water damage in a basement — this is what happened, this is the extent, this is what was lost. The heckler had gone quiet. The heckler's wife or date had put her phone down. The room had gathered around Doug's voice, and Doug recognized what was happening because he'd felt it for ninety seconds in Pittsburgh — not laughter, but the attention you give to something true.

He talked for eight minutes. About the coffee. About the parking lots. About how every Holiday Inn in America smelled the same — industrial cleaner and carpet that had absorbed the sleep of ten thousand strangers — and how the sameness was supposed to be comforting but wasn't, because comfort that was identical everywhere was not comfort but a product, and what you actually wanted at midnight in a hotel room in a city where nobody knew you were there was not a product but a place that knew you were in it.

And then he tried to go back to the material.

He could feel himself doing it — the shift from talking to performing, from saying what was there to saying what was planned. He reached for the cereal bit, which was next on the set list, and the reaching killed it. The room loosened. The tension bled out. The last four minutes were the same polite attention that Cleveland had been, and when Doug said "Thank you, that's my time" and put the mic back in the stand, the applause was generous and confused — the applause of people who had seen something they didn't know what to do with, mixed with something they knew exactly what to do with, and the two things hadn't added up to a show.

---

He left Detroit at ten-forty, still in his stage clothes, the parking lot damp from a rain that had come and gone while he was inside. The Elantra's GPS said three hours and twenty-two minutes to Columbus. Doug pulled onto I-75 south and the highway was mostly empty — semis, a few sedans, the occasional minivan with someone asleep in the back seat. The headlights made a corridor of light and the rest was dark Ohio, flat and invisible.

He drove in silence for thirty miles. He thought about the eight minutes in Detroit. He thought about the ninety seconds in Pittsburgh. He thought about the zero seconds in Cleveland where anything he said was genuinely his own. Three cities, three audiences who had paid money to see a man from a video, and the video's man had appeared for a total of maybe ten minutes across three nights. The rest had been Doug performing an imitation of someone performing, which was two layers of falsehood stacked on top of a man who could only tell the truth by accident.

He passed a sign that said TOLEDO 47. He thought about how road signs told you the distance to the next city but not why you would want to go there.

He called Simone at eleven-twenty. She picked up on the second ring.

"How was Detroit?"

"A guy heckled me for the toast. I tried to handle it and made it worse. Then I talked about the drive for eight minutes and the room went quiet."

"Quiet like they were bored, or quiet like they were listening?"

"Like they were listening."

"And then?"

"Then I went back to the material and lost them."

The highway was dark. A semi passed him going north and the wake rocked the Elantra, a push of air he felt through the steering wheel.

"The material doesn't work," Doug said.

"I know."

He waited for more. Simone did not offer more. She let silences do what words couldn't, and usually that was the right instinct. Right now it felt like being left alone in a field.

"So what do I do?" he said. "The taping's in two weeks. I've got thirty-two minutes of material that dies every time I perform it and eight minutes of talking about gas station coffee that works for reasons I can't use."

"You understand the reasons."

"I don't."

"You do. You just don't like them."

He drove past a rest stop — a gas station, a Subway, a building with restrooms, all lit up for nobody. Open twenty-four hours for the ghosts of travelers who had already come through. He thought about what Simone meant. He understood. The gas station coffee worked because he wasn't trying to make it work. The pool story worked in Pittsburgh because he'd forgotten he was on stage. The toast worked because he was drunk and furious and had never intended to say any of it. Everything that worked was accidental, and everything that was planned was dead, and the Netflix taping — scheduled, scripted, cameras and a crew of nineteen at the Lincoln Theatre, a building he had never been inside — required him to do on purpose the thing he could only do by accident.

"I understand the reasons," he said.

"I know you do."

"I just can't use them."

Simone didn't say anything for a long time. Long enough that Doug checked the phone to make sure the call was still connected. The screen glowed in the dark car. Call duration: 4:17.

"Get home safe," she said. "We'll figure it out."

She hung up. Doug put the phone in the cupholder and drove south through dark Ohio. Findlay, then Lima, then somewhere south of Lima where the highway straightened and the land flattened and the dashboard clock read 1:15 a.m. He was tired in a way that was not just the driving. Three cities, three rooms, three versions of failing to be the person eighty million people thought he was. The data was in. The assessment was complete: the damage was structural, not cosmetic, and no amount of rehearsal was going to bring the depreciated value of his performance up to replacement cost.

He pulled into his parking spot in Clintonville just after two. The apartment was dark. He went inside and stood in the kitchen and looked at the cereal on the counter — Cheerios, the yellow box — and did not eat. He was not hungry. He was the specific thing that happens after you have done something badly three times in three different ways and you know that doing it a fourth time will not be the time it starts working, but you are going to do it a fourth time anyway, because a contract says so, and because somewhere inside the bad there were ten minutes where you were yourself and a room full of strangers leaned forward, and you don't know how to get back to those ten minutes, and you don't know how to want them without killing them, and you are standing in your kitchen at two in the morning trying to figure out if wanting something is the same as ruining it.

He went to bed. The ceiling was his ceiling. The sheets were his sheets. The streetlight made the shadow it always made. In two weeks he would stand on a stage in front of 580 people, and whatever had found him in those eight minutes in Detroit and those ninety seconds in Pittsburgh would either find him again or it wouldn't, and he could not practice for it any more than he could practice for a house fire.
