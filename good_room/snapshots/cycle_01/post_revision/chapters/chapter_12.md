# Chapter 12

Alex Chen arrived at Doug's apartment on a Tuesday morning in May carrying a canvas messenger bag with a fraying strap, a digital recorder, and a thesis. The thesis was that Doug Bridge represented something about contemporary masculinity, its failures, its accidental honesty, its unwillingness to perform. Alex had explained this over email in language that Doug read twice and understood about forty percent of. He'd agreed to the profile because Layla told him to and because saying no would've required a reason he didn't have.

Alex was thirty-one. He'd chosen journalism late enough to know the business was collapsing and still carried himself like the decision needed defending. He wore a denim jacket that was either vintage or just old, Doug couldn't tell, and he had a pen clipped to the breast pocket that he kept removing, chewing the cap, and replacing. The cap was destroyed, soft plastic cratered by months of thought. He also had allergies. He'd blown his nose three times before Doug got the coffee made, and he kept rubbing his eyes with the heel of his hand in a way that made him look like he'd been crying, which he hadn't. Columbus in May was a pollen event. Doug had processed a claim once involving mold remediation triggered by spring pollen accumulating in an HVAC system, a fringe case but not an unprecedented one.

"So," Alex said. He pressed record on the digital recorder and set it on the kitchen counter between them, next to a box of Grape-Nuts that Doug hadn't put away. "I want to start with something maybe a little abstract."

"Okay," Doug said.

"What drives you?"

Doug looked at the recorder. The red light was on. "Traffic, mostly," he said. "If I leave after 7:20 Henderson backs up."

Alex smiled and waited, apparently trusting the silence to do some of the work. "No, I mean what motivates you? What gets you up in the morning?"

Doug thought about this. His alarm was set for 6:40. He got up because it went off. Before the alarm, he'd been getting up because his bladder woke him around 6:15, but he'd adjusted his water intake after 8 p.m. and that had mostly resolved. He didn't think this was what Alex meant.

"I have a job," Doug said. "The job starts at eight. So I get up in time for the job."

Alex wrote something in his notebook. The notebook was a Moleskine from a bookstore, and its pages were dense with small handwriting that tilted left. Doug noticed the tilt because he'd processed a claim once involving handwriting analysis, a forged receipt, a guy in Reynoldsburg, nine hundred dollars on a pressure washer that didn't exist. The analyst had said left-tilting handwriting indicated introversion. Doug didn't know if that was true for Alex or for the Reynoldsburg guy.

"Tell me about the toast," Alex said.

"Which part?"

"How it felt. That night. Standing up there."

"I was drunk," Doug said.

"Right, but"

"I'd had eight whiskeys. I don't normally have eight whiskeys. I don't normally have four. The bartender was pouring them tall because the venue was all-inclusive and he'd stopped measuring."

"But what made you stand up? What made you give the toast?"

"Brendan had just given one," Doug said. "My ex-wife's husband. His was very good. People cried."

"And that motivated you to"

"I don't know if motivated is the right word. I was already standing. Or I was sitting and then I was standing. I don't remember the transition."

Alex wrote something else. Doug could see, upside down, the word "transition" with a box around it. He wondered what Alex thought that word was going to mean in the profile. He had a feeling it was going to mean something different from what Doug meant, which was that he physically did not remember going from chair to upright.

They talked for another hour. Alex asked about Doug's childhood, his marriage, his divorce, his relationship with Kelsey, his job, his apartment, his daily routine, his feelings about fame, his feelings about comedy, his feelings about his feelings. Doug answered as if each question were a coverage inquiry, the specific information requested and nothing additional. Alex kept waiting for the additional part. Doug kept not providing it.

At one point Alex asked, "Do you think of yourself as funny?"

"No," Doug said.

"But eighty million people watched that video and laughed."

"Some of them were probably just watching. You don't know everybody laughed."

"The comments"

"I've read the comments. Some of them think I'm an asshole. One guy said I should be arrested for public intoxication. He got twelve hundred upvotes."

Alex paused. He rubbed his eye again. His allergies were getting worse. The morning light through Doug's kitchen window had a visible quality to it, a fine yellow-green dusting on the sill that Doug wiped down every other day and that returned every other day because that was what May in central Ohio did. Alex had stopped wiping his nose and had moved to a strategy of sustained sniffling that he seemed to think was less noticeable. It was not less noticeable.

"Can I use your bathroom?" Alex asked.

"Down the hall," Doug said. "Door on the left. Door on the right is the office."

"You have a home office?"

"It doesn't have a computer in it," Doug said. "Just boxes."

Alex hesitated, clearly wanting to ask about the boxes, and then went to the bathroom. Doug heard him blow his nose four times, a sequence that built in intensity like a man trying to clear a drain. Doug sat at the kitchen counter and looked at the recorder. The red light was still on. It was recording the silence, the refrigerator hum, the muffled honking from the bathroom. Somewhere in a digital file, this would exist: Alex Chen's sinuses and Doug Bridge's Frigidaire, captured for journalism.

---

Alex was supposed to shadow Doug for two days. Tuesday was the work day, office, lunch with Roy, then Simone in the late afternoon. Layla had called it "the full Doug experience," and Doug had not asked what that meant because he suspected the answer would involve the word "authentic" and he'd heard that word enough times from enough people who did not mean the same thing by it.

They took Doug's Civic to Mutual Fidelity. Alex sat in the passenger seat studying the cracked dashboard, the floor mat salt-stained from winter, the gas receipts folded in the center console.

"How long have you had this car?"

"Three years. Three years and five months, actually. I bought it in December."

"Do you like it?"

"It runs," Doug said. "The heat works. The AC works when it feels like it. The passenger window sticks in cold weather but we're past that now."

Alex typed something into his phone. Doug drove.

The Mutual Fidelity parking lot was gray, flat, half-full at 7:52 a.m. Alex photographed the building. Doug had worked there for fifteen years and had never once thought it required a photograph.

Inside, Doug introduced Alex to Janet, who shook Alex's hand and said, "We're very proud of Doug." In fifteen years Janet had never expressed pride in anything outside the department's quarterly throughput numbers. Doug filed the sentence under information requiring a new category and kept walking.

Alex sat in a chair Doug borrowed from the break room and watched him work through a kitchen fire claim from Grove City: grease, not electrical, which mattered because the policy excluded appliance failure and the homeowner was trying to argue ignition malfunction. Doug reviewed the photographs, noted the pour pattern on the stovetop, wrote his assessment. Forty minutes passed. Alex kept watching, notebook on his knee, pen cap in his mouth, eyes going red again from whatever Columbus was doing to his face.

"Is this typical?" Alex asked when Doug closed the file.

"It's a Tuesday," Doug said.

"I mean, are your days usually like this?"

"Usually. Sometimes there's a site visit. Today there isn't."

A coworker named Diane Posner paused at Doug's desk with a yogurt cup in one hand and a plastic spoon in the other. "My daughter showed me a TikTok of you," she said. "She's fifteen. She said you're iconic."

Diane pronounced "iconic" as if it might require follow-up care.

"Tell her thanks," Doug said.

"She wants to know if you're on TikTok."

"I'm not."

"She says you should be."

"I don't know how it works."

Diane nodded, satisfied and disappointed at the same time, and moved on. Alex wrote something down.

Lunch was at the sandwich place. It had been the sandwich place for nine years, and it was called Sal's, though there was no evidence a person named Sal had ever been involved in its operation. The menu hadn't changed since Doug started going there. Roy hadn't changed either. Roy ordered turkey on wheat, mustard, no tomato. Doug ordered ham and Swiss. They sat.

"Roy, this is Alex. He's writing a profile."

Roy looked at Alex with the measured disinterest he brought to most new information. "About what?"

"About me," Doug said.

"Huh," Roy said. He unwrapped his sandwich.

Alex set the recorder on the table. "Roy, what was your reaction when you saw the toast video?"

"Didn't see it," Roy said.

"You never watched it?"

"No."

"Why not?"

Roy took a bite of his sandwich. He chewed. He swallowed. The pause went on long enough for Alex to shift in his seat, which was something Doug had learned not to do years ago. You waited for Roy. Roy did not speed up.

"I work next to him," Roy said.

"But your best friend went viral. Eighty million people"

"Doug's my coworker," Roy said. "He's also my friend. He was both before the video and he'll be both after. I don't need a phone to tell me what he's like. I've been eating lunch with him for nine years."

"That's actually a really interesting perspective. Can I quote you on that?"

Roy looked at Doug. Doug knew the look. It was the look Roy gave when someone treated a plain fact like a philosophical position.

"It's your article," Roy said.

They ate. Alex asked more questions. Roy answered them in the fewest possible words. At one point Alex asked what Roy thought about Doug's fame and Roy said, "I don't think about it," and Alex wrote this down as if it were a revelation instead of a plain description of Roy's operating system.

Alex asked Doug if his friendship with Roy had changed since the video. Doug looked at Roy. Roy was eating his sandwich. Nothing about Roy had changed.

"No," Doug said.

"Not at all? Even"

"It hasn't changed," Doug said.

Alex wrote something else down. Doug ate his sandwich.

The session with Simone was at Doug's apartment at four o'clock. They'd been meeting four days a week for over a month now, and the apartment had accumulated evidence of the work: index cards on the kitchen table with phrases scribbled on them, a digital recorder that belonged to Simone, a legal pad full of notes in handwriting Doug couldn't read because it was worse than his, which he had not previously thought possible.

Simone was sitting at the table when they got back. She had a coffee from somewhere and was reading her phone with flat irritation, scrolling the way people did when they'd already had enough of the internet and were still making themselves take more. She looked up at Alex and then at Doug.

"He's the journalist," Simone said. It wasn't a question.

"Alex Chen," Alex said, extending his hand.

Simone shook it. She had rings on three fingers, not decorative rings, working rings that stayed on because taking them off was more effort than leaving them there. Her hair was up, held in place by a clip that was losing its structural battle. "Layla told me you were coming. What's the angle?"

"I'm interested in Doug as a kind of"

"A symbol?"

"I was going to say a case study in"

"He's not a case study," Simone said. "He's a guy. We're trying to write material." She turned to Doug. "Sit down. Tell me about the thing from yesterday, the deductible one."

Doug sat. Alex's recorder was on the table next to Simone's recorder, two red lights blinking at slightly different intervals. He felt like a claim being documented from two angles.

He told the deductible story. It was about a man in Hilliard whose roof leaked during a storm and who filed a claim not realizing that his deductible was five thousand dollars. The total damage was fifty-two hundred, so the insurance company's portion was two hundred. The man called Doug to ask if this was a mistake. Doug explained that it wasn't. The man said, "I've been paying premiums for twelve years." Doug said, "The premiums purchased the policy. The policy has a deductible." The man was quiet for a long time and then asked, "So what did I pay for?" Doug hadn't had a good answer because the man was technically right. He'd paid for the idea that something would be there when things went wrong, and something was there, but the gap between the idea and the two-hundred-dollar check was a gap Doug had spent his career standing inside, explaining to people why it was that size.

Simone listened without interrupting. When Doug finished she said, "The last part. The gap. That's the bit."

"It's not a bit," Doug said. "It happened."

"That's why it's the bit."

Alex watched this exchange and Doug could see him watching it, the slight forward lean, the pen cap in his mouth, the notebook open on his lap. Doug wondered what Alex saw. Two people at a kitchen table, one of them talking about insurance deductibles, the other one finding comedy in it. He suspected Alex saw something more significant than that. Alex found significance the way Doug's apartment found pollen: it drifted in through every opening and settled on every surface and could not be kept out regardless of effort.

They worked another forty minutes. Doug talked about claims and Simone kept stopping him at the moments where the mundane turned specific enough to be strange, or sad, or accidentally precise about something that wasn't insurance at all. At one point Simone looked at Alex and said, "You're going to make this sound like therapy, aren't you."

"I'm just observing," Alex said.

"Observing is what therapists say right before they ask how that makes you feel."

Alex didn't answer. He wrote something down. His pen cap was in his mouth again. He was already turning the room into sentences, which was different from actually seeing it, and Doug wondered if Alex knew the difference. Journalists, in Doug's limited experience, were a lot like adjusters. They assessed what happened and wrote it into a format that turned mess into narrative. The difference was that adjusters knew the narrative was a reduction.

After the session, they stopped at the Kroger on High Street because Doug needed groceries and Alex apparently needed groceries to happen on the record. Doug bought eggs, bread, and coffee. He explained the coffee sale cycle because Alex asked. Near the cereal aisle, a college kid in a Buckeyes hoodie recognized him, asked for a photo, looked at it, and said, "You look like you're getting arrested." Alex wrote that down too.

In the parking lot, while Doug loaded the trunk and put the eggs on top, Alex asked, "How's fame changed you?"

Doug shut the trunk. "I switched from store-brand Raisin Bran to Grape-Nuts," he said. "Dollar forty more. Sometimes a dollar sixty, depending on the week."

"That's it?"

Doug leaned against the Civic. The money was real. The attention was real. The contract with Layla, the sessions with Simone, the Netflix deal, the pharmaceutical conference in Indianapolis where four hundred people had watched him fail and then clapped, the kid in the hoodie, the photo that would end up on somebody else's feed, all of it was real. But the cereal was the only part he could line-item.

"That's the main change," Doug said.

---

The profile published nine days later. Layla texted Doug the link at 6:47 a.m. with three exclamation marks and a fire emoji. Doug was brushing his teeth. He read it standing at the bathroom sink, his mouth full of toothpaste, because he didn't want to wait the thirty seconds it would take to spit and rinse.

The headline was: **"America's Most Reluctant Comedian."**

The article was long and very good. Alex could write. He had built a version of Doug who was charming, quietly wise, and much more deliberate than the man standing in the bathroom with toothpaste drying at the corner of his mouth. In the second paragraph Doug was a "midwestern Everyman." In a subheading he was "the last honest man in America." The piece made it sound as if he'd stood up at the wedding on purpose.

Alex had also described the apartment as "spartan but deliberate" and set Doug's line about not knowing how to be a public person under a paragraph about modern masculinity. Doug had said it because Diane asked whether he had TikTok. He meant he didn't know how to use the app. On the page it read like a confession.

Layla called at 7:30. "It's incredible," she said. "The engagement's insane. It's already been shared like forty thousand times."

"Forty thousand," Doug said.

"I've had three calls this morning. A podcast wants you. The literary agent's back about the book deal. And, okay, don't freak out, the *Times* wants to do a follow-up."

"The Dispatch?"

"The New York Times, babe. The *Times* Times."

Doug said nothing. He was still standing in the bathroom. The toothpaste had dried on the corner of his mouth. He looked at himself in the mirror: the same face, the thinning hair, the reading glasses on the back of the toilet where he'd left them two days earlier. He still looked like an insurance adjuster from Columbus who had once gotten very drunk at a wedding. The mirror didn't know about the article. The mirror showed what it showed.

"Doug? You there?"

"Yeah," he said. "I'm here."

At the office, three people mentioned the profile before Doug reached his desk. Janet stopped him in the hallway and said, "I read it. Very thoughtful," in a tone usually reserved for eulogies. A woman from accounting whose name Doug could not remember said, "My husband liked the cereal part." Doug thanked them both, sat down, turned on his computer, and opened the claims queue.

Roy was already at his desk. His coffee was half-drunk.

"Morning," Roy said.

"Morning," Doug said.

"I read the headline."

"Just the headline?"

"It's a headline." Roy took a sip of his coffee. "They called you a comedian."

Doug waited.

"You gave one speech, Doug. You were drunk."

Roy turned back to his screen.

Doug looked at his own. Fourteen claims. A burst pipe in Westerville. A chimney fire in Dublin. A woman in Upper Arlington whose sump pump had failed during last week's rain. She'd photographed her basement from four angles. She'd kept the receipt for the pump. Her handwritten description got tighter and more frantic in the margins where she'd added what she'd remembered later.

The phone's red light blinked through the morning. Layla, probably, or a podcast, or somebody who wanted the man from the article. That version of Doug was already easier to handle than the real one. He fit in headlines. He fit in share text. He fit in other people's sentences.

Doug opened the Upper Arlington file. Coverage applied. No exclusions. He wrote the assessment, entered the determination code, and moved to the next screen.

The phone kept blinking. Doug picked it up. It was Layla. He put her on hold and dialed the woman in Upper Arlington.
