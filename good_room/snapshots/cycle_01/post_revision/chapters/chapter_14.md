# Chapter 14

The rental car was a gray Hyundai Elantra that smelled like the previous renter's choices: a pine air freshener clipped to the vent, dried coffee in the cupholder, the stale salt-and-grease trace of a fast food meal that had happened somewhere between here and wherever that person had been going. Layla had booked the tour in a single phone call while Doug was on his lunch break. Three cities, three comedy clubs, three nights. "Warm-ups," she called them. "Before the big show." The Netflix taping was in two weeks. Doug pulled onto I-71 north toward Cleveland and did not feel warm.

He had driven this route to visit Kelsey when she first moved there, before the visits became less frequent, then occasional, then something they planned over text three weeks in advance. She lived twenty minutes from the club. He had not told her he was coming.

Wiley's sat in a strip mall between a Great Clips and a vape shop. Doug went on in an untucked button-down and jeans. For half an hour he did borrowed comedy: pauses he'd studied on YouTube, callbacks he'd diagrammed in a Holiday Inn room, crowd work he'd rehearsed and then lost in the moment. People stayed patient in the specific way audiences stay patient when they're waiting for a person who has not arrived.

Eighteen minutes in, a woman near the back yelled, "Do the toast!" Doug said, "I can't. I'm sober." That got him the only real laugh of the night. Everything after that sounded secondhand, including his thank-you. In the parking lot he called Simone and told her he'd tried to do it like a comedian. "Don't," she said. When he got back to the maroon Holiday Inn bedspread he was twenty minutes from Kelsey and did not call her.

---

The drive to Pittsburgh was two and a half hours through northeastern Ohio and across the western edge of Pennsylvania, where the flat farmland wrinkled into hills and the highway started asking for more attention. Doug stopped for gas at a Sheetz outside Youngstown. The coffee cost a dollar eighty-nine and tasted how truck stops smelled: burnt, industrial, somehow fine if you did not examine it too closely.

Pittsburgh was more vertical than Cleveland, which was more vertical than Columbus. His hotel was another Holiday Inn near the airport. It had the same floor plan as Cleveland, but with a blue bedspread and a water stain on the ceiling that looked like it had almost made up its mind. Doug lay there for fifteen minutes before he had to leave for the club. He kept replaying the Cleveland laugh. Simone had said it came because he told the truth. Doug believed her and still had no idea how to use that.

The Comic Attic sat beneath a bar called O'Reilly's, down a narrow staircase that smelled like spilled Yuengling and industrial cleaner. Maybe seventy-five people fit down there. The MC, a young woman named Diane with a sleeve tattoo and a laugh that carried through walls, introduced him as "the man who broke the internet at his daughter's wedding, Doug Bridge."

The applause was genuine. Doug took the mic, looked at the room, opened his mouth, and nothing came. One second passed, then three, then five. The audience shifted. At seven seconds a man in the front row uncrossed his arms. At nine, a woman by the wall looked at the person beside her. At eleven, someone coughed.

"I wrote it down," Doug said. "I said it in the car. In the hotel. In a Holiday Inn bathroom. It was there an hour ago."

He started the set. The bits were present, but the connections between them were gone, and Doug moved through them the way you walk through a house after a break-in, checking what was still where you had left it. Twelve minutes in, he ran out of what he could remember and told the pool story instead: the above-ground pool in Westerville, the wall that split, the water that went everywhere, the neighbor's seventeen hybrid tea roses documented with receipts at forty-two hundred dollars, the coverage gap no policy addressed. He told it the way he had told it at his kitchen table, as a problem. Chairs stopped squeaking. Somebody in the third row laughed. Then other people did. For about ninety seconds Doug felt the room come toward him.

Then he reached for the replacement-cost bit Simone had helped him shape, and the reaching was enough. Glasses touched tabletops. Coats shifted against chair backs. Whatever had moved toward him went back where it had been. He finished to applause that was better than Cleveland and still wrong.

He called Simone from the hotel parking lot before he went upstairs. "I stood there for eleven seconds," he said.

"That's a long time."

"The pool story worked."

"How long?"

"Ninety seconds."

"And then?"

"Then I remembered it was supposed to be a set."

Simone was quiet for a second. "What were you thinking about during the pool story?"

"Nothing. I wasn't — I don't know. I was just talking."

"Okay," she said. "Call me after Detroit."

---

The drive from Pittsburgh to Detroit was the longest leg, four and a half hours west on the turnpike and across northern Ohio to Toledo, where I-75 ran the last stretch north into Detroit. Doug drove it Saturday morning. The sun hit the windshield at an angle the visor could not fix. He stopped for gas outside Akron. The coffee tasted the same as the Youngstown coffee. He bought a gas station sandwich, unwrapped it, looked at it, and rewrapped it. He ate it twenty miles later because hunger was a simpler problem.

Layla called at noon. "So how's it going? I heard Cleveland was a little, you know. Finding your legs."

"It was bad, Layla."

"It wasn't bad. Gerry said you had good energy."

"Gerry lies to comedians after they bomb. That's his whole job."

"Okay, so Cleveland was a learning experience. What about Pittsburgh?"

"Ninety seconds worked."

"That's great. Ninety seconds is a real start. We can build on ninety seconds."

Doug looked at the highway. "I need to focus on tonight."

"Totally. Detroit's gonna love you. The venue's great, babe. You've got this."

The Mark was a real comedy club, not a strip mall or a bar basement but a dedicated room with proper lighting, a real sound system, and tiered seating that climbed toward the back. It held about a hundred and fifty, and it was close to full. The MC, a local comic named Terrence, did five minutes on Detroit traffic that got the kind of laughs Doug had started to think of as physical evidence. Doug watched from behind the curtain and felt how separate Terrence's ease was from anything Doug knew how to do.

Doug walked on stage. The lights were warm enough to turn the back half of the room into shapes. He started with the material he and Simone had built: insurance, cereal, depreciation. It was clean. It was serviceable. Three minutes in, a man in the second row lifted both beers and yelled, "Toast!"

He was maybe forty-five, heavy, wearing a Tigers cap and a polo shirt tucked into jeans. He was grinning already. Doug could see he was not angry. He was having the best time in the room, which was the problem.

"Toast! Toast! Toast!"

Doug looked at him and thought about every YouTube clip of a comedian destroying a heckler. "Hey, man," he said. "I appreciate the enthusiasm."

It was wrong. He heard it as soon as it left his mouth. It was supervisor language, claims-department language. The guy brightened, as if Doug had opened a door for him.

"Come on, man. Do the toast!"

"I don't do the toast," Doug said. "That's not — I've got different material tonight."

"We don't want different material!"

"Sir," Doug said, and then knew he had made it worse.

"Sir? He called me sir." The man turned to the woman beside him, who had already started looking embarrassed on his behalf and everyone else's. "DO THE TOAST, DOUG."

Half the crowd looked embarrassed for him. The other half wanted to see how bad it could get. Doug could feel the set list leave him. It was not the whole stage or his voice, just the labeled tabs he had planned to move through in order. He stood there with a hundred and forty people looking at him and a drunk man demanding a wedding toast and nothing in his head but the last two days of interstate.

"Okay," Doug said. "Here's what I actually have. I drove here from Pittsburgh this morning and the coffee at a gas station outside Akron tasted exactly like the coffee I had outside Youngstown yesterday. I think it's the same coffee. I don't know how that would work logistically, but it tasted the same."

A woman near the bar laughed into her beer. The man's date stopped tugging on his sleeve.

"I've been in three Holiday Inns in three nights. Cleveland was maroon. Pittsburgh was blue. Detroit is green. Somebody decided those count as three separate experiences."

That got more of them. The heckler lowered one of his beers.

"Every room has that chair by the window, the one nobody sits in, so you can look at the parking lot from a second location."

The laugh widened. Terrence, by the bar, uncrossed his arms.

"And rest stops aren't places. They're pauses with vending machines. Everybody in there is holding food they did not want an hour earlier and looking at the interstate like it personally offended them."

Doug heard the room with him now and kept going because stopping would have meant thinking.

"I-80 through Pennsylvania is actually beautiful, which seems unfair, because nobody is there on purpose. You're paying tolls to go through hills and trees that would matter more if you were allowed to arrive in them."

Someone said, "Jesus," softly, and laughed right after.

"And every Holiday Inn smells the same. Cleaner, carpet, other people's sleep. It's supposed to be comforting. Really it just means nobody knows you're there."

The heckler went quiet. His date put her phone down. What Doug got from them then was not laughter exactly. It was the attention people give something true.

"That chair's still by the window when you get back, the parking lot's still there, and you can be in Cleveland or Pittsburgh or Detroit and it doesn't make much difference after midnight. It's still you in a room with an ice bucket and a Gideon Bible and a key card that stops working too early in the morning."

He kept talking that way for eight minutes, about toll roads and hotel carpets and how every fluorescent hallway made him feel temporarily misfiled. Then he felt himself reach for the cereal bit waiting on the set list, and the reaching killed it. People settled back into being an audience. The last four minutes were polite again. When he said, "Thank you, that's my time," the applause was generous and confused.

---

He left Detroit at ten-forty, still in his stage clothes, the parking lot damp from a rain that had come and gone while he was inside. The Elantra's GPS said three hours and twenty-two minutes to Columbus. Thirty miles outside the city, on I-75 south, he called Simone.

"How bad?" she said.

"A guy spent half the set yelling for the toast."

"And?"

"I called him sir."

Simone made a sound through her nose that might have been a laugh. "Jesus."

"Then I talked about hotel coffee and parking lots and they listened."

"Listened how?"

"Like they wanted the report, not the brochure."

The line went quiet except for highway noise under it.

"What we built doesn't work," Doug said.

"I know."

"The set list's a stack of forms. I can organize it, label it, read every line in order, and the room still knows I'm filing paperwork instead of telling them what happened."

"The taping's in two weeks."

He waited for the part where she would tell him what to do next. She did not offer it.

"Get home safe," she said. "Tuesday we'll look at what was alive."

He got to Clintonville a little after two. The apartment was dark. He let himself in, set his keys beside Simone's pen cap on the kitchen counter, and looked at the yellow Cheerios box without opening it. In two weeks he would stand at the Lincoln Theatre in front of 580 people and cameras. He turned off the kitchen light and left the cereal where it was.
