# Chapter 2

In the bathroom before work, Doug checked the number again. Fourteen million views. He was in boxers and a t-shirt that said MUTUAL FIDELITY CLAIMS DIVISION SOFTBALL 2019. They had never had a softball division. Someone in marketing had ordered the shirts anyway, and Doug had kept his because it was soft and because he had no one to be embarrassed in front of.

Fourteen million. He said the number to the mirror, to his own face, which looked approximately the way a face looks after four hours of sleep, three of which had been spent reading what strangers thought of it. The number had no meaning. It was the population of a moderately sized country. Doug had looked this up at 1 a.m., when the count was still at eleven million and he'd needed some frame of reference — Belgium, or close to it. More people had watched him say things he couldn't remember deciding to say than lived in a nation with its own parliament and a functioning rail system.

He drove to work in the Civic with the heat on high because February in Columbus was the color of wet concrete and roughly as warm. His phone sat in the cup holder, buzzing at intervals that had stopped corresponding to individual events and had become ambient, like sitting on a washing machine. He'd stopped looking at each notification around midnight. The 310 number — Los Angeles, he'd figured out — had called six more times since Saturday. He hadn't answered. He didn't know anyone in Los Angeles. He didn't want to know anyone in Los Angeles.

The internet had been busy overnight. Doug knew this because he'd watched it happen before giving up on sleep, lying on his couch in the dark with the phone held above his face, reading the terms of his own situation.

There were memes now. Someone had captured the moment — three seconds into the toast, roughly — where Doug's left hand was raised in a gesture that, in context, was him reaching for words and, out of context, looked like a man delivering a papal blessing over his daughter's open bar. This image existed now with white block text: YOUR MARRIAGE HAS BEEN ADJUSTED. There was another one, his face mid-sentence, mouth open, eyes half-closed in what was clearly the alcohol but read on screen as beatific certainty: WHEN THE WHISKEY KICKS IN AND THE TRUTH COMES OUT. There were dozens more. Doug's face with the caption PROOF OF LOSS, which he recognized as insurance terminology someone had pulled from his job title, and which was more accurate than whoever made it probably intended.

Someone had auto-tuned the toast into a song. Doug had listened to thirty seconds. His voice — his actual voice, the one that came out of his body — had been pitch-corrected and layered over a beat that sounded like it belonged in a car driven by someone thirty years younger than him. The words were still his words. The cadence was still roughly his cadence. But the musicality made every sentence sound like it was building toward a chorus that never arrived, which was, Doug thought, not a bad description of his marriage.

He'd closed the app.

A Twitter thread by someone whose profile identified them as a "body language analyst and certified life coach" had broken down the toast frame by frame. Doug's posture (defeated). His hand gestures (subconsciously reaching). His eye contact with Kelsey (avoidant but searching). His single glance toward Megan, which the analyst called "the micro-expression of a man who has rehearsed saying nothing for twelve years and just forgot the script." The thread had four thousand retweets. Doug had watched the original video twice and could not identify any of the things this person was identifying, which either meant the analyst was making it up or that Doug's body had been doing things he hadn't authorized, which was, given the toast itself, not surprising.

A Reddit post titled "This man just eulogized his own marriage and I can't stop watching" had, when he'd last checked, forty thousand upvotes and a comment section that scrolled for what appeared to be geological ages. Doug had started reading the comments. This had been a mistake he intended to repeat.

The Mutual Fidelity parking lot looked the same as it always did. Doug pulled into his spot — not assigned, but his through the accumulation of years that turns habit into tenure — and sat in the Civic for a minute with the engine running. The building was three stories of beige brick. It processed insurance claims. It was the most uninteresting structure in a city that had several strong contenders, and Doug had never been so grateful to see it.

He walked in. Greg was at the security desk with a Louis L'Amour paperback spread face-down on the counter. Every morning Greg gave Doug the same two-finger salute. This morning he gave it, then dropped his eyes to the bent corner of the paperback before Doug reached the desk.

"Morning, Greg."

"Morning, Doug." A pause. "Cold one today."

"Yeah."

By the time Doug got to his cubicle, enough people had looked up and then back down that the second floor seemed to be doing it in sequence. His work inbox had seventeen new messages. Two were from people in other departments who had somehow developed urgent questions about claim numbers they could have answered themselves.

Janet appeared at 9:40 with the Hoffmeyer file and her white mug that said WORLD'S OKAY-EST MOM. She kept a dish of butterscotch hard candies on her desk for difficult conversations. She hadn't brought one.

"Doug. Do you have a minute?"

"Sure."

She sat in Roy's overflow chair, opened the folder, and walked him through the contractor's estimate and the roof-pitch calculation. Doug said he'd double-check the measurements. Janet thanked him, closed the folder, and stayed where she was.

"So," Janet said.

"Yeah," Doug said.

Janet looked at the Blue Jackets schedule pinned to the partition beside his monitor, opened her mouth, and shut it again. "Let me know about the Hoffmeyer measurements."

"I will."

She went back to her office. Before she sat down, Doug's email dinged. Janet had forwarded something from HR titled "Social Media Guidelines: A Reminder for All Employees." It had gone to the full office. Doug understood, with the practiced clarity of a man trained to read subtext in policy language, that it was for him.

---

Roy arrived at 10:15, late because of a dentist appointment, the right side of his jaw swollen from a root canal. He sat down, opened his computer, and said "Morning" out of the left side of his mouth.

"Hey. You okay?"

"Root canal."

"Sorry."

"It's fine. It's a tooth."

"Roy."

"Yeah."

"Did you—" Doug stopped. He wasn't sure how to phrase it. "Have you been on the internet this weekend?"

Roy blinked once. The swollen side of his jaw made the pause look slower than it probably was. He kept his eyes on Doug long enough for the fluorescent lights to fill the silence. "No," he said. "I check the weather. That's about it."

"Okay."

"Why?"

Doug turned his phone face-down on the desk. "No reason."

Roy opened a file — paper, because Roy still preferred paper files even though the system had been digital for three years and Janet had stopped fighting this preference approximately eighteen months ago.

"I heard something," Roy said, without looking up.

"Yeah?"

"Something happened at the wedding. A couple people mentioned it."

"Yeah."

"I don't need to know what it was."

"Okay."

"I'm just saying that. In case you were going to tell me."

"I wasn't."

"Good." Roy turned a page. He read it. He turned another page. Doug watched him read until he felt his hands unclench from the chair arms.

The desk phone rang. The landline, which almost never rang because most business came through email and the only people who called the desk line were older policyholders who didn't trust computers and Kelsey, who sometimes tried the office when Doug's cell went to voicemail. He picked it up.

"Doug Bridge, Mutual Fidelity."

"Mr. Bridge, this is Tammy Hess from the *Today* show. I'm calling to see if—"

"I'm sorry, this is my work line."

"I understand, and I apologize. We got your office number from—"

"I'm sorry," Doug said, and hung up.

He looked at the phone. The *Today* show had his work number. Someone in New York had called the main line for Mutual Fidelity Insurance, navigated the directory, and selected Doug Bridge, Claims Division, as though he were the sort of person who ought to be reachable.

"Who was that?" Roy asked.

"Wrong number."

Roy accepted this without investigation. He turned another page.

Before lunch Doug's Gmail had added eleven more emails: a production company, a podcast called *Dads Being Real*, two literary agents, a brand partnership consultant, a morning radio show in Cincinnati. He put the phone in his desk drawer. It kept buzzing under the file folders like something trapped alive.

He opened the Hoffmeyer file. He measured the roof pitch calculation. He documented the square footage. The numbers were straightforward and the numbers asked nothing of him and he read them with a care that anyone watching might have mistaken for dedication but was actually something closer to need.

By noon the video was at twenty-two million. Doug knew this because he checked in the bathroom, standing in a stall with his pants on and the seat down, reading the screen with the focused attention of a man studying the results of a test he hadn't known he was taking. He refreshed the page. Twenty-two million and climbing. He put the phone away and went back to his desk and ate the turkey sandwich he'd brought from home and didn't taste any of it.

By two o'clock it was at twenty-six million. By the time he left the office at 5:30, walking across the parking lot in the dark — because February in Columbus meant darkness at 5:30, and the parking lot lights made the slush look orange, and the cold was the kind that found the gap between his collar and his neck and settled there — the count had reached thirty-one million.

---

He drove home. The apartment was the same as he'd left it — the couch cushion still dented from where he'd sat reading comments until two the night before, the suit from the wedding draped over the kitchen chair where he'd finally taken it off Sunday afternoon, the one dress shoe by the front door and the other one still unaccounted for. He'd been wearing one shoe when he woke up Sunday morning. The other was somewhere between the reception hall and his apartment, and the fact that he'd been home for two days and hadn't looked for it or thought about it until now was the kind of thing that, in a different person's life, might have been funny.

He stood at the kitchen counter and ate a bowl of cereal. Raisin Bran, the store brand, not because he preferred it but because it was $2.19 and the name brand was $4.49 and the taste difference wasn't enough to justify the markup. This was the kind of calculation Megan had watched him make for eighteen years — the per-unit comparison, the coupon logic, the small satisfactions of rational purchasing — and the look on her face during the last three years of their marriage, when he'd hold up two boxes of something in the cereal aisle and explain the math, had been the look of a woman who had realized that the man she'd married would comparison-shop his way through every experience, including the ones where the point was not the savings but the standing next to someone in a grocery store because you wanted to be there.

The cereal was fine. The apartment was quiet. Outside, a car drove past with its bass turned up so loud the glasses in the cabinet above the sink rattled against each other, a sound Doug heard every night at roughly this time and had stopped noticing and now noticed again because noticing was all he'd been doing for two days and he couldn't turn it off.

He checked the phone. Forty-four million.

The number had more than tripled since the morning. Doug tried to visualize forty-four million people. He could not. He tried to think of it in insurance terms — forty-four million policyholders, each one a file, each file a name and an address and a specific thing that had gone wrong with their house. He couldn't process that either. The number existed outside any frame he had for understanding the world. He assessed claims for houses in central Ohio. He understood square footage, depreciation schedules, the rate at which a water-damaged hardwood floor loses value. He did not understand forty-four million strangers forming opinions about seven minutes of his life that he himself could barely remember living.

He sat on the couch. He opened the comments.

He knew this was a mistake. He'd known it was a mistake the first time, Sunday morning, and had done it anyway, the way he sometimes couldn't stop re-reading a claim file that didn't add up — the compulsion of looking for the one detail that would make the whole thing cohere.

The comments didn't cohere. They weren't supposed to. They were the sound of a fraction of forty-four million people processing Doug Bridge into something that fit their needs.

**This guy is my hero.** Doug as folk hero, Doug as truth-teller, Doug as the man who said what everyone thinks and nobody says. These came with exclamation marks and crying-laughing emoji and the phrase "I felt that" repeated so many times it stopped sounding like a feeling and started sounding like applause.

**What an asshole.** Doug had ruined his daughter's wedding. He had humiliated Megan in front of everyone she knew. He had taken a room that was not about him and made it entirely about him. Those comments came in short, certain sentences, and Doug read them with the numb attention of a man reviewing a denial he had expected to receive. They weren't wrong.

The ones he couldn't file were the comments that treated the toast like a tool. A woman wrote, "The part about the table made me leave my husband," and Doug stared at that until the screen dimmed. Another commenter named InsuranceBro614 wrote, "I work in claims and this is the most accurate description of a marriage I've ever heard from an adjuster. 10/10 would recommend the whiskey." Doug didn't know which part bothered him more: that something he'd said about a table might have shoved a stranger out of her marriage, or that somebody who might be sitting on the second floor of Mutual Fidelity had recognized himself in it.

He read comments until 1:45 a.m. The apartment was dark except for the phone's glow. His cereal bowl was in the sink, unwashed. The suit was still on the chair. The missing shoe was still missing.

At 1:47 he put the phone down and picked up the Gahanna file.

The Gahanna claim was a water heater. A standard forty-gallon residential unit in a house on Agler Road — the homeowner had submitted it Friday, before the wedding, and it had been sitting in Doug's take-home stack since then, untouched, while Doug was getting drunk and saying true things and becoming a person forty-four million strangers had opinions about. The water heater had burst at the base seam. The basement had taken four inches of standing water before the homeowner found it. He'd documented everything: photographs of the water line on the basement walls, the receipt for the original unit purchased in 2016, a contractor's estimate for remediation and replacement. The documentation was clean. The coverage was clear. The depreciation on a 2016 unit was calculable — ten years of expected life, six years in service, the math was straightforward and the math didn't care what Doug had done at a wedding.

He read the file. He read it again. He read the contractor's estimate line by line — demolition of damaged drywall, removal of saturated carpet padding, installation of dehumidification equipment for a minimum of seventy-two hours, replacement of the forty-gallon unit with a comparable model, labor at $85 per hour for an estimated fourteen hours. The numbers were real. They referred to actual things — water that had actually damaged a floor, carpet that was actually ruined, a heater that had actually failed at a seam the way heaters do when they're six years old and the anode rod hasn't been replaced and nobody checks the anode rod because nobody thinks about the thing that keeps the tank from corroding until the tank corrodes. The numbers didn't have opinions about Doug. They didn't think he was brave or pathetic or a genius. They were a claim, and he was an adjuster, and the distance between the problem and the resolution was a thing he could measure and close.

He made notes on the file. He flagged a question about whether the homeowner's policy covered mold remediation or whether that would require a separate rider — the standing water had been there for several hours before discovery, and four inches for several hours in a basement with limited ventilation was enough to start a mold issue that the homeowner might not see for weeks. He paper-clipped the estimate to the photograph of the water line. He put the file on the kitchen table, aligned with the edge, and set his pen on top of it.

It was 2:20 a.m. The phone sat face-down on the couch where he'd left it. The screen was dark. Somewhere — on the internet, in Belgium, in all the places people look at their phones in the middle of the night — millions of people were watching Doug Bridge say the truest things he'd ever said, and none of them knew that the man in the video was sitting in a two-bedroom apartment in Clintonville, in his underwear, at a kitchen table, at 2:20 in the morning, processing a water heater claim from Gahanna because it was the only thing left that made sense.
