# Chapter 4

After the call he'd ignored over the Burris timeline, she called three more times before Doug answered. Twice more he'd watched the same 917 number glow on his phone and let it ring through to the voicemail he had never set up. The fourth time she left a message with the front desk at Mutual Fidelity, and Janet had walked the pink message slip to Doug's desk with a look that said she had questions she was choosing not to ask.

"A Layla Banks called," Janet said. "She says she's a talent manager. She says it's urgent." Janet placed the slip on Doug's keyboard with the care of someone delivering evidence. "She called on the main line, Doug."

"Thank you," Doug said.

"The main line," Janet said again, and went back to her office.

Doug called back that evening from his apartment, standing in the kitchen with a bowl of Grape-Nuts going soft on the counter. He didn't know why he chose that moment. Maybe because the cereal was already ruined and the evening had nowhere else to go.

She picked up before the first ring finished.

"Doug. Doug Bridge. Oh my god, finally. Okay, hi. Hi. I'm Layla Banks, I run Banks Management, and I — okay, first of all, I've watched your video probably thirty times, which I know sounds insane, but I'm going to be really honest with you because I think that's what you respond to based on, you know, the whole toast thing. I think you're sitting on something massive. I'm not talking about the video. I mean, yes, the video, but the video is the door, not the room. The room is — okay, can I just — are you free Thursday? Or Friday. I can fly out tomorrow."

"Fly out where?" Doug said.

"To you. Columbus. I want to sit down with you and talk about what's possible here because what's possible here is — I don't want to do this on the phone. Can I come to you?"

"You want to come to Columbus?"

"I'm looking at flights right now. I can get there Thursday afternoon. Is there somewhere we can meet? Your office, or — actually, I'd rather do it somewhere relaxed. Your place? Is that weird? That's probably weird. A coffee shop. Or your place, whatever's easier."

Doug looked at his apartment. The Ikea bookshelf with the shelf that sagged on the left side. The kitchen table with three chairs because the fourth had broken in the move and he'd never replaced it. The art print from the Columbus gallery that Megan had left behind, which he'd never taken down because the wall behind it was a slightly different shade of white.

"My place is fine," he said, because a coffee shop seemed worse.

"Amazing. Okay. Thursday. Text me the address. This is going to be so good, Doug. I'm — you have no idea. Okay. Thursday."

She hung up. Doug picked up his cereal. The Grape-Nuts had achieved the consistency of damp gravel. He ate them anyway, standing at the counter, because it was seven-thirty on a Tuesday in February and a woman in New York had just booked a flight to come see him, and the Grape-Nuts were the only thing in the apartment that hadn't changed.

---

Layla Banks arrived on Thursday at 4:20 p.m. She was smaller than her voice. Five-three, maybe five-four, in boots that added two inches but didn't change the fundamental impression, which was of someone who moved through space as if she were late for something better. She wore a black leather jacket that looked expensive over a white shirt that looked deliberate, and she carried a bag — not a purse, a bag, the kind with structure and intention — from which she produced, in the first four minutes of being in Doug's apartment, her phone, a laptop, a legal pad, two pens, and a folder with a printed logo that said BANKS MGMT in a font Doug associated with dentist offices.

She shook his hand. Her grip was firm and practiced. She smelled like something — not perfume, something sharper, something that had a name Doug would never know.

"Okay," she said, and she was already past him, already in the living room, scanning. Doug knew that look from damaged houses: first the inventory, then the assessment, then the quiet decision about what was worth what. The bookshelf. The table with three chairs. The print on the wall. The coat hooks by the door with one coat and one jacket, both brown. The kitchen visible through the pass-through, the counter with the cereal box he'd forgotten to put away.

"I love this," Layla said, meaning the apartment, or meaning she was ready to start.

She sat on his couch — the one he'd bought at a Cort furniture clearance eight years ago — and set her phone face-down on the coffee table. She picked it up within ten seconds, checked something, set it down again. This happened three more times before Doug sat in the armchair across from her.

"So," Layla said. "Let me tell you what I'm seeing."

What Layla was seeing, as it turned out, was everything. She talked for twenty-two minutes. Doug knew this because he'd glanced at the microwave clock when she started and glanced again when she paused for the first time, and the difference was twenty-two minutes. In that time, she had outlined what she called "the landscape of opportunity," and the landscape was vast and detailed and apparently ready for immediate construction.

There was the Netflix special. "I've already had preliminary conversations with a development exec there — Charlie Falk, he's great, he totally gets it. They want to meet you. They're thinking a one-hour special, shot somewhere real, not a studio, somewhere that feels like you. Your apartment, maybe. Or not your apartment. We'll figure out the venue."

There was the book deal. "Three publishers have already reached out to me — well, to you, technically, but they can't find you because you don't have representation, which is why you need representation, which is why I'm here. We're talking a humor book, or a memoir, or a humor-memoir hybrid. The category's flexible. The advance would be — I don't want to throw numbers around prematurely because nothing's real until it's real, but we're talking probably mid-six figures. Maybe more. The market for this right now is insane."

There was the TED talk. "They love regular people with a moment. You're the definition of a moment. We'd position it around, like — I don't know — vulnerability? Honesty? The comedy of being real? We'd figure out the framing. But TED is a door that opens other doors."

There was the corporate speaking circuit. "This is where the money actually is. Pharmaceutical companies, insurance companies — oh my god, you literally work in insurance, that's perfect — tech companies, sales conferences, leadership retreats. They pay between fifteen and forty thousand dollars for a keynote, and you'd be doing, I don't know, two or three a month at first. You don't have to be slick. They don't want slick. They want the toast guy."

There was the CBD endorsement.

"Just hear me out," Layla said, raising both hands.

"I don't know what that is," Doug said. It was the first complete sentence he'd managed in twenty-two minutes.

"It's — okay, it's a wellness brand. They do gummies and tinctures and — the point is, they're looking for a spokesperson who's real, who's relatable, and they specifically asked about you. The money is — it's not huge, maybe twenty or thirty thousand, but it's easy money. You hold a bottle, you say something about relaxing, they put it on Instagram."

"I don't have Instagram."

"We'll get you Instagram. That's — honestly, that's a whole other conversation. Your social media is — I mean, you don't have social media, which is actually kind of amazing as a branding angle but it's a nightmare operationally, so we'll deal with that."

There was also, possibly, a podcast.

"Everyone has a podcast," Doug said.

"Exactly! And none of them are interesting! Yours would be interesting because you're not trying to be interesting, you're just —" She gestured at his apartment, at him, at the space between them, encompassing everything. "This."

Doug sat in the armchair. His hands were on the armrests. The upholstery had a texture like corduroy but wasn't corduroy — Megan had told him the name of the fabric once, years before the clearance sale, when they were still people who bought furniture together, and he'd forgotten the word but remembered the conversation, which was the kind of thing his memory did to him.

"Can I ask a question?" Doug said.

"Absolutely. Yes. Ask me anything."

"Who are your other clients?"

Layla's phone buzzed on the coffee table. She flipped it face-down without looking.

"So, Banks Management is a boutique firm. We're intentionally small because I believe in focused attention and — I have a roster of about twelve clients right now. A few podcast hosts, some branded content creators, and Derek Voss — do you know Derek? He was on The Bachelorette, season — I want to say fourteen? Fifteen? He's doing really well. He has a fitness brand and a supplement line."

"I don't watch The Bachelorette."

"That's fine. That's not the point. The point is, I know this space. I know how to take a moment — what you have right now is a moment — and turn it into a career. Not a flash-in-the-pan thing, not a meme lifecycle. An actual career."

She leaned forward. Her hands were on her knees. Her nails were short and painted a color that Doug's mother would have called "unserious." A silver ring on her right hand, not her left — thin, worn to a smooth shine, the kind of ring you didn't take off.

"Doug. People are going to come for you. They're already coming. Producers, publishers, brands, every talent manager in New York and LA who watched that video and did the math the same way I did. The question isn't whether someone's going to help you navigate this. The question is whether you have someone in your corner who understands how this works, or whether you're sitting here answering calls from people who don't have your best interest at heart."

She paused. Her voice had dropped half a register, and Doug couldn't tell if this was strategy or something closer to honest.

"I have your best interest at heart. I also have my own best interest at heart, and I'm going to be straight about that because I think you can tell when someone's bullshitting you."

"Usually," Doug said.

"I think you'd be great for my company and I think I'd be great for you. I think in six months we could be looking at a — I don't want to say a number, but it would be a number that would change what your year looks like. What your next few years look like." She stopped herself, recalculated. "What do you make now? If you don't mind."

"Sixty-one thousand, four hundred," Doug said. "Before taxes."

Layla absorbed this number. Something happened in her face that she recovered from quickly, a microexpression Doug recognized from claims work — the brief recalculation when a homeowner revealed the full extent of the damage and it was less than the adjuster expected. Not disappointment, exactly. Recalibration.

"Right," she said. "Okay. So."

"So," Doug said.

She picked up her phone, set it down, picked it up, set it down. The fourth time, she left it in her hand.

"Can I be really honest with you?"

"You said you were going to be."

"Yeah, I — okay. Here's what I think. I think you could make more money in the next six months than you've made in the last five years. I think the Netflix deal alone could be — I really don't want to say numbers until something's signed. But significant. And the corporates, the book, the — look, I know this is a lot. I know I've been sitting in your apartment for twenty minutes telling you everything's about to change, and you're probably thinking, who is this person and why should I trust her."

Doug wasn't thinking that, exactly. He was thinking about the number five years, which at his salary was roughly three hundred and seven thousand dollars, and about the microwave clock and whether she'd notice if he looked at it again. Then he wasn't thinking about either of those things. He was thinking about how Layla Banks had said his name on the phone — "Doug. Doug Bridge" — with a certainty that made him think she'd been repeating it to herself for days, practicing it because it mattered.

"Okay," Doug said.

"Okay what?"

"I'm interested."

Layla's whole body changed. She sat up straighter. She smiled with her whole face, not just the professional part, and Doug could see the strategy in it and something else he wanted to believe wasn't strategy. It didn't matter that he couldn't separate the two.

"The CBD thing," Doug said. "I'm not sure about the CBD thing."

"Then we don't do gummies. That's side money. I'm talking about the stuff that actually moves your life: Netflix, the book, corporates. That's what this is."

"And the TED talk."

"And the TED talk, eventually. TED moves slow. TED is a longer play."

"And the podcast."

"Okay, the podcast is — honestly, the podcast is just an idea I had on the flight. It might not — we'd figure out the podcast later. The podcast is phase two."

"How many phases are there?"

"There aren't official phases. I'm using 'phases' loosely. The point is, we'd start with the big-ticket items and build from there. You don't have to say yes to everything tonight. You just have to say yes to me."

Doug looked at her. She was leaning forward on his couch, phone in hand, the folder with BANKS MGMT on her lap, and for once she wasn't talking. The thought came to him gradually, factually, without drama: he might not be the only person in the room being offered something they wanted. Layla Banks ran a twelve-person management company out of New York. Her biggest client had been on a reality dating show three or four seasons ago. She was sitting in a two-bedroom apartment in Clintonville on a Thursday afternoon, on a couch that cost four hundred dollars and smelled faintly of its previous home.

She needed this too. Maybe more than he did.

"Okay," Doug said. "Yes."

"Yes?"

"Yes."

"Okay! Oh my god, okay." She was already opening the folder, unclipping something, sliding pages across the coffee table. "I have the paperwork. It's standard representation. I brought it because I was hopeful, but I also didn't want to fly home and have to FedEx it, so —"

She placed the contract in front of him. It was eleven pages, held together with a black binder clip.

---

Doug picked up the contract and read it.

This was not what Layla expected. He could tell because she shifted on the couch three times in the first minute, crossed and uncrossed her legs, and picked up her phone twice without looking at it — a new behavior. Previously she'd at least checked the screen. By page three, the pen in her hand was clicking open and shut against her thumb. He'd seen homeowners get to the same place while he read policies in their living rooms: respect cut with irritation, the irritation winning.

"It's pretty standard," she said, when he was on page four.

"Mmhm," Doug said.

He read in full: every word, every clause, every defined term tracked back to its first appearance. This was the fundamental skill of his profession, the competence on which his career rested: reading a document past the point where most people stopped. Doug had processed over four thousand claims in his time at Mutual Fidelity. He had read policies, endorsements, riders, amendments, proofs of loss, contractor estimates, and enough subrogation correspondence to fill the second bedroom twice over, which was saying something because the second bedroom was already full. He did not skim. He did not flip to the signature page. He read.

The contract was with Banks Management LLC, a New York limited liability company. It established a management relationship granting Layla Banks exclusive representation rights for all entertainment, media, publishing, and endorsement opportunities. The commission structure was twenty percent of gross revenue on entertainment and media, fifteen percent on publishing, and twenty-five percent on endorsements. The term was two years with automatic renewal unless terminated by either party with sixty days' written notice. The exclusivity clause was broad. The termination provisions were narrow.

"Page six," Doug said. "The indemnification clause."

"What about it?"

"It says I indemnify you against any claims arising from my public appearances. That's one-directional. If you book me somewhere and the venue has a liability issue, or if there's a contractual dispute with a third party you introduced, I'm the one carrying the exposure."

Layla blinked. "That's — I mean, that's standard language. Every management contract —"

"It's standard because most people don't read it. Can we make it mutual?"

"Mutual?"

"If I'm indemnifying you, you indemnify me. Same language, reversed. My exposure shouldn't be asymmetric."

Layla looked at him for a long moment. Then she pulled out one of her pens and wrote something on her legal pad. "Okay. Sure. We can do mutual indemnification. What else?"

"Page eight. The revenue definition. It says 'gross revenue from all professional activities entered into during the term of this agreement.' All professional activities."

"Right."

"Does that include my job?"

"Your — at the insurance company?"

"At Mutual Fidelity. If I keep working there — and I'm going to keep working there — this language could be read to include my salary as a professional activity entered into during the term. You'd be entitled to twenty percent of sixty-one thousand, four hundred dollars."

Layla opened her mouth, closed it, and tilted her head at him. For the first time since she'd arrived, she didn't have the next sentence ready. Then she pulled the legal pad closer.

"I would never — that's not the intent, Doug."

"The intent isn't in the contract. The language is in the contract. Can we add an exclusion for pre-existing employment income?"

She wrote on the legal pad again. Faster this time. "Absolutely. We'll carve that out. What else?"

Doug turned to page nine. The apartment was quiet except for the refrigerator, which had a hum that Doug had stopped hearing three years ago but which reasserted itself in silences like this one. Layla sat on the couch. She was not checking her phone. This was, Doug would later realize, the longest he ever saw Layla Banks sit still.

On page nine he found the termination clause. Either party could terminate with sixty days' written notice, but termination did not end the commission obligation — Layla would continue to receive her percentage on all deals initiated during the term, regardless of when revenue was received. Doug recognized this structure. He'd seen it in commercial insurance brokerage agreements: a trailing commission designed to protect the broker after the relationship ended. It was reasonable in principle. In application, it could go on forever.

"The trailing commission," Doug said. "After termination, you receive your percentage on deals negotiated during the term. For how long?"

"For the life of the deal."

"So if you negotiate a book contract in month one and I terminate in month three, you receive fifteen percent of that book's earnings for as long as the book earns?"

"That's how these work."

"I believe you. I'd like to cap it at twenty-four months post-termination."

"Doug."

"It's a trailing commission on a management contract, not a life estate."

Layla stared at him long enough for the refrigerator to take over the room. She bit the inside of her cheek, jaw working. When she answered, she did it slower than she'd said anything all afternoon.

"Thirty-six months," she said.

"Twenty-four."

"Thirty. Final."

Doug considered this. Thirty months was reasonable. It gave Layla protection on deals she'd invested time in while giving him a defined exit. He'd seen worse terms in commercial brokerage. He'd also seen better, but those were negotiated by attorneys billing four hundred dollars an hour, not by insurance adjusters sitting in armchairs on a Thursday evening with cold cereal on the counter.

"Thirty months," Doug said. "With the mutual indemnification and the employment income carve-out."

"Done."

She wrote it down. Her handwriting was fast and angular, the letters connected by strokes that were more intention than ink. She looked up.

"Anything on ten and eleven?"

Doug read pages ten and eleven. Page ten was a standard confidentiality provision — mutual, limited to proprietary business information, with a two-year survival period. Reasonable. Page eleven was signature blocks and a choice-of-law clause specifying New York.

"The governing law is New York," Doug said.

"That's where the company's organized."

"I'm in Ohio."

"Right, but it's — the contract is with a New York LLC. Every one of my agreements uses New York law."

Doug looked at the clause. In insurance, choice-of-law provisions could determine the outcome of a coverage dispute. Which state's regulations applied, which courts had jurisdiction, what standards governed interpretation — these were real decisions with real consequences. But this was a management contract, not a commercial liability policy, and the practical difference between Ohio and New York contract law for a document this size was marginal. He was not going to fight for this one.

"Fine," he said. "The rest is fine."

Layla let out a breath she'd been holding since page six. She put her pen down, sat back, and for a second didn't reach for her phone or her folder or the next point in the pitch. It was the same pause Roy took when a claim file turned out to be unusually complete: respect edged with the discomfort of having underestimated somebody.

"You know," she said, "most of my clients sign these in about thirty seconds."

"Most of your clients are podcast hosts."

She laughed, short and rough enough that she put a hand to her mouth and looked at the floor. When she looked back up, the laugh was gone and the contract was back in her hands.

"Okay," she said. "Let's make it ugly."

She flipped to page six, drew a line through the indemnification language, wrote MUTUAL in the margin, and initialed it. On page eight she wrote EXCLUDES PRE-EXISTING EMPLOYMENT INCOME beside the revenue definition and initialed that too. On page nine she crossed out the open-ended trailing commission language, wrote 30 MONTHS above it, and initialed the change with a fast, angular L.B.

"My attorney will clean this up on Monday," she said. "But if we both sign this version, this is the deal."

Doug read each handwritten change. He checked the page numbers. He checked the signature block.

"You'll sign it too," he said.

"Yes," she said. "Doug. Yes."

He initialed page six, page eight, and page nine. Then he signed page eleven above his printed name, slowly enough that the black ink pooled at the downstrokes. Layla signed beneath him without rereading anything. She clipped the pages back together and slid the contract into her folder.

"Okay," she said, and the speed was back in her voice. "We're signed. I'll have the clean copy in your inbox Monday, and I'll set up the Netflix meeting first thing. Charlie's been waiting. The book publishers can wait another week but not much longer because the window on this kind of opportunity is — it's not forever. The interest is real but interest decays. We need to move."

"How fast?"

"Fast. Not reckless fast. Strategic fast."

She pulled on her jacket. Doug stood up. They were in his living room, which had never felt particularly small until someone with Layla's velocity was in it. She took up more space than her body accounted for. The room had to reorganize itself around her.

"I'm staying at the Hilton downtown," she said. "I fly back tomorrow afternoon. If you think of anything between now and Monday, call me. I answer my phone."

"I know," Doug said. "You picked up before the first ring."

"Yeah, I — I was waiting for you to call back. I'm going to be honest about that. I've been waiting for you to call for two weeks."

She said it without the pitch rhythm, without the forward lean. Just a sentence, flat and unadorned, delivered at the door of his apartment while she adjusted the strap of her bag. For once there was no next idea arriving behind it. Doug believed her. Or wanted to. He pictured the small company, the twelve clients, the former Bachelorette contestant with the fitness brand, and her phone lighting up over and over with numbers that weren't his. He had no way to know if the last two weeks had looked like that. It was what he saw anyway.

"Thank you for coming out here," Doug said.

"Thank you for picking up the phone, babe. I'll talk to you Monday."

And she was through the door, and the folder with his signature in it was under her arm and then in the hallway and then gone. The apartment returned to its normal dimensions. The refrigerator hummed. The bookshelf sagged on the left. The cereal box was still open on the counter.

He went to the kitchen. There was a black streak of ink along the side of his hand where the pen had dragged when he signed. The terms were still in his head: twenty percent on entertainment and media, fifteen on publishing, twenty-five on endorsements, exclusivity, automatic renewal, mutual indemnification, the salary carve-out, thirty months on the tail, New York law.

He poured a bowl of Grape-Nuts, left a thumbprint on the side of the bowl, and ate standing at the counter.
