# Chapter 10

By morning, the clinic was three subway stops away and half a block up from Roosevelt Avenue, over a laundromat that vented hot detergent breath onto the sidewalk. The morning was wet and warm, the kind of early-summer air that stuck to Anika's right shin before she even got off the train. Every step up the stairs made the bone throb in a thin bright line, like somebody had slipped a heated wire under her skin and pulled it tight. Ravi walked ahead with a gray folder tucked under his arm, hospital imaging reports in plastic sleeves, insurance letters folded and refolded until the creases looked polished. He kept glancing at his watch and then at her feet, counting how evenly she landed. He stayed on logistics. "You ate?" When she nodded, he followed with, "Protein too?" He treated meals as one more strap on a brace.

The door said KEATING SPORTS REHAB in white vinyl. Under it, a second sign said RETURN-TO-PLAY COMPLIANCE IS A SAFETY REQUIREMENT, NOT A SUGGESTION. The waiting room had six molded chairs and a water cooler that coughed every thirty seconds. A college sprinter in a knee brace scrolled through highlight clips with the sound on, then turned it off when a front-desk woman raised one eyebrow at him. Anika sat with her backpack between her shoes. She had Maya's digital watch on her wrist and Maya's half-used notebook in the bag, the page for today already ruled into boxes: pain, sleep, swelling, minutes standing, minutes pretending not to limp.

When Tess Keating came out, she carried a chipped steel mug that said DON'T SKIP THE BORING STUFF. She was broader through the shoulders than Anika expected, and a pale old scar curved from the edge of her left tank strap toward her collarbone. Her shoelaces were bright orange, double-knotted, mismatched by one eyelet, and she fixed the mismatch mid-step without looking down. "Anika Iyer?" she said. Her voice was low and steady, not soft. "Ravi, good to meet you in person. Come on back." She tapped her clipboard twice with a mechanical pencil before leading them through the gym.

The rehab room smelled of disinfectant, rubber mats, and the citrus cleaner they used on treadmills. Bands hung by color on a pegboard. A camera sat on a tripod facing a strip of blue tape on the floor for gait footage. Somewhere behind the treatment curtain a printer kept spitting single pages, each one followed by a frustrated little curse from somebody Anika could not see. She liked that sound more than she should have. Adults usually hid panic. Here someone let it out in plain English and then kept working.

Tess set the folder on a table. "Before we test anything, I need three numbers from you," she said to Anika. "Pain right now, worst pain in the last twenty-four hours, and how much you're scared of making it worse if you move. Zero to ten for all three." Anika said, "Two, four, one." She gave the numbers quickly so they would not be argued with. Tess looked at her for a beat that was not accusatory, just patient in a way that felt harder to dodge. "Try again," she said. "Use the number your body would give me, not the number you think keeps everyone calm." Ravi shifted his weight. "She just told you." Tess nodded at him but kept her eyes on Anika. "I heard her. I'm asking her to answer."

Anika stared at the seam where two mats met, a dark line full of chalk dust. "Three, six, seven," she said. The seven came out like a theft. Tess wrote it down without reaction. "Good. Shoes off. We'll start simple." She filmed Anika walking, first normal pace, then heel-to-toe, then single-leg balance. On the right side Anika's toes clawed at the mat and her shoulder tipped forward. Tess crouched by her ankle, fingers warm and dry against swollen tissue. "You're guarding before load, not just during load," she said. "That means your brain's already bracing for impact." Ravi leaned in. "She's careful. That's good." Tess stood. "Protective movement is useful short term. Long term it shifts force into places that are still healthy and turns one injury into two."

The first real test was a step-down from an eight-inch box. Left leg planted, right heel tapping the floor, ten reps each side. Anika flew through the left side. On the right she made it to rep five before her knee wobbled inward. By rep seven her jaw clenched so hard her molars squeaked. By rep eight a hot pulse climbed from shin to hip, and her right foot landed flat with a slap loud enough to turn the sprinter in the brace. Tess held up a hand. "Stop." Anika did one more rep anyway because stopping in the middle of a set made her throat close up. Tess stepped in and physically guided her down from the box. "That right there," she said, still calm, "is exactly what I mean."

"We're doing calf raises next. Fifteen on each side, ninety seconds rest between sets." Tess handed her the rail. Anika finished the first set and started again at twenty-three seconds without looking at the timer. Tess put a flat palm on the rail in front of her. "Rest means rest." Anika bounced on her toes. "I'm fine." Tess shook her head once. "No. You're scared of stillness. That isn't fine and it isn't your fault, but it is part of the rehab." Ravi exhaled through his nose. "Can we stay on the injury and not do pop psychology?" Tess took the pencil from behind her ear, clicked it once, and said, "This is the injury. If she can't tolerate thirty quiet seconds, she can't tolerate the load decisions we need her to make on court."

Ravi crossed his arms. "She's had six weeks down already. She's got regionals in July. We need a return date, not a lecture." Tess pulled up the gait video on a tablet and replayed it at half speed. Anika watched her own body flinch before her foot hit the ground. On screen her shoulders pitched forward and her right hip dropped a beat early, her whole frame braced for impact before impact arrived. Tess tapped the frame where her hip dropped. "Here's your return date answer," she said. "Phase one is no-impact conditioning and movement correction until this pattern changes. Phase two is controlled court footwork at capped volume. Phase three is monitored competitive load. You skip phase one and she lands back in imaging, maybe with both legs compromised." Ravi's voice stayed low enough that it sounded polite to anyone not standing three feet away. "You're being conservative because you've got legal exposure. We're the ones paying when she misses her window."

The word pay sat in the room heavier than the medicine balls by the wall. Anika could hear the collections voicemail from last week in her head, the same woman each month reading digits in a flat commuter cadence: thirty-eight thousand four hundred due, minimum payment due fifteenth, account status at risk. Tess did not pretend not to understand. "I know rankings are time-sensitive," she said. "I also know a repeat fracture can shut down an entire season, not a month. My job is to keep her playing longer than one desperate summer." Ravi gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Your job ends at this room. Our life doesn't."

Tess set the tablet facedown. "I need five minutes with Anika alone. Standard for pediatric overuse cases." Ravi's head came up fast. "No." Tess held his stare. "It's standard. You can stand at the glass if that helps." He looked at Anika for a second, not asking but expecting. She kept her face still. After a long beat he stepped back, moved to the hallway, and stopped exactly where he could see through the half-open door.

Tess sat on a low bench so her eyes were level with Anika's. "I'm going to ask direct questions," she said. "You can say pass, but if you pass on everything I still have to document that." Anika nodded. The room sounded louder without Ravi breathing in it. "How often do you hide pain from your dad?" Tess asked. Anika picked at a frayed seam on her shorts. "Not hide. Just... round down." Tess waited. "How often?" "Most days before the fracture. Sometimes now." "Why?" Anika shrugged. "If I say five, everything stops. If everything stops, I can't catch up." Tess's expression did not soften, but it changed shape. "Catch up to what?" Anika looked at Maya's watch instead of Tess's face. The seconds flashed and reset and flashed. "To debt. To everyone else. To the part where people stop looking at me like broken glass."

Tess wrote a note. "Thank you for being honest. Next question. Are you sleeping?" "Enough." Tess lifted one eyebrow. "That sounded like another rounded number." Anika pressed her tongue against a sore spot in her cheek. "I sleep with my headphones in. I play my mom's voicemail until I pass out. If I wake up, I play it again." She waited for pity, then for a speech. Tess gave neither. "That's grief management, not sleep," she said. "It makes sense and it isn't sustainable. The program protocol includes counseling check-ins for exactly this pattern. It isn't punishment. It's injury prevention for the part of you we can't tape." Anika stared at the resistance bands, all those clean colors waiting to be pulled thin. "Do I have to talk a lot?" she asked. "You have to show up and answer truthfully enough that the counselor can do their job."

When Ravi came back in, Tess switched to public voice, precise and unadorned. "Here's the plan, in order. Pain log in the compliance app by nine p.m. every night." Ravi stayed standing, one hand on the back of the chair he refused to use. "If pain goes above four during work, we stop immediately and hold load for forty-eight hours." Anika felt her throat tighten at the word stop. "Two PT sessions a week with me, one counseling check-in with program behavioral health, no unsupervised impact work until phase two clearance." Ravi did not sit. "Counseling isn't in her orthopedic order." Tess pointed at the top page in the folder, where Return-to-Play Restriction Order was stamped above the imaging summary. "The order gives the medical team discretion on safety conditions. Sleep disruption and fear spikes are safety conditions. This is one." "This is mission creep." "This is what keeps her eligible."

Ravi stepped closer until his knuckles pressed the folder flat. "Strike counseling from her file and we'll finish your sessions. Leave it in, we walk tonight and I file a parent complaint before breakfast." Tess did not raise her voice. She pulled a one-page form from the clipboard, slid it across the table, and tapped the header: WITHDRAWAL AGAINST MEDICAL ADVICE. "If you walk, this goes in her case by 8 a.m.," she said. "Tournament medical sees it, marks noncompliance, and freezes her slot pending review. You're her parent. You can choose conflict. You can't choose immunity from policy." The air felt packed tight enough to bite. Anika looked from one face to the other and understood something she had not seen before: adults did not agree in private and then perform unity for children. Sometimes they fought in front of you because the rule lived somewhere bigger than the kitchen table.

The session ended with clamshells, glute bridges, and ten minutes on an assault bike with the seat set too low for comfort. Anika's shin did not spike above four, but fear did. Every rest interval felt like swallowing a stone. Tess wrote exercises on a printout and drew a column labeled "What I felt when I stopped." At the bottom she added her direct line number and then, in smaller handwriting, "If Ravi asks for extra, tell him I said no." She handed the page to Anika, not Ravi. "Bring this back with real numbers, not pretty ones."

On the subway home, Ravi kept scrolling through the protocol packet with his heel hammering a hard metronomic beat into the train floor. At Jackson Heights-Roosevelt he almost missed the stop because he was drafting an email with his thumbs moving so hard the phone clicked against its case. He did not show her the message, but she saw the subject line reflected in the train window when the lights hit it: REQUEST FOR ACCELERATED RETURN EXCEPTION - ANIYA IYER. He always misspelled her name when he was angry and typing fast. She wanted to correct him and did not.

The apartment was hot and smelled of curry leaves toasted too long in oil. Ravi turned on the box fan in the kitchen and called program operations before he even took off his shoes. Anika stood in the hall and listened to his half of the conversation while she unlaced her sneakers slowly, one loop at a time. "No, I understand policy," he said. "I'm asking for individualized judgment." Pause. "Because she's not average. Because we can't lose another month." He listened, then rubbed his eyes hard enough to leave red crescents under them. The voice on speaker was a woman named Nadia from compliance; she sounded young and exhausted and kept pausing to sip water before each sentence, her throat clearing between lines. "Mr. Iyer, I'm not dodging you," she said. "It's just... this case is in the auto queue now. If your daughter misses a report or breaks plan, the system slaps a lock on the slot and kicks it to medical review." Ravi said, "Then unlock it." Nadia gave a tired little laugh that did not mock him, just admitted the machine. "I wish I could. I can't touch that switch from ops, and my lead can't either." Anika stepped into the kitchen before she could talk herself out of it. "If I do every part exactly, does my slot stay live even if he files complaints?" she asked toward the speaker. Ravi snapped, "Anika, not now." Nadia was quiet for a beat, then answered her anyway. "If you comply, your slot stays provisional-active. Complaints don't erase medical terms." Ravi ended the call without saying goodbye.

At 6:42 p.m., the formal warning arrived. Ravi's laptop chimed once. He opened the message, read the first paragraph, and went very still. The subject line was in all caps: COMPLIANCE NOTICE - CHAPTER 8 FRACTURE CASE - ELIGIBILITY CONDITIONS. Anika watched over his shoulder from the doorway while the fan chopped the room into warm gusts.

> Any breach of prescribed rehabilitation protocol, including unsanctioned impact activity, missed daily pain reports, or refusal of scheduled behavioral-health check-ins, will trigger immediate provisional suspension of Junior Performance Program eligibility pending medical review.

Ravi read the line twice, fingers dug so hard into the trackpad the cursor jittered.

The next section opened in bullet points, each line appearing with a soft blue check as Ravi scrolled: daily pain report due by 9:00 p.m.; pain above four triggers a forty-eight-hour load hold; appeals had to be filed within one business day of a suspension flag. He read each line under his breath like he could wear the ink down. Anika tracked the deadlines against the red circle around the fifteenth on the wall calendar.

It copied Tess, program medical staff, and tournament administration. It ended with a plain signature block: *Nadia Serrano, Compliance Coordinator*. Under her name, in smaller text, was a line that made Anika blink: *Please allow up to one business day for response; I coach my son's Little League team from 5-7 p.m.*

Ravi shut the laptop halfway and opened it again. "This is intimidation," he said. "They're putting language in writing so they have cover later." Anika stepped into the kitchen. Her shin ached from standing, but she planted both feet and stayed there. "If we follow it, my slot stays live, right?" Ravi snapped, "It's not that simple." Then, softer, "These people only see a checklist." She looked at the open email and the boxes Tess had drawn on the exercise sheet and the red circle around the fifteenth on the wall calendar where the oncology payment was due. "Then the checklist still runs the slot," she said. "For now, that's enough."

He pulled up the compliance app and turned the screen toward her. "Enter pain as two tonight. You handled the session." Anika took the phone and typed four for peak pain, three for fear, and yes for sleep disruption. Her fingers trembled, but she left each number where it landed. Ravi saw the numbers and reached for the phone. "Keep it at two." The words came out sharp, then frightened. She held the phone against her chest. "This is what happened." He drew breath to argue. She beat him to it, voice smaller than she wanted but steady. "No. I'm not typing fake numbers again. It hit four at clinic, and my shin shot fire up to my hip on rep eight. If I shave it to two, the crack comes back and we're back in imaging. I'm done calling that bad luck."

For a long second he stared at her as if she had switched languages. The fan rattled. Somebody upstairs dragged furniture across the floor. "Send it," he said finally. Then he pointed at the screen. "From tomorrow, every entry happens with me in the room. You don't talk to Keating alone again. You don't send numbers behind my back." He sat down hard, opened his laptop, and started a new email to program medical with the subject line REQUEST FOR CASE REVIEW.

Heat rushed into Anika's face so fast her ears rang. The watchband bit into the damp skin of her wrist, and for a second she heard Maya's voicemail voice in her head: write it down before anybody renames it. Because Ravi had just claimed the room, she opened the notebook on the counter, drew a hard line under the app entry, and printed in block letters: 6:49 P.M. - DAD WATCHING REQUIRED FOR ALL REPORTS.

Later, after dinner, Anika filled in Maya's notebook with the app numbers and the exercises Tess had given her while Ravi typed in the kitchen and did not turn on the TV. In the margin she added a new column: "Who said no." For years that column would have had one name in it, always his. Tonight it had two: Tess and Anika. She sat on the edge of her bed, put in one earbud, and did not play the voicemail. The apartment pipes knocked and hissed; a siren climbed and fell on Northern Boulevard; the fan in the kitchen clicked every ninth turn. Stillness was not quiet. It was a waiting room.

At 8:58 she submitted the second pain check Tess had asked for after evening exercises while Ravi stood in the doorway and watched her thumb hit send. The app flashed green: COMPLIANT. In the kitchen his laptop pinged as the draft went out to medical and compliance, contesting the counseling requirement and requesting provider reassignment. On the wall calendar, noon at hospital records was underlined twice; storage billing posted at midnight whether anyone slept or not.
