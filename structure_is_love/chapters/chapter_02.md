# Chapter 2

The schedule Ravi had taped to the fridge the night before was already curling at the corners, even after he had pressed it flat with both thumbs. Black marker blocks filled the page: court, shower, school, commute, court again. The only white space sat between 12:02 and 12:26, lunch period, and even that had `EAT` written across it in hard capital letters.

Both signatures still sat at the bottom margin, his fast slanted line under her careful letters, as if ink could hold the week together.

At 6:38 a.m., after ninety minutes of ghosting drills and one bus transfer in wet cold, Anika stood in the apartment kitchen in socks that smelled like damp rubber. The radiator clicked without warming the room. Ravi worked the stove with one hand and his phone with the other, leaning his hip against the counter to spare his stiff left ankle. Mustard seeds popped for his eggs; curry leaf smoke threaded into the steam from her instant oatmeal.

"You're three minutes behind," he said, still looking at the pan. "Eat while it's hot."

Anika sat, opened her math notebook, and forced down two spoonfuls. Her throat still tasted like iron from sprint repeats.

Ravi's phone buzzed. He had already built her afternoon in his calendar: bus to rec center, dynamic warm-up, feet-and-lunge circuit, thirty serve targets. He forwarded it anyway. Her own phone lit up on the table with his name and the same plan in short lines.

`Leave school gate 3:22.`
`Route Q49 or walk to 74 if late.`
`No detours.`
`Text when you're on bus.`

He finally glanced at her bowl. "You ate half yesterday and cramped."

"I'm eating."

He pointed his spoon at the oatmeal, then at the wall clock. "Finish it. You need fuel."

The words were practical. The tone was practical. The feeling in the room was not practical at all. It was the feeling of being watched while she chewed.

She got through three more spoonfuls and chased them with water because her stomach had started climbing into her throat. Ravi slid a banana toward her lunch bag and a folded napkin with almonds counted into a neat stack, twelve exactly.

"You look pale," he said. "Did you sleep?"

"Yeah."

"How many hours?"

"Six. Maybe six and a half."

He started to correct her estimate, then stopped. "Tonight you need seven. We can't keep wasting recovery."

He said `we` when he meant her body.

By the time she got to school, her fingers were still wrinkled from the too-hot shower she had used to scrub off court smell. A clean hoodie did not help. Rubber ball, bleach mop water, and old sweat clung to the fabric like a second skin.

In homeroom, fluorescent lights drilled straight through her molars. Social studies started with a worksheet about city government. She wrote her name, watched the letters drag across the page, and stalled at question one: Explain the role of public oversight in municipal decision-making.

Ms. DeMarco moved between desks with iced coffee in one hand and hall passes in the other. She taught with a soft Queens rasp and twisted a fox-shaped ring on her thumb whenever she was trying not to snap at kids.

"Morning, team," she said. "Ten minutes silent work, then we'll discuss. Heads up, no phones out."

Anika pressed her pencil harder so the graphite left grooves she could feel through the paper. She answered two questions by memory, one by guessing, and then lost three minutes in a blink that did not feel like a blink. Her chin dropped. The pencil slipped from her fingers and carved a dark line across the worksheet and onto her wrist.

A hand tapped her desk once.

"Anika."

She jerked up so fast her chair screeched. Jerome in the back snorted. Zoe, two rows left, turned in her seat with one eyebrow raised.

Ms. DeMarco crouched beside the desk instead of calling her out across the room. It made everything worse.

"Walk with me for a second," the teacher murmured.

The hallway was empty except for a custodian rolling a trash bin toward the stairwell. Anika stood against cinderblock while Ms. DeMarco checked the worksheet and then checked her face.

"You fell asleep mid-sentence," Ms. DeMarco said quietly. "Are you sick?"

Anika wiped graphite off her wrist with her sleeve. "No."

"Did you eat breakfast?"

"Yeah."

"Are you getting enough sleep at home?"

Anika kept her eyes on the chipped blue stripe painted across the wall. "I'm fine. I just woke up early."

Ms. DeMarco waited, like patience might pull a truer answer out of the first one. When nothing came, she twisted the fox ring once and exhaled.

"You don't have to tell me everything," she said. "But you just fell asleep on a worksheet. If this keeps happening, I call home and loop in the counselor. I can't ignore it."

Anika's stomach folded tighter. "Please don't call my dad. I'm okay."

"I'm sending a quick email now so we can get ahead of it. You're one of my strongest kids, and this isn't your usual."

The email already existed in Anika's head before Ms. DeMarco finished the sentence: Concern about fatigue in class. She could see Ravi opening it between dispatch calls, jaw tightening, turning worry into rules.

"I said I'm fine," Anika said. The second time came out flatter.

Ms. DeMarco did not argue. "Go splash water on your face, then come back in."

In the bathroom, Anika cupped cold tap water against her skin until it stung. Her eyes looked puffy, purple moons sat under both, and damp roots still clung to her scalp from the morning shower. She held her breath and counted to twelve the way she counted ghost reps. At eleven, her phone buzzed in her hoodie pocket even though it should have been off.

Dad: `How was first period?`

She stared.

She typed: `Fine.`

Three dots appeared almost immediately.

Dad: `Teacher said you nodded off.`
Dad: `Send lunch photo before bell.`
Dad: `If you don't reply, I call the main office.`

Her lie felt soft for one second, then set like plaster. `Fine` had been one word. It hardened fast.

When she got back to class, Ms. DeMarco drew a flowchart on the board. "Oversight isn't punishment," she said. "In theory, oversight protects people when one person has too much unchecked control."

Anika copied the sentence into the margin because it sounded like something she should remember, even if she did not know where to put it yet.

By second-period math, hunger had turned mean. It bit, vanished, then bit again under her ribs. She ate the almonds during independent practice, one at a time, hiding each bite behind her hand. The worksheet held fractions; her brain kept substituting route numbers and court times. 3:22 gate. 4:05 court. 4:12 first drill.

At 10:47 another message arrived while classes changed.

Dad: `Main office copied me on the wellness note.`
Dad: `If anyone asks, say you had insomnia.`
Dad: `Do not mention 4:12 drills.`

She read the last line twice. It was the first time he had written the concealment out plain.

In science, the room smelled like vinegar and old aquarium water. Mr. Patel asked for volunteers. Zoe raised her hand before the instructions were finished because Zoe always liked anything that might explode. While Mr. Patel measured baking soda, Zoe leaned back and whispered without moving her lips.

"You look dead," Zoe said. "Like Victorian dead, not regular dead."

Anika snorted once despite herself. "Thanks."

"I'm serious. You okay?"

"Yeah."

Zoe squinted. "That was a fast yeah."

Anika pulled her science notebook closer and pointed at the board as if acid-base reactions had suddenly become urgent. The demo foamed over the beaker lip. Kids cheered. Mr. Patel laughed and cursed when fizz hit his sleeve.

Under the noise, her phone buzzed again.

Dad: `Counselor request came through.`
Dad: `I told them no intervention needed.`
Dad: `Do not contradict me at school.`

Zoe caught the movement and lifted both eyebrows. "You're texting in Patel's class? Bold."

"It's just my dad."

"Everything okay at home?"

The wording was casual. The way Zoe asked was not casual at all.

Anika tucked the phone away. "Yeah. He's just being annoying."

Zoe nodded, but she kept watching.

Lunch started with rain tapping the cafeteria windows and a pizza line snaking past the vending machines. Anika took the standard tray: square slice, milk carton, bruised pear. She was not hungry until food sat in front of her. Then she was starving and nauseated at the same time.

Zoe dropped into the seat across from her, backpack still on one shoulder. "My mom said I can go to Astoria Park if I finish homework first. You wanna come? Me, you, maybe Dani if she stops fake-flirting with eighth graders."

Anika peeled the pear sticker and stuck it to the table edge. "I can't. I've got practice."

"You always have practice."

"It's Tuesday."

"So? Tuesdays still have sky."

Anika tried to smile. Zoe drummed chipped black nails against her milk carton.

"I'm not asking you to skip your whole life," Zoe said. "Just one hour. We hit the deli, we get fries, we do math on a bench, we pretend we're normal middle school idiots for sixty minutes."

The thought of fries made Anika's stomach lurch, half desire, half acid.

Her phone buzzed.

Dad: `Lunch pic now.`
Dad: `Finish tray or warm-up becomes suicides.`
Dad: `Leave gate 3:22. Rain slows buses.`

She angled the tray, snapped a photo before Zoe could see, and sent it.

Zoe watched her thumb move and leaned back. "Wow. You have parole-officer texts."

"Shut up."

"I'm not joking, Niks."

Anika bristled at the nickname and the tenderness inside it. "It's not like that. He's tracking practice, not my whole life."

"Then what's it like?"

Anika bit pizza crust and chewed too long so she would not have to answer. She could feel Ms. DeMarco's email hanging over the day like a second fluorescent light. She could feel Ravi building rules from every adult who noticed.

"It's temporary," she said finally. "My sleep got messed up. That's all."

"From what?"

"From waking up at 4:12 and getting lunch audits by text."

"That's not temporary, Niks."

Anika set down the crust. "I can't do this every lunch, Zoe."

"Do what."

"The home questions. The dad questions. Don't ask me again at school."

A ketchup packet burst at the next table, red fan on dirty tile. A lunch aide shouted for paper towels. Under the cafeteria roar, Zoe lowered her voice.

"Is this about your mom?"

Anika's throat closed on dry crust. She reached for milk that was already gone.

"I'm fine," she said, crushing the carton until the seam popped and milk dampened her knuckles. "It's sleep. It's practice."

Zoe slumped forward. "You keep saying that like you're reading from a card."

"I mean it, Zoe. Don't do this at school," Anika said, and heard how dead she sounded.

The words left chalk on her tongue.

Zoe sat back. Hurt flashed across her face, then annoyance, then the ghost of a joke that never arrived. "Okay," she said. "Cool. Be fine."

She opened her sketchbook and started drawing the cafeteria salt shaker with violent little strokes. Anika watched graphite carve shadow and forgot to ask about Zoe's art magnet open house, the one Zoe had mentioned twice this week.

Her screen lit again.

Dad: `Did you finish tray? yes/no.`
Dad: `I need the same answer if school follows up.`

She typed `yes` even though half the pizza was still there.

Dad: `Good.`
Dad: `Pack at 3:18.`

The afternoon dragged and jumped. In English she missed two inferencing questions because she read the same paragraph four times and kept seeing route numbers instead of words. In art, paint thinner gave her a headache. At 2:41, Ms. DeMarco appeared at the classroom door and asked to see her in the hallway again.

"I heard back from your dad," Ms. DeMarco said quietly. "He says this is a new training routine and he's monitoring your food, sleep, and movement."

Anika's pulse stuttered. "He told you that?"

"He told me enough to worry me."

Anika stared at the locker vents. Rain rattled the stairwell windows.

Ms. DeMarco kept her voice low. "Hear me. When a grown-up controls your food, your time, and who you see with threats, that's coercive control. Calling it coaching doesn't make it okay."

The phrase hit harder than any of Ravi's texts had. She had felt the shape of it all day and never named it.

"I'm not trying to blow up your life," Ms. DeMarco went on. "But you keep falling asleep in class and freezing every time that phone lights up. If that keeps up, I file before dismissal. That means counselor first, then assistant principal, and maybe ACS screening. I'm not threatening you. I'm telling you what happens next."

The institutional consequence was spelled out in hallway light.

Anika gripped her sleeve seam until thread cut into her finger. "Please don't file anything today. I'll fix it."

"You can't fix this alone," Ms. DeMarco said. "And you shouldn't have to."

"Just give me one day."

Ms. DeMarco studied her for a long beat, then nodded once. "One day. Tomorrow, first period, we check again. If you nod off in my room, I file."

Anika nodded.

As she turned back toward class, Ms. DeMarco added, "You're eleven. You're allowed to act eleven. Remember that."

The line followed her inside and sat there like an impossible assignment.

At 3:11 the warning chime rang. Backpacks zipped. Chairs scraped. Hallway noise surged toward stairwells. Zoe caught Anika at her locker, breathless, holding two bus cards like a magic trick.

"I topped this one for you," Zoe said. "Grandma gave me ten bucks for helping with laundry. Don't fight me. We're going to the park, we split fries, we do math on a bench, and we act normal for one hour."

Anika froze with one sneaker half-tied. The lace bit into her fingers. Her pulse kept thudding in her throat like the warning chime had moved under her skin.

"I can't take your card."

"You can. It's ten dollars, not a kidney."

"I've got to go."

The words came out thin, almost polite.

Zoe stepped in front of the locker before Anika could close it. "Practice can wait one day."

"No, it can't."

"Why? Is your dad gonna send a SWAT team?"

Her phone buzzed once, then again, then a third time.

Dad: `3:18. Pack now.`
Dad: `Where are you?`
Dad: `Gate photo in one minute.`

Each vibration jumped through her pocket seam and into her jaw. She did not show Zoe. She shoved books into her backpack and missed the zipper track the first time.

Zoe's voice lost its joke layer. "Tell me what's going on."

"My dad hears a late minute like it's a smoke alarm. That's what's going on."

"That's bullshit."

"If I'm late, he cuts warm-up and adds suicides."

"So that's normal now? Your dad runs your day by text and I'm supposed to shut up?"

Ms. DeMarco's phrase from the hallway flashed hard and clean in her head: coercive control. Hearing Zoe say it in friend voice made Anika want to peel out of her own skin.

The hallway thinned as kids poured toward the stairs. Rain hammered the stairwell glass. Anika could feel each second like a countdown beep.

"I've got training," she said. "He's already asking for gate proof."

"You haven't explained anything."

"I don't have time to miss this bus and pay for it on court."

"Right. You've got time for ladders and ghosting and your dad's control texts, but you don't have one hour for me."

She could already see Court 3 in her head: orange cones, Ravi's watch, the look he wore when minutes became punishment.

Anika yanked the zipper free where it had caught fabric.

"Zoe."

"What."

"I've got ninety seconds to make the stop."

Zoe laughed once, sharp. "You mean friendship? Yeah. I noticed."

Her chest pinched so hard she almost said the true sentence: I'm scared of what happens if I'm late.

That landed harder than morning drills. Anika wanted to say she was sorry. She wanted to say please wait. She wanted to say she did not know how to hold grief and school and Ravi's fear and hunger and still sound like herself. What came out was the smallest, safest sentence.

"I'm fine."

Zoe stared at her for a long second, then folded the extra bus card in half and shoved it back into her pocket.

"Have fun being fine," she said, and turned for the stairs without looking back.

Anika stood by the locker bank while the corridor emptied. Another vibration crawled up her pocket seam.

Dad: `Miss this bus and we add fourteen suicides.`
Dad: `Move.`

She moved.

Outside, rain slid down the back of her neck. She pulled up her hood and ran the half block to the Q49 stop, backpack pounding between her shoulder blades. The bus arrived crowded and breathing diesel. She wedged into the aisle, one hand on a wet pole, and sent a gate photo through the fogged window.

Dad: `Good.`
Dad: `Sip water now.`

She obeyed automatically, unscrewing the bottle with numb fingers. Water tasted like plastic and old metal. At the next light, two girls from school crossed in front of the bus sharing earbuds, laughing hard enough that one had to grab the other's sleeve to stay upright. Anika watched them until the doors hissed shut.

She opened her notes app. It started as logistics and slipped toward confession:

`social studies asleep 8:19`
`Ms D said coercive control`
`welfare report if tomorrow same`
`Zoe's face when she said Be fine`
`lunch finished (not true)`
`gate 3:24`

The list sat there like evidence she had typed against herself.

Rain smeared the bus window until Queens looked waterlogged and unreal. Then the rec center pushed through the blur: chain-link fence, wet concrete, windows bleeding dull yellow. Rubber-ball echo leaked through the doors before she touched the handle.

Ravi stood by the vending machine in depot uniform, reflective stripes at his cuffs, eyes on the clock. He checked her wet hoodie, then her phone screen, then his own.

"Gate photo says 3:24," he said. "Schedule says 3:22."

"Zoe stopped me at my locker."

"Then you keep walking next time." He turned toward Court 3. "Warm-up is gone. Fourteen suicides for the delay, then ladder."

"Dad, the bus was--"

"No. You don't lose minutes and negotiate."

Court 3 smelled like wet rubber and old mop water. Two middle-school boys on the next court went quiet when Ravi set orange cones on the service line and back wall. The phrase Ms. DeMarco had used in the hallway came back hard: coercive control. Here it looked like stopwatch math, three late minutes turned into fourteen suicides in front of witnesses. He clicked his stopwatch and pointed.

"Touch the line every turn. Hand down so I can see it."

Anika dropped her backpack by the glass and started running. Baseline to short line, backpedal, baseline again. On six, her calves knotted. On nine, acid climbed the back of her throat. On eleven, her right palm slapped varnish a fraction late.

"That one didn't touch," Ravi said. "Again."

She ran it again with her hand flat. Then one more because she stumbled at the wall and he kept the watch moving.

By fourteen, her lungs were tearing at air and black dots swam in the corners of her vision. Kids from the next session filed past the glass pretending not to look. A seventh-grade girl she recognized from Saturday clinics did look, then looked away so fast her ponytail snapped.

When Ravi finally said stop, Anika bent over the trash can and vomited thin milk and bile while her hamstrings trembled. Her chest burned. Spit hung from her lip and hit the rim.

Ravi wrote `+14` on the printout clipped to his board. "Now we start," he said. "No overthinking."

The phrase landed like a lock turning.

Anika wiped her mouth with the back of her wrist, straightened, and stepped back onto the service box because she did not know what else to do with a body that had already been made into proof.
