# Chapter 5

Rain had started before dawn and did not commit to it. It stayed in the air like spit, too fine to dodge, enough to make the bus windows sweat. Anika stood over the heater grate with her bag against her shins, watching traffic slide and stop and slide again on Northern Boulevard, every red light eating into the warm-up block Ravi had written on last night's torn envelope and taped by the fridge. Maya's old digital watch sat loose on her wrist and clicked each minute with a tiny plastic jump. 5:22. 5:23. 5:24.

When they got off at the rec center, the sidewalk shone like dark fish skin. Ravi already had his keycard out, already moving fast enough that she had to half-jog behind him under the awning.

"You're two minutes under where we need to be," he said without looking back. "We trim dynamic stretch, keep racket prep. Qualifier volume starts now."

The lobby smelled of bleach and wet coats. Beyond the desk, the courts were lit in hard white strips, each glass wall ghosting a second copy of whoever moved inside it. The HVAC coughed awake and settled into its metallic rattle, louder than the rain. Anika dropped her bag beside Court 3 and started rope.

First fifty jumps, her legs felt wooden and obedient. On jump sixty-two, the right shin gave a small bright warning, like somebody snapping a pencil inside the bone. She clipped the rope, reset, went again. The second warning came earlier, at thirty-eight. She kept her face flat and looked at the service line like it had offended her.

Ravi stood outside the glass with his phone timer and a pen tucked over his ear, already drafting the morning in rows: ghosting, lunge matrix, feeds, retrievals, interval sprints. He tapped the glass once and held up four fingers. Four rounds.

She switched to ghosting. Front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right. Split step, push, recover. On the first set she could keep the pain in a narrow lane, a hot stripe along the inner edge of her right shin that vanished if she landed exactly on the ball of the foot. On the second set it thickened, climbed toward her knee, and bit every time she decelerated. The court floor felt half a centimeter farther away than usual. She kept missing it.

"Lower center," Ravi called through the door gap. "You're standing up out of the lunge."

"I know," she said.

"Don't know it. Fix it."

She dropped lower. In the front-right corner, the pain sharpened into a clean point that made her vision blink once, white at the edges. She came up too fast and pretended she had meant to reset her grip.

Ravi stepped in, took the racket from her, and placed her right foot with two fingers at the toe. "You can't cheat the distance. If you shorten here, you get punished later. Again."

His hand was warm through her shoe. His own left ankle made a faint hitch when he stepped back, the old stiffness he never named unless it was to call it laziness in his younger self. She had learned his body the way you learned court lines: where it would hold, where it would not.

She lunged again, driving into the front-right corner three times in a row.

By rep eight, each push-off knocked her breath off count; inhale came late, exhale leaked out as a hiss. By rep twelve the throb had teeth. Even standing still, she felt a low buzz in the bone, as if the drill kept striking after she stopped moving.

Ravi checked the timer. "Thirty seconds. Get the reps."

Anika dragged a breath through her teeth and gave him three quick lunges on the left side first, pretending she had misheard the call, buying herself two breaths before she had to load the right leg again. She landed soft and silent. She took the next right lunge shallow and pulled out early.

"Full depth," Ravi said.

She made the next one long enough to touch the floor with her knuckles. Pain shot up so fast she had to clamp her teeth to keep from making noise. She stood and walked it off in a small circle as if she was tracking an imaginary ball trajectory.

Ravi frowned. "What's that limp?"

"My sock twisted," she said. "It's rubbing."

He looked at her shoe. "Then fix it and stop wasting reps."

She crouched, tugged nothing, and rose before he could kneel beside her and touch the leg himself. Her watch read 5:46. She had twenty-four minutes until shower and bus transfer. If she lost those, she would lose first period, and Ms. Donnelly would email home again.

The rain had soaked into the court building, into everyone's hair and shoes, and now damp climbed the inside of her calf sleeve. Damp days always made the shin angrier. She knew that before she knew the word tibia.

Ravi fed balls from mid-court. Straight drives, quick recoveries, no chatter. Every third feed he forced her forward, low, then flicked behind her to make her pivot on the right leg. She could do it if she landed exactly under her center and did not let the heel kiss down. She could do it if she let the front-left corner be ugly. She could do it if she did not think about the point of pain, because thinking about it made it expand.

At 6:03, she misjudged a low ball and banged her racket frame against the tin. The crack echoed, thin and humiliating.

Ravi opened the door. "Focus."

"I'm focused."

His eyes narrowed at the snap in her voice. "Then show it. Qualifier draw closes in three weeks. Nobody gives extra slots because your foot feels weird."

She bit back the first thing in her throat and said, "Got it."

By the time they finished feeds and moved to court suicides, the right shin burned with each turn. Start line to short line and back, then to half-court and back, then full and back. She ran with the right foot slightly turned out, ugly but less sharp. On the third ladder Ravi stepped to the side door to take a call from the bus depot. She heard his voice flatten into work tone: route shortage, swap sheet, night shift maybe.

She straightened her foot and gave him six clean lengths before he returned.

When he came back, she was breathing through her mouth and trying to look like only breathing through her mouth.

"Good," he said. "That is the pace. Remember it."

The praise landed in her chest the way cold water landed in a hot pan, violent and brief. She lifted her racket a few inches in answer, because even that felt safer than shifting weight onto the right leg.

They made the 7 train by forty seconds. She stood the whole ride, not because there were no seats, but because bending the knee to sit made the shin pulse harder when she stood again.

At school, the hallway floor had fresh wax and smelled like lemons trying to be cleaner than they were. Anika kept to the wall so nobody would bump her from the right side. Her backpack felt heavier than its actual math book and lunch box weight, because every stair made her recalibrate where to place the foot.

Second period science, Ms. Donnelly asked her to pass out lab sheets. Halfway down the aisle, her right leg misfired and the room tilted left. She set the stack on Ben Cho's desk too fast and held the back of a chair for one breath too long before her balance came back.

"Anika?" Ms. Donnelly said.

"I'm good." She resumed moving, handing out papers with her left hand because her right fingers had gone a little numb.

By lunch she had started counting steps between classes in groups of sixteen, a way to keep panic from sloshing over the sides. Stairwell to cafeteria: sixty-four. At the landing by the trophy case, two seventh graders gave her the careful look people used on cracked glass; she smiled so they would keep walking. Cafeteria to English: one hundred twelve. By the top of the second-floor stairs, her mouth tasted like pennies and she smiled at Mr. Patel before he could ask. English to nurse after he spotted her slowing on the landing: forty-eight, with a rest on step twenty-nine she called a shoe retie.

Nurse Eileen Park's office was too warm and smelled faintly of peppermint gum and vinyl exam-table paper. A small fan turned in the corner even though the heater was running. On the windowsill, three succulents leaned toward a square of pale sun, and one had a strip of medical tape around the pot where it had cracked. A Yankees mug held tongue depressors. On the wall behind her desk, a photo of a teenage boy in a marching-band uniform was pinned beside a handwritten grocery list: oat milk, cumin, batteries.

"Sit," Nurse Park said, already rolling away from her computer. She wore bright green clogs with little tomatoes printed on them, the kind of shoes people bought because they stood all day. "Mr. Patel says you've got a limp."

"It's not really a limp."

"That answer usually means yes." She offered a half smile and pulled on gloves. "Where?"

Anika tapped the outside of her shin first, then corrected to the inside when Nurse Park waited.

"How long?"

"Since this morning."

Nurse Park lifted an eyebrow. "Try again without trying to get back to class fast."

Anika fixed on the floor tile between her shoes, the one with a chipped corner shaped like a tooth. "A few days. Maybe after practice last week."

"Give me numbers I can use. Right now, sitting still."

"Three."

Saying it out loud made the pain feel less negotiable.

"Walking flat stays at three unless somebody clips my right side. Stairs push it to four."

"On court?"

Anika picked at a loose thread on her sleeve and pictured Ravi's face if she said the high number first. "Front-right lunges hit five. If I shorten depth and land quiet, it's more like four."

Nurse Park's gaze sharpened. "You're already editing movement to bargain with pain. That's not random soreness."

Nurse Park made a noise in her throat that was not disbelief and not agreement, just filing. She palpated along the tibia with two careful thumbs. At the second press point, hot static burst across Anika's ankle and stole her breath; she grabbed the table edge before her knee could dip.

"Sorry," Nurse Park said quietly. "I know."

She turned her laptop around and clicked through a chart. "Localized bony tenderness plus increased pain with impact in a kid who trains hard. I want imaging. Today if your family can manage it, tomorrow at the latest."

"It's just sore." Anika heard herself and added too quickly, "We had extra ladder drills because qualifiers are coming."

"I heard you." Nurse Park's voice stayed even, almost gentle, which made refusing her harder. "I'm not saying your season is over. I'm saying we need to rule out a stress reaction before it becomes a fracture. Those are different problems if you catch them early."

She set a printed referral form on the desk and wrote in block letters: NO RUNNING/JUMPING UNTIL CLEARED. PE EXCUSED.

Anika watched the marker move and felt heat rise up her neck. "Can you not write that part?"

"I can, and I did." Nurse Park capped the marker with a firm click. "You get one body. I don't care if you're trying out for the moon."

Anika stared at the paper. No running. No jumping. The words looked like a prank somebody meaner than Zoe would pull.

"I need to call your guardian," Nurse Park said. "Policy for a possible significant sports injury."

"My dad's at work."

"Then I'll leave a message and send this through the parent portal."

"He hates portal messages."

Nurse Park gave her a look that said many parents hated many things, and none of that changed bones. She picked up the phone.

Ravi did not answer. Nurse Park left a voicemail in her clipped professional cadence, then sent the school note and tucked two copies into a yellow envelope with ANIKA IYER in blue ink.

Before she handed it over, she tapped the corner of the envelope once. "I know you're worried about missing training. My son plays trumpet. If he misses one rehearsal, he thinks his life is over. It's not. Bodies heal on their own clock, not ours."

Anika forced out a clipped "Okay" because compliance was the price of being released, then slid off the table and tested one step. The leg ached even before weight settled.

"No gym, no after-school conditioning," Nurse Park said. "And if your pain spikes tonight, urgent care. Don't wait."

"Okay."

The lie came out smooth from overuse.

She left the office with the envelope in her binder and spent the rest of the day calculating how fast she could get home, ice for fifteen minutes, and be changed before Ravi checked the planner app. By final bell the sky had turned the color of old dishwater. The sidewalks steamed where the morning rain met late sun.

On the bus back to Jackson Heights, she opened the envelope under her backpack and read the note twice. The second time she folded it smaller until it fit inside the pocket of Maya's notebook, between a page of interval times and a grocery list in Maya's rounded handwriting from months ago. cilantro, ginger, dish soap. Anika touched the word cilantro with her thumb, then shut the notebook before the bus hit her stop.

Ravi was in the kitchen when she got in, tie loosened, depot lanyard still around his neck. Mustard seeds popped in hot oil in the pan. Curry leaf smoke stung the back of her throat. He was chopping onions too fast, the knife blade making hard staccato taps on the cutting board.

"You're late by eleven," he said. "Transfer delay?"

"Train held at Queensboro."

"Eat a banana now. We leave in forty. Jonah gave us Court 2 and 3 overlap. Good slot."

She set her bag down carefully, keeping her right leg straight. "I had to go to the nurse today."

The knife stopped mid-air. "Why?"

"Shin pain."

Ravi turned off the burner and faced her. For one breath she saw the fear before he arranged it into irritation. "How bad?"

"Not bad-bad. Just sore."

"Which side?"

"Right."

He crouched, hand already reaching, and she had to force herself not to flinch before he touched. He pressed along the bone, quick, practical, less gentle than Nurse Park, then looked up.

"Tender," he said.

"Yeah."

"What did she say?"

Anika pulled one folded school note from her pocket and held it out. Ravi read it once, jaw tightening at the all-caps restriction line.

"Imaging," he said flatly. "Temporary rest."

"She said maybe stress reaction."

"School nurses write maximum caution because they have to." He refolded the paper smaller than she had, then slid it under the half-packed folder by his lanyard instead of leaving it out. "We run this through sports med, not through a portal warning."

"She said no running or jumping until cleared."

"Cleared by imaging," Ravi said. "We're not pretending this note is the final verdict."

He moved to the fridge and pulled down the calendar. Blue ink for school deadlines, black for training blocks, red stars for tournament dates. He crossed out that night's suicides, wrote YELLOW PLAN over the next three days, then tapped the red star in three weeks.

"If volume drops this week, your match legs disappear. Then you go into qualifiers half-ready. Then you lose early and everybody says you peaked at borough. You want that?"

The answer sat ready in her throat: no, because losing felt like drowning in public. No, because she had just learned pain could be translated into praise if she survived it. No, because he looked less broken when there was a plan pinned to a magnet.

"No," she said.

"Exactly. We're not stupid. We're strategic." He softened the tone a fraction. "Yellow plan until scan: no road work, no bonus courts, no front-corner repeats if your gait slips. You drift, we stop."

"What do I tell Jonah?" she asked.

"You tell him nurse flagged soreness and we're waiting on the appointment. You don't hand him that note and invite a shutdown before we even get scanned."

Anika tracked the folder by the microwave, imagined Nurse Park's face in this kitchen, and said, "Okay."

"Say it straight."

"Okay. I'll run the yellow plan."

Ravi exhaled hard through his nose, relief disguised as annoyance. "Good. Change. Bring both sleeves."

At the rec center that evening, Court 2 smelled of damp rubber and old dust. Jonah Feld was already there with a bucket of balls and his timing clipboard, the chronic strip of white athletic tape wrapped around his right wrist. He wore the same faded navy cap he always wore, brim salt-marked from sweat. A half-finished black coffee sat on the bench beside a paperback mystery with a transit map sticking out as a bookmark.

"You look cooked," Jonah said when Anika stepped inside.

"Long day," she said.

"Everyone has a long day." He bounced a ball once and watched how she moved to pick it up. His eyes narrowed. "What's the hitch on your right side?"

Ravi answered from behind the glass before she could. "School nurse flagged soreness and wrote a restriction note. We're managing load until imaging."

Jonah kept his gaze on Anika. "That what happened?"

Anika tightened her grip on the racket. "It's fine."

"Fine is not a metric." Jonah walked to the front-right corner. "Give me six lunges, controlled."

She did three clean and three compromised. On lunge five, her right leg went slack for a split second; her back foot skidded and her knee caved inward.

Jonah set down the ball. "Stop."

Ravi opened the door. "She can continue. It settles after warm-up."

"No," Jonah said, still calm. "Not with that load pattern."

"We have qualifiers in three weeks."

"I can count weeks." Jonah's tone stayed dry, almost bored, which made Ravi angrier. "I can also count how many times she's off-center on that leg. She's protecting the tibia."

Ravi stepped onto the court, palms up, argument posture. "Then we build around it. Back-court drives, no front-corner lunges, no jump work."

"That is still impact."

"Everything is impact."

"Exactly." Jonah pointed toward the door with his clipboard. "Then you reduce it before it chooses reduction for you."

Anika stood between them, sweat cooling under her shirt, pulse thudding in her shin. If she spoke, one of them would use her words like a weapon. She kept her mouth shut.

Ravi cut his hand toward her. "Tell him it's manageable."

She said, "It grabs on push-off and turns sharp in front-right. I can still finish the block."

Jonah snorted once. "That's not manageable. That's you negotiating with a bone."

"I can finish today's block," she said quickly. "I just need a minute."

Ravi latched onto it. "There. She can finish."

Jonah picked up his coffee, took a sip, and set it down with care. "I'm not running a kid through a possible bone stress injury because a calendar looks scary. We do a technical session only for twenty-five minutes. We do no suicides, no extra courts, and no heroics." His focus snapped back to Anika. "If pain climbs, you stop. That's not a suggestion."

Ravi's laugh was one dry syllable. "Heroics? She's training."

"Call it what you want," Jonah said. "I'm done arguing labels."

For twenty-five minutes he fed soft-length balls and had her work pattern recognition from mid-court without deep corners. Even then, pain threaded every right-foot plant, a metal wire pulled tighter each rally. Jonah saw it. Ravi saw it and pretended he did not, tapping splits on his phone, muttering target times under his breath like prayer.

When the timer beeped, Jonah held up his palm. "Done."

Ravi reached for another ball from the bucket. Jonah stepped in front of the feed line and shook his head once.

"Session's closed," Jonah said.

Ravi's jaw worked. "You're overcorrecting because of one school note."

"Maybe," Jonah said. "I'd rather overcorrect now than explain a fracture later."

He began collecting balls, each one thudding into the bucket with a hollow pop. Then he walked to the wall tablet by the court door and typed with one finger. On the evening board, Court 3 vanished from Ravi's reservation line.

"I logged technical-only and removed your second-court block until imaging," Jonah said without turning around. "Front desk gets the same note."

"You're putting this in the system?" Ravi said.

"Yes. If you want it reversed, send a written override with your name on it."

Ravi stood very still for three seconds, then turned to Anika. "Shower. We'll do mobility at home."

She turned and went to the locker room, where the mirror above the sink had a crack running through its corner like lightning. She peeled off her right calf sleeve. The skin over her shin was faintly swollen, tight and shiny where her finger pressed and left a pale dent.

She ran cold water over a paper towel and wrapped it around the leg until the ache dulled from knife-edge to hammer beat. In the next row, two older girls from the basketball program argued about prom dresses and laughed so hard one of them snorted. Their life sounded illegal in the best way, a place where people said stupid things and nobody measured them in rep counts.

When she came out, Ravi had already packed most of the gear.

"We're still behind this week," he said quietly while they walked to the bus stop. "Don't let Jonah's caution become your excuse."

"It's not."

"Good."

The bus was late and crowded, damp coats pressed shoulder to shoulder. Anika stood with one hand on the overhead bar and counted stoplights to keep from noticing each jolt. Ravi scrolled through tournament forums on his phone, jaw set. At one point he murmured, almost to himself, "If we lose this window, we can't buy another one."

She pretended she had not heard.

At home he made her sit on the couch with an ice pack duct-taped around her shin while he answered depot emails and revised the week schedule. "No pavement runs," he said. "Court movement only. We front-load weekends."

"Okay."

"Walk to the sink and back."

He was watching her face, not her leg, checking whether the week he had built was still standing.

She crossed ten kitchen tiles and came back before the shin could wobble.

"Better," he said. "See? Already settling."

It was not settling. The ice had numbed the surface while the deeper ache stayed bright and mean. But one clean lap was useful evidence. A clean lap got people off your case.

Before brushing his teeth, Ravi looped a bike cable through both of her main racket bags and locked them to the kitchen radiator. "No court tonight," he said. "Give me your word."

"You have it."

He gave one curt exhale like a deal had been signed and left the key on his ring in his pocket.

By ten-thirty Ravi was asleep at the kitchen table with his glasses tilted and one hand still on the calculator app. Anika watched him for a full minute from the doorway, the lines around his mouth deeper than they had been last year, his hair grayer at the temples than the photos on the fridge. Grief had hollowed him and then filled the hole with schedules.

She should have gone to bed.

Instead she dug out an old aluminum backup racket from under her bed, tied her school sneakers again, slid the front door open without letting it click, and took the late bus back to the rec center with her hoodie pulled over the racket throat so she would look like any kid hauling homework. The custodian at the desk knew her by now, knew whose daughter she was. He glanced at the screen, frowned at Jonah's technical-only flag, and waved her through when she said, "Mobility work, no match play." The court availability board showed Court 4 empty for one short block before adult league.

Inside Court 4, the air was colder than Court 2, the overhead light slightly dimmer on one side. The metal rattle from the HVAC made the glass hum. She set her watch timer for twelve minutes and chose suicides because numbers never argued back.

On the first ladder, she ran short line, half-court, full, and back, and the pain started as a thin burn and climbed each turn.

Sweat chilled between her shoulder blades, and she pictured Ravi's key still hooked to his pocket seam.

On the second ladder, the shin stopped feeling like a warning and started feeling like glass dust under skin. By half-court it had become white static, and on the hard pivot she had to stutter-step to keep the leg from buckling.

She told herself it was fear, not tissue. She told herself pain lied. She told herself Nurse Park had to scare kids by job description and Jonah had the luxury of caution because he did not sit at their kitchen table when bills came.

On the third ladder, the right shin flashed so hard on the full-court turn that her foot did not push; it collapsed. She caught herself on one hand, scraped her palm against painted floor, and tasted bile in her mouth.

She stayed crouched for three breaths, listening to the ball thump from the next court where grown men shouted split and let and stroke like legal terms in a language she was not old enough to speak.

She shoved herself upright and tried to restart. On the first turn, a dry click fired deep in the shin, her toes went prickly and numb, and the leg folded again before she reached the short line.

She killed the timer at 7:41 and stood against the wall, breathing through her teeth while the numbness ebbed into a heavier throb.

By the time the adult league players knocked to claim the court, her right leg trembled when she stopped. She bent to pick up her bag and had to use the wall for balance. The man at the door, maybe forty, racket tucked under his arm, looked at her swollen shin and said, not unkindly, "You okay, kid?"

"Yeah," she said. "Cramps."

He gave a noncommittal grunt, like he didn't believe her and didn't plan to contest jurisdiction.

At the desk, the custodian handed her an incident clipboard because minors who got hurt on court had to be logged. She wrote her name, wrote slipped during conditioning, and checked DECLINED EMS before he could call Ravi.

The ride home felt longer than the map said it should be. At every transfer she had to choose between taking stairs fast and arriving on time or taking them sideways and arriving late enough for questions. She chose time.

In the apartment, the kitchen light was still on. Ravi had moved from table to couch, one arm over his face. The ice pack sat melted in the sink.

Anika showered with one foot out of the spray because hot water made the shin throb harder. In her room she opened Maya's notebook to the current week page. She had drawn columns after chapter three and kept them strict: date, session, load, response, notes.

Monday AM session included ghosting, lunge matrix, feeds, and suicides, and the response line read: right shin lit hot by rep twelve; right foot turning out to survive pivots.

She remembered biting the inside of her cheek after rep twelve so Ravi would not hear a sound.

Monday PM session stayed technical, and she wrote: wire-tight ache on every right-foot plant.

She could still feel Jonah's eyes on her footwork, waiting for honesty she had not given him.

Late PM session included unauthorized suicides, and she wrote: near buckle, numb toes, visible swelling.

She stared at the entries until they blurred. Then she uncapped her pen and dragged a line through each one, slow and careful so the page would not tear.

Monday AM response became: settled once warm.

That line looked like a forged signature, close enough to pass.

Monday PM response became: manageable.

Late PM included mobility only.

In the notes column she replaced near buckle with managed with form correction.

She also rewrote the incident line in her head before sleep could catch it: no collapse, no numb toes, no form that could surface later.

The edits looked almost neat, as if there had never been harsher lines under them. She took the second nurse note from the notebook pocket, unfolded it, and read NO RUNNING/JUMPING UNTIL CLEARED one more time. Then she folded it into a rectangle small as a tea tag and tucked it inside the back cover where the binding had come loose.

From the living room came Ravi's sleep cough, dry and stubborn. Anika closed the notebook, pressed her palm flat over it, and listened until the apartment settled into pipes and distant traffic and the soft electrical buzz of the refrigerator. On the counter, a half-packed folder sat under his depot lanyard with pay stubs, tournament papers, and the first folded restriction note clipped together for the morning.

Her shin kept pulsing anyway, steady as a second heartbeat.
