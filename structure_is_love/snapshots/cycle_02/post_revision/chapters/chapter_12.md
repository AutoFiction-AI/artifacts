# Chapter 12

By noon, the reminder she'd set before sleep had done its job and carried her to Tess Keating's clinic, where July air sat on the sidewalk like wet cloth. A produce truck idled at the curb and made the bus-stop bench vibrate under Anika's knees. She had an ice sleeve Velcroed around her right shin and Maya's watch damp against her wrist. Tess stood in the doorway with a clipboard tucked to her ribs while Ravi's voice came through Anika's phone on speaker from a depot break room that sounded like metal doors and vending machines.

"I'm saying the same thing I wrote," Tess said. "No impact outside the posted plan. If either of you freelances, she loses eligibility."

Ravi's voice crackled. "I'm not freelancing. I'm trying to keep her from falling behind."

"Then stop bargaining with anatomy," Tess said. "Go home tonight, set terms in writing, and put one weekly rest day on the fridge where both of you can see it."

Anika tightened the bottle cap in her hand until the ridges bit her skin. "We'll do it tonight."

"You'll do more than do it." Tess pointed her pen at the phone screen and then at Anika. "You'll follow it when it costs you something."

By the time Ravi came in from the depot that evening, the kitchen table looked like school instead of dinner. Anika had pushed the dented steel plates to one side and laid out three things in a straight line: Tess's return-to-play printout with the clinic logo, a blank July calendar she had copied by hand because the printer had died in May, and Maya's half-used notebook opened to a clean page. The fan in the window dragged humid air through the room and spread the smell of bus diesel off Ravi's shirt across mustard seed and old tea.

He dropped his keys in the blue bowl and studied the table like it had accused him of something.

"You didn't eat?" he asked.

"I ate," she said. "Can we do this first?"

He kept standing. "Do what first?"

"Terms." She pointed at the chair across from her. "Not hallway yelling. Actual terms. Twenty minutes each side. Then we write what we agreed."

Ravi gave a short laugh that had no amusement in it. "Each side? You sound like a court hearing."

"Maybe that's better than what we do now. Sit down."

He took the chair with his work bag still strapped across his chest, as if he planned to bolt. The zipper had split near the handle weeks ago. She watched his thumb worry that torn seam while he read Tess's protocol line by line, lips moving on certain words: no-impact progression, load cap, immediate symptom disclosure.

"You copied this wrong," he said. "It says gradual return. It doesn't say tiny return."

"It says no jumping volume."

"Volume is how you get fit enough to keep your place."

Anika flipped to the notebook page she had ruled with a geometry ruler. She had titled two columns in block letters: `Non-negotiable` and `What happens if broken`.

Ravi looked at the heading and his shoulders went up. "What happens if broken? Are you giving me punishments now?"

"I'm writing consequences so we don't pretend we forgot." She checked the watch on her wrist, Maya's old digital rectangle with a scratched face. "I start. You don't interrupt. Then you go."

"Anika."

"You said in the clinic we'd talk when we got home. We're home."

He shut his mouth. The kitchen clock clicked loud enough to count with.

She read from the page because her throat went tight if she looked at him. "First: one full rest day every week. Full means no court, no running stairs, no ghosting in the hallway, no 'light touch' disguised as drills. Second: school time is school time. No leaving before last period unless it's an official tournament day with paperwork. No texting me six times during math about split-step videos. Third: pain has to be said immediately. Not after session, not at night. If my shin spikes, I say it right there and we stop arguing about whether it counts."

Ravi leaned back and rubbed his left ankle, that old habit when he was cornered. "Who said anything about arguing?"

She looked at him then. "You did. Every time."

He inhaled through his nose and held it. "You have this tone from Priya now."

"This tone is from me."

The fan coughed and slowed. Somewhere above them, the upstairs neighbor dragged furniture across the floor, heavy legs thudding in uneven bursts.

Ravi tapped the protocol sheet with one finger. "You want me to talk?"

She nodded.

"Fine. Here's reality. You're already behind after six weeks. Cora isn't doing yoga and journaling while you heal. She's training. Every kid in that pool is training. You take one full day off, plus school restrictions, plus early stop for every ache, and you get buried. Rankings don't wait. Brackets don't care that we had a bad spring." He pointed toward the pile of envelopes by the microwave, the ones with red windows and final notice bands. "Bills definitely don't care."

The word bills was never just one word in this apartment. It was the oncology number she had seen in itemized rows on the hardship forms. The total was thirty-eight thousand four hundred. It was the utility warning they had folded three times and tucked under a coupon circular like paper could be shamed into silence. Anika felt the old arithmetic start in the back of her head, the part that translated every hour into possible rescue.

Ravi kept going. "You think I like pushing? You think I enjoy being the bad guy? We don't have private-club money. We don't have backups. We have your game and my shifts. That's it."

She let him finish. She did not let the numbers finish her.

"Are you done?" she asked.

"No, I'm not done."

"Then do your twenty minutes. But answer this first." She slid the notebook toward him and tapped the blank line under `What happens if broken`. "If we break protocol and I get suspended, what do we have?"

A cheek muscle twitched. "We don't break protocol."

"We almost did last week in the clinic parking lot."

"I was talking."

"You were negotiating my bone." Her voice shook, and she hated that he would hear it, but she did not stop. "My right shin is still hot after stairs. Tess wrote it in front of you. You heard her say noncompliance means suspension. You heard it." She put her palm flat over the notebook page to hide the tremor. "I'm not doing secret extras. I'm not lying again."

Ravi's eyes cut to the watch on her wrist. "You're talking like you're alone in this."

"I'm talking like I'm inside this."

Silence sat down between them. It was not the old silence that followed shouting and slammed doors. This one had shape. It had both of them in it.

Ravi unstrapped his bag and set it on the floor. He folded the protocol sheet once, then unfolded it, aligning corners too carefully.

"One rest day," he said finally. "Saturday."

Anika wrote it down.

The pen scratched louder than it should have. Sweat collected under Maya's watchband, and a thin pulse kept tapping along the sore ridge of her right shin.

"School," he said. "You attend full day unless tournament or medical appointment. If a coach asks, we say no."

She wrote that too, then looked up. "If you text during class, I won't answer."

"Fine."

"Not fine. Say it clearly."

His mouth tightened. "I won't text you during class unless it's urgent."

She wrote: `No training texts during class.`

Tiny was one of his favorite escape words, loose enough to stretch around anything. If she let it sit undefined, he'd pry every locked rule back open by Thursday.

He rubbed his forehead with two fingers, smearing depot grime across his skin. "Pain disclosure... immediate. But you don't weaponize tiny sensations to skip hard work."

"Define tiny."

"You know what I mean."

"No. Define it."

He gave her a look that used to end discussions. It did not end this one. He looked away first.

"If pain changes your movement," he said. "If you start compensating, limping, changing lunge depth. You report. We stop and assess."

"And if you disagree with my pain report?"

He swallowed. "Then we stop anyway and call Tess."

She wrote that exactly.

Ravi reached for the pen. "Add this. We keep weekly volume targets within her limits. I need something measurable."

"Within whose limits?"

"Within protocol limits," he snapped. "Don't do that cross-exam thing with me."

"Then say protocol limits." She passed him the pen.

He printed in careful block letters, each stroke pressed hard enough to emboss the paper underneath: `All volume targets must remain within PT protocol caps.`

He drew a line under it before handing the pen back.

Anika read the list aloud from top to bottom, as if she was reading directions in science lab and any skipped step could blow glass apart.

"One full rest day every Saturday. School stays whole-day, no early pull-outs. I report pain right away; if we argue, we stop and call Tess. Volume stays under PT cap. No training texts during class." She took a breath. "If either of us breaks it, court dies that day and tomorrow's canceled."

Ravi's head came up. "Canceled by who?"

"By both of us."

"That's not enforceable."

"It's enforceable in this kitchen."

He stared at her long enough that she could hear water begin to hiss in the pipes, someone's shower kicking on. Then he gave one short nod.

"Fine," he said.

She hated that this small word could feel like winning and grief at the same time.

"Calendar," she said.

She slid the hand-drawn month grid to the center. Ravi leaned in despite himself. Planning language was his native language. She watched him choose a blue pen instead of black, a detail he would insist meant nothing.

Together they blocked the week. Monday after school: PT clinic, three subway stops, no court. Tuesday and Thursday: forty-five minute controlled court sessions at Queens rec, monitored movement only. Wednesday: homework first, mobility work at home, no impact. Friday: school plus short technical feed if pain-free. Saturday in thick letters: `REST - NO EXCEPTIONS`. Sunday: capped practice and video review.

Ravi added transit times in tiny print between blocks. `Q32 bus 3:20.` `F train backup.` `Bring tape.` He started to write `stairs` under Tuesday commute and stopped when Anika's pen touched the paper beside his.

"No hidden conditioning," she said.

"I know what no means."

"You know how to rename it."

His nostrils flared. For a moment she thought the room would flip back to the old pattern. Then he capped the pen and pushed it away from himself.

"Put it up," he said.

Anika stood on the chair and taped the calendar to the fridge with two magnets: the cracked Ganesh magnet Maya had bought from a temple fundraiser, and a Midtown club magnet with gold lettering that looked expensive even when it held coupons and overdue notices. The paper sat above eye level for Anika and exactly eye level for Ravi. Every morning and every night, they would both have to look at it.

She climbed down. Ravi kept his eyes on Saturday's `REST - NO EXCEPTIONS` block long enough to make his silence obvious, then reached for the sink and shook the colander once, rice slapping wet against steel.

"Eat," he said quietly. "Then ice ten minutes."

"It's not hot now."

"Still. We treat the warning, not just the heat."

"Okay. Timer on the counter so it doesn't turn into twenty."

The word okay felt less like surrender than it had two months ago.

When she went to brush her teeth, she passed the living room and saw him still in front of the fridge, arms folded, reading Tuesday as if he could force a second line to appear beneath it.

---

By Thursday the heat had thickened enough to stick her shirt to her back before warm-up. Queens rec was louder in summer. The HVAC unit above Court Two rattled like a coin in a tin can, and every hard drive hit the side wall came back with a doubled echo because the door seals had started peeling.

Anika sat on the wooden bench and retied her right shoe twice. The knot kept landing wrong against the tender ridge on her shin. She could feel Ravi watching from the hallway glass where parents gathered with tote bags and coffee cups and opinions.

Jonah was crouched by his ball bucket, wrapping fresh tape around his wrist with the concentration of someone threading a needle on a moving bus. He had a dented lunch tin next to him, decorated with faded cartoon dolphins. Inside were mints, spare tape, and a nicotine patch in a foil sleeve he kept forgetting to throw away.

"You gonna play today or marry that shoelace?" he asked without looking up.

"Playing," Anika said.

"Good. I don't officiate weddings." He snapped the tape with his teeth, stood, and squinted at her legs. "How's the right side?"

"Baseline two."

"Speak human for me."

"Mild ache. No gait change."

He nodded once. "That's human enough."

Ravi stepped in from the hallway, already holding his notebook. "Cap's forty-five, hard stop," he said to Jonah, loud enough for the two younger kids on the next court and their mothers by the door. "Clock hits zero, session's done. If her movement changes, we shut it down and call Tess."

Jonah raised both eyebrows. "Historic day. Somebody log it."

"Log whatever you want," Ravi said. "Run the posted block."

Anika caught the brief glance between the men, years of quiet argument in half a second. Jonah clicked his timer and tossed her a warm-up ball.

"We start with controlled drives," he said. "Seventy percent pace. Hold your lines and skip hero shots."

The first five minutes felt wrong in the way everything healed felt wrong. Her body remembered panic speed and had to be pulled back by force. She hit too hard, ball jumping long off the back wall. Jonah did not yell. He just held up two fingers and said, "Softer hands," until she heard him.

Sweat crawled down her spine. On her watch, seconds stacked with cruel neatness. Every drill had an old ghost of itself beneath it, the version where Ravi would have added two more sets, then three, then one for character. She kept waiting for that familiar command, for volume to return like weather.

At minute nineteen, lunging to her forehand, a needle of pain flashed along the inside of her right shin. Not sharp enough to buckle, sharp enough to warn.

She straightened.

Jonah saw it first. "What changed?"

Her tongue stuck for half a beat. The old reflex arrived on schedule: hide, finish, confess later. She could almost feel the lie forming, thin and easy.

Ravi's voice came from behind the glass. "Anika?"

She wiped her face with the back of her wrist. "It's up a click," she said. "Right shin, inside line. I was at two, I'm at three."

The words tasted metallic, like biting foil.

Jonah stepped closer but kept his voice even. "Movement change?"

"I'm not limping. Push-off's sharper."

Ravi was through the door in two strides. "Heat shift?"

"Same as warm-up."

Ravi planted himself at the edge of the service box, notebook still open in his fist. Jonah didn't yield an inch. "We cut lunge depth and keep eyes on the right side," Jonah said. "If it jumps again or she compensates, we kill court. She calls every shift."

Ravi's jaw worked once. "Run it."

Jonah handed Anika a towel. "Good report. That's how athletes keep seasons." Then, almost as an afterthought, he dug a mint out of the dolphin tin and popped it into his mouth. "And maybe lungs."

They resumed with shorter ghosting patterns and controlled straight drives. They took no dives and made no desperate reaches. At minute thirty-four she wanted to speed up; at minute thirty-eight she almost did. Ravi rapped once on the glass and held up an open palm: steady.

By minute forty-five she was in the middle of a serve-return sequence, only four of ten cycles complete, when Jonah's timer screamed.

"Time," Jonah said.

"I'm in the set," Anika said. "Let me finish this one."

Ravi stepped through the door with her water bottle and towel. He did not hand either over yet. "Clock says stop. No extensions."

"Two minutes isn't an extension."

"Two minutes is still overtime."

Her grip tightened on the racquet handle until the tacky wrap bit into her palm. "I've finally got timing on the back wall."

"Good," Ravi said. "Bank it for Friday."

"If Friday gets canceled, I lose it."

He held her gaze, and she could see the old urge in him, the one that loved one more set more than sleep or sense. It flickered and settled.

"We're done," he said. "Pack."

She hated him for saying it. She loved him for saying it. Both felt dangerous.

They packed in silence. Jonah signed her practice card with a thick black marker and added `45:00 cap honored - drill incomplete` in neat all-caps. Then he slipped the marker back into the dolphin tin and muttered, mostly to himself, "Quitting cigarettes should've been easier than quitting bad coaching habits. Turns out nothing is easy." He nodded at Anika. "See you Friday if Tess clears it."

Outside, heat slammed into them from the sidewalk. Ravi unlocked his phone and started tapping before they reached the bus stop. Anika watched his thumb move fast, already drafting updates before the bus even turned the corner.

"Who are you messaging?" she asked.

"Schedule update," he said.

"To who?"

"Jonah. Tess. And me."

He held the screen out. New calendar entry: `Friday court tentative - pending AM pain report.` Beneath it, in smaller text he had started and not sent yet: `Optional mobility stairs if pain-free.`

Anika pointed at the second line.

"Delete that."

"It's mobility, not impact."

"It's hidden conditioning with nicer words. Delete it."

He bit the inside of his cheek. A bus wheezed up to the curb and exhaled hot brake air around their ankles.

"You're relentless," he muttered.

"You taught me that."

For one second she thought he would refuse, right there with strangers boarding around them and coins clinking in the fare box. Then he hit backspace, once, twice, erased the line, and showed her the blank space where it had been.

"Happy?" he asked.

"No," she said. "Clear."

The word scraped her throat on the way out. Clear meant she had to hold the line when it cost them money, court time, and whatever fragile peace they had bought on Tuesday.

"Good," she said. "Then Friday's canceled."

His head jerked toward her. "What?"

"You tried to sneak conditioning right after we posted terms. We wrote the consequence in your own handwriting. Session ends. Next day's court canceled."

"That line never got sent."

"You still tried it."

Ravi stepped into her lane and braced his forearm on the bus-door rail, not touching her but turning the curb into a narrow chute. "Don't do this on a sidewalk like I'm some stranger trying to grab you."

Her pulse punched up into her throat. She shifted back, felt the curb edge under her heel, and pain zipped hot up the inside of her right shin hard enough to blur the next breath. The bench rattled when her hand hit it. People were squeezing past them with backpacks and grocery bags, pretending not to look.

For a second her body tried to run the old script: back off, de-escalate, save the session. She hated how quickly that obedience came.

"Then move," she said, voice cracking anyway. "You're blocking me and arguing semantics. Say it plain. You tried to put stairs back in."

The bus driver leaned on the horn and shouted, "Board or clear the doorway." Ravi dropped his arm but did not step away. His jaw worked. "That court's prepaid. No refund after six."

"Then that's what this costs."

When he dropped his arm she didn't feel safe, only steadier. Relief and anger tangled tight in her chest, impossible to separate.

For a beat he looked like he might call her bluff. Instead he opened the rec booking app, canceled Court Two, and held up the red banner when it flashed: `Late cancellation confirmed. Credit not issued.`

Only then did he reopen the message draft and type with both thumbs: `Friday court canceled. I tried to add stairs after agreeing not to. No workaround session. Review with Tess before next impact.` He stopped on `I tried` as if the phrase had teeth.

Watching him type that sentence felt like watching him sign a debt in public. It didn't fix anything, but it made the cost impossible to pretend away.

"Read it," he said.

She read every word, then pointed to the address line. "Add me."

"You were standing here."

"Add me."

He added her contact, hit send, and shoved the phone into his pocket so hard the case smacked his hip.

On the ride home, he stood so she could sit by the window and keep her leg straight. At Roosevelt Avenue a man with grocery bags argued with the driver about a transfer slip. At 82nd Street two women got on carrying salon aprons that smelled like acetone and coconut oil. It was normal weekday noise. No one in that bus cared about pain scales or junior rankings. Anika found that comforting and insulting at the same time.

At home she iced her shin while Ravi made tea too strong and forgot sugar in both cups. He asked if she had finished her science worksheet in the same voice he used for weather reports. She said yes without looking up, and the question died there. They ate reheated lemon rice with pickle from the jar while the fan wobbled and his phone buzzed once with the no-credit receipt from the rec app.

After dishes, Anika opened the fridge for water and saw the calendar. Saturday still shouted `REST - NO EXCEPTIONS.` Tuesday and Thursday still had hard borders. Friday now had Ravi's updated handwriting in blue: `COURT CANCELED - REVIEW W/ TESS.`

She touched the paper edge. It had gone soft where steam from the rice pot hit it.

Ravi came up behind her with a roll of clear tape. "Corners are curling," he said. "We'll need a new sheet before August."

"It's still July," she said. "We're not even through this week."

His mouth twitched like he wanted to answer with a joke and could not find one.

He flattened each corner like he was sealing a wound: top left, top right, bottom left, bottom right. His fingers were rough and careful in the same motion.

"It'll stay," he said.

"Until when?"

"Until we replace it next month." He met her eyes in the reflection of the microwave door. "Not before."

Later, in bed, Anika listened to the apartment settle. Pipes knocked. A distant siren rose and fell. Someone laughed on the stoop below. Her shin throbbed dull and regular, less pain than message.

She heard Ravi in the kitchen after midnight, the fridge opening, paper rustling, magnet tapping once against metal. She got up and stood in the hall without turning on lights.

He was at the calendar with a pencil behind his ear, not writing, just holding it. The clock over the sink turned 12:07. The rec-app receipt sat faceup by the fruit bowl, `Credit not issued` glaring in thin red letters. He stared at Saturday's `REST - NO EXCEPTIONS` block like he could thread extra hours through it without anybody noticing.

He did not move until he felt her there.

"Couldn't sleep?" he asked.

"No. I heard the fridge and thought you were rewriting Saturday."

He looked down at the pencil, then set it on the counter. "I was looking for a loophole. I didn't find one."

They stood shoulder to shoulder in the weak refrigerator light, both looking at the same taped page, both pretending the paper was stronger than it was and that morning would leave it untouched.
When she finally slept again, Saturday had already started.
