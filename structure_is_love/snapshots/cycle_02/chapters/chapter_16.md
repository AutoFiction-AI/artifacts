# Chapter 16

At 7:43 on Saturday, Anika woke anyway.

No alarm, no text from Ravi with a split time, no shoes lined by the door like an accusation. Her right shin still gave its old warning when she swung out of bed, a thin hot thread along the bone, but the pain settled after two steps. Humidity pushed through the window screen. Someone in the next building had already started frying onions, and the smell mixed with the detergent steam from the laundromat vent below.

She checked Maya's watch out of habit. The plastic strap had cracked at the third hole months ago, and she had reinforced the back with clear tape that had gone cloudy at the edges. 7:43 blinked in green digits. The seconds ticked loud enough in her head to become counting, so she shut it down and sat on her hands until the urge passed.

In the kitchen, cabinet doors opened and shut in the wrong rhythm. Ravi never moved quietly when he was trying to look quiet. Metal hit metal, then the high whine of the kettle.

Anika stood in the doorway. Ravi had not shaved. He wore his depot polo untucked and a pair of old shorts, and the left ankle he used to pretend was fine had wrapped itself in a black elastic brace.

"You're up," he said, like he had not been listening for her floorboards.

"Yeah."

He held up a dented steel bowl. "I found Amma's lemon rice card. Want to do that for lunch?"

She blinked once, then twice. "Today?"

"It's Saturday." He shrugged. "We need lunch anyway."

They were work words and safe words. She recognized the move and felt herself matching it.

"We're low on curry leaves," she said. "And peanuts."

"And lemons," he said. "We'll go down to Patel's. Ten minutes."

Neither of them said no drills, no video review, no bracket talk. The omission sat between them like a third person who knew all their passwords.

They left at eight-thirty with two tote bags and exact cash folded into Ravi's wallet in stiff, sorted stacks. The stairwell smelled like floor cleaner and wet shoes. On the second-floor landing, Mrs. Alvarez was kneeling beside her potted basil, pinching dead tips with two pink clips still in her hair from the night shift.

"Look at this child, up at a human hour for once," she called, wiping her hands on her Knicks T-shirt. "My Tito slept in more than you."

Her dog, eight pounds of jaw and opinion, stood in a yellow raincoat although it was not raining and barked once at Ravi's ankle brace.

Anika crouched and let Tito sniff her fingers. "Morning, boss."

"He likes you now because you dropped chicken last week," Mrs. Alvarez said. "That is not love. That is strategy."

Ravi huffed through his nose and tugged once at the ankle brace strap. "Same as Tito's owner."

"Please. I'm pure romance and unpaid overtime." Mrs. Alvarez stood with a hand against the wall. "You making your wife's rice?"

Ravi lifted the steel bowl in a small salute.

"Use fresh lemons. Not that bottled nonsense. Maya would've cursed you out."

"I know," Ravi said quietly.

Mrs. Alvarez looked at Anika, then back at Ravi, like checking whether either of them would break if touched too hard. "If you have extra, bring me one spoon. My son still refuses turmeric like he's allergic to joy."

"We'll bring," Anika said.

Outside, the block had gone from early quiet to neighborhood loud. Two boys chalked goalposts on the sidewalk and argued over whether the curb counted as in bounds. A halal cart hissed beside the pharmacy gate. Above the 7 train tracks, pigeons shifted in synchronized waves, then settled as if an invisible hand had pressed pause.

Ravi and Anika walked without rushing. That was new enough to feel strange in her body. Her stride kept trying to lengthen, and each time she dragged it back. At the corner, the crossing light flashed white, then red hand, and she waited through the full cycle instead of darting.

At Patel's Market, the air conditioner fought a losing battle against wet produce and human breath. Mr. Patel stood at the register with a Mets cap pinned at the brim where the fabric had torn; he had fixed it with a tiny gold safety pin shaped like a fish, an old gift from his daughter. He was lining up scratch-off lottery tickets by color like they were spice jars.

"Champ and coach," he said when they came in. "No court this morning?"

Anika reached for a basket before Ravi could answer. "We're making lemon rice."

Mr. Patel put one hand over his heart. "Then you require proper lemons. Bin three, not bin one. Bin one tastes like disappointment."

In the back aisle, Ravi chose onions by weight, pressing each one with his thumb. Anika checked the curry leaf packets for black spots. She held up two brands of peanuts and Ravi said, "Cheaper one has more salt. We rinse if needed."

"You can just buy the right one."

"I can buy both if you found oil money behind the sofa."

He said it dry, half-joke, but she felt the debt numbers click in her head anyway: 38,400, then 1,860, then late fees crawling like ants. She hated how fast her mind could find the list.

"I've got twenty from tutoring Devon in math," she said. "Use that."

Ravi stopped with an onion in his hand. "Keep your money."

"It's not my money if this is lunch for everyone."

"It's your money. Keep it."

Mr. Patel appeared at the end of the aisle, holding a crate of tomatoes against his stomach. "If you two are fighting over twenty dollars, take my cilantro free and restore peace."

"We're not fighting," Ravi said.

Mr. Patel raised his eyebrows in a way that said he had watched families argue over less and also over everything. "Then I'm giving free cilantro to celebrate your not-fight."

They took the cilantro.

At the counter, Ravi peeled bills from the correct stack and counted twice before handing them over. His thumb had a fresh split at the nail, a clean line where skin had cracked from soap and cold water and maybe from gripping steering wheels too long at night. Anika looked away before he could see her looking.

Back on the block, they passed the laundromat where Jorge, the owner, sat on an upside-down detergent bucket smoking clove cigarettes while his machines thumped behind him. Jorge had tattooed two tiny socks on his left wrist after his twins were born and still rolled his sleeve up to show people when business was slow.

"Big cooking today?" he called.

"Lemon rice," Anika said.

Jorge whistled. "Save me from my own empanadas."

"No promises," Ravi said.

At home, the kitchen shrank around them the way it always did when both tried to work at once. Counter on one side, stove on the other, just enough space between for one person and one argument. The recipe card lived in a jar by the window, thick paper soft at the edges from oil fingers. Maya had written it in blue pen with slanted letters and three underlines under "LOW HEAT."

Anika read aloud to prove she could do it without help. "'One and a half cups basmati. Wash until water runs clear. Do not bruise curry leaves when tearing.'"

Ravi placed the rice canister on the counter. "You do rice and lemon. I'll do tempering."

"I can do tempering."

"You can. I'm saying I want to."

There it was again: request dressed as instruction. She took a breath and chose not to slice it apart yet.

She poured rice into the steel bowl and ran cold water over it. The first rinse turned the water chalky white. The second was less cloudy. The third was mostly clear. She rubbed the grains lightly between her fingers, hearing Maya's voice in memory and not memory, two channels slightly out of sync: not too hard, you'll break it, this is rice not gravel.

Ravi measured oil into the kadai. When it heated, mustard seeds started to pop like static. He added chana dal, urad dal, dried red chiles snapped in half. The curry leaves hit hot oil and exploded in a sharp green crackle that made both of them jump and then laugh at themselves.

"Still sounds like gunfire," Ravi said.

"It scared Amma every time too. She'd pretend it didn't."

"It would scare her, then she'd scare me by laughing while holding hot oil."

"She did that thing with her wrist so it wouldn't splatter."

Anika copied the flicking wrist motion in the air. Ravi watched, then handed her the turmeric without speaking.

They moved around each other in short practiced arcs, shoulder almost touching, then not. Ravi cut onions too fine and Anika told him so. He said fine onions disappear better. She said disappearing was not always the goal. He gave her a look she could not read and kept cutting.

The rice went into the pressure cooker. They waited through the hiss.

While they shelled roasted peanuts, Anika's phone buzzed on the fridge. Zoe, four messages in a row.

`u alive?`
`my brother shaved one eyebrow for ten dollars`
`mrs p looked at him for thirty full seconds and said "choices are data"`
`also school rumor: vice principal might get replaced by some ex-marine`

Anika laughed before she could swallow it.

Ravi glanced up. "Good news?"

"Zoe's brother did cosmetic self-destruction for money."

"Entrepreneur."

"Dumb entrepreneur."

She typed with peanut salt on her fingers.

`alive. making lemon rice.`
`tell your brother symmetry matters`

Zoe sent back a photo of one tragic eyebrow and a proud grin.

Ravi leaned against the sink. "How's Zoe doing with the new math teacher? The one who speaks like a podcast?"

"Mr. Kline. He says 'circle back' every ten minutes. If he says synergy one more time we're staging a coup."

"Please don't get suspended while staging your coup."

"We're not that organized."

He flicked peanut salt from his fingertips into the sink. "And Devon? Still asking you for homework help?"

"Yeah. He thinks fractions are a conspiracy."

"He's right about many conspiracies, maybe not that one."

They stayed there longer than the recipe required, talking about people who had nothing to do with ladders or rankings or injury protocols. The super's daughter had painted a mural of mangoes on the back alley wall and got yelled at, then got permission when everyone liked it. The bodega switched bread suppliers and half the block had opinions. Mrs. Alvarez was threatening to report the landlord again because the hallway lights died every third night and she was done climbing with grocery bags in the dark.

It felt almost easy until Ravi said, "High school tours start in a month. We should map weekends now before they fill."

Anika's shoulders tightened before her mind caught up. Map weekends. The old script sliding in with clean shoes.

"We can do school tours," she said carefully. "I'm not mapping every weekend."

"Not every weekend. Just the key ones."

"Key for what?"

He tossed peanut skins into the trash and missed. "For the calendar: school tours, training blocks we agreed to, your rest day, my shifts."

"That's not an answer."

Ravi's jaw worked once. "For the week not swallowing us whole."

There it was. Everything used to mean squash, and squash used to mean obedience.

She set the lemon on the cutting board and pressed the knife down harder than she meant to. Juice sprayed onto her wrist, into the small old scar near her thumb.

"We signed terms," she said. "Rest day stays a rest day. No surprise sessions. No hidden sparring. No turning school into dead time between drills."

"I'm not saying that."

"You always start with 'I'm not saying that.' Then somehow we end up there."

He reached for the salt and found none because he had put it back in the wrong cabinet. He opened three doors before noticing.

"I'm trying to plan like a responsible parent," he said, voice flattening into depot tone. "Tournaments don't care about our feelings. Applications don't pause because we need emotional weather reports."

She laughed once, short and sharp. "See? That's exactly it. I said school tours and you heard tournaments."

"I heard your future."

"You heard your panic."

The cooker clicked off with a metallic sigh. Neither moved.

Ravi wiped his hands on a dish towel, then folded it, then unfolded it. "Don't talk to me like I'm your opponent."

"Then stop coaching me in my kitchen."

He looked at her hard at that, at the claim inside the pronoun. My kitchen. Not his. Not the house's. The one place where Maya had let recipes matter more than outcomes.

Anika lifted the cooker lid and steam fogged her glasses. She blinked through it, eyes stinging from onion and lemon and old anger.

"I'll finish," she said.

"Anika..."

"I said I'll finish."

She expected him to argue. Instead he took his phone from the counter and his wallet from the hook.

"I've got to go pay the utility before noon cutoff," he said. "I'll walk. Twenty minutes."

He paused with one hand on the doorframe. "Don't skip the asafoetida. Last time we forgot and blamed each other for an hour."

His mouth twitched like he wanted to soften it, then thought better of it. Then he left.

Anika stood still until she heard the stairwell door slam at street level. Her heart kept racing anyway. She turned back to the stove and added asafoetida, peanuts, turmeric, rice, salt, lemon juice, then tossed it all on low heat with a wooden spoon. Her hands knew the order even when her head ran wild.

The rice changed color slowly, white to yellow to that exact bright not-neon shade Maya called festival yellow. Steam lifted lemon and curry and roasted nut, and for a second Anika was nine again with her feet swinging off a chair and Maya tapping the spoon against the pan to get her attention.

Taste, then adjust, Maya had said. Don't commit to a dish before tasting.

Anika tasted. Needed more salt. Needed one more lemon half. Needed less anger, which was not measurable and refused adjustment.

She packed one small steel container for Mrs. Alvarez and set it by the door. She packed another for Jorge. Then she saw Ravi's opened bill envelope on the table where he'd forgotten to hide it. City Housing Utility Arrears. Red stamp: FINAL NOTICE. A second paper clipped behind it, payment stub filled in with his handwriting.

Amount paid: $620.00.

She swallowed. Six hundred twenty was overtime and then some. It was maybe three night shifts and three mornings of pretending he was not tired. It was maybe her tutoring money multiplied by thirty-one.

Her phone buzzed again. Zoe.

`u okay?`
`if lemon rice is code for crying i can bike over`

Anika typed, deleted, typed again.

`i'm okay. just loud kitchen day.`

Zoe replied with a sticker of a raccoon holding a spatula like a weapon.

Ravi came back sweating through his shirt, receipt folded in his fist and a paper cup of tea in the other hand. He set both down and rinsed his face at the sink.

"Long line?" Anika asked.

"System down. Kiosk froze twice." He toweled his neck with the same dish towel and winced when his left ankle took weight. "Paid enough to stop the shutoff warning. Not enough to stop me from hating that office."

She tapped the damp corner of the receipt without touching the numbers. "Six-twenty is a lot."

"It's less than darkness and no fan on a hot night."

"Still."

"Still."

He drank tea, made a face, and added sugar straight from the spoon. "I forgot to eat breakfast. Bad planning."

She did not say you always forget your own body first. She handed him a handful of roasted peanuts instead.

He ate them and looked surprised, like kindness had arrived at the wrong address but he was taking delivery anyway.

By one-thirty, the table held lemon rice, cucumber raita, papad toasted directly on the flame until it blistered, and sliced mango gone soft at the edges. The ceiling fan clicked once every rotation. Outside, someone tested car speakers with a bass line that shook the window glass and then cut out mid-chorus.

Ravi served Anika first. He used the spoon Maya liked, the one with a bent handle from being caught in the garbage disposal in 2019.

"Tell me one piece of school gossip that would make your aunt pretend she disapproves while clearly enjoying it," he said.

Anika chewed and considered. "Evan in seventh grade started selling answer keys for quizzes. They were fake. He made forty-three dollars before anyone realized."

Ravi barked a laugh. "Forty-three?"

"He offered discounts for bulk purchases."

"That kid's going into finance and then prison."

"Maybe prison first."

Ravi reached for his water, and the utility receipt slid from his pocket onto the table, damp at one corner from sweat.

Anika tapped it with one finger. "You working again tonight?"

"Two app hours. Just till eleven."

"You said just till eleven last month and came home at two-thirty."

He held her gaze instead of ducking it. "You're right. No ghost shift. If it runs late, I text before midnight."

She watched him a second longer, then drummed her thumb once against the table. "I'm saving that sentence."

Ravi shook his head. "Any teacher casualties?"

"Ms. Patel slipped on glitter after art class and said a very creative swear in front of everyone."

"I hope it was deserved."

"It was magnificent."

They moved through stories like stepping stones. The crossing guard on 82nd had started carrying dog treats for every stray in a two-block radius and now got escorted to work by six mutts like a mayoral parade. Zoe's mother had forbidden horror movies after the eyebrow incident, so Zoe was reading plot summaries out loud and spoiling them for her brother on purpose. The upstairs teenager had failed his road test three times because he kept driving with both hands floating above the wheel "for aura."

Ravi offered his own: a depot dispatcher had tried to schedule the same driver on two routes at once, then blamed a frozen route screen when caught. He started another and caught himself halfway through. "Also, sponsor liaison emailed orientation windows for the invitational."

Anika's spoon stopped against her plate.

"I'm not springing anything," he said quickly. "I'm saying it out loud because hidden calendars are how I wrecked us."

She let the silence sit, then used her spoon to push one grain of rice into a straight line. "Keep saying it out loud."

"Aye aye."

A passenger had left a live turtle in a tote bag on the bus and no one claimed it for two days, so the whole office took turns feeding it lettuce.

Anika laughed so hard rice came up the wrong way and she coughed until her eyes watered. Ravi passed her water without fuss, and she took it.

When the plates were almost empty, the room slowed. Laughter drained first. Then sound from outside pressed in again: a siren two avenues over, a child crying in the hall, the old radiator hissing even though the apartment was already warm because the building did what it wanted.

Ravi stacked his fingers against his glass. "Can I ask you something without it becoming a fight?"

Anika stared at the mango slice on her plate. "Depends on the question."

He rolled his glass a quarter-turn between both palms, accepting the answer as fair. "Fall's coming. School gets harder. Invitational prep starts soon. I want to know what you want your week to look like. Not what you think I want. Yours."

She felt the old reflex rise: give numbers, give a schedule, give the answer that minimized blast radius. Monday ninety, Tuesday active rest, Wednesday ghosting. Her mouth was already building a neat weekly grid.

Instead she said, "I want one day where my body isn't a project."

Ravi looked down.

"And I want school to be school. Not recovery between sessions." Her voice stayed steady because she was gripping the underside of her chair hard enough to ache. "I want to see Zoe without calculating what it costs me. I want to play because I want to play, not because the debt counter is screaming."

Ravi rubbed his forehead. "Debt counter is screaming at me too, in case that wasn't clear."

"I know."

"I don't think you do."

He stood and carried both plates to the sink in one trip, too fast, shoulder clipped on the cabinet. He did not react. Water ran full force, then stopped. He came back and stayed standing, one hand on the chair back like he needed it.

"When you were at Priya's," he said, "I didn't sleep in our bedroom for four nights. I sat in the kitchen chair and watched the hallway. Like you were five and might wander out looking for water."

Anika stayed still.

"I kept thinking if I'd had better rules, tighter rules, none of this would've happened. The fracture. You leaving. Everything. So I made more rules in my head. More hours, more control, more... structure. Like structure could bargain with death."

The last word landed heavy and plain. He said death, not hospital complications or what happened to your mother.

He sat again because his knees seemed to decide for him. "You want plain? Here. I used your training to avoid my grief. I turned your life into timers and color-coded boxes on the fridge because if I stopped counting reps I had to count days since Maya stopped breathing, and I couldn't do that. I didn't know how."

Anika's chest tightened so fast she thought for a second she might throw up. She had wanted him to say it for months and now that he had, the room felt tilted.

Ravi kept going, voice rougher. "I told myself it was discipline, opportunity, protection. Some of it was. Some of it was me being terrified and calling it coaching. I was scared you'd break the way she broke. I was scared I'd fail you both. I was scared if you weren't in front of me every hour, something would take you too."

He pressed his thumb into the split nail until it whitened. "That's not an excuse. It's the truth."

Anika heard herself ask, "Why now?"

"Because you asked me to stop hiding behind calendars and checklists, and you were right." He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "And because I almost said 'we should increase volume' three times today while cutting onions. I can hear myself doing it. I don't want to keep doing it."

Silence sat with them. Not the old punishment silence. This one had edges but also room.

Anika folded and unfolded her napkin. "I can't be your anti-grief plan."

Ravi swallowed. "You're not. You're my kid, and I made you carry that weight."

"You don't get to panic and make it my problem."

"I don't. If I start spinning out, I call Priya or Tess. I don't dump it on you."

"You said that before."

He looked past her shoulder at the fridge clips, then back at her. "Then put conditions on me."

She looked at him for a long second. His face had gone smaller somehow, like stress had eaten whatever posture he used at work. She remembered him at the mediation table promising with clean sentences and measured breath, and she remembered his voice on the morning she left for Priya, sharp enough to use Maya like a lever. Both were true men sitting in the same chair.

"Okay," she said. "Extra training is a question. You ask. I answer. You don't sneak it in with words like maybe or just."

Ravi opened his mouth. "If the invitational date moves, we might need to-"

"See?" Her voice jumped. "That's the move."

Her nails bit crescents into her palm under the table.

He shut his mouth, breathed through his nose, and tried again. "You're right. I ask once. If you say no, it's no."

"If I say my shin hurts, we stop."

"Even if it's warm-up ache?" He lifted a hand before she could cut in. "I'm asking where the line is."

"One clarifying question. Then we stop. No bargaining me out of my own leg."

His fingers tightened on the chair back. "One question, then stop."

"No using Amma's name to win arguments. If you miss her, say you miss her. Don't put words in her mouth to corner me."

The split in his thumbnail reopened; a thin red line surfaced where he'd been pressing it.

"I did that," he said. "I won't do it again."

"And rest day stays rest day. Stretching is fine. Drills aren't. No ghosting disguised as recovery."

He set his bleeding thumb flat on the table and left it there. "Rest day is rest day."

She exhaled, but the hard part was still there. "If any of that slips, I go to Priya's that night."

Ravi flinched like she'd thrown cold water. "For one bad sentence?"

"For pain or surprise training, yes. For words, you get one in-the-moment correction. If you push past it, I'm gone."

He stared at the table, then at her, swallowing once. "I hate that this has to be a rule."

"It has to be."

"Okay." He pressed his palm over the wet ring his glass had left, as if pinning himself in place. His voice came out scraped raw. "If I slip, you leave. No debate."

"It's not a threat. It's the plan."

"I hear you."

He leaned back and covered his eyes with the heel of his hand for three seconds, maybe four. When he lowered it, the skin under his eyes was mottled red and one tear had dried into his stubble. His next breath snagged halfway in his chest before he forced it out through his teeth.

"I don't know how to grieve correctly," he said.

Anika almost said nobody does, but the sentence felt like a poster in a counselor's office, too clean for this table.

"You could start by not scheduling over it," she said. "Like today."

He rubbed at his mouth with his wrist and let out a thin breath. "Like today."

She stood and carried the mango plate to the sink. He moved to take it and she shook her head. "I got it."

At the sink, water ran cool over her wrists. Her hands trembled, then steadied. Ravi came beside her with the rice pot and waited until she shifted so they could stand shoulder to shoulder in the narrow space. The contact was light, just cloth touching cloth at the upper arm, but she did not move away.

After dishes, Anika packed leftovers into mismatched containers. She wrote names on blue painter's tape: `ALVAREZ`, `JORGE`, `HOME`. Ravi capped each container and lined them up military straight, then noticed what he was doing and deliberately made the line crooked. She snorted.

"You don't have to perform casual," she said.

"I know." He nudged one container half an inch further off-center. "I'm naturally chaotic."

"Liar."

He took the container marked `ALVAREZ`. "Want to drop with me?"

She considered. Leaving the apartment together could feel like pretending everything was fixed. Staying could feel like punishment. She chose ordinary.

"Yeah."

In the hall, the light on the third-floor landing had burned out again. They climbed by phone flashlight. Mrs. Alvarez opened on the second knock, wearing reading glasses at the end of her nose and holding a crossword book where she filled answers in blue gel pen and then circled clues she hated in red.

"My heroes," she said, taking the container. "If this is bland, I'm filing a complaint."

"It isn't bland," Anika said.

Mrs. Alvarez peered around her at Ravi. "You look less haunted today."

Ravi rubbed the back of his neck, eyes still red-rimmed. "Work in progress."

"Aren't we all." She tapped the crossword with her pen. "Tell your daughter the clue for seven down is wrong. 'Mythic bird, four letters.' They printed PHNX. That's not a word."

"Ask Zoe," Anika said. "She'll invent one."

Back downstairs, they dropped the second container at the laundromat. Jorge accepted it like a ceremonial object and swore he would trade only one shirt this week for emergency cash because "I've got nutrients now." Ravi laughed. Anika rolled her eyes.

By the time they got home, afternoon had softened toward evening. Sunlight hit the opposite building and bounced into their kitchen in a copper strip across the table. The air smelled like lemon and dish soap and the faint rubber note from the hallway mats.

Ravi took the utility receipt from his pocket and stuck it to the fridge with the old Eiffel Tower magnet Maya hated. Below it, the signed training agreement still held in place by two clips. Paper on paper: debt and terms, due dates and boundaries, practical proof that love in this house had to learn paperwork.

Anika watched him smooth the receipt flat.

"Want tea?" he asked.

"Yeah."

He filled the kettle. "Strong?"

"Strong."

While water heated, she opened her notebook from chapter three, the one with columns for reps and pain and memory that had changed shape over months. She turned to a blank page and wrote the date. No split times. No weight targets. Just:

`Saturday. Lemon rice.`

After a second she added:

`He said the word death.`

Then, below that:

`I stayed.`

Ravi set a mug by her elbow and did not read over her shoulder. He sat across from her, both hands around his own cup, and for several minutes they drank in the shared quiet without trying to improve it.

When the sky outside went from white to bruised blue, Anika's shin gave one dull pulse and then settled again. She pressed her thumb against the bone, felt nothing sharp, and let her hand fall away.

For once the day had not been won. It had only been lived, badly in places, honestly in others, and all the way through.
