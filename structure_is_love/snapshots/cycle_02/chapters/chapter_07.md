# Chapter 7

By the time Anika got off the Q66 and crossed Northern Boulevard, she had already done the math three different ways and every version made her chest tight.

The folded strip from last night was still tucked behind Maya's cracked watch strap, the edge rubbing her wrist each time the bus jerked.

If she stayed at Zoe's apartment until nine, she would miss one hundred and forty minutes of footwork, one full ghosting block, and forty serves from the left box. If she left at eight-thirty, she could still get in fifty minutes in the hallway between the bedrooms, if Dad was out on app-driving and did not hear the ball taps. If she left at eight, she could do all of it except the serves.

Then she remembered she had promised Zoe she would stay for cake, and cake was never at eight.

The plastic gift bag handle sawed a red line into her fingers. Inside was a sketchbook with thick paper, the best one she could afford at the pharmacy after school, plus a six-pack of neon pens that clicked when she shook the bag. The cashier had offered a glitter card for another three dollars. Anika had said no and watched the woman put it back on the spinning wire rack without looking at her.

Zoe's building smelled like laundry steam and old frying oil. On the second-floor landing, the number two had fallen off one apartment door and somebody had drawn it back with black marker, a crooked swan neck of ink. Music thudded through the wall before Anika knocked: bass from a pop song, a burst of shrieking laughter, then the flat digital gunfire of a video game.

Zoe opened the door on the second knock, already grinning, chipped black nail polish flashing as she grabbed Anika's wrist and hauled her in.

"You made it. Holy shit, I thought your dad was gonna invent a training emergency."

"I said I'd come."

"You also said that last month." Zoe shut the door with her heel and softened her tone so quickly it almost counted as kindness. "Come on. Shoes by the rack before my mom yells."

The apartment was hot with bodies and oven heat. Eight kids from school were packed into a living room that should have held five. Someone had draped silver streamers over the curtain rod. Two younger cousins chased each other under the coffee table, screaming in high falsetto every time their knees hit the rug. A boy from math class, Malik, sat cross-legged by the TV with three bottle caps lined up on the floor in front of him like tiny medals; he collected them from every soda he ever drank and rubbed the edges with his thumb when he was thinking.

On the dining table, four pizza boxes sweated grease into the cardboard. Anika's eyes landed there and stayed.

There were two plain slices, three pepperoni slices, and one pepper-and-onion slice that nobody had touched. The ranch cup was half full, and the garlic-knot tray was empty except for one torn-off end crust dusted with parmesan.

Calories, she thought before she could stop herself. Then she saw the watch on her own wrist and looked away, as if the numbers might have shown up on the face uninvited.

From the kitchen, Zoe's mother called, "If anybody puts soda on the sketchbooks again, I'm turning this into a silent reading party."

Ms. Martinez stepped into the doorway with a tray of paper plates balanced on one forearm and reading glasses pushed up in her hair like a second headband. She always dressed like she was about to leave for an errand and had forgotten to leave: jeans, old green cardigan, one hoop earring missing. Tonight there was a streak of blue frosting on her wrist. She looked at Anika and smiled with the kind of relief adults got when a child had actually come where she said she would.

"Anika, hi, baby. You want lemon-lime or cola?"

"Water is fine."

"This house is not a hospital waiting room. You can have sugar at a birthday party." Ms. Martinez lowered her voice as she set plates down. "But take your time. You look tired, mija."

Anika nodded and did not answer. She could hear herself breathing too shallow, the way she did before a hard rally. The living room noise pressed on her skin from every direction: song changes, controller clicks, somebody yelling that a character was cheating, the cousins shrieking again when one crashed into the couch. It was ordinary kid noise, untidy and loud and pointless.

The cracked strap pinched her pulse point.

Zoe pulled the gift bag free and peered inside. "No way. The good paper? Dude." She hugged the bag to her chest. Her eyes snapped up, suddenly sharp. "You remembered I hate pages that bleed through."

"Yeah."

"Thanks." Zoe squeezed Anika's shoulder once, quick and awkward, and shouted at the room. "Nobody touch this. If I catch your greasy fingers on this, I'll end you."

Malik did not look away from the game. "You said that at lunch and then traded me your fries."

"Different legal system," Zoe shot back.

Anika smiled without meaning to. It felt strange on her face, like trying on somebody else's glasses.

They ate on paper plates in laps and on the floor. Anika took one plain slice and folded it in half to keep grease from running down her wrist. She chewed each bite until it went almost sweet and counted swallows to keep her hands still.

Her molars ached from the control.

She could hear Dad's voice inside those numbers anyway: every hour matters, you lose shape quickly, and nobody outworks us.

A few minutes later Zoe dropped onto the rug beside her and held up a second slice. "You're doing the thing again."

"What thing?"

"Spreadsheet face." Zoe angled her own eyebrows down and pinched her mouth into a parody of concentration. "The one where you're calculating whether mozzarella is morally wrong."

"I'm just eating."

"You're auditing your pizza." Zoe bit into her slice and talked around the bite, shameless. "Relax your forehead before it gets stuck like that forever."

Anika's forehead did hurt. She had not noticed until Zoe said it.

She stretched her jaw and tried to look at the TV instead of the ache behind her eyes. On-screen, cartoon racers banged into rainbow-colored walls. Malik muttered predictions before each turn under his breath, too low for anyone except the person next to him: "He's gonna miss the boost. Yep. Banana peel, told you."

When the music playlist shifted to an older song, Zoe's cousin Camila jumped onto the couch and started conducting with a plastic lightsaber as if she were leading an orchestra. Each downbeat nearly took someone's eye out. Kids screamed, ducking and laughing. Ms. Martinez yelled from the kitchen that nobody was allowed to bleed on the rug because she had just paid the carpet cleaner.

The noise should have been unbearable.

Instead it loosened something.

Not all at once. Not enough to make breathing easy. But enough that the numbers in Anika's head stopped lining up in neat columns for almost a minute.

After pizza, Zoe clapped loudly until everyone looked at her.

"Game time. And yes, this one is humiliating." She held up a sleeve of store-brand chocolate sandwich cookies. "Forehead cookie challenge. No hands. Get it from your forehead to your mouth. Winner gets first cake slice and bragging rights forever."

"That's disgusting," Malik said, already standing up.

"Exactly. That's why it's perfect."

Anika started to shake her head, but Zoe saw it and cut her off.

"Nope. You're in. Birthday law."

"I don't want to."

"That's not a legal phrase in this room. Sit down. Tilt your head."

The circle closed around them on the rug. Camila counted down with both hands in the air, bracelets clacking. On three, everyone tipped their heads back and balanced cookies above their eyebrows.

Anika tried to solve it like drill work by using small movements and keeping her center.

The cookie slid down to the bridge of her nose and stuck there. She wrinkled hard, trying to walk it lower. It jumped sideways and lodged against her temple.

"You're making a face like a lizard doing algebra," Zoe wheezed.

Anika snorted. The cookie dropped straight down into the collar of her T-shirt.

For half a second there was stunned silence. Then the room detonated. Camila fell over laughing. Malik slapped the floor so hard one of his bottle caps skittered under the sofa. Even Ms. Martinez leaned on the kitchen doorway, laughing with her hand over her mouth.

Anika sat frozen for a beat and reached into her shirt, pulling out the crumbed, warm cookie in two pieces.

"Disqualified," she said.

"Tragic," Zoe said, wiping tears from her eyes. "Legendary, but tragic."

Anika heard a sound and did not recognize it as her own laugh until it happened again, louder this time, bent in the middle. Her stomach hurt from it, not from planks or hunger but from laughing too hard.

The game ended in chaos and cheating accusations and frosting on the carpet anyway. Zoe got first slice after declaring herself commissioner and judge. Nobody objected because everyone wanted cake before Ms. Martinez changed her mind and made them sing first.

When they did sing, it was off-key and too loud and full of fake dramatic vibrato, and Zoe bowed to an imaginary balcony like a washed-up singer in a sparkly jacket. Anika joined in on the second line.

The cake was chocolate with blue frosting roses. Anika ate the whole piece without doing the math until she was already licking icing from her thumb.

Then guilt hit all at once, mean and efficient. She thought about a sugar spike, slower feet tomorrow, one hundred and nine minutes lost now and one hundred and thirteen by the time she got home, worse shin pain when she got heavy, and no extra drills because neighbors would complain if the ball hit after ten.

She set the paper plate down too hard and the plastic fork snapped.

"Hey." Zoe touched her elbow. "Walk with me? Balcony break. My cousins are entering a feral phase."

Anika nodded, grateful for an excuse to leave the room before somebody asked why her eyes had gone flat.

The fire escape outside Zoe's bedroom window was rusted orange and cold through thin leggings. The street below smelled like wet concrete and bus exhaust. Somebody on the corner argued in Spanish, the words quick and bright, while a delivery scooter idled with its hazard lights blinking. Inside, the party noise came through the glass as a muffled pulse.

Zoe sat with one knee tucked up and scraped blue frosting off her thumbnail with the edge of her other nail.

"So," she said. "You gonna pretend that was fun, or should I write down that you suffered bravely through my birthday?"

"It was fun."

"Cool. Because you looked like you were timing your chewing." Zoe waited a second and added, "I know that face too."

Anika stared at alley light striping the wet bricks.

"I've got practice in the morning," she said. "If I mess up, I feel it all week."

"You have practice every morning."

"Exactly."

Zoe exhaled hard through her nose. "See, this is the part where you turn into a robot and I want to throw a shoe at you."

"Don't. Your mom would kill us."

"My mom would kill me for ruining her own shoe. She likes those little death heels." Zoe leaned back against the brick and looked at Anika directly. "I'm not joking now, though. You disappear for weeks. Then you text me like, hey, want to hang out tonight? Like I'm takeout you can order when your schedule opens."

Anika flinched.

"It's not like that."

"It's exactly like that." Zoe's voice stayed low, not cruel, which made it worse. "I miss you, and then when you show up you're halfway gone anyway, counting something in your head. Minutes, bites, whatever."

Anika worried the cracked plastic strap with her thumb until the edge pressed a groove into her skin.

"I'm trying," she said.

"Trying what?"

"To keep everything from falling apart."

"Everything what? School? Squash? Your dad's mood?"

"All of it."

Zoe held her gaze and did not let her look away.

"You never say her name anymore," Zoe said quietly.

The words landed like a ball to the sternum.

Anika's first instinct was to answer the wrong question. She heard herself begin: "My serve percentage dropped three points last week and—"

"Nope." Zoe shook her head once. "Don't stats me right now. I'm right here."

Anika shut her mouth.

Below them, a bus wheezed to a stop and knelt for passengers. The hiss of the doors opening sounded, for one terrible second, like the oxygen line in the hospice room when the nurse turned the valve.

She blinked hard.

"Every night," she said, barely above a whisper. "I still listen for my mom's key in the door."

The sentence came out plain, almost calm. Her body did not match it. Her shoulders had climbed to her ears. Her jaw shook.

"I hear somebody in the hallway and I think maybe she forgot she died." Anika swallowed and wiped her nose with the back of her hand before she could stop herself. "I know how stupid that sounds."

"It doesn't sound stupid." Zoe's voice went softer than Anika had heard it in months. "It sounds awful."

"I keep waiting for the jingle. She had that ugly plastic tag from the hospital, remember? It clicked against the keys. I can hear it even when it isn't there." Anika pressed her palm against her chest as if she could flatten the panic. "Then nobody comes in. Or it's Dad, and I can tell by his step on the third stair because his ankle still catches, and then I feel relieved and mad at the same time, and then I hate myself because relieved at what? That he came home? That she didn't?"

Zoe let the silence sit. She did not fill it with comfort lines from a movie. She just put her shoulder against Anika's shoulder, steady pressure through sweatshirt fabric.

"I wasn't mad because you train," Zoe said after a while. "I was mad because you cut me out and acted like that was normal."

"I didn't mean to cut you out."

"Doesn't matter what you meant. This is what it felt like." Zoe nudged her with her elbow, not gentle this time. "You ghost me for weeks and blow up my phone at two in the morning when you're scared. I'm not doing the reset-button thing anymore."

Anika nodded before she could talk herself out of it.

"Okay."

"Say it like you heard me."

"I heard you. I can't do that anymore."

"Good." Zoe scraped at the rust with the toe of her sneaker. "Also, for the record, if you text me at two in the morning because you're listening for keys again, I'll answer. I'll be pissed, but I'll answer."

A laugh escaped Anika, wet and broken.

"That's not a good rule."

"It's the honest one, that's all." Zoe tilted her head. "Can you text me when you're starting to spiral, not after you've sat there listening for keys for an hour? Maybe once?"

Anika stared at her hands.

"I found paperwork last week that made everything feel like a timer," she said. "Now I think if I train harder I can stop something bad from happening, like if I get good enough fast enough maybe the house stays standing and Dad stops looking like he's about to crack, and maybe—"

She stopped because the words sounded ridiculous as soon as they were outside her mouth.

"You're eleven," Zoe said, sharp with disbelief. "That's not your job."

"I know."

"Do you?"

Anika did not answer.

Inside the apartment, Camila pounded on the window and shouted that they were opening presents. Zoe yelled back that they were coming and flipped her cousin off with theatrical affection. Camila cackled and vanished.

Zoe stood and offered a hand down to Anika.

"Come on. We can be tragic for exactly one more minute. Then you're helping me open twenty-seven slime kits and pretending to love all of them."

Anika took her hand and stood.

"Twenty-seven?"

"People think I like slime because I said that one time in fourth grade. This is my burden." Zoe squeezed Anika's fingers once before letting go. "You staying till the end?"

The watch on Anika's wrist read 8:41.

In her head, she still tracked the missed ghosting block, missed serves, and missed hallway drills.

In front of her, Zoe waited for an answer that was not a schedule.

"Yeah," Anika said. "I'm staying."

"Good."

Back in the living room, wrapping paper flew. Ms. Martinez passed around cut fruit because she did not trust any of them to survive on frosting alone. Malik narrated each gift before it was opened and was correct enough times that everyone accused him of cheating. Zoe opened the sketchbook last and held it up like a trophy.

"This one wins," she announced.

"Biased," Malik said.

"Absolutely. It's my party."

When parents started texting that pickup rides were outside, the room thinned quickly. Kids found mismatched shoes and yelled goodbye from the hallway. Camila fell asleep upright against a pillow, blue icing dried on her cheek like war paint.

Anika helped stack paper plates without being asked. Ms. Martinez rinsed bowls at the sink and spoke over the running water.

"Thank you for coming," she said, keeping her eyes on the dishes so it would not feel like a speech. "She missed you."

Anika folded a pizza box flat. "I missed her too."

Ms. Martinez nodded once. "Good. Keep missing each other out loud, not silently. Silent is where people get lost." She bumped the faucet off with her wrist. "You need me to call your dad and say this was my fault for ending late? I can sound very persuasive."

Anika almost said yes. Then she pictured Ravi's face if another adult managed his schedule for him.

"No. I'll handle it."

"All right. Take cake for home."

"He won't eat it."

"I didn't ask about him." Ms. Martinez slid a foil-wrapped wedge into Anika's hand. "For you. Midnight if necessary."

On the walk to the bus stop, the foil packet warmed her palm. The street had gone glossy from a thin rain while they were inside. Storefront gates were half down. A man sprayed bleach water across the sidewalk outside his deli and pushed the gray foam toward the curb with a broom.

Zoe walked her to the corner, arms folded against the wind.

"Text me when you get in," Zoe said.

"I'll."

"Not a thumbs-up. Actual words."

"Bossy."

"Correct."

The bus took twelve minutes to come and another sixteen to crawl through traffic. Anika stood the whole ride because every seat smelled like wet coats and old fries. The foil packet in her pocket knocked against her thigh at each stop.

She watched her reflection in the dark bus window, overlaying the city lights: small face, hair frizzed from steam and rain, frosting still at the cuff of her sleeve. She looked like a kid who had been at a birthday party. She could not remember when she had last looked like that.

At her stop she got off, crossed under the tracks, and climbed the apartment stairs two at a time until the third flight, where her shin pinched and forced her to slow. By the time she unlocked the door, the hallway motion light had already clicked off once.

Ravi sat at the kitchen table under the yellow overhead light, still in his depot polo, collar dark with sweat at the neck. His left ankle was stretched out straight in front of him, shoe off, sock heel rubbed thin. A legal pad lay open beside his mug. Columns of times and checkboxes marched down the page.

He looked from the legal pad to her face, measuring the gap.

"You're late."

"Bus was slow."

"You were supposed to be back by nine-thirty."

"Cake was late." She set her keys down and slipped out of damp sneakers. "I texted at nine-twenty-two."

"I saw it." Ravi rubbed his forehead. "What did you eat?"

The question came out automatic, same tone as what time is warm-up, but the legal pad made it feel like an audit.

"Pizza and cake."

His mouth tightened. Blue frosting was still under her thumbnail, bright as evidence.

"How much pizza?"

Heat climbed up the back of her neck. She could hear the scratch of his pen before she answered.

"Dad."

"How much?"

"I'm asking because tomorrow starts at five and if your stomach is heavy we adjust intervals."

One ordinary night, she thought, and still the room turned into a test station.

"One slice. One piece of cake." She paused. "And fruit."

He nodded as if logging data. "Hydration?"

"Two cups of water and one soda." She put the foil packet on the table without comment.

Ravi glanced at the foil packet and searched her face.

"Did you do any mobility before coming up?"

Her right shin pulsed once, sharp, like it wanted a vote.

"No."

"Then do ten minutes now. Calves, hip openers, band work if your shin is tight."

The old script rose in her throat, ready-made, and she heard it in full: she was sorry, it was her fault, and she would make it up.

She swallowed it.

"I'm going to shower and sleep," she said. "I'll do warm-up in the morning."

Ravi's chair scraped as he sat forward. "Anika, you can't treat this casually. One loose night turns into a loose week."

"It wasn't loose." Her voice stayed level, surprising her. "I went to my friend's birthday. I stayed for presents. That's all."

"That's not all. It changes recovery, schedule, focus."

"Maybe. But I still went." She met his eyes and held them. "I'm not taking it back."

Ravi reached across the table and held out his hand. "Give me your phone."

The words landed colder than the rain on her sleeves.

"What?"

"If you want to improvise your nights, you lose phone privileges. No calls except school logistics. No hanging out after school this week. Home, training, sleep. Hand it over."

"No." The answer came out before fear could sand it down. "I'm not giving you my phone because I ate cake at a birthday."

His jaw flexed. "This isn't about cake. This is about control. You don't get to drift right before qualifiers."

"I'm not drifting. I'm living in my own life for three hours."

Ravi grabbed the legal pad and wrote in hard block letters, the pen digging grooves through the page.

4:40 WAKE-UP.
PHONE CURFEW 9:00 PM.
NO SOCIAL PLANS UNTIL QUALIFIERS.

He tore the sheet free and slapped it flat beside her keys. "Those are the consequences. Starting tomorrow."

Her chest tightened so fast it hurt. She wanted to sweep the paper onto the wet floor and grind it under her heel until the ink smeared.

Instead she picked up the foil cake and turned toward the hall.

"I had fun," she said without looking back.

Behind her, his answer came clipped and tired. "Go sleep. Don't be late for your own penalty."

In her room she peeled off damp clothes, showered quickly, and changed into an old camp T-shirt with cracked letters across the chest. She sat on the edge of her bed with the foil packet and ate half the cake with her fingers in the dark, blue frosting staining the pad of her thumb.

Through the wall she could hear Ravi moving in the kitchen, cabinet doors opening and shutting in measured rhythm, kettle filling, followed by the low creak of his ankle on the third hallway board as he passed her door toward his room.

Anika froze, listening.

It was not his step she waited for. She waited for the other one, the imaginary lighter one with the quick key-jingle and the plastic hospital tag tapping metal. She listened until her ears rang and the building settled and nothing happened.

She reached for her notebook on the floor.

She wrote the usual headers: date, time home, food.

Her pen hovered.

Then, underneath the usual columns, she wrote a sentence she had not written in months, the words uneven because her hand shook.

I laughed so hard a cookie fell into my shirt and everyone saw.

She stared at it. She did not cross it out.

Before turning off the lamp, she texted Zoe.

Home now. Dad just grounded me till qualifiers.

Three dots appeared immediately.

That's bullshit. I'm still here. Don't disappear again.

Anika typed I won't, deleted it, and replaced it with I'll try. She sent that version and hated how small it looked.

The watch on her wrist flashed 10:47. In five hours and fifty-three minutes, the 4:40 alarm would go off. Training would begin. Her shin would complain on the first lunges. Dad would track intervals on the legal pad. By six-thirty, they'd be under the Midtown lobby lights for qualifiers. The new nine p.m. curfew would lock her phone on the kitchen table tomorrow night whether she wanted it there or not.

All of that was still true.

She lay down anyway, frosting sugar on her tongue, party noise ghosting in her ears, and listened to her own anger breathing beside the old waiting at the door.
