# Chapter 15

The Midtown club smelled like lemon cleaner and old money, two sharp notes under the colder air that blasted through the vents above Court Three. Anika stood at the back wall in her warm-up jacket and watched condensation crawl down the outside of her water bottle. Outside, the city had been wet heat and bus exhaust and people swearing at stalled trains. In here, everybody's hair looked dry and expensive.

Her name sat on the white bracket board in black marker: IYER, A.
Under it, the board listed BELL, C.
It was a quarterfinal match, best of five.
The signed agreement from Priya's table rode in the side pocket of Anika's bag, folded small and loud.

Ravi had arrived early enough to claim two seats up in the second row, off to the right of the glass. He had not taken the coaching box, and he was not pressed against the door the way he used to stand with his hand flat on the frame, breathing like he could push her into each point by force. He sat with his knees spread and his elbows on his thighs, ticket stub folded into a hard little rectangle between his fingers. Every few seconds he unfolded it and folded it again.

Anika bounced once on her toes and felt her right shin answer with a quiet, dull warning. It wasn't pain exactly, just a knuckle tapping from inside the bone. Damp weather always did this. She pressed two fingertips against the ridge through her sock, waited, then let go.

The sensation stayed blunt, a muted knock instead of the knife-edge she feared.

She kept her face blank and walked to the service box for ghost swings.

Cora Bell came in from the far hallway with a navy academy duffel and a kit that looked pressed. Even her compression sleeve, black and pulled over her left knee, was clean enough to reflect the fluorescent strip above the bench. She set her bag down, lined up three practice balls by the red dot like she was arranging medication, then crouched to retie the same lace she had already tied. On the second knot she paused, looked up at the scoreboard monitor, and rolled her shoulders back until her posture clicked into place.

When she saw Anika watching, she gave one short smile.

"Hey," Cora said. "You made it back quick. Thought they'd keep you on a leash longer."

Her tone stayed moderate, not loud, and it still scraped.

Anika bounced the ball against the sidewall and caught it on the third rebound. "I'm cleared."

"Yeah." Cora's mouth tilted half an inch. "I read. Compliance superstar."

There was no cartoon sneer in it, no hiss. Cora sounded like she was discussing weather, which made it worse.

Anika nodded once. "Good luck."

"You too." Cora picked up her racquet and took two fluid shadow swings. "Seriously. I don't want an asterisk if I beat you."

She said it with the same polished tone she'd use for an interviewer. Anika could not decide whether that made it honest or mean.

The referee for their court, a woman with a silver braid and a pink insulin pump clipped at her waistband, called them in for the pre-match check. Her name tag read M. GARCIA in block letters. She looked at Anika's shoes, then at Cora's, then at both racquet strings with the fast competence of somebody who had repeated these gestures for years.

"Any equipment issues, say it now," Garcia said.

Neither spoke.

"Good. Warm-up starts when I call it. Keep your traffic clear and your appeals clean. I'm not interested in drama."

Cora's expression did not move, but she pressed both thumbs under the edge of her knee sleeve and tugged it up a centimeter. Anika noticed a faint line of old tape residue on Cora's calf where the skin was lighter. Private evidence under public polish.

Ravi stood as they walked toward the door, then remembered himself and sat back down before she reached the service box. His mouth opened, closed, then he raised his hand as if to straighten imaginary hair behind his own ear. A gesture with no message in it.

Anika stepped inside the glass.

The door seal thudded shut behind her. Sound changed immediately: HVAC hum, soles squeaking, ball on wall like a hard rubber heartbeat. Spatial pressure slid into place around her ribs. Four white walls, red lines, tinny crowd noise leaking through the door seams.

She had to compete under the new limits.
She could not try to dominate or prove she was indestructible; she had to compete.

She said the word to herself once, lips barely moving.

Warm-up was ninety seconds each. Cora hit hard drives early, then softened into high lobs that died deep with no warning. It was the first hint that pace change was habit for her, not trick.

Anika answered with straight length, right side then left, feet set each time before contact. She used discipline as architecture. If she rushed, she sprayed. If she sprayed, Cora would own the T in under a game.

"Time," Garcia said.

Cora won the spin and chose serve. She rolled the ball in her palm, bounced it twice, and waited until the gallery quieted before starting her motion. Not long enough for a violation. Long enough to make everybody look at her.

First rally: hard serve nicked the left wall, fast enough that Anika had to lunge and lift. Cora pounced and drove low to the back right. Three shots later Anika guessed wrong and moved up for a drop that never came.

Garcia called the point for Cora.

Second rally: Cora opened with a high defensive arc that bought herself five seconds and reset both lungs. Next shot, she cut the pace in half again with a float to the back. Then she suddenly ripped a straight drive like a slap.

Anika got there late and framed it, and the score moved to 2-0.

By 4-1, Anika's calves were already hot. Not burning, just full. Cora was changing the speed of each exchange the way some people changed tone mid-sentence: quick-quick-slow, hold, burst, feather, smash. The rhythm would not settle.

Anika went to process. She wiped her palm, reset her feet, and chose one target per rally. Deep backhand. Deep backhand again. Make Cora hit from behind her hip. On the next point, she got that exact ball, stepped in, and sent a hard straight length tight enough to die before the nick.

Cora could only scrape a boast short, and Anika punished it crosscourt for 4-2.

Ravi made a tiny fist in the stands, then smoothed his hand flat against his knee like he had touched something hot.

With Cora up 6-3, she took a full extra beat before serve, looking down at her strings as if checking a fray that was not there. Garcia said, "Play." Cora nodded, served, and then attacked with immediate pace, catching Anika still adjusting to the pause.

The score stretched to 7-3.

Anika swallowed hard. The cool air tasted chemical. She could hear two kids in the row behind Ravi whispering odds and names of academies and who had gotten private-sponsor shoes this summer. Somebody said, "Bell's got this in four." Somebody else said, "Iyer used to outlast everybody."

The phrase used to stung worse than the score. If she answered that with speed just to prove she still could, she would hand Cora exactly what she wanted and burn through the load limits before game two.

Down 8-4, Anika changed her own tempo for the first time. She slowed on purpose before returning serve, bounced once on her heels, then sent a high cautious lob that forced Cora to take it late. Next shot, Anika drove low and hard. Then she held, showed crosscourt, and straightened at the last instant.

Cora guessed cross and ended up wrong-footed, and the score became 8-5.

Anika took two more points with disciplined width, but Cora closed the game 11-7 with a backhand drop that died as if the floor had swallowed it.

She refused panic because it was only one game.

Anika walked to the towel hook and did not look at the stands. She knew Ravi would be there with his jaw locked and numbers running behind his eyes. She could not carry his math and her own.

Game two opened cleaner. Anika found a serve variation to Cora's body that jammed her left arm and drew short returns. She converted twice. 3-1.

Cora answered with a grin that showed one chipped canine on the left side. New detail Anika had never noticed from across a court. Human, not poster.

"Nice," Cora said, almost approving. "Do it again."

Anika did. This time Cora expected it and volleyed early to the front right. Anika scraped it up. Cora lifted. Long rally. Twenty-two shots, maybe more. Anika stopped counting after twelve because counting made her breathe wrong.

She felt her shin on shot nineteen, a low pulse under the bone.
She stayed on it anyway and won the point with a high dying length that clung to the sidewall, pushing the score to 4-2.

Ravi shifted in his seat, leaned forward, then leaned back. His right hand rose two inches from his thigh, fingers splayed in the old signal for widen angle. He froze, looked at his own hand, and closed it slowly. Then he tucked both hands under his legs.

Anika saw it in the mirror reflection of the front glass, blurred but unmistakable.

No code was coming from the seats. She had to pick the next pattern herself.

The thought came unwelcome and stayed.

Cora changed tactic at 5-4. She stopped going for outright winners and started feeding Anika medium-paced balls to the same back corner, over and over, forcing her to generate her own pace from tired legs. Mean in a smart way. No hero shot, just attrition arranged like homework.

Anika recognized the trap and stepped up half a pace on the T. She took the ball earlier, cut angles short, and refused to overhit because discipline mattered more than ego. The score moved to 6-5 and then 7-5.

When Cora finally forced a loose ball with a disguised hold, Anika chased, got a racket face under it, and somehow found a high defensive lift that bought breath. Crowd noise swelled and collapsed around her like surf.

She took game point at 10-8 and converted on the next rally with a straight kill that kissed the nick and barely bounced for 11-8.
The match stood at one game all.

She did not celebrate. She only flexed her fingers and felt the pulse at her throat. Her chest worked hard but even.

Her knees held steady.

Between games, Garcia drank from a stainless bottle covered in stickers from half the borough leagues and made a note on her clipboard. "Let's keep the clear requests early," she said to both girls. "I'm not reading minds."

"Yes, ma'am," Cora said.

"Got it," Anika said.

Game three became a knife fight in small spaces. Cora sped up the start of rallies with quick serves to the backhand, slowed the middle with hanging length, then spiked the finish with sudden low drives. Anika met it with structure: breathe in on bounce, breathe out on contact, commit to depth, recover to T, no drama.

They collided lightly in the middle at 3-3 as both moved for a tight drop. Garcia called a let. Cora lifted a hand in apology. Anika nodded without words.

Cora tried a boast at 5-4 and left it sitting up; Anika put it away. The next rally burned Anika when she guessed cross and Cora went straight to level at 5-5. They fought through a forty-shot exchange to 6-6, ending with Cora clipping the tin and saying a clipped, private "damn" that did not match her polished face.
Anika edged ahead at 7-6.

Ravi was no longer trying to signal. He sat with his shoulders pulled high, ticket stub now shredded into paper curls in his fist.

By 8-7, the first real fatigue wave rolled through Anika's hips, thick and sudden. Reduced volume had made her cleaner in short bursts and less certain in these long grinding exchanges. That gap was why Priya had forced signatures onto paper and why Tess would bench her if she lied. She could chase one flashy winner for the row-three voices, or she could keep forcing Cora to hit from bad space. She chose depth.

Cora saw it too. She lengthened the next three rallies, forcing corner to corner, and took two points to lead 8-9.

Anika served wide. Cora returned short. Anika drove deep. Cora counter-dropped. Anika got there with her last clean stride and flicked it up. Cora volleyed aggressively and clipped the top of the tin, bringing it to 9-9.

The gallery had gone still enough that Anika could hear the squeak of Cora's knee sleeve fabric when she bent before receive.

Anika moved to 10-9, game ball, after a stroke call.
She lost it on a rushed drop, and the score returned to 10-10.

Deuce turned into another tempo trap. Cora took extra seconds before one serve, wiped her brow with the back of her wrist, bounced three times, and sent a high conservative ball that forced Anika to generate. Next rally she skipped the pause and fired low with immediate volley pressure. By the time Anika mapped the shift, Cora had edged ahead.

Cora moved ahead 11-10.

Anika held with a tight drive that died deep. 11-11.

Next point lasted forever. Anika's lungs scraped. Sweat stung her eyes. Her right shin buzzed with warning but stayed blunt, never sharp. Cora tried to pin her in the back left. Anika lifted. Cora cut it off early. Anika scrambled, retrieved, recovered, hit width, hit width again.

On the eighteenth shot Cora guessed drop and moved in. Anika saw the movement and punched length instead for a winner that made it 12-11.
She had game ball.

She finished it with patience, not violence: deep straight, deep straight, then a measured drop when Cora's feet stayed back for 13-11.
She now led two games to one.

When she came out for the ninety-second break, Ravi was standing now, then forcing himself down into the seat as she looked up. He pressed both palms to his thighs like he was pinning himself there.

He did not mouth instructions.

He only said, quiet but audible through the crack at the door when Garcia opened it for ventilation, "Breathe, Niks. Just breathe."
No command pattern followed, no verbs about winning, no shape of punishment behind it; only breath.

Game four punished her anyway.

Cora started taking the ball absurdly early, volleying from midcourt before rallies could settle. When Anika tried to counter with height, Cora waited and hammered. When Anika went hard and low, Cora feathered and made her sprint. Pace whip, pause, whip again.

The score turned to 2-5 and then 3-7.

Down 4-8, Anika hit a perfect length and still lost the rally when Cora countered with a drop so soft it looked accidental. Anika reached it half a beat late and shoved the ball into tin with a sound like a small gunshot.

The crowd inhaled together, and the scoreboard read 5-9.

Her face burned. Her shoulders felt poured full of wet sand. She heard, from somewhere in row three, a man laughing and saying, "She's cooked."

Shame came hot and immediate, old as chapter five, as the day she had limped alone running suicides because pain had felt like proof. The old script rose in her body and told her that after this she should punish herself with extra ghosts and stair repeats until nausea so she could earn the right to stop hurting by hurting more.

She nearly said yes to it on instinct. On the next rally she could have gone for a reckless front-court nick and maybe bought one roar from the crowd. Instead she sent three high lengths to reset the exchange and made Cora earn the finish.

Then she saw her own reflection in the side glass, small and flushed and still upright, and the script hesitated.

She had to compete under the new limits.

That was the contract she had signed with Priya's black pen while Ravi stared at the table and then signed too.

Cora closed game four 11-6 with ruthless efficiency and no showboating. She took what was there and moved on.
The match was tied at two games all, and the fifth was the decider.

Garcia called for a brief wipe because sweat had pooled near the front left. A volunteer ran in with a towel and did the quick circular mop. He was a teen boy with acne along his jawline and an ankle bracelet peeking above his sock. On his way out he whispered to nobody, maybe to himself, "Don't slip, please don't slip," with the prayerful panic of somebody who had already watched one bad fall this season.

Anika drank. Her hand shook enough to tap bottle against teeth.

Ravi stayed seated. He lifted his hand once, index finger half-raised in a tiny old signal for hit deep middle. He looked at it, exhaled, and lowered it to his lap. He rubbed his left ankle through his slacks, a private habit when pain or panic climbed, then folded both arms.

He let her go back in without another word.

Game five started with a rally that announced the terms: thirty-one shots, no clean winner, both girls refusing short vanity. Anika took it with a backhand drive tight enough to shave paint, and the score opened at 1-0.

Cora answered with two fast points and a delayed serve that made Anika start then stop, burning a little extra oxygen before contact and moving ahead 1-2.

At 2-2, Anika hit a good width and recovered late. Cora punished the open court to make it 2-3.

Anika forced Cora deep at 3-3 and earned a loose reply she could kill. She did, then caught herself wanting to glance at the stands for approval. She kept her eyes on the back wall instead, and the score moved to 4-3.

Cora nodded once, as if checking a box.

"There you go," she said between points, no smile now.

Anika did not answer.

They traded to 6-6, then 7-7. Each point pulled a little more from her legs. Her breathing stayed loud but controlled. Her shin still buzzed but never spiked. She filed that fact in a hidden ledger even while she fought.

Facing 7-8, she retrieved a dying drop, then a second, then a boast, and finally forced a weak ball she could have slapped crosscourt for style. She chose straight and deep instead, tying it at 8-8.

The rally that broke it came at 8-9. Cora served quick. Anika dug it out. Cora slowed with two high lifts, then accelerated with a hard body line that jammed Anika's swing. The return floated short, and Cora killed for 8-10.
Cora had match ball.

Anika took one deep breath and stood still until the tremor in her forearm settled.

Cora bounced the ball once, twice, with no extra theatrics now.
She served tight to the backhand.

Anika got it up, deep enough to keep rally alive. Cora drove hard to the opposite corner. Anika reached, set, sent length. Cora held, showed cross, went straight. Anika slipped half a step on the still-damp patch near midcourt and recovered late.

Her final shot sat up in the middle.

Cora stepped in and cracked a forehand winner that hit three inches above the tin and died.
Garcia called the score 11-8 and the match to Cora Bell, three games to two.

For half a second the world went silent in Anika's head. Then sound flooded back: claps, one sharp whistle, somebody saying "Hell of a quarter," Garcia announcing official score, sneakers squeaking as people stood.

Anika stood upright.

Her lungs hurt. Her shoulders trembled. Her right shin throbbed like a warning light that had not yet turned red.

She kept standing under her own weight, breathing through the shake instead of folding into the old theater of worth.

She walked to the center and held out her hand.

"Good match," she said.

Cora's palm was dry and warm. "Good match," she replied, then, lower so only Anika heard: "You made me work for every point. That's not fake."

Up close, Cora's mascara had smudged at the outer corners and she had a crescent moon of ball fuzz stuck to sweat on her neck. She looked thirteen then, not polished property of an academy.

"Thanks," Anika said.

She meant it and hated that she meant it.

Cora glanced toward her own side of the gallery where two adults in club blazers were already motioning for her. "I've got to go do the polite thing," she muttered. "See you next draw."

She jogged off with her posture back in place before she hit the door.

Garcia signed the score sheet and held it out for both girls. "Strong discipline," she said to Anika, tapping the page once with her pen. "You kept your shape late."

The referee's compliment was clinical and brief, but it pressed under her ribs where shame had been blooming.

Outside the court, phones were already up. A boy from another bracket had posted a clip before she had even zipped her bag. She saw her own face frozen mid-lunge on somebody's screen with the caption CLOSE ONE.

Public disappointment had texture. It came as eye contact dropped a fraction too soon. It came as coaches she did not know saying "tough one" with practiced sympathy. It came as Ravi's friends from the rec center sending him messages that lit his phone one after another while he kept it face-down on his thigh.

Ravi waited until they were clear of the bench area before he spoke.

"Water first," he said.
He left the nine-all rush alone and kept tonight pointed at recovery.

Anika uncapped the bottle and drank until her stomach sloshed.

They walked down the corridor toward the locker rooms, past framed black-and-white photos of champions with perfect teeth and surnames carved into silver plaques. The hallway smelled like detergent and hot electrical wiring.

At the end, near the vending machine that never took crumpled bills, she stopped.

Her hands had started shaking harder now that motion had ended. Shame moved through her in pulses: you lost, you lost, everyone saw, everyone saw. Right behind it, quieter and therefore harder to trust, another feeling: relief with teeth still in it.

Her shin had held.
Her body had held.

She hated how good that felt.

Ravi set both bags down and leaned against the cinderblock wall. For a long moment he said nothing. He watched her the way he watched weather radar at home before deciding whether to leave early for a shift: not passive, not in control, just paying attention.

"I'm sorry," she said before he could start. The words came out rough. "I should've taken that nine-eight rally earlier. I went safe and then I got jammed and then at match ball I slipped and--"

"Hey." He lifted one hand. "Stop."

"You don't owe me an apology for losing a quarterfinal in five."

The sentence hit her at a strange angle and nearly made her laugh, except her throat was too tight.

"I let her set pace," Anika said. "She kept changing tempo and I kept reacting. If I had more volume in my legs I could've forced longer at the speed I wanted."

"That's true," Ravi said.

He met the loss head-on and left it in plain language.

"It's also true that you adapted three different times when she shifted. You won game two by taking the ball early. You stole game three by refusing your ego." He rubbed his forehead with thumb and forefinger. "And it's true that at eight-all in the fifth you still had options. That means you're in the right match."

His voice held analysis, but it did not have the old edge that turned every sentence into a demand note.

Anika stared at the vending machine spiral where a peanut-butter cracker pack hung crooked.

"I wanted to beat her so bad," she said. "Not just beat. I wanted to make everybody shut up about the fracture and the limits and how I used to be this and now I'm that."

Ravi nodded. "I know."

"And also"--she swallowed, angry at herself already--"also when it ended and nothing tore and I could still stand, I felt..."

She could not find a word that did not sound cowardly.

Ravi supplied the word first.

"Relieved."

She looked at him, startled.

"Yeah," she said. "Relieved. Which is disgusting because I just lost."

"No." He shook his head once. "It's not disgusting. It's information."

He crouched to retie his shoe, then winced and rolled his left ankle before standing again. "For months we logged pain like dues: shin barks, add ghosts, call it discipline. That's not training. That's bad load management with a stopwatch." His mouth tightened. "I taught you that."

Anika waited for the follow-up command, the corrective program, the punishment disguised as plan.

It did not come.

Instead he said, "You can want two things at once. Be furious about 11-8 and still be glad you're not limping to the train. That's not a contradiction. That's a clean read."

Her eyes burned. She turned toward the cinderblock wall so the corridor cameras and passing players would get her profile, not the full face.

"Everyone saw," she said. "The whole draw watched game five."

"Yeah," Ravi said. "They did."

No platitude about not caring what people think.

"What'd they see?" he continued. "They saw--they saw you lose in five and still keep your shape late. They saw you hold depth at eight-all instead of spraying for highlight shots. That's usable tape."

"You sound like Tess."

"I can live with that."

Somewhere down the hall, a girl screamed in celebration from another court and the sound bounced hard off tile. Anika flinched, then laughed once at herself.

"I hate this feeling," she said.

"Which one?"

"All of it. The shame. The relief. The part that already wants tomorrow's session like I need it in my blood." She pressed her thumb into her palm until it hurt. "I didn't lose and think maybe this isn't for me. I lost and thought I need to get better at this exact thing that hurt me today."

Ravi's expression shifted in a way she had seen only since the mediation table: grief and pride arriving together and not canceling each other out.

"That's ambition," he said quietly. "Not the costume version. The kind that shows up for film and boring reps."

He picked up her bag and handed it over, but did not sling it onto her shoulder for her the way he used to when he wanted to steer where she walked.

"We stick to the agreement tonight," he said. "Food. Stretch. Ice if you need it. No secret extras."

There it was, a plan, but framed as a shared rule, not his private law.

Anika almost snapped back out of reflex. Then she realized she did not want to fight this sentence.

"Okay," she said.

"And tomorrow we review film for twenty minutes. Then we decide one thing to build for Cora's pace shifts. One thing."

"One thing," Anika repeated.

He nodded toward the lobby. "Before we go, there's a board by check-in with tournament stipend notes. Top four gets travel reimbursement. It's not huge, but it's something."

Money entered the air and changed its weight. Nothing abstract about it: subway fare, utility arrears, the fifteenth of the month, and Queens Oncology Billing Services printed in red on envelopes they stacked under the fruit bowl.

"How much?" Anika asked.

"Probably one-fifty, maybe two," Ravi said. "Depends what they classify."

It would not save anything, but it would matter.

Anika exhaled slowly. "Let's get it before someone says they lost the form."

That made him snort a tired laugh.

In the lobby, the stipend sheet was taped crooked beside a fundraiser flyer for academy uniforms. The woman at check-in wore cat-eye glasses and had a tiny tattoo of a rolling pin on her wrist. She stamped Anika's page and said, "Quarterfinalists get partial transit reimbursement. You'll receive it by email in three to five business days, assuming accounting isn't asleep."

"Thank you," Ravi said.

"If accounting was awake," the woman added dryly, "none of us would be here this late on Friday."

Outside, late-summer humidity grabbed them by the throat the second the glass doors opened. Anika felt sweat bloom again under her shirt within ten steps. City noise hit different after court acoustics: horns, bus brakes, one guy on a bike screaming at a rideshare, somebody's music leaking tinny bass from a backpack speaker.

Ravi and Anika walked to the subway in silence at first, and it was processing silence rather than hostile silence.

At the corner, they waited for the light with three other players and two parents discussing draw politics too loudly. Someone said Cora would cruise semis if her knee held. Someone else said Anika looked "less explosive than spring." Both things might be true. Hearing them still felt like getting pinched.

She kept her eyes on the pedestrian signal as the white walking figure changed to a red hand and then back to white again.

On the platform, a train thundered through without stopping, wind kicking wrappers and old MetroCard receipts down the tracks. Ravi stood half a step in front of her without touching, body angled to block the crowd surge in a habit from childhood that was both protective and practical.

Anika leaned back against the tiled column and did a stealth body scan like Tess had taught her: jaw, neck, shoulders, ribs, hips, shin.

Everything felt tender but stable, with no hot spike and no electric pull.

Relief came again, annoyingly pure this time.

With it came shame's twin: if you're relieved, maybe you wanted an excuse. Maybe you're soft now. Maybe Cora was right about the leash.

She shook her head once as if clearing water from her ears.

Ravi glanced over. "What?"

"Nothing."

He waited.

"I was thinking about that thing Cora said before the match," Anika admitted. "About the leash."

"She was poking," Ravi said.

"Yeah, but she wasn't totally wrong. I can feel the missing volume late." Anika stared at her shoes. "I hate that she could feel it too."

"Any good opponent will feel what you're missing," he said. "The question is what they pay for trying to exploit it. Today she paid for games two and three." He looked down the tracks, then back at her. "Next time we make the price higher."

The train finally came, already crowded. They squeezed into a corner by the door. Anika's shoulder touched the plexiglass map, and every station stop shifted strangers against her forearms, sweat and detergent and cheap cologne mixing into one city smell.

Across from her, a little boy in a Mets cap watched her racquet bag like it held a sword.

"Did you win?" he asked bluntly.

His mother hissed, "Mateo."

Anika surprised herself by answering. "I lost in five."

The boy considered this with solemn seriousness. "Five is a lot," he said.

"Yeah," Anika said. "That's a lot."

He nodded like that solved something and went back to pressing his face to the window.

At Queensboro Plaza, the car lurched hard and Ravi grabbed the overhead rail just before his weight pitched into her. His left ankle bent slightly. "Fuck," he said, sharp enough that the boy in the Mets cap snapped his head around. Ravi set his foot flat again and reset his stance.

"You okay?" she asked.

"Yeah," he said. "Old stupid ankle."

They rode the rest of the way mostly quiet, except for Ravi asking once if she wanted food from the cart by their station or leftovers at home.

"Cart," she said. "Egg and cheese. And one of those bad orange drinks."

"Hydration science is crying," he said, deadpan.

"Hydration science can wait."

When they got off, evening rain had started in thin diagonal needles, warm and sticky. The bus shelter was packed, so they walked the fifteen minutes to the apartment under one folding umbrella that leaked at the seam. By the time they climbed the walk-up stairs, both had wet cuffs and damp hair.

In the kitchen, the smell of mustard seed and old tea hit her first, familiar and slightly sour. Ravi put her bag by the table and went straight for the freezer pack without saying anything performative about care. He wrapped ice in a dish towel and set it within reach.

She sat, pulled up her pant leg, and laid the cold against her right shin.

Pain stayed mild, and it remained mild even as the ice bit deeper.

Ravi cracked eggs into a skillet and tore open two cheap rolls. Oil hissed. Rain tapped the fire escape outside. Their silence filled with domestic sounds that had nothing to do with winning.

After they ate, Anika opened her notebook. She chose the private ledger from chapter three, not the school one, with a bent cover and paper soft at the edges from sweat and being shoved into bags.
She wrote the date and then wrote, Regional quarterfinal, followed by Result: loss, 2-3.
She paused with the pen hovering and then wrote two new lines under the score: Body intact. Wanted more anyway.

She let the words sit there, ugly and true, no moral pinned to them.

From the sink, Ravi rinsed plates and said without turning, "Lights out by ten-thirty."

"Yeah," she said.

"And if you wake up wanting to do ghosting at midnight, wake me instead."

She almost laughed. "You'd hate that."

"I'd hate you rebreaking your leg more."

He turned then, drying his hands on a frayed towel, and met her eyes.

"We'll do this the hard way," he said. "Slow enough to keep you in one piece."

The sentence landed flat and practical. It offered a schedule, not salvation. It offered work, not trophies.

It sounded like work.

Anika looked down at the notebook line again, where she had written that she wanted more anyway.

Outside, a siren rose and faded. Upstairs, somebody dragged a chair across bare floor. The apartment felt small and humid and real.

She pressed the ice tighter to her shin until the ache dulled, then set a timer for twelve minutes and let it run all the way down.
