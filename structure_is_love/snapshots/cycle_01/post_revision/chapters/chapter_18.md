# Chapter 18

The whiteboard outside Court 2 still had yesterday's handwriting on it.

Ravi had waited for her pace from the train, then peeled off toward the depot with his phone already in his hand.

BEGINNER LADDER 6-8 PM, someone had written in purple marker, and then underlined it hard enough to tear the skin of the board. Jonah erased only half of it before his shoulder gave up on the job and he tossed the marker eraser to Anika. The square of felt hit her chest and dropped to the floor.

"You want this clean, you do it," he said, already moving toward the equipment closet with his bucket of balls knocking against his knee.

He always moved like he had three places to be and only two knees left. Tape wrapped his right wrist from mid-palm to forearm, the same yellowing athletic tape he reused until the edges curled gray. His thermos, dented at the bottom, sat open on the scorer's table and smelled like strong coffee and cardamom.

Anika bent and picked up the eraser. The HVAC started one of its loud metal coughs above Court 1, then settled into a rattle that never quite became steady. Somebody had already turned on one of the old floor fans in the hallway, and its loose grill clicked every fourth rotation. She could hear all of it at once: fan click, ventilation clank, rubber ball pop-pop-pop on a side wall where an older player warmed up alone.

On her left wrist, Maya's watch sat snug under the cuff of her long-sleeve tee. She had changed the battery two weeks ago with a tiny screwdriver from Priya's junk drawer. Now the watch numbers were crisp again: 3:24, then 3:25, then 3:25 and forty-three seconds. She kept looking at it as if it might change into something else if she watched long enough.

By 3:35 the first kids drifted in, each with a parent and a different version of nerves.

A girl in oversized safety goggles arrived first, dragging a racket bag with a faded unicorn patch and one broken zipper tooth. She introduced herself to the desk volunteer without looking up.

"Rina Patel," she said. "My mom's outside parking. She says if I don't text her when we're done she'll come in and embarrass me."

She said it like it was a scientific warning. Her right shoelace was neon green and the left was plain white. Both were tied in perfect double knots.

Two minutes later a skinny boy with a swim-team parka tied around his waist leaned on the hallway wall and bounced on the balls of his feet while he waited. Chlorine seemed baked into his skin. He had black Sharpie on his hand where somebody had written a grocery list and then washed half of it off.

"Luis Ortega," he told Jonah. "Can we start with serves? I watched videos. I know where to hit."

"We're starting with feet," Jonah said.

Luis groaned like he'd been handed taxes.

Then came twins, Nia and Nikhil, who argued in whispers about whether squash balls were hollow, and a tall girl named Huda who wore a denim jacket over her athletic shirt and kept touching the silver crescent pin at her collarbone whenever anyone looked at her. She parked herself near the back wall and studied everyone like she was compiling data.

Anika stood by the front wall with a clipboard and a whistle she did not want. She had said yes to assisting the clinic in the voice she used for school attendance, flat and efficient and impossible to read. The yes had come out of her mouth last week before she could stage a better answer. Jonah had called it helpful. Priya had called it healthy. Ravi had called it strategic, because every hour she volunteered counted toward reduced court fees.

Anika had not called it anything.

She had told herself it was court time. She had told herself she'd run beginners through split-step and racket prep, then stay another hour and ghost until her calves shook, maybe another twenty minutes of star drills after that, quiet and private while Jonah locked up his ball bucket and pretended he did not see. She had planned the extra work down to water breaks and bathroom minutes on the Q32 ride home. In her notebook she had drawn a tiny box next to 3:00-6:00 PM and left it empty, waiting.

When 3:48 flashed on her watch, Jonah clapped once, hard. "Circle up. If you're chewing gum, it's done. If you're scared, fine. Move anyway."

The kids made a loose circle. Rina stood with her toes exactly on a paint chip in the floor, as if balance depended on it.

"You all know Anika?" Jonah asked. "She's helping me this season. Listen when she talks."

Six pairs of eyes turned to Anika. She felt the old spark in her chest, the one that liked being looked at as if she knew more than she did. It came with danger every time.

"Shoes tied tight," she said. "Racket up before the ball comes off the wall. Don't wait to see what happens. Move first."

Her voice came out sharper than she intended. Nia blinked. Luis stopped bouncing.

Jonah gave her a side look, not disapproving, just measuring.

They began with shadow swings, then split-step timing, then simple feed-and-return drills near the short line. The younger kids kept drifting backward toward the glass as if distance could protect them from missing. Anika corrected grips one by one, pressing fingers into place on sweaty handles.

"Thumb lower, Rina. No, lower than that."

"Like this?"

"Like you're holding a bird you don't want to crush."

Rina's forehead wrinkled. "I've never held a bird."

"Okay. Like a banana that's almost bruised."

That one worked. Rina nodded once, serious.

By the second rotation Luis had already started asking for extra repetitions.

"Can I do doubles?" he asked. "Can I do, like, twenty in a row while they rest?"

Anika set another ball on the floor with her shoe and watched him lean forward, desperate to go.

There it was. The ache she recognized before she had words for it. It showed up as hunger in the eyes, but the hunger was not for the game. It was for rules that hurt and then rewarded. It was for the clean equation where pain plus obedience equaled safety.

"No doubles," she said.

Luis frowned. "Why not?"

"Because you're skipping your feet when you're tired. Then your shoulder does weird stuff. Then you start serving with your elbow and we spend next month fixing it."

"But I can handle it."

"So can a shopping cart with one bad wheel," Jonah said from behind them. "Doesn't mean it's going straight."

A couple of kids laughed. Luis did not.

They rotated again. Huda missed two forehands and stepped back with her jaw clenched, pretending to retie a shoe that did not need tying. Nikhil took too big a backswing and hit himself in the shin; he pretended it did not hurt until his eyes watered. Nia started coaching him in a fierce whisper that sounded like their grandmother lived in her throat.

Anika moved down the line, feeding easy balls with her left hand and catching returns with her racket face. Her own body kept trying to speed up the drill. She wanted to tighten everything: less talking, more reps, shorter breaks, sharper commands. She could feel the old script scrolling in her muscles, the one that said if you were not near collapse you were lying about effort.

Her watch buzzed once at 4:11, a reminder she had set this morning: water.

She ignored it.

Three minutes later it buzzed again, persistent now because she had set the repeat by mistake.

"Coach," Rina said, quiet but direct. "Your watch is yelling."

The kids looked at Anika's wrist.

Heat ran up Anika's neck. She hated being caught by an alarm, by an object, by anything that made her seem less in control.

"Water break," she said. "Three minutes."

Jonah did not smile, but his mouth shifted like he approved.

The kids dropped rackets and swarmed the cooler. Luis drank too fast and coughed. Nia and Nikhil argued about whose bottle had the Spider-Man sticker first. Huda stood apart and took tiny careful sips, then pulled a folded paper out of her jacket pocket and read it while she drank.

Anika glanced at the paper when Huda looked up.

"What is that?" she asked.

Huda folded it fast. "My grandma's blood pressure numbers. I've got to remind her after Maghrib to take the blue pill. I wrote it down so I don't forget if practice runs long."

She said it defensively, like she expected ridicule.

"That's smart," Anika said.

Huda looked surprised, then shrugged. "She says all sports are fine as long as I do my homework and nobody breaks my face."

"Reasonable policy," Jonah muttered.

When the break ended, they switched to conditioned rallies: one bounce only, hit crosscourt, recover to the T. It was supposed to be boring enough for fundamentals and hard enough to keep kids from wandering. Luis asked again for more.

"Can I stay after? I can do solo ghosts. My brother says if I don't grind now I'll be behind forever."

Anika's throat tightened at the word grind.

"How old's your brother?" she asked.

"Seventeen. He quit basketball because coaches were trash."

"And now he's your coach?"

Luis grinned, then saw her face and straightened. "He's... loud."

"Mine was too," Anika said before she could stop herself.

Luis waited for more. She did not give it.

Near 4:53, during a footwork relay, Nikhil landed wrong and grabbed his calf. He tried to wave everyone off with a huge embarrassed smile.

"I'm fine, keep going, keep going."

The relay stalled around him.

Anika knelt so they were eye level. "Where?"

He touched the outside of his lower leg.

"Cramp or pull?"

"Cramp. I think."

"Any sharp pain?"

He hesitated. His twin watched him with a stare that could strip wallpaper.

"A little sharp."

"Then you sit."

"No, I can do one more."

"Nope."

Nikhil's face collapsed into the kind of anger kids use when fear has nowhere else to go. "I hate sitting."

"Yeah," Anika said. "Me too. Bench anyway."

Jonah tossed her a thumbs-up from across the court, quick and almost hidden.

Nia escorted her brother to the bench and sat beside him without being asked. She took out a tiny packet of gummy bears from her pocket and tore it open with her teeth.

"Don't tell Amma I gave you sugar before dinner," she whispered.

Anika heard it and looked away so they could keep the privacy.

By 5:20 the clinic energy had thinned into sweat and shouts and the squeak of soles on old wood. Parents crowded behind the glass, checking phones, tapping weather apps, calculating traffic. The sky outside the high windows had gone pewter. Rain started, light at first, then steady enough to drum the roof.

Damp weather always found Anika's right shin. The tenderness crept in like a wire threaded under skin and tightened by one turn each minute. Not severe. Just enough to remind her that body and consequence were still connected, no matter how badly she wanted to believe in pure will.

Jonah blew the whistle for final cleanup.

"Balls in the bucket. Thank your hitting partner. If you leave tape or wrappers on my floor, I'll make you run lines while I eat a sandwich and judge your life choices."

The kids laughed, which was the point.

They lined up for fist bumps. Rina bumped with absolute seriousness. "Can we do that banana grip again next week?"

"We'll do it every week," Anika said.

Huda lifted her crescent pin and smiled with half her mouth. "My grandma says thanks for not yelling. She can hear yelling through the phone and then she starts yelling too."

Luis hovered after everyone else moved away. "If I come early Saturday, can I hit before clinic?"

Anika knew the old answer. Wake earlier. Do more. Earn worth.

That reflex stood in her mouth like metal.

"Come fifteen minutes early," she said. "We'll do warm-up right. No hero stuff."

Luis looked disappointed for a second, then nodded. "Okay."

When the hallway emptied, Jonah rolled the ball bucket toward the closet. "You looked like you wanted to crawl out of your skin halfway through."

Anika stacked cones by color. "I was fine."

"Sure." He leaned the bucket against the wall. "You want a solo court for thirty?"

There it was. Permission shaped exactly like temptation.

Her chest jumped. Thirty could become sixty. Sixty could become ninety if she kept her head down and told nobody. She pictured it in detail: ghosting ladders to ten, then diagonal bursts, then serves until her shoulder burned clean. She pictured the calm afterward, the dangerous clean calm where numbers replaced thought.

She also pictured Nikhil's face when she told him to sit. She pictured Luis asking to grind. She pictured herself saying no and meaning it for them but not for herself.

She pulled open her notebook, the one with Maya's old grocery list still faintly visible on the inside cover. The page for today had two columns: clinic and train. In the clinic column she had written the kids' names and tiny notes: Rina stiff wrist, Huda hides confidence, Luis speed without brakes. The train column was blank.

"I can do feeding drills for the six o'clock group," she said.

Jonah raised one eyebrow. "Not what I asked."

"I know."

"You trying to be noble?"

"No. I'm trying not to do dumb math."

For the first time all afternoon, he smiled fully. "Good answer. Court 3. Kids in ten."

So she fed balls for another hour to a different set of beginners with rental goggles and brand-new blisters and the same bright panic in their eyes. She did not run a single secret ghost.

By the time she got home, her socks were damp from rain leaking through the seam of her right shoe. The apartment stairwell smelled like bleach and old frying oil. She unlocked the door quietly, but Ravi still heard and called from the kitchen, "You eat yet?"

"No."

"There's upma on the stove. Heat it."

His voice carried the clipped workday rhythm he used when he had ten things to do and no spare tone for any of them.

The kitchen light was too bright. Ravi had spread envelopes across the table in three stacks, each weighted down with something small so the fan from the window unit would not scatter them. One stack had the TV remote on top. One had the salt cellar. One had Maya's old measuring spoon set, the quarter teaspoon missing as usual.

Red notices sat in the middle stack.

Anika did not pretend not to read. QUEENS ONCOLOGY BILLING SERVICES. ACCOUNT CURRENTLY PAST DUE. AMOUNT DUE THIS CYCLE: $640.00. NEXT REVIEW DATE: NOV 15.

The utility envelope had a yellow stripe and less ceremony: FINAL REMINDER before service interruption on outstanding balance. $1,240.12 remaining after Aug 28 payment of $620.00.

She had first seen that debt in April at the school fee-waiver desk, printed in black at the top of the hardship packet: $1,860.12. Ravi had paid $620 at the bodega bill terminal in late summer while she watched the receipt spool out and curl on itself. $1,860.12 minus $620.00 left $1,240.12. Same debt, thinner by one rent-sized bite.

Ravi stood at the counter in his dispatch uniform undershirt, tying his shoes for a second shift. Not depot this time. App-driving. His regular lanyard hung from a cabinet knob, bus logo turned inward. He massaged his left ankle once before tightening the laces. A small hiss escaped him. He ignored it.

"You got in late," he said.

"Clinic ran long."

"Paid long?"

"No."

"Okay." He tapped the red envelope with two fingers. "Then it has to convert to something else. Goodwill, lower fees, contacts, whatever."

Anika put her bag down and opened the pot on the stove. The upma had formed a skin. She stirred it back in.

"Did you eat?" she asked.

"At the depot. Bad vending machine almonds and shame." He checked his phone. "I've got airport queue from eight to midnight if demand holds."

"And tomorrow?"

"Depot at six. Home for two hours. Then maybe one more app shift."

He said maybe the way some people said please, as if politeness might make the schedule less brutal.

Anika spooned food into a bowl. Her stomach clenched from the first smell even though she was hungry. She set the bowl down and reached for water instead.

Ravi watched without looking like he was watching.

"How was clinic?" he asked.

"Loud."

"Useful loud or chaos loud?"

"Both."

"How's the shin?"

She took two beats to answer. "Sore in the rain. Not sharp."

"Okay." He nodded once. "No heroics tonight. You still have your planned session?"

"Forty-five minutes plus mobility."

"With Jonah?"

"He'll start me. Then he has the adult league check-in."

Ravi picked up the oncology notice and folded it back along the same crease, precise and angry.

"If you need to cut a session, cut a session," he said. "We are not doing another injury cycle."

The old Ravi would have said the opposite. The new one sometimes forgot himself, but tonight he sounded like a man repeating a promise to keep it alive.

Anika leaned on the sink and stared at the wet alley beyond the window. Somebody had hung jeans on a fire escape rail; rain made them flap like flags.

"Do you ever miss it?" she asked before she could edit the question.

Ravi frowned. "Miss what?"

"The old plan. More hours. More everything."

His laugh was short and dry. "I miss certainty. I don't miss the plan." He put both hands on the table and looked at the envelopes instead of her. "The old plan was me pretending I could outwork a funeral bill."

The words landed heavy and practical, like tools dropped on tile.

"You don't have to come watch tonight," Anika said. "I can go and come back."

"I know." He checked his keys, then checked them again. "I want to watch first twenty. Then I leave for the shift."

He grabbed his light rain jacket from the chair and shrugged into it. The sleeve cuff had begun to fray. He noticed Anika looking.

"Not buying a new one," he said. "Before you even ask."

"I wasn't asking."

"Good."

He pulled a folded sticky note from his pocket and set it near her bowl. Two phone numbers were written in his compact block handwriting: depot desk and app support.

"If I don't answer, call twice. If it's urgent, call Priya after that."

Anika nodded. She tucked the note under the salt cellar so it would not slide.

They rode the bus to the rec center mostly in silence, knees angled away from other passengers. The windows fogged. A toddler in front of them sang one line from a cartoon theme over and over while his grandmother pretended not to hear. Ravi kept checking the time and rubbing his thumb against the corner of his phone case until the plastic squeaked.

At the courts, he stood outside the glass with his arms folded while Anika warmed up: easy drives, controlled length, short movement patterns Jonah had approved. She felt him there like weather.

Eighteen minutes into warm-up, Ravi tapped his watch and pointed to the door. He mouthed, "Call me," then left at a jog that looked painful in the first three steps and less painful after momentum took over.

Jonah stepped in for the last part of warm-up, fed her a sequence of deep rails, and then handed her the timer puck they used for interval sets.

"Plan?" he asked.

"Two blocks of six-minute movement, one block serve targets, then mobility." 

"And?"

"And stop." 

"At?"

"Forty-five."

He nodded. "I'll be on Court 1 with adults. If you start negotiating with yourself, don't."

"Okay."

"I'm serious, kid. You have lawyer brain when you're scared. You'll draft an exception clause by minute thirty."

"I know."

He walked away before she could say anything else.

The court felt huge with only one body in it.

Anika started the timer and moved.

Split-step, lunge, recover. Split-step, back corner, recover. Her breath found rhythm by the second minute. Sweat came easy in the wet air. The right shin hummed but held.

Twelve minutes in she switched to serves. Target towels hung from the front wall: low left, mid right. She hit six good, four long, two tins, reset. The old hunger sharpened under her ribs. She could feel how little forty-five minutes was compared with what she had done before. Forty-five felt like a warm-up pretending to be discipline.

She served again and missed the towel by half a racket length.

Her brain started the old bargaining in a voice that sounded calm and reasonable.

Just add ten. No one will know.

You already paid the bus fare. Make it count.

Debt does not care about moderation.

She wiped her face with the bottom of her shirt and kept serving.

On the back wall, her reflection flashed with each turn: small body, damp shirt, watch face catching light at the top of her swing. The watch looked like command again if she stared at it wrong.

By minute thirty-two she felt the first pull to continue past the plan. Not pain. Not fatigue. A cleaner, meaner sensation. Compulsion in neat clothes.

She walked to the service box, bounced the ball, and looked up at the glass. No one was watching now. Jonah's adult group was arguing about lets on Court 1. Two men in business polos raised their voices while a woman in neon shoes rolled her eyes and rubbed her hamstring. Nobody cared what Anika did on Court 3.

That made it harder.

She hit one more serve, then another.

Her watch buzzed at forty minutes, the first warning.

She served anyway. One, two, three, four.

On the fifth she lunged late and her right shoe slipped on sweat near the short line. A hot wire of pain jumped up her shin. She slapped her left palm to the side wall to catch herself, and the racket frame clipped her own knee on the rebound.

The sixth ball hit tin and died.

She bent over, coughing. Acid rose into her throat. Her hand shook so hard the ball rattled in her grip.

Nobody had called stop. Nobody had noticed. That old freedom felt like a trap door.

The watch buzzed again at forty-five, longer. End.

She pressed stop on the timer puck, then held her thumb over reset until it trembled.

In her mind, memory snapped open without asking: Maya in the kitchen, in the blue house shorts with tiny yellow lemons, tightening this same watch around Anika's wrist because the band was one notch too loose.

"Not too tight," Maya had said. "You're growing every week. I need to leave room."

Back then Anika had wanted it tight, almost painful, so it would not slip during school recess. Maya had laughed and loosened it back.

She dropped the puck on her notebook outside the glass and wrote in the train column with a damp, cramped hand:

40 warning ignored. +6 serves.
Would've kept going if I hadn't slipped.

The ink bled where her knuckles spasmed.

She tried to start mobility work, but her right calf seized on the first stretch. She swore under her breath and smacked the mat once with an open palm before she could stop herself. A player on Court 1 glanced over through the glass.

Jonah appeared in the doorway while she was shaking out the cramp.

"Time?" he asked.

"Forty-five."

His eyes dropped to her notebook. "And?"

"I went past the first warning. Six shots."

"Why'd you stop?"

"I slipped. Shin lit up."

He held out his hand. "Timer."

She passed him the puck.

"No solo block tomorrow," he said. "You open clinic with me, feed for six o'clock, and leave when I leave."

"That's a lot of rails."

"Yeah." He pocketed the timer. "That's the point. If you ask for extra court, answer's no."

He pulled out his phone. "I'm texting your dad so we're aligned."

Anika nodded once. "Do it."

Jonah looked at her for another beat. "Rina still wants banana grip Saturday."

"I can do banana grip."

"Do it for yourself too."

"I'll try."

"Trying counts if you actually change the plan."

Outside, rain had slowed to a mist. She packed her bag, slung it over one shoulder, and stepped into the hallway where the floor smelled like wet rubber and disinfectant. A little boy from the adult class stood by the vending machine in socks, shoes off, counting quarters into his palm. He came up short and stared at the coins like betrayal had happened personally.

Anika dug in her pocket and found two dimes. She handed them to him.

"Thanks," he said. "My dad says sports drinks are fake, but he buys one if I win points."

"How many points?"

"Three."

"Brutal policy."

He grinned and fed the coins into the machine.

Anika walked to the bus stop alone. The sidewalk shone black under streetlights. Her damp hair stuck to the back of her neck. Across the avenue, a laundromat glowed with rows of spinning silver drums, shirts and towels tumbling in identical circles.

She checked her phone. Two texts from Ravi.

8:41 PM: Airport line dead. Trying Midtown hotels.

9:03 PM: Don't wait up.

A third came while she watched.

9:11 PM: How was session?

She typed: `45 total. Went six past first warning. Slipped. Stopped. Jonah took my timer for tomorrow.`

Three dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

`Saw Jonah's text. No solo tomorrow and no debate. Priya can pick you up after clinic so you don't hang around the courts.`

Another bubble followed.

`Not mad. I just won't pretend six extra is nothing. Also picked up one airport fare finally. Might cover the oncology minimum if tips aren't trash.`

She read the message twice. No solo tomorrow sat next to oncology minimum like they belonged in the same sentence.

The bus arrived with wet brakes squealing. She climbed on and took a seat near the back. The watch on her wrist ticked tiny, steady beats against her pulse.

Back home she left her shoes by the door, reheated the upma, and ate standing at the sink while steam fogged the window. The red envelopes were still on the table, waiting for morning and the next set of decisions.

Before bed she set two alarms on Maya's watch: 6:10 for school, 3:00 for clinic prep. Then she took the watch off and left it face-down on the desk, out of reach.

She lay in the dark and listened to the building settle, pipes knocking twice in the wall, somebody upstairs dragging a chair over tile. Her right calf twitched every few minutes. Twice she reached toward the desk before she was fully awake, already rehearsing how to bargain for ten more minutes.

When sleep came, it felt thin and conditional, like a truce she still had to defend in the morning.
