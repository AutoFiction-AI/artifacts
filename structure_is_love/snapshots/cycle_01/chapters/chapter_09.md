# Chapter 9

At 4:13 a.m., Anika woke before the watch could buzz. The apartment was dark except for the green microwave clock over the sink, and she lay still long enough to hear three separate sounds from the building: pipes knocking two floors down, somebody's toilet tank refilling, the elevator cables whining through their own metal throat. Her body had learned this hour the way skin learns a scar. At this hour she used to think in a strict sequence: court time, water bottle, bandage tape, bus.

Then the fact landed again, blunt as a hand on the sternum. There was no court and no impact for six weeks. The restriction sheet on the fridge said `right tibia, stress fracture` in blue stamped letters.

She rolled onto her back and felt for pain before she moved, the way she had been doing since the hospital. The bone did not throb while she was still. It only answered when weight asked it a question. Humid air stuck to her neck, and she could not tell if she was sweating or just already awake enough to notice every part of herself. She flexed her toes under the sheet, testing, counting: one, two, three, four, five. She hated that she was counting before she had even sat up.

The hallway outside her room was seven and a half strides if she took normal steps and six if she made her hips turn fast like ghosting drills. She had measured it three nights ago. Tonight she waited until 4:31, because the rule on the notebook page said she had to try breathing first. In for four counts, hold for four, out for six. A school counselor had taught the class this in sixth grade after a fire alarm drill went wrong and made two kids cry. It had sounded fake then. It sounded fake now.

By 4:38 she was pacing anyway, left foot, right boot, left foot, right boot, one hand on the wall whenever she had to pivot. The fracture boot made a dry plastic click each time it hit tile. She kept the volume low by rolling through the sole, a clumsy motion that worked the outside of her calf and made her ankle ache. She did twelve loops, stopped, wrote 12 in the notebook on the kitchen counter, then did twelve more because twenty-four looked better than twelve and better looked safer.

Stillness had been hard on court too, but there was always the next serve, the next sprint to a corner, the next instruction barked through glass. Here stillness had no whistle and no end. It expanded. It pulled memory toward it.

When she paused at the sink to drink, the metallic taste in the tap water tipped her into a hospital flash she did not ask for. It gave her a bright room, a plastic cup, and her mother saying very gently that the ice chips were too cold for her mouth today. Anika gripped the edge of the sink until her knuckles hurt and forced herself to look at the chipped yellow tile instead of the memory. She could name four cracks that looked like letters. C. I. T. Y. She did that twice and the room came back to Queens, to their narrow kitchen with curry leaves dried to the underside of the spice rack and one dead fruit fly on the windowsill.

She opened the notebook to the first pages where the columns still said `reps`, `load`, `pain / 10`, `food`, `mistakes`. She had started those entries in Chapter 3 of her own private season, before she thought of chapters as anything except math textbook sections she could finish faster than everyone else. Now new pages carried different headers in her blocky pen pressure: `sleep onset`, `awake minutes`, `night wakings`, `shin ache @ rest`, `panic spike`, `hallway loops`.

She wrote:

Night 5
asleep 01:42
awake 03:56-04:13
panic 6/10
loops 24 (quiet)

Then she added, on a line below she had not planned:

heard Amma cough in memory even though no one coughed.

The sun rose slow and dirty behind the opposite building, light catching on the satellite dish that never pointed right. At 5:47 Ravi came out of his room in his depot polo and navy work pants, lanyard already around his neck, keys clipped to his belt. He had not shaved. Stubble grayed the lower half of his face and made him look older than forty-four, older than her teachers' dads, older than last week.

He did not seem surprised she was up.

"You take your tablet?" he asked, opening the cabinet for tea leaves.

"After food." Her voice came out scratchy. "It's better if I eat first. That's what the sheet says."

"Good." He set water to boil and rubbed his left ankle against the back of his right calf, a habit he did when he stood still too long. "You need protein at breakfast. Don't just drink tea."

"I'm not five."

"I didn't say you were."

He kept facing the stove. She watched his reflection in the dark microwave door because he would not turn. Steam fogged the pot lid. He killed the flame before it hissed over.

"What time are you home?" she asked.

"Late."

"Late like eight or late like midnight?"

"Depends on dispatch." He reached for his lunch container, then checked his phone and frowned. "Maybe I'll do app runs after shift."

"Again?"

"Again."

She waited for him to say why, but he only tightened the lid on the steel tiffin and slid it into his worn black backpack. The zipper tab was half broken and he always had to pinch it with two fingers. He had never replaced it.

"You don't have to leave this early," she said. "My checkup isn't until next week."

"I'm not leaving for that." He finally looked at her, only for a second. His eyes moved from her face to the boot and back to the sink. "I'm leaving because I'm on the six-thirty board."

The kettle clicked dry against the burner. He swore, low and tired, and moved it off heat. Then he went to the fridge, stared at the restriction order like he was reading a language he almost remembered, and stuck a utility envelope under the magnet beside it.

Red block letters stamped across the front: FINAL WARNING.

He saw her seeing it and put his palm over the amount line before she could read the number. She had already read it upside down: 1,860.12.

"Eat," he said. "And keep your foot up between classes."

"It's June. We barely have classes."

"Then keep it up at home."

He left without drinking his tea.

By noon the apartment smelled like old heat and detergent from downstairs, and Anika had done every seated thing on the discharge sheet twice: ankle alphabet, glute squeeze, calf tension holds without movement. They took thirteen minutes total and left the rest of the day untouched. She stared at the minute hand on the watch she wore, Maya's old digital one with a scratched face and a strap repaired with clear nail polish, and felt anger rise without a target: anger at the bone, anger at Ravi, and anger at kids posting summer-camp photos in bright team shirts like the world was a straight line from effort to reward.

Zoe texted at 1:06 p.m.

`u alive?`

A second text came before Anika answered.

`my brother tried to microwave a spoon. fire. mom screamed his government name.`

Anika smiled despite herself and typed back, `alive. doing ankle alphabet like a prisoner.`

`gross`

`yeah`

`want me to come over w popsicles?`

Anika looked at the dishes in the sink, the folded laundry she had not touched, the notebook open to a page where numbers kept trying to become rules. She wanted Zoe there. She did not want to be watched while she failed at being still.

`can't today. house is weird.`

`k. tell weird house i said fight me.`

She put the phone down and listened for her own laugh. It did not come out.

At 3:20 she lay on the couch with her leg propped on two cushions and tried to read the summer novel packet for school. The sentence on the page stayed readable, then dissolved, then snapped back. After four pages she realized she had not absorbed one thing except that her heartbeat was audible in her right ear. She shut the packet and put her wristwatch speaker against her cheek to feel the faint vibration when the seconds changed.

The apartment was loud with absence. There was no squash ball echo, no timer alarms, and no Ravi calling split times from the doorframe. The silence did not feel quiet. It felt like being trapped under the court glass while everyone else left and the lights switched off one by one.

She got up, forgot the boot by instinct, stepped full weight on the right leg, and saw white. Pain shot up clean and electric from shin to hip. She grabbed the table edge, swore into her shoulder, and hopped backward until she could jam the boot on. The pain settled into a deep hot line along the bone and stayed there like punishment.

She wrote it down because writing it down had become the only proof that she was not making it dramatic.

15:36 accidental full load on R leg
pain spike 8/10 for ~20 sec
residual ache 4/10

Underneath, in smaller letters: idiot.

***

Ravi came home after nine with diesel and city heat in his shirt and a fast-food coffee cup gone soft in his hand. He set his bag on the floor, washed his hands, and stood at the sink longer than necessary, palms flat on porcelain as if waiting for instructions from water. He still had the lanyard around his neck.

Anika sat at the table with reheated lemon rice and yogurt she had stirred too hard. The spoon clinked against ceramic every time she lifted it. She had timed his absence without meaning to. Fifteen hours and fourteen minutes had passed since he left.

"There's food," she said.

"I ate on break."

"You said you'd come by seven."

"Dispatch got short. Two drivers called out." He dried his hands and checked his phone. "Then there was a collision on Roosevelt. Whole line backed up."

"You could've texted."

"I did text."

He had: `late. eat.` at 7:41. She hated that she had checked the timestamp three times.

He opened the fridge, looked at nothing, then took out the utility envelope she had seen that morning. He tore it with his thumb and scanned the page while standing. His jaw moved once, side to side.

"What did it say?" she asked.

"It says pay by Friday." He folded the letter into a precise rectangle. "It says what these things always say."

"How much?"

"You don't need to track that."

"I already saw it."

"Then why ask?"

The question hit like a slap because it sounded like her own defensive voice, older and meaner. She pushed her bowl away.

"Because no one tells me anything until it's on paper with red ink."

He kept his eyes on the envelope. "You're eleven."

"I was eleven when you told me if I wanted to win I had to know every number."

"That is not the same and you know it."

"It's exactly the same."

"No." He looked up then, finally, and she saw how bloodshot his eyes were. "One set of numbers helps you move better on court. This set tells me whether we have lights next month."

The kitchen went still. Even the fridge compressor seemed to wait.

She heard herself ask, too quickly, "Is it because of me?"

"It's because your mother got sick in America," he said, voice suddenly flat with exhaustion. "It's because hospitals bill like casinos and grief doesn't come with a payment plan and I'm trying to keep the utilities on. It's because life is expensive. It's not because you got hurt."

He exhaled hard through his nose and rubbed the bridge of it with two fingers, as if pinching off a headache before it spread. When he spoke again, his tone had dropped back to procedural.

"Did you do the exercises?"

"Yes."

"Ice?"

"Twice."

"Any sharp pain at rest?"

"No." She lied without blinking. The ache was still there from the forgotten step.

"Good."

He took his plate from the rack and served himself standing up. He did not sit. He ate quickly, eyes on the counter, chewing like food was fuel and never comfort. Halfway through he hissed and shifted weight, rolling his left ankle once before setting his heel down again.

"Your ankle's bad," Anika said.

"It's old."

"You should get it checked."

"I should get twelve things checked."

His phone buzzed with the depot ringtone, a harsh two-note chirp she hated. He answered before the second ring.

"Yeah, Sandeep." Pause. "I can take it." Pause. "No, I'll keep the app on after. Send me the reroute map." Longer pause, his face turned toward the window where the fire escape cast ladder shadows across the wall. "Yeah. I said I've got it."

He hung up and finished the last bite of rice.

"You're going back out?" she said.

"Half shift." He rinsed his plate and set it in the rack with exact care. "Maybe four hours."

"You just got home."

"I know."

"Then stay."

His mouth tightened. "Don't start this tonight."

"I'm not starting anything. I'm asking you to sit down and look at me for five minutes."

"Anika."

"No, seriously. Five." She held up her hand, all five fingers out, childish and furious at the same time. "You don't talk to me unless it's pills or icing or forms. You're here and you're not here."

He turned off the kitchen light even though she was still at the table; the overhead went dark, leaving only the stove hood bulb and the streetlight through blinds. "I'm trying to keep us afloat," he said. "If you want me to be home more, tell ConEd to wait. Tell oncology billing to forget our file."

"Stop using bills like a shield."

"Stop making everything about my eye contact." The words came out sharper than he intended. He looked away immediately, ashamed and angry at once. "I'm tired."

She stood too fast, chair scraping. "I'm tired too and I'm not even allowed to run around one stupid court and hit one stupid ball. I don't have anywhere to put it."

"Put what?"

"Any of it."

That landed. For one second she thought he might sit down.

Instead he picked up his keys.

"We'll talk Sunday," he said. "I've got day shift only then."

"You said that last Sunday."

He flinched, tiny but visible. "I know."

He left with the apartment key held between his first two fingers, an old habit from late shifts. The lock clicked twice behind him. She stood in the dark kitchen listening to his steps go down the stairwell, counting each landing until the outer door shut and the building swallowed him.

She wrote in the notebook without sitting.

21:48 attempt conversation w Dad
result: failure
voice escalation both sides
control: partial

Then she crossed out `partial` and replaced it with `poor`.

At 11:17 he texted from somewhere on Queens Boulevard.

`You asleep?`

She looked at the message bubble until the screen dimmed. Then she typed, `No.`

Three dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

`Take med before bed.`

There was no apology and no question. She locked the phone.

***

By the sixth night the apartment had taught her a new map. She knew where the floorboard outside Ravi's room complained, which window needed a folded receipt under the frame so the rattle stopped, how long the upstairs couple fought before one of them cried and the other ran the shower to hide it, and how many seconds passed between subway rumble and glass tremor in the kitchen cabinet.

None of that helped when lights-out came.

The worst time was the seven minutes after she switched off her lamp and before her eyes adjusted. In that strip of dark her body waited for instruction: split step, lunge, recover. No instruction arrived, and grief did.

It came specific and physical, never abstract. She remembered her mother's thumbnail flattened from years opening lab vials, the exact medicinal-sweet smell of chlorhexidine wipes, a strand of hair caught in the seam of the hospital pillowcase that Anika had pretended not to see, and the way Maya had once asked for mango and then could not swallow more than one cube.

Anika sat up, reached under the bed, and pulled out the old silver phone she had charged in secret. It had a crack across the upper corner and a sticker of a cartoon squash racket Zoe had stuck there in fourth grade. The screen took a full five seconds to wake. She opened `Saved Voice` and pressed play on the file she now knew by timestamp: 00:43, February 18.

Maya's voice filled the tiny speaker, a little compressed, background noise from a TV somewhere far off.

"Hey, Niks. It's Amma. I put lemon rice on the middle shelf, with extra peanuts because you always steal those first. Heat it with a spoon of water so it doesn't dry out. Tell your dad to wait for the mustard seeds to pop before he throws in the curry leaves, or he'll smoke out the whole kitchen again."

There was a soft laugh, then a breath that hitched once.

"I know you're pretending you're not tired. Eat first. Homework after. You can be stubborn tomorrow. I love you."

The message ended with the old iOS click.

Anika replayed it.

On the third loop she mouthed along with `middle shelf` and `spoon of water`. On the fifth she noticed a sound she had missed before, the scrape of a chair leg in the background. On the seventh she anticipated the hitch in Maya's breath and felt panic jab her ribs anyway. She pressed repeat harder than necessary, thumb white against glass.

Night after night, this became the set. It was not ghosting drills and it was not ladders; it was forty-three seconds, then start over.

She made rules because rules were what she had.

No replay before 10:30 p.m.
No replay in daylight.
No replay if Ravi was home in the room.
Maximum ten loops.

By night three she was at fourteen. By night four she was whispering each line a half-second ahead of the audio, like calling a shot before the ball hit wall. By night five she stopped hearing the words and started timing the spaces between them.

At 12:52 a.m. on Thursday, she was on loop sixteen when Ravi knocked once on her partly open door.

"You're awake," he said.

She yanked one earbud free though she had only been wearing one. "Yeah."

He stayed in the doorway, one hand on the frame, shoes still on. "Screen time this late isn't helping."

"It's not random videos."

"Then what?"

She hesitated. The phone was warm in her palm. "Mom."

His face changed, not soft exactly, but unguarded for half a beat. "Voice note?"

She nodded.

"How many times?"

"A few."

"A few is not a number."

"Why do you need a number for this?"

"Because numbers tell me when something is becoming a problem."

"Everything is a problem for you unless it has a chart."

"That's not fair."

"It's true."

He looked past her at the notebook on the nightstand, open to columns she had not meant him to see. `awake 5x`. `loops 18`. `replay count 11`. His mouth tightened.

"You should tell Tess this when you start PT," he said.

"We haven't started." Her throat felt scraped from disuse and swallowed words. "And she's not a sleep wizard."

"She's part of recovery."

"Recovery from what? The fracture or being a person you can manage?"

He inhaled slowly and leaned his shoulder against the frame as if he needed its support.

"You think I want this," he said. "You think I wanted you in that x-ray room?"

"I think you wanted me to finish no matter what. You said it with your hand through the glass." Her own voice rose; she forced it down before it cracked. "I did exactly what you wanted and now I can't shut my head off for one hour."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Yes." He rubbed his chest once, quick, like he could smooth the ache there with his palm. "I hear your room light at two in the morning. I hear the boot in the hallway. I'm not blind, Anika."

She almost corrected him to `you're not blind`, then didn't. He sounded older in that moment than she had ever heard him.

"Then come in," she said quietly. "Don't stand there like a supervisor doing rounds."

He gave one humorless huff of breath that might have been a laugh in another life. He stepped in, sat on the edge of her desk chair, and kept both hands clasped between his knees. He still did not meet her eyes for more than a second at a time.

"Play it," he said.

She pressed the screen. Maya's voice rose again in the dark room, practical and teasing and alive in all the wrong tense. Ravi closed his eyes at `tell your dad to wait for the mustard seeds` and pinched the bridge of his nose until the message ended.

"Again?" Anika asked.

He stood up immediately. "No." His voice was rough now. "If I listen twice, I won't sleep at all."

He moved to the door, stopped, and spoke without turning.

"Set a limit. Please. For both of us."

Then he left.

She set a limit that night. She broke it twenty minutes later.

On Friday she copied the voicemail into the notebook verbatim from memory, then checked against the recording and corrected three words. She did the same on Saturday and got every word right. By Sunday she could recite the entire thing with her eyes closed, including the tiny laugh and the breath hitch.

The ritual worked, which was the danger. Her pulse dropped by the third replay. Her hands stopped shaking by the fifth. She could usually fall asleep after the ninth, except on nights when the memory opened farther and showed her details she could not close again: the way Maya's hospital gown had never tied fully in back, the adhesive rash under the chemo port tape, the plastic pitcher of water on the bedside table with untouched lipstick on the rim from a day before she was too nauseated to drink.

Sunday afternoon, while Ravi was at an extra depot shift that was supposed to be his only shift, she sat at the kitchen table with ice melting down her shin and rewrote her recovery schedule into something she could survive.

Doctor sheet said: rest, elevate, anti-inflammatory, no impact, begin PT intake next week.

Anika version said:

06:00 wake (even if slept late)
06:15 meds + breakfast
06:30 seated drills
07:00 shower
...
22:30 voicemail loops (cap 10)
23:30 lights out

It looked like discipline. It looked like control. It looked almost exactly like the training plans Ravi used to tape on the fridge, except this one had no court time and a new column labeled `night waking reason`.

When Ravi came home after dark, she showed him the page before she could lose nerve.

"I made a schedule," she said.

He scanned it fast, lips moving slightly at each time stamp. "This is too strict for injury week."

"Strict is the only thing I know how to do."

"You need flexibility too."

She gave him a flat look. "Since when?"

He accepted the hit. "Since now." He tapped the `voicemail loops` line with one finger. "Ten becomes twenty very quickly."

"So does one overtime shift." The words slipped out before she could weigh them.

He did not argue. He only nodded once, tired and unwilling to concede fully, and handed the notebook back.

"We'll figure it out," he said.

It was the kind of sentence adults used when they had no plan and no spare strength and wanted the room to stop hurting.

That night, after he went to bed, Anika sat cross-legged on top of her blankets with the old phone in her lap and the notebook open beside her.

She pressed play.

Maya said: middle shelf, spoon of water, mustard seeds, stubborn tomorrow, I love you.

Anika whispered each line in perfect time, her mouth shaping the words before the speaker did. She could have done it without the recording. She could have done it in the shower, on the bus, in math class, with the phone dead in her pocket. The recording was no longer information. It was dosage.

At 1:41 a.m., she wrote the final entry for the week.

Night 7
sleep: not yet
wake episodes: n/a
replay count: 19
panic peak: 8/10 then 3/10
shin ache at rest: 2/10

Then, on a line by itself:

I can say her exactly.

She closed the notebook, set the phone face down, and lay back without turning the lamp off. Light pooled on the ceiling. In the next room Ravi coughed once, rolled in bed, went quiet.

Anika listened for sleep and found only the familiar minute-click of Maya's watch against her wrist, counting forward whether she wanted it to or not.
