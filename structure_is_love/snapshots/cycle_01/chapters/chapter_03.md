# Chapter 3

Ravi brought home boxes that still smelled like oranges and detergent, flattened them on the kitchen floor, and stood each one up like he was building lanes for traffic. The apartment was quiet in the way it got quiet only after morning drills, after school, after the two buses and one transfer that left Anika with rubber-ball echo still ticking in her ears. It was late enough that the light coming through the narrow kitchen window had gone from white to the thin gray of late winter, and the radiator hissed without warming anything above ankle height.

He wrote in black marker on each box flap: `DONATE`, `KEEP`, `TRASH`, `MAYBE`. Then he crossed out `MAYBE` so hard the cardboard tore and wrote `DONATE 2` over it.

"We can finish this before five," he said. "Pickup window is four-thirty to six. If we stack by the door by four, we won't miss them. Tomorrow we'll do the hall closet."

Anika was still in her school sweater, one sleeve damp where she'd wiped sweat off her neck on the bus. Her calves buzzed from ghosting drills at dawn. She watched him tear tape with his teeth and smooth each strip down with the side of his thumb, fast and exact, as if bad tape would cost him minutes he did not have. He shifted weight off his left ankle when he turned, a hitch she only noticed when he thought she was not looking.

"Start with kitchen shelves," he said. "No overthinking. We have to clear space."

He said it like an instruction at court: no overthinking. Move your feet. Get to the ball. She went to the first cabinet anyway and froze with her hand on the knob.

The top shelf held three stainless-steel canisters with dented lids, one plastic jar of tamarind paste crusted at the rim, and two tea tins Maya had reused for cumin and fenugreek long after the labels peeled off. The smell hit before she touched anything. It carried stale curry leaf in metal, cardamom dust, and overboiled tea that had lived in the walls for years. Behind that was something floral and dry that should have been impossible six months after the funeral and still arrived like a slap: jasmine hair oil and the cheap hospital hand soap Maya brought home on her skin.

Anika took down one canister, then put it back, then took it down again.

Ravi had already filled half a box with duplicate mixing bowls and extra mugs from the top rack. "Anything chipped goes," he said. "Anything we haven't used in months goes."

"We use those," she said, pointing at the tea strainer with the bent handle.

"We have two." He held one up. "See?"

"The bent one doesn't drip."

He looked at her like he was deciding whether this was true, then dropped both strainers into `DONATE` and reached for the next shelf. The metal clicked against a glass jar. She flinched hard enough that he paused.

"Niks."

She hated when he used the short name in command voice. "What?"

"Don't do this all night. Pick and move."

She set the canister down on the counter and opened it. Masoor dal sat like red lenses against steel. Maya's handwriting on white masking tape around the side read `masoor / quick` in dark blue pen, slanted right, the tail of the `q` cutting under the word like an underline. The letters made something in Anika's chest pull tight and sharp.

She was nine again and standing on a chair at this same counter while Maya tapped a spoon against the rim of a pot. Not now, little engineer, Maya had said, smiling without looking up, because Anika kept asking if boiling changed protein. Count to sixty before you stir. Not forty. Sixty. She had smelled onions and mustard seed and the bitter edge of burned garlic because Maya answered a text and forgot to lower the flame. Anika had laughed. Maya had cursed once, soft and annoyed at herself, then fed her the rescue batch out of the pot with a spoon too hot for a kid's mouth.

The memory landed full-body, not like thinking but like being dropped back into old weather.

Ravi was still moving. He opened and shut drawers. He made one pile for forks and another for cracked containers. Plastic lids rained on the counter. He worked by categories, not by object. She worked by object and did not move.

"This one stays," she said, touching the tape label.

"One canister set stays," he said. "Not all six."

"There aren't six."

"Three here, three in the pantry."

"Then keep four."

"Keep three."

"Why three?"

"Because we're two people, not six."

The sentence sat there, too plain, too finished. They were two people, and that was all he would allow the room to hold. She counted backward from twenty because her face was getting hot and she did not want to cry in front of open cabinets and old cumin.

"If we stack the heavy ones in the bottom box," she said, voice flat, "the pickup guys can carry faster and the handles won't rip."

It was not an answer. It was logistics. She heard herself do it and could not stop.

Ravi nodded once, relieved to have procedure back. "Good. Do that."

They worked thirty more minutes in mismatched rhythm. Ravi's side of the counter cleared to wood grain. Anika's side became careful little islands: one chipped measuring cup with turmeric shadow in the numbers, one rolling pin with a hairline crack near the handle, one packet of cloves expired two years ago, one cloth bag of dried red chiles Maya had hidden behind rice because they were too spicy for Ravi.

At one point she opened the cutlery drawer and found a stack of folded paper napkins with Maya's grocery list written on top. `onion (small)`, `curd`, `sanitary pads`, `AA battery`, `orange if cheap`. The words were compressed, practical, no extra loops, written in the margin of an old pharmacy receipt. She held the paper to her face like that could bring smell back. It smelled like drawer dust and cumin.

"Trash that," Ravi said without looking. "It's old."

"It's writing."

"It's a list."

"It's her writing."

He stopped taping. The silence after tape noise felt violent.

"Anika." He put both hands on the counter and leaned in, not angry yet, not gentle either. "If we make every piece of paper sacred, we'll drown in this apartment."

"I'm not drowning."

"You are. You just call it something else."

She stared at the list until the `g` in `orange` blurred. He reached for it. She folded it in half and slid it into her sweater pocket before he could touch it.

His jaw moved once to the side, the way it did when he was swallowing words. "Fine," he said. "One list. Keep moving."

By three-thirty, they had six boxes by the entryway and one bag of garbage tied at the neck with a knot too tight for bare hands. Ravi made tea without asking if she wanted any, too much milk, too much sugar, the way he made it when he was trying to keep his hands from shaking. He stood at the sink while it boiled over and did not curse, only turned the flame down and scraped the sticky line off the burner with a spoon.

"After tea, bedroom closet," he said. "That's the hard one. Better to do it now."

Anika wrapped both hands around the steel tumbler and watched steam stripe the kitchen window. He had called it the hard one, as if there had been an easy one.

The bedroom closet door stuck in winter, swelling from damp, and Ravi had to shoulder it open. The smell rolled out first: naphthalene from mothballs, sandalwood from old drawers, the faint clean-sour trace of detergent Maya used because it was cheap and came in giant powder bags, not pods. Saris hung in a narrow block at the back, each one in a clear plastic cover Maya had slit at the top so the fabric could breathe. On the floor sat two canvas bins of folded sweaters and one shoe box full of scarves.

Ravi brought in three empty bags from the supermarket and opened them with quick snaps. "Winter coats we keep. Work clothes maybe we donate. Shoes mostly donate."

"Mostly?"

"One or two for memory. We're not running a museum."

Anika crouched at the sweater bin and lifted a navy cardigan she had seen every morning for years, usually with a pen clipped upside down to the collar because Maya forgot where she put pens. The cardigan smelled like old wool and Vicks and the lavender packets Priya had mailed last summer. She pressed the cuff to her upper lip, eyes closed.

Memory came again, concrete and cruel: Maya at the table at midnight, cardigan on over hospital scrubs, trying to help Anika memorize science vocabulary while fighting nausea that made her hold her breath between words. "Evaporation," Maya had said, tapping the flashcard with one finger. "It means something leaves and still belongs to the water cycle. That can happen."

Anika had rolled her eyes because she was ten and angry at everything. "That's not how belonging works."

"It is tonight," Maya had said, then thrown up in the sink and come back five minutes later with mint toothpaste still on her tongue, pretending nothing had happened.

Anika folded the cardigan and set it in the keep pile.

Ravi glanced over. "One cardigan, okay."

She nodded, then picked up a green salwar kameez with one missing button and held it long enough that he noticed.

"Donate that," he said.

"Why?"

"Because it's torn and you won't wear it."

"I could fix it."

"You don't sew."

"I can learn."

"Not this week."

His tempo was faster now, voice clipped around time markers. He checked his phone every few minutes for the pickup message as if the driver were a proctor and late fees might follow if he missed the slot. He folded without looking at labels, by weight and shape only. Every clean fold looked like an erasure.

Anika moved to the shoe box of scarves. Beneath two cotton scarves lay Maya's old work ID lanyard with the hospital logo half rubbed off. She touched the plastic badge sleeve and felt her throat close. There was no badge inside, just a faded transit card and one movie ticket stub from a night Anika had not gone because she had fever and Maya stayed home with her while Ravi went alone and came back with popcorn in a paper cup. The stub was for a Tamil movie Maya had called terrible and secretly loved.

"Can I keep this?" Anika held up the lanyard.

Ravi did not turn around. "What is it?"

"Her lanyard."

"Keep. Small things are fine."

Small things are fine. The rule sounded practical until she realized it meant he had decided what size grief was allowed.

In the top closet shelf sat two shoeboxes of papers and one plastic pouch with jewelry: glass bangles wrapped in newspaper, one chain with a tiny Om pendant, one digital wristwatch with a black resin strap scuffed white at the edges.

Anika saw the watch and did not breathe.

Maya wore it every day, not because it was good but because it survived bleach and bus poles and sink water. She used the timer for cooking and for medication alarms during treatment. In the last months she had stopped wearing rings because her fingers swelled, but the watch stayed on until the hospice nurse removed it and set it on the dresser next to a half cup of cold tea.

Ravi reached for the jewelry pouch first. "These we can put away. Later."

"Can I look?"

"Quickly."

She opened the pouch. The watch was warm from the room, not from skin, and that made grief feel stupid and physical all at once. She turned it over. The back was scratched deep near the battery cover. She remembered the exact scratch: Maya had caught the watch on the metal edge of the bus farebox and laughed because blood came before pain and she bled onto the dispatch forms Ravi had printed for his own shift.

"Put it in keep," Anika said, too quickly.

Ravi's hand hovered. "We can decide later."

"Why later?"

"Because we're not doing one-item arguments. We're finishing."

"It's a watch."

"Exactly. A watch."

She put it back in the pouch because his eyes were on her and she needed him to look away.

They moved to papers: receipts, test reports, warranty cards, temple donation slips, and a stack of old school forms with emergency contact lines that still had Maya's number in neat print. Ravi sorted by future legal risk. He kept tax forms, trashed expired insurance cards, saved anything with signatures, and set old novels aside for the building lobby shelf.

Anika found a spiral notebook half full. The cover showed sunflowers gone dull with kitchen grease fingerprints. First pages: grocery budgets in Maya's hand; columns of prices from two different stores; bus fare totals; one page of recipes measured in handfuls and caution notes for Anika in parentheses: `less chili for her`, `soak rice 20 mins if you remember`. Midway through, the handwriting changed from lists to short entries, not dates, just fragments: `A vomited before math test + still wanted to go`, `R forgot lunch again`, `today pain 6 but manageable`, `owe Leela $40`.

Anika closed the notebook before Ravi could see the line about pain.

"What is that?" he asked.

"Nothing. Old notebook."

"If it's bills, keep. If it's random, donate."

"It's half used."

"Then donate."

He did not ask what was written. She hated him for that and understood him at the same time: he was moving through land mines by refusing to look down.

"Why are you doing this like a race?" she said.

"Because if we stop, we won't restart."

"So that's it? Just bag everything before we can feel it?"

"We have felt it for six months."

"You haven't."

He turned, finally full on, one hand still gripping a stack of insurance envelopes. "Don't tell me what I've done or haven't done."

"You wake me up at four-thirty and make me run until I throw up. That's not feeling."

"That's surviving."

"For who?"

His face changed on that question, not softer, not harder, just stripped. For one beat she saw the exhausted man from the kitchen sink, not the coach voice. Then he put the envelopes down and went back to command.

"We're done talking," he said. "Take this bag to the door."

"No."

"Take the bag."

"No."

"Anika, don't start this."

"You started it when you called everything random."

"Memory-hoarding is not grief work. It's avoidance."

The phrase was ugly in his mouth, like something borrowed from an article he had skimmed at 2 a.m. and decided to weaponize.

"Says who?" she said.

"Says reality. We don't have space. We don't have money for storage. We don't have a second apartment for ghosts."

There it was: money and space, the true gods in their kitchen. He took a breath and tried for measured. "One box. You get one memory box. Anything beyond that, it eats the house."

"You mean it eats you."

"Fine," he snapped. "Me. It eats me. Happy?"

She was not. The fight had shifted and she did not know whether she had won anything except a sharper version of the same wall.

He rubbed his left ankle with the toe of his right shoe, an old habit from long shifts when the joint locked in cold weather, and looked away from her toward the closet as if the clothes themselves were loud.

"Pickup text came," he said after a moment, voice flatter. "Forty minutes. Move."

He handed her the linen bag and turned back to the top shelf.

Anika took the bag into the hallway where the light was always one stop too dim, dropped it by the door, and stood with her palms on the peeling wall until the urge to scream thinned into a sharp buzz behind her teeth.

When she came back in, Ravi was kneeling by the open jewelry pouch, separating pieces into a smaller tin for `KEEP`. He had put the watch on the edge of the shelf while he taped the tin shut.

"Can you carry these pillowcases?" he asked without looking up.

"Yeah."

She took the pillowcases with both arms. On the turn toward the hallway she let one slip, crouched to pick it up, and with her left hand took the watch from the shelf in one practiced scoop, palm closing so hard the buckle pin dug skin. She slid it into the inside pocket of her hoodie before she stood.

Ravi kept talking behind her about weight distribution in the donation bags. "Books separate from clothes. If they mix, the bottom tears."

"Okay," she said, heart kicking too hard.

She carried linens to the door, then came back twice for coats and towels, once for kitchen towels stiff with old starch. Each return was a test. Did he notice the empty edge on the shelf? Did his eyes track her pockets? He watched everything and nothing, counting bags under his breath, muttering numbers in a sequence she knew from training sets: eight, twelve, sixteen.

The half-used sunflower notebook was still on the dresser where she'd left it between insurance forms. Ravi was in the hall lifting a box and swearing quietly at the tape seam. Anika slid the notebook under her sweater against her stomach, cold spiral wire pressing skin, and pulled her shirt down.

When she bent to lift a second box, the notebook edge scratched her ribs. The sting felt like proof.

At four-fifty, two donation workers knocked and moved through the apartment fast, practiced and polite in the way of people who had seen a hundred versions of this room and this family. One of them wore mismatched gloves, one gray, one black, and apologized every time a box bumped the wall. The other had a tiny silver cross pinned upside down on her jacket zipper and kept saying "Got it, got it" under her breath as she carried heavier loads than her partner. Ravi over-thanked them. Anika stayed out of the doorway and counted each box leaving: one, two, three, four, five, six, seven.

When the last bag went, the apartment looked larger and poorer at the same time.

Cupboards showed raw empty shelves where spice tins had hidden water stains. Closet rod exposed a run of bare metal between Ravi's shirts and the one cardigan Anika had saved. The floor near the dresser had a pale rectangle where a storage bin used to sit. Even sound changed. Ravi set down a mug and the clink bounced farther.

"Eat," he said at seven, setting rice and reheated dal on the table. He had made too much and salted it late; she could taste the fix. "You've got school tomorrow. We still train in the morning."

"How many sets?"

"Standard Monday. Ghosting plus serves. Maybe light movement after school if homework is done."

"How light?"

"We'll see your legs."

She nodded like this was normal bargaining and forced down bites while the hidden notebook warmed against her skin. The watch in her hoodie pocket dug into her hip whenever she shifted in the chair.

Ravi did dishes without speaking. Water ran. Plates clicked. The radiator knocked twice like someone at the door. He left her lunch container drying upside down and went to the living room with his phone and a legal pad, writing numbers in columns that looked like dispatch schedules, overtime shifts, and rent dates. She did not ask.

In her room, Anika locked the door even though the latch stuck and did not fully catch. She took the watch out first.

The watch had a black strap, scratches at the buckle, and one faint crack across the plastic face that only showed if she tilted it under light. She pressed the side button. The display blinked to life, weak and green: `19:43`. The seconds ticked. The battery was not dead.

She put it on her wrist.

It was too loose. Maya's wrist had been narrower by the end, then wider on steroid weeks, then narrow again. Anika threaded the pin through the second-to-last hole and shook her hand. The watch slid once, settled against bone, and stayed.

She sat on the floor with her back against the bed frame and pulled out the sunflower notebook. First half held Maya's lists and fragments. Last third had blank pages. The paper smelled faintly of turmeric and drawer dust.

She opened to a fresh page and wrote the date, then crossed it out because she did not want to start like school. She tried again:

`after donation day`

That looked too dramatic. She scratched that out too. Third try:

`MON`

The third try felt better. It was neutral.

She made columns the way math teachers wanted: narrow for numbers, wide for notes.

`food`
`training`
`pain (0-10)`
`grief spike (0-10)`
`output`

She stared at the headings until they stopped looking stupid. Then she filled the first line from memory.

`banana half + tea (5:05) / ghosting 12 x full-court, serves 80 / pain 6 at shin, 4 chest / grief 2 during drills / output: better split step on last four sets`

Second line:

`school lunch: skipped half sandwich / stairs hurt after 6th period / pain 5 / grief 1 / output n/a`

She paused, pen over paper, then wrote the event she had been avoiding naming.

`kitchen + closet sorting (3:10-5:00) / no training / pain 3 legs, 8 throat / grief 9 when smelled cardigan + saw handwriting / output: none`

She added one more line under output before she could talk herself out of it.

`when pain is in muscles, I can move better. when pain is in chest/throat, timing goes bad unless I count.`

The sentence made her nauseous with accuracy.

She flipped back through Maya's pages. Grocery arithmetic. Short notes about bus delays. A list of medicines where one refill had a star next to it and `ask discount` written in the margin. On one page Maya had tracked her own pain for nine days in small numbers at the top corner, no explanation. `5 6 7 8 7 7 9 8 8.` Anika put her finger on the `9` and held it there until ink smudged.

From the living room, Ravi's voice rose as he argued with someone on the phone about shift coverage. "I said I can take Friday if Patel swaps Wednesday. No, not double nights. I can't do both. I've got a kid."

He sounded like himself at work: clipped, bargaining, polite until he was not. Anika imagined him in the depot office under fluorescent lights, ankle swelling in his left shoe, drinking burnt coffee and pretending fatigue was a scheduling problem instead of grief.

She wrote another line.

`Dad says move fast or drown.`

Then, below it:

`Fast is his float.`

She stared at that one longer than the others. It felt too close to mercy. She crossed out `float` and wrote `weapon`. She crossed out `weapon` and wrote `air tank`.

The revision felt better. It was not kind and not cruel. It was useful.

A knock came, two quick taps with no waiting. "Lights out in fifteen," Ravi said through the door. "And leave your phone in the kitchen tonight."

"I don't sleep with it anyway."

"Good."

His steps moved away. She waited until the floorboards stopped complaining, then tore the page corner with the crossed-out words and swallowed the strip dry before she could think about how weird that was.

She slid the notebook under the mattress near the wall, then changed her mind and pulled it back out. Too obvious. Ravi changed sheets on Sundays and hated lumps in the mattress. She moved it again, this time into the old geometry kit box under her desk, beneath protractor sets she never used. On top she placed two broken mechanical pencils and a packet of erasers so it looked like junk.

The watch stayed on her wrist.

At ten, the apartment settled into night noises: radiator hiss, upstairs toilet flush, one distant siren crossing Northern Boulevard, Ravi coughing once in the living room before his bedroom door shut. Anika lay on her side facing the wall, watch face against pillow, feeling each second tick through fabric. She tried to slow her breathing to match it and failed.

Memory came in fragments that refused order. She saw Maya at the stove, tapping the spoon twice before tasting. She saw Maya in clinic shoes, unlacing one-handed while she asked about math homework. She saw Maya in the hospital bed saying, with lips dry and cracked, "You don't have to be good all the time, kanna," and then asking for water before Anika could answer.

Anika squeezed her eyes shut until color flashed behind them. She counted backward from one hundred. At sixty-three she lost track and started again.

On the third attempt she remembered the donation workers carrying bags down the stairs, careful not to scrape walls. She imagined those bags opened in another apartment, her mother's cardigan on someone else's shoulders, spice tins in another kitchen, list pages in the bottom of recycling. A panic wave rose so fast she sat up and pressed both palms to the mattress.

She wanted to run suicides in the hallway until her lungs burned and her mind narrowed to feet, turn, feet, turn. She wanted to wake Ravi and scream that he had let strangers carry away things that still knew Maya's hands.

Instead she got out of bed, took the notebook from the geometry box, and wrote in the dark using the green glow from the watch as a guide.

`22:18`
`urge to run: 8/10`
`pain now: legs 2, chest 7`
`if chest > 6, do not trust decisions.`

She read the line twice, then added:

`replacement: wall-sits 3 x 60 sec + count tiles`

She set the notebook down, put her back against the wall, and did the wall-sits in socks on cold floor, thighs shaking by forty seconds, watch timer beeping softly at each minute. On the second set she bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste blood. On the third set the shaking blurred into heat and the panic slid one notch lower.

She logged it.

`after wall-sits: chest 5, legs 5, grief 7, urge to run 4`
`output tomorrow unknown`

The word `unknown` looked like failure and relief at once.

She replaced the notebook in the geometry kit box and went back to bed with quadriceps buzzing. Through the wall she could hear Ravi's television low, some sports panel arguing over players she did not care about. Every few minutes his cough cut through the commentators, dry and restrained, the cough of someone trying not to wake a child.

At 4:25 the watch alarm vibrated against her wrist before it beeped. She killed the sound instantly. For one second she did not know where she was and reached toward the other side of the bed for a body that had not been there in months.

Then the apartment, the wall, the winter dark, and the watch all snapped back into place.

She stood, pulled on training shorts under sweatpants, and opened the notebook to last night's page.

`AM target: keep grief below 4 during drills by counting splits`
`if grief > 4, increase reps not speed`

She added a final line before going to the kitchen.

`watch on. do not let him see.`

In the hallway mirror she tucked the watch under her sweatshirt cuff, then rolled the cuff once to make it look accidental, not hidden. Her face looked older in the mirror's warped edge, not because of bone or skin but because she had started carrying a secret weight and the body knew before the mind did.

Ravi was already in the kitchen tying his shoes, one lace frayed white near the knot. He had laid out two bananas, one steel bottle of water, and a packet of salt crackers on the counter in strict parallel lines. He glanced at her wrists, her shoes, her eyes.

"You sleep?" he asked.

"Enough."

"Eat half now, half after warm-up."

"Okay."

He checked the time on his phone and frowned. "Bus in nine. Move."

She peeled the banana, swallowed without tasting, and followed him down the stairs. At the landing he slowed for one step because of his ankle, hand briefly touching the rail. He caught her seeing it and straightened.

Outside, the air smelled like wet concrete and old exhaust. The sky was still dark blue over Roosevelt Avenue. They walked fast to the stop, not talking. Ravi kept one arm angled behind her when trucks rushed past, an old protective habit he never mentioned.

At the shelter, while he checked transit updates, Anika slid her cuff down and pressed her thumb over the watch face until the seconds disappeared under skin. She felt the edges of the case, the place where Maya's wrist had worn the plastic smooth, and did not cry.

When the bus came, they climbed in and stood near the back door where there was space for lunges if the route stalled. Ravi began reviewing the morning set out loud, short imperative phrases timed to each streetlight: "First twenty minutes footwork. Then serves. No drifting. Don't chase power early."

Anika nodded at each one, the dutiful athlete, the manageable daughter.

Inside her sweatshirt cuff, the watch warmed against her pulse. In the geometry kit box under her desk, the notebook waited with columns and lies and true things mixed together. The chapter of the house they had cleared yesterday did not feel closed. It felt like an incision with stitches that had not taken.

She touched the pen mark still faint on her finger from the night entry and looked out the smeared bus window as Queens slid by in dark storefronts and shutter grates. She counted passing intersections until counting became breath.

At the rec center doors, Ravi held one open and said, "Let's go."

She went.
