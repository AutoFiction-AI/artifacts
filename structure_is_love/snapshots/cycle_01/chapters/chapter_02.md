# Chapter 2

The schedule Ravi taped to the fridge last night is still curling at the corners even though he pressed it down with both thumbs. Black marker blocks: court, shower, school, commute, court again. The only white space sits between 12:02 and 12:26, lunch period, and even that has "EAT" written in capital letters like an order from somebody who knows you won't.

At 6:38 a.m., after ninety minutes of ghosting drills and one bus transfer in wet cold, Anika stands in the apartment kitchen in socks that smell like court dust and damp rubber. The radiator clicks without heating the room. Ravi is at the stove, stirring oats with one hand and typing with the other. His left ankle is stiff this early, so he leans his hip against the counter and keeps his face blank while he works around the pain. Mustard seeds pop in the oil for his own eggs; curry leaf smoke lifts into the same steam as instant oatmeal packets.

"You're three minutes behind," he says, not looking up. "Eat while it's hot."

Anika sits, opens her notebook to tonight's math homework, and makes herself swallow two spoonfuls. Her throat still tastes like iron from sprint repeats. When she bends over the bowl she can feel the heartbeat in her gums.

Ravi's phone buzzes. He has already built her afternoon in his calendar: bus to rec center, dynamic warm-up, feet-and-lunge circuit, thirty serve targets. He forwards it to her anyway. Her own phone lights on the table with his name and the same schedule in short lines.

`Leave school gate 3:22.`
`Route Q49 or walk to 74 if late.`
`No detours.`
`Text when you're on bus.`

He finally looks at her. "You ate half yesterday and cramped."

"I'm eating."

He points his spoon at the bowl, then checks the wall clock. "Finish. You need fuel."

The words are practical. The tone is practical. The feeling in the room is not practical at all. It is the feeling of being watched while you chew.

She gets through another three spoonfuls, then chases them with water because her stomach is climbing into her throat. Ravi slides a banana toward her lunch bag and a folded napkin with almonds counted into a neat stack, twelve exactly.

"You look pale," he says. "Did you sleep?"

"Yeah."

"How many hours?"

"Six. Maybe six and a half."

He starts to correct her estimate, then stops. "Tonight you need seven. We can't keep wasting recovery."

He says we when he means her body.

By the time she gets to school, her fingers are still wrinkled from the shower she took too hot, trying to rinse off court smell before first period. A clean hoodie does not help. Rubber ball, bleach mop water, and old sweat stay in the fabric like a second skin.

In homeroom, fluorescent lights hum hard enough to make her molars ache. The day begins with a social studies worksheet about city government. She prints her name at the top, then stares at question one for too long: Explain the role of public oversight in municipal decision-making. The sentence swims and steadies and swims again.

Ms. DeMarco drifts between desks with a mason jar of iced coffee in one hand and a stack of hall passes in the other. She teaches with a soft Queens rasp and keeps a tiny fox-shaped ring on her thumb that she twists when she is trying not to snap at kids. The ring catches the light each time she stops at Anika's row.

"Morning, team," she says. "Ten minutes silent work, then we'll discuss. Heads up, no phones out."

Anika presses her pencil harder so the graphite leaves grooves she can feel through the paper. She answers two questions by memory, one by guessing, and then she loses three minutes in a blink that does not feel like a blink. Her chin drops. The pencil slides from her fingers and draws a dark line across the worksheet and onto her wrist.

A hand taps her desk once.

"Anika."

She jerks up so fast the chair screeches. Several kids look over. Jerome in the back snorts. Zoe, two rows to the left, turns in her seat with one eyebrow up, not smiling.

Ms. DeMarco crouches beside her desk instead of calling her out across the room. It makes it worse.

"Walk with me for a second," the teacher murmurs.

The hallway is empty except for a custodian rolling a trash bin toward the stairwell. Anika stands against the cinderblock wall while Ms. DeMarco checks the worksheet and then checks Anika's face.

"You fell asleep mid-sentence," Ms. DeMarco says quietly. "Are you sick?"

Anika wipes the graphite smear from her wrist with her sleeve. "No."

"Did you eat breakfast?"

"Yeah."

"Are you getting enough sleep at home?"

Anika keeps her eyes on the chipped blue stripe painted across the wall. "I'm fine. I just woke up early."

Ms. DeMarco waits, as if patience might make a second answer appear behind the first one. When none does, she twists the fox ring once and exhales through her nose.

"I don't need details you don't want to share," she says. "But if you're dropping in class, I need to loop in adults. That's my job."

Anika's stomach folds tighter. "Please don't call my dad. I'm okay."

"I'll send a quick email so we can keep an eye on this. You're a strong student, and this isn't your normal."

The email is already written in Ms. DeMarco's voice before she even says it. Anika can hear the subject line: Concern about fatigue in class. She can see Ravi opening it between dispatch calls, jaw clenching, building a fix.

"I said I'm fine," Anika says. The second time comes out flatter.

Ms. DeMarco does not argue. "Come back in. Splash water first."

In the bathroom, Anika cups cold tap water to her face until her skin stings. She studies herself in the mirror: eyes puffy, small purple moons under both. Her hair is still damp at the roots from the shower. She holds her breath and counts to twelve like she counts ghost reps. At eleven her phone buzzes in her hoodie pocket even though it should be off.

Dad: `How was first period?`

She stares at the screen. Ms. DeMarco sent the email immediately.

She types: `Fine.`

Three dots appear almost at once.

Dad: `Teacher said you nodded off.`
Dad: `You need to eat lunch fully today. Send pic before bell.`
Dad: `No talking in hallways after class. Straight to gate.`

The lie she just sent feels soft for one second, then sets like plaster. Fine was one word, and it hardened.

When she gets back to class, Ms. DeMarco draws a flowchart on the board. "Oversight isn't punishment," she says. "In theory, oversight protects people when one person has too much unchecked control."

Anika sits down and writes the sentence in the margin because it sounds like something she should remember, even if she does not know where to put it.

By second period math, hunger turns mean. It bites and then disappears and then returns behind her ribs. She eats the almonds during independent practice, one at a time, hiding each bite behind her hand. Powdered salt from the skin sticks to her thumb and the side of her notebook. Her worksheet has fractions; her brain gives her training times instead. 3:22 gate. 4:05 court. 4:12 first drill. She writes 4/5 where 5/8 should go and has to erase the same box twice.

At 10:47 another message arrives while the class changes.

Dad: `Did you finish almonds?`
Dad: `Need carbs at lunch.`
Dad: `No chips.`

She does not answer. The phone buzzes again anyway.

Dad: `Reply.`

In science, the room smells like vinegar and old aquarium water. Mr. Patel starts a lab demo and asks for volunteers. Zoe raises her hand before the instructions are finished because Zoe always likes any task that involves making a mess. She has chipped black nail polish that she refuses to repaint because she says chipped looks "more honest," and she keeps a sketchbook in her backpack with duct tape over the spine and stickers peeled halfway off. While Mr. Patel measures baking soda, Zoe leans back and whispers to Anika without moving her lips.

"You look dead," Zoe says. "Like Victorian dead, not regular dead."

Anika snorts once despite herself. "Thanks."

"I'm serious. You okay?"

"Yeah."

Zoe squints at her. "That was a fast yeah."

Anika pulls her science notebook closer and points to the board as if she is deeply invested in acid-base reactions. The demonstration foams over the beaker lip. Kids cheer. Mr. Patel laughs and curses softly when fizz splashes his sleeve.

Under the noise, Anika's phone buzzes again against her thigh.

Dad: `Hydrate.`
Dad: `If teacher asks, tell them you had trouble sleeping.`
Dad: `Don't mention training volume.`

She reads that line twice. Don't mention training volume. It is the first time he has said it plain.

Zoe catches the movement and raises both eyebrows. "You're texting in Patel's class? Bold."

"It's just my dad."

"Everything okay at home?"

The question is casual on the surface. The way she asks it is not casual at all.

Anika tucks the phone away. "Yeah. He's just being annoying."

Zoe nods, but she keeps watching.

Lunch period starts with rain tapping the cafeteria windows and a line for pizza that snakes past the vending machines. Anika gets the standard tray: square slice, carton milk, bruised pear. She is not hungry until food is in front of her. Then she is suddenly starving and nauseated at the same time. Cheese smell turns her stomach, but the milk goes down fast, cold and sweet and gone in four pulls.

Zoe drops into the seat across from her, backpack still on one shoulder, sketchbook wedged under her arm. "My mom says I can go to Astoria Park after school if I finish homework first. You want to come? Me, you, maybe Dani if she stops fake-flirting with eighth graders."

Anika peels the pear sticker and sticks it to the table edge. "I can't. I've got practice."

"You always have practice."

"It's Tuesday."

"So? Tuesdays still have sky."

Anika tries to smile. Zoe drums black-polished fingernails against the milk carton. The nail on her index finger is chipped so low the corner catches on paper whenever she flips pages, and she keeps filing it with the blade of a pencil sharpener during class because she hates the snagging sound.

"I'm not asking you to skip your whole life," Zoe says. "Just one hour. We were gonna hit the deli for fries first. The guy with the neck tattoo finally fixed the fryer and now it doesn't taste like old fish."

The thought of fries makes Anika's stomach lurch and clench, half desire, half acid.

Her phone buzzes.

Dad: `Lunch pic now.`

She angles the tray, snaps a picture before Zoe can see the screen, and sends it.

Dad: `Eat all of it.`
Dad: `Need energy for footwork ladder.`
Dad: `Leave school gate 3:22. I can see weather. Buses slow.`

Zoe watches her typing and leans back. "Wow. You have parole officer texts."

"Shut up."

"I'm not joking, Niks."

Anika bristles at the nickname and the tenderness inside it. "It's not like that."

"Then what's it like?"

Anika takes a bite of pizza crust and chews too long because she needs the time. She can feel Ms. DeMarco's email hanging over the day like a second fluorescent light. She can see Ravi reading it at work, deciding to tighten everything.

"It's just temporary," she says finally. "My sleep got messed up. That's all."

"From what?"

"Just stuff."

"Stuff has a name."

A group of boys at the next table starts a ketchup packet war. One bursts on the floor, red fan across dirty tile. A lunch aide shouts for paper towels. The cafeteria roars and then settles and roars again. Inside that noise Zoe lowers her voice.

"Is this about your mom?" she asks.

Anika's throat closes on the crust. She reaches for milk that is already gone, then pushes the empty carton around the tray.

"I'm fine," she says.

Zoe slumps forward, elbows on table. "You keep saying that like you're reading from a card."

"I'm fine," Anika repeats, and this time she hears exactly how dead the line sounds.

The lie hardens in real time. It goes from sentence to habit while she is still speaking it.

Zoe sits back. Her face does a quick flicker from hurt to annoyed to joke-ready, and then the joke does not come. "Okay," she says. "Cool. Be fine."

She opens her sketchbook and starts drawing the cafeteria salt shaker with violent little strokes. Anika watches the pencil carve shadow over paper, fast and dark. On the top corner of the page there is a half-finished drawing of Zoe's little brother with his new braces and one giant front tooth still hanging in because baby teeth do not respect orthodontist appointments.

Anika knows Zoe has been worried all week that her mom will make her babysit after school and cancel the art magnet open house she has been counting down to. Zoe has told her this twice. Anika forgets to ask about it now.

Her phone buzzes again.

Dad: `Did you finish tray?`
Dad: `Reply yes/no.`

She types `yes` even though half the pizza is still there.

Dad: `Good.`
Dad: `Pack at 3:18.`
Dad: `Don't miss bus.`

She locks the phone and stares at her own reflection in the black screen for a second. Her face looks older when it is flattened by glass.

The afternoon drags and jumps. English is a reading quiz she usually aces. Today she misses two inferencing questions because she reads the same paragraph four times and keeps seeing route numbers instead of words. In art, paint thinner smell gives her a headache and she asks for water twice. At 2:41 Ms. DeMarco appears at the classroom door and asks to see her in the hallway again.

"I heard back from your dad," Ms. DeMarco says, keeping her voice low. "He says you're on a new morning training routine and adjusting."

Anika's pulse stutters. "He told you that?"

"He said it's athletics-related and he's monitoring nutrition and sleep."

The word monitoring lands like a hand on the back of her neck.

Ms. DeMarco leans against the lockers. Up close, Anika can see a tiny crescent scar under the teacher's eyebrow, pale and old. "I'm not trying to interfere with your goals," Ms. DeMarco says. "But school is not optional collateral. If this routine means you can't stay awake through first period, the routine needs to change."

Anika hears Ravi's voice answering for her: This is discipline. She hears the part he will not say to teachers: We can't afford to drift.

"I'll sleep more," she says.

"That's not a full plan."

"I know."

Ms. DeMarco studies her for another beat, then softens. "Do you want me to connect you with the counselor? Not because something is wrong with you. Just because grief plus pressure can scramble people."

Anika grips her sleeve at the cuff seam until the thread cuts her finger. "No. Thank you."

"If you change your mind, come find me. And if you nod off again, we revisit this."

Anika nods.

As she turns to go, Ms. DeMarco adds, "You're allowed to be eleven. Remember that."

The line follows her back into class and sits there like an impossible assignment.

At 3:11 the final bell warning chimes. Backpacks zip. Chairs scrape. Hallway noise surges, bodies funneling toward stairwells in damp winter jackets. Zoe catches up at Anika's locker, breathless, holding two bus cards between her fingers like a magic trick.

"I topped this one for you," Zoe says. "My grandma gave me ten bucks for helping her with laundry. Don't fight me. We're going to the park, we split fries, we do math on a bench, we pretend we're normal middle school idiots for like sixty minutes."

Anika freezes with one sneaker half-tied.

"I can't take your card."

"You can. It's ten dollars, not a kidney."

"I've got to go."

Zoe steps in front of the locker door before Anika can close it. "Practice can wait one day."

"No, it can't."

"Why? Is your dad going to send a SWAT team?"

Anika's phone buzzes in her pocket, once, then again.

Dad: `3:18. Pack now.`
Dad: `Where are you?`
Dad: `Send gate photo.`

She does not show Zoe. She shoves books into her backpack and misses the zipper track the first time.

Zoe's voice loses the joke layer. "Tell me what's going on."

"Nothing is going on."

"That's bullshit."

"Don't."

"Don't what? Ask if my best friend is turning into a ghost?"

The hallway thins as kids leave. A teacher shouts for people to clear the stairs. Rain rattles harder against the stairwell windows. Anika can feel each second like a countdown beep.

"I've got training," she says. "I can't keep explaining this."

"You haven't explained anything."

"I don't have time."

"Right. You've got time for footwork ladders and ghost whatever and your dad's weird control texts, but you don't have one hour for me."

Anika zips the bag so hard the teeth catch fabric. She yanks it free.

"Zoe."

"What."

"I can't do this right now."

Zoe laughs once, sharp. "You mean friendship? Yeah, I noticed."

That hits harder than the training this morning. Anika wants to say she is sorry, wants to say please wait, wants to say she does not know how to hold grief and school and Ravi's fear and hunger and still sound like herself. What comes out is the smallest, safest sentence.

"I'm fine."

Zoe stares at her for a long second. Then she folds the extra bus card in half and tucks it into her own pocket.

"Have fun being fine," she says, and turns toward the stairs without looking back.

Anika stands alone by the locker bank while the corridor empties. Her phone buzzes again.

Dad: `If you miss this bus, warm-up gets cut and we add sprints.`
Dad: `Move.`

She starts moving.

Outside, the rain is thin but cold enough to slide down the back of her neck. She lifts her hood and runs the last half block to the Q49 stop, backpack pounding between her shoulder blades. The bus comes crowded and breathing diesel. She wedges into the aisle, one hand on the wet pole, and texts a photo through the fogged window as proof.

Dad: `Good.`
Dad: `Sip water now.`

She obeys automatically, unscrewing the bottle with numb fingers. The water tastes like plastic and metal and relief. At the next light, two girls from school cross in front of the bus sharing earbuds, laughing so hard one of them has to grab the other's sleeve to stay upright. Anika watches them until the doors hiss shut again.

She opens her notes app and writes what she tells herself is logistics:

`social studies asleep 8:19`
`Ms D emailed`
`lunch finished (not true)`
`Z invited park 3:15 said no`
`gate 3:24`

The list sits there like evidence. She reads the line with not true and does not delete it.

By the time the rec center comes into view, the sky is already dimming behind the chain-link fence. The building's windows throw back a dull yellow, and she can almost hear the rubber-ball echo before the doors open. She slides the phone into her bag, squares her shoulders, and steps off the bus.

The lie is not just in what she told Zoe. It is in what she told Ms. DeMarco, and Ravi, and herself. "I'm fine" becomes fine and then becomes nothing at all, a smooth stone she keeps in her mouth so no other words can get out.

Inside the lobby, she sees Ravi by the vending machine, still in depot uniform, reflective stripes at his cuffs, eyes on the clock. He lifts his hand once in a hurry signal and starts toward the courts before she reaches him. She follows.

The social part of her day has already ended. It is 3:58 p.m.
