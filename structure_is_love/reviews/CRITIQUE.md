# CRITIQUE: *Untitled Novel* ("Squash")

**Premise:** Following her mother's death, an eleven-year-old girl channels her grief into a brutal squash regimen overseen by her father.

**Structure:** 18 chapters, approximately 130,000 words. Literary fiction / coming-of-age / sports-pressure drama.

---

## I. OVERALL ASSESSMENT

This is a serious, ambitious novel about grief, coercive care, and the body as currency. At its best — and it is frequently at its best — the prose achieves a density of physical and psychological observation that recalls Katherine Mansfield's domestic interiors transplanted into contemporary Queens, or the ruthless child-perspective clarity of Emma Donoghue's *Room*, but without that novel's single-gimmick architecture. The premise is narrow in the best sense: one apartment, one sport, one death, one father, one daughter. The novel earns its length by refusing to let any of these elements simplify.

Its weaknesses are real but largely structural: certain repetitive textures across the middle third, an occasional over-reliance on the same somatic vocabulary, and a continuity architecture that — while impressively maintained — sometimes produces the feeling of a novel that has been audited rather than dreamed. These are correctable problems. The novel's central achievement — making the reader feel the weight of an eleven-year-old's body as both instrument and hostage — is not.

**Verdict:** A work of genuine literary distinction with the emotional architecture of a prizewinner and the craft flaws of a strong early draft that has been revised twice rather than five times.

---

## II. WHAT WORKS

### A. The Body as Narrative Engine

The novel's greatest accomplishment is its treatment of physical sensation as the primary medium of storytelling. Anika's body is not a metaphor for grief — it *is* the site where grief, ambition, poverty, and love become indistinguishable. The shin pain that begins as a whisper in Chapter 5 and crescendos through the stress fracture in Chapter 8 is tracked with the precision of a medical case study, yet it never reads as clinical. Every lunge, every vomiting episode, every blister is located in specific anatomy and specific emotion simultaneously.

The opening chapter's training sequence is exemplary: the progression from salt water to nausea to the absence of grief during exertion is rendered with an almost unbearable clarity. By the time Anika discovers that "if she kept the effort high enough, there was no space left for the other thing," the reader has been inside the mechanism rather than told about it. This is show-don't-tell executed at an elite level.

### B. Ravi Iyer

Ravi is the novel's most complex creation and its greatest risk. A lesser novel would make him a villain or redeem him by Act Three. This novel does neither. Ravi is a man whose love expresses itself as spreadsheets and whose terror of loss has calcified into control. The novel is patient enough to show why: the debt arithmetic is never abstract (the $38,400 oncology balance, the $1,860 utility arrears, the $640 monthly payment due the fifteenth), and the midnight app-driving shifts are given the same physical specificity as Anika's drills. When Ravi says "Structure is love, Niks," in Chapter 1, the reader understands both the truth and the violence in the statement.

His arc across the novel — from unilateral commander to a man who asks "What do you want me to do?" in Chapter 17 — is earned through accumulation rather than epiphany. The mediation scene in Chapter 14 is the pivot, but the real turning point is the small, devastating moment in Chapter 16 when he admits: "I used your training to avoid my grief. I turned your life into timers and color-coded boxes on the fridge because if I stopped counting reps I had to count days since Maya stopped breathing."

### C. The Institutional Ecosystem

The novel understands that children exist inside systems, not just families. The school nurse, the tournament physio, the compliance coordinator, the mediator aunt, the physical therapist — each of these figures is drawn with specificity and given enough interiority to resist type. Tess Keating's mug ("DON'T SKIP THE BORING STUFF"), Nurse Park's tomato-print clogs, Lidia Cruz's fennel seeds and cartoon turtle lanyard, Dr. Morales's turmeric-stained fleece — these details are not decoration. They establish a world in which institutional authority has human texture, where the people enforcing rules also have sons taking road tests and Little League teams from 5-7 p.m.

The compliance notice in Chapter 10, with its bureaucratic language and Nadia Serrano's signature block noting her son's Little League schedule, is a small masterpiece of tonal control. It is simultaneously threatening and humane, which is exactly how institutional power operates in children's lives.

### D. The Debt Architecture

Very few novels about childhood poverty get the arithmetic right. This one does. The oncology balance is introduced through a hardship waiver form in Chapter 6, and from that moment forward, every decision in the novel is freighted with specific dollar amounts. The reader knows exactly what a lost tournament slot costs, what a cancellation credit looks like, what an app-driving shift yields. The novel never aestheticizes poverty but also never reduces Ravi to his ledger. The debt is a character in the novel — silent, patient, compounding.

### E. Maya's Presence

The dead mother is the most difficult character in any grief novel. Here she is handled with extraordinary restraint. She exists through grocery lists ("onion (small), curd, sanitary pads, AA battery, orange if cheap"), voicemails about lemon rice, and letters that arrive as physical objects with specific textures. The letter from Locker 214 — "You're not my memorial project. You're not the oncology payment plan with a racket in your hand" — is the novel's moral center, and it arrives through paper that has been folded and refolded until the creases have gone soft.

The voicemail replay scenes in Chapter 9 are harrowing precisely because they are rendered as addiction rather than tribute: Anika sets rules for herself (maximum ten loops), breaks them by loop sixteen, and recognizes the mechanism ("she was using her mother's voice the way she used drills, as anesthesia with a timer") without being able to stop.

### F. Dialogue Differentiation

The novel's dialogue work is strong and well-differentiated. Ravi's clipped, procedural register ("Banana. Half now, half after warm-up. Salt water in the blue bottle. We leave in six minutes") is unmistakable from Zoe's quick, interrupt-ready cadence ("You look dead. Like Victorian dead, not regular dead") or Priya's boundary-focused plainspokenness ("Don't swallow all of it for him"). The anti-transcript principle — no back-and-forth volleys without new information — is largely honored, and leverage shifts are embedded in most confrontation scenes.

## III. WHAT DOESN'T WORK (OR DOESN'T WORK YET)

### A. Repetitive Somatic Vocabulary

The novel's greatest strength is also its most persistent weakness. Certain physical descriptors recur with a frequency that eventually dulls their impact:

- "Hot wire" / "thin hot line" / "bright wire" for shin pain (appears in at least 8 chapters)
- "Throat closed" / "throat tightened" as the default grief response
- "Jaw jumped" / "jaw flexed" / "jaw worked" for Ravi's anger (nearly every chapter)
- "Black static" / "white spots" / "vision narrowed" for exertion limits

By Chapter 12, the reader's own somatic response to these phrases has habituated. The novel needs a wider palette of physical language in its middle third, or it needs to earn repetition by making each recurrence structurally meaningful rather than reflexive.

### B. The Middle-Third Pacing Problem

Chapters 9-12 form the novel's softest stretch. The enforced rest period after the stress fracture is dramatically necessary, but the narrative struggles to generate sufficient forward motion without court action. The apartment scenes and PT clinic scenes begin to feel interchangeable in their emotional texture: Anika wants to move, Ravi wants to control, institutional figures enforce boundaries, Anika logs data in her notebook. The variations are real but subtle enough that a less committed reader might feel stalled.

Chapter 9 in particular carries too much weight as a pure interiority chapter. The voicemail replay sequences are powerful, but the chapter lacks the dramatic counterweight that every other chapter provides through confrontation or consequence. A single scene of external pressure — a school interaction, a Zoe visit, a neighbor encounter — would give the reader a fulcrum.

### C. Notebook Entries as Structural Crutch

Anika's notebook is a brilliant conceit in Chapters 3-6: it establishes her as a child who processes the world through data, and it creates a private space inside a surveilled life. But by the second half of the novel, the notebook entries have become a structural tic. Nearly every chapter ends or pivots on a notebook entry, and the device begins to substitute for dramatic resolution. The reader starts to anticipate the moment when Anika will "open the notebook and write" rather than act, speak, or be changed by contact with another person.

The notebook should be pruned by roughly 40% in the second half, with entries reserved for moments of genuine surprise or self-confrontation (the "I laughed so hard a cookie fell into my shirt" entry in Chapter 7 is perfect; the routine logging entries are expendable).

### D. Leah Sethi — A Missed Opportunity

The ladder-match opponent in Chapter 4 is the novel's most vivid antagonist, and she disappears entirely after that chapter. Her little brother with the shark lunchbox, her friendship bracelet wound around the racket throat, her weaponization of Maya's death — these details suggest a character with her own internal pressure system, not merely a dramatic obstacle. The novel would benefit from a single later encounter with Leah, even a brief one, to close the circuit her introduction opens. As it stands, she is the novel's most memorable minor character and its most conspicuous loose end.

### E. Cora Bell — Slightly Underwritten

Cora is positioned as Anika's primary rival but remains somewhat opaque. The dachshund charm, the chipped canine, the "compliance superstar" comment — these are good details, but Cora never quite escapes the functional role of "obstacle with manners." A moment of genuine vulnerability from Cora — not just post-match sportsmanship, but something that reveals her own internal costs — would give their rivalry the bilateral depth it needs.

### F. Occasional Over-Specification

The novel's commitment to concrete detail sometimes tips into cataloguing. Certain scenes accumulate sensory data past the point of dramatic return: the exact contents of every cabinet during the sorting scene in Chapter 3, the full inventory of the rec center hallway in multiple chapters, the complete text of compliance notices. The principle should be: every detail earns its place by doing emotional work, not merely establishing verisimilitude. When a detail exists only to prove the author has imagined the room, it should be cut.

## IV. CONTINUITY AND CRAFT

### A. Tense Consistency

The novel now maintains consistent past tense throughout all eighteen chapters. This was not always the case — Chapters 1 and 2 were originally drafted in present tense, creating a jarring discontinuity that was corrected during revision. The fix is seamless; no residual present-tense narration survives.

### B. Factual Continuity

The novel's continuity across 18 chapters and nine months of story time is remarkable. Ravi's left ankle stiffness, the precise oncology debt figures, Anika's right tibial stress fracture, the utility arrears balance ($1,860.12 reduced to $1,240.12 after the August payment), the fridge calendar's evolution, Maya's watch battery and strap repairs — all are tracked with meticulous consistency.

One minor inconsistency was caught and corrected: Ravi's ankle laterality was briefly switched from left to right in Chapter 13 before being normalized. The financial arithmetic is internally consistent across all chapters, which is a genuine achievement given the number of specific dollar figures involved.

### C. Character Naming

An earlier draft contained a character-name collision (two distinct characters named "Marisol Vega"), which was resolved by renaming the compliance coordinator to Nadia Serrano. No naming collisions remain.

### D. Process Leakage

An earlier draft contained instances of chapter numbers appearing as in-world labels (e.g., characters referencing "Chapter 8" as a location). These have been fully excised. No process leakage remains in the final text.

---

## V. THE ENDING

Chapter 18 is the novel's quiet triumph. After seventeen chapters of escalating crisis, the novel ends not with a tournament victory or a family reconciliation but with an eleven-year-old coaching beginners and failing to follow her own rules. The six extra serves past the forty-minute warning are the perfect coda: Anika recognizes the compulsion, names it honestly in her notebook ("I ignored the 40 warning and hit 6 extra serves. I would've kept going if I hadn't slipped"), and accepts the consequence (Jonah takes her timer).

The final image — Maya's watch set face-down on the desk, out of reach, while Anika lies in the dark still rehearsing how to bargain for ten more minutes — is exactly right. It refuses recovery-narrative closure. Grief is not resolved. Debt is not resolved. The father-daughter relationship is not resolved. What has changed is the infrastructure of accountability around these unresolved things: the group text thread, the shared calendar, the third-party witnesses, the notebook entries that are no longer cleaned up before submission.

The novel understands that for an eleven-year-old in this situation, "better" does not mean healed. It means: the adults have agreed to stop lying, the child has agreed to stop performing, and the systems that were supposed to protect her are now, imperfectly and conditionally, doing so. That is a more honest ending than most adult literary fiction manages.

---

## VI. FINAL VERDICT

This novel belongs in the conversation with the best contemporary fiction about childhood under pressure. Its closest peers are not sports novels but works like Elizabeth Strout's *My Name Is Lucy Barton* (for its understanding of how poverty shapes the body), Hanya Yanagihara's *A Little Life* (for its refusal to look away from damage, though this novel is far more disciplined in its restraint), and Shehan Karunatilaka's *The Seven Moons of Maali Almeida* (for its insistence that the dead remain active agents in the living world).

Its flaws — the somatic vocabulary repetition, the middle-third pacing, the notebook-entry reliance — are the flaws of a novel that has been built with enormous care and revised with intelligence but not yet subjected to the final, pitiless editorial pass that separates a very good novel from a great one. That pass would take perhaps three months and would involve cutting 15-20% of the word count while deepening Cora, returning to Leah, and trusting the reader more in the second half.

As it stands: a novel of striking ambition and frequently brilliant execution, with an emotional core that will not leave the reader easily. The squash court as grief chamber, the spreadsheet as love language, the broken watch as inheritance — these images will stay.

**Rating: Strong recommend for further development. Prize-caliber premise and execution with identifiable, addressable weaknesses.**
