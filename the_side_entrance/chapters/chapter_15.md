# Chapter 15

An hour after the Ashdowns' basement kitchen door had shut him out, the buzzer went while Beni was ironing the white shirt Mercer wanted for gala work the next day, the iron balanced on a folded towel over Laci's coffee table because the board had lost a leg in October and never been replaced. Rain ticked against the window in a thin patient way, enough to silver the glass but not enough to wash the soot off anything. The flat smelled of damp plaster, frying oil gone cold, and the lemon cleaner Laci used only when he thought the mildew had started winning. Beni set the iron upright and listened. Nobody came for him here unless they wanted money or had brought the wrong takeaway upstairs.

The buzzer went again.

He pressed the street-door release out of habit and crossed the narrow hall in his socks. Cold air came up the stairwell when he opened the flat door. Bea Ashdown stood on the landing under the weak bulb with a navy overnight bag on one shoulder and rain shining on the sleeves of her school coat. Her tie was gone, the top button of her shirt undone, and one thumbnail was half stripped of clear polish, the edge bitten raw on the ride from Belgravia. She did not look frightened or theatrical. She looked like she'd missed a train and decided that was now everyone else's problem.

"Can I come in," she said, "before your cousin sees me and thinks I'm selling God?"

Beni stared at her long enough to feel the draught crawl up his bare legs. "Bea?"

"Yeah." She shifted the bag higher. "Not a ghost. Can I come in or do you need a minute?"

Leaving her on the landing was impossible. Leaving her on the pavement outside the block was worse. He stepped back. She came in without brushing him, bringing cold wool and expensive shampoo gone flat with rain. Her eyes moved once around the front room that was also his bedroom: the sofa bed folded out against the wall, the damp map blooming above the skirting board, Laci's boots under the radiator, the iron steaming faintly on the towel. She took it in with one quick sweep and did him the mercy of not pretending not to see.

"What are you doing here?" he asked.

"Not going home."

Laci came out of the kitchenette before Beni could say anything else. He had one trouser leg rolled to the knee and was re-fastening the beige ankle brace he had been wearing for weeks, the Velcro clogged with plaster dust but still catching if he yanked it hard enough. His T-shirt was inside out. He stopped when he saw Bea, then the bag, then Beni's face.

"No," he said at once. "Whatever this is, no."

"She just came," Beni said.

"I can see she just came. I can also see she's a child with luggage."

Bea gave him a tired, precise look. "I'm seventeen."

"Perfect," Laci said. "A seventeen-year-old with luggage. Even worse."

He limped the last step into the room, tea glass in one hand, and the smell of lager and mint rub came with him. Beni could feel the situation turning ugly before it had even found shape. The flat was too small for surprise. Every new fact had to sit on someone's knees.

"Laci," he said in Hungarian, low. "Just ten minutes."

"You can have eight." Laci looked at Bea again, still hard-faced, weighing trouble against trouble. "If police knock because rich people lost their daughter, I'm not learning polite English for them."

Bea hitched the overnight bag down to the floor. "I'm not missing. I know where I am."

"That helps me enormously," Laci said. "Do your parents?"

She did not answer. She had the self-control to leave the silence there instead of decorating it. That worried Beni more than tears would have.

He switched off the iron and moved it to the windowsill. "Sit down."

"On what?"

He almost laughed, which would have been the wrong sound. The only free chair had a clean shirt drying over it, and the sofa bed was unmade because it was where he slept. Bea solved it by perching on the edge of the windowside chair after lifting the shirt with two fingers and handing it to him. The bus outside ground through its gears at the stop, making the glass hum in the frame.

"How did you find this place?" he asked.

"I called Mercer and said you'd left a scarf in our car and I needed to send it back. The girl on the phone gave me the building and postcode." Bea worried at the split thumbnail with her teeth for one quick bite. "People tell polite girls stupid things."

Laci made a disgusted noise that was half admiration. "Of course they do."

Beni felt a quick stupid pulse of panic at Mercer hearing her voice, at traces he could not now control. "Why didn't you go to a friend?"

"Because my friends have mothers who lunch with my mother, or fathers who'd ring my father before I'd got my shoes off. Also because I didn't want a house in Chelsea full of people pretending this was interesting."

"And you thought this was better?"

"Not better." She glanced at the damp patch on the wall and then back at him. "More honest."

The words landed harder than if she had said something cruel. She sat very straight with the bag between her knees, one hand hooked through the strap like she expected someone to take it away. Mascara had smudged under one eye from rain and the drag of hours. He could see where she had packed in a hurry: the zipper caught on a sleeve, the side pocket bulged with charger cables and a rolled pair of socks.

"What happened?" he asked.

Bea looked at Laci, then at the hall, then back to Beni. "Do you want the short version or the version where your cousin starts charging by the minute?"

"Short," Laci said. "Definitely short."

"Tomorrow is the gala," she said. "The literacy one. Children's books, bad champagne, rich people bidding on first editions so they can feel literate in public. My father had the photographer booked before breakfast. Family on the stairs at six-thirty, then table, then auction, then whatever version of us pays best."

Laci leaned back against the kitchenette doorframe. "Rich people are disgusting."

Bea ignored him. "They want me pressed into it. On the stairs, at the table, in the photographs. Smile here, stand there, don't look like I know anything. I'm not doing it."

"So you left," Beni said.

"Yes."

"Without telling anyone."

She held his eyes. "If I'd told my father, he'd have turned it into drivers, security, and some kind of lesson. If I'd told my mother, she'd have made me carry her feelings first. I needed to get out before either of them put a hand on it."

The room felt smaller. Rain tapped the window harder for a second, then slackened. Beni thought of Oscar's voice when he was controlling himself: quieter, not louder, each word set down like paperweights. He thought of Nina making Bea's absence into fresh material for shame, fear, self-display, all of it at once. He thought of Laci, already at the edge of his tolerance, with rent numbers never far from his mouth and a four-thirty alarm for work. There was only one adult Bea trusted who was not one of her parents, and that was the woman Beni had most recently lied to, humiliated, and lost.

"Why me?" he asked.

"Because I don't know where Eszter's staying now." Bea shrugged one shoulder. "And because you'd at least open the door before ruining my evening."

Laci barked a laugh into his tea. "She has your number."

Beni ignored him. "You can't stay here."

Bea looked around the front room once more, this time without flinching. "I gathered that when I walked in."

"It's not about--"

"The damp? The sofa? Your cousin hating me? I know." She pulled at the split thumbnail. "I can sleep on the floor. I've slept on floors."

Laci made a face. "At Glastonbury, maybe. Not here."

Bea's head turned. "I haven't been to Glastonbury."

"Then congratulations on your moral purity. You're still not sleeping here."

Beni wished, briefly and uselessly, for Noor. Noor would have made three practical calls and told him what order to do the rest in. Beni only had the facts in front of him and the one behind them: Bea was seventeen, rich, angry, and in his flat. Keeping her overnight in a front room where his bed opened six feet from Laci's coat pile was not shelter. It was another kind of danger with worse paperwork.

He looked at Bea's phone on her lap. The screen was black, but he could feel it there like a lit fuse. "Do they know you're gone yet?"

"Probably not." She paused. "Maybe my mother thinks I'm at revision. Maybe my father thinks I'm upstairs being difficult. The house is busy when there's a performance coming. People only count heads when the guests matter."

That line, too old and too clear, made him cold in a place the weather had not reached.

"Did you bring money?" he asked.

Bea gave him a flat look. "A little. I'm not trying to buy your floor."

"I didn't say that."

"No, but you looked worried in a counting way."

He was worried in a counting way. He always was. He had forty-three pounds in his wallet, two twenties and three coins and a limp five he had put aside for tomorrow because gala shifts ran long and staff meals never reached the drivers. The cash Nina had given him sat hidden in a sock in his bag, contaminated even before he touched it. The night had already started costing money, and money was the easiest part of it.

Laci set the tea glass down on the counter with a click. "What is the plan?"

"I'm thinking."

"Think faster. I've got work."

"You always have work."

"Exactly. So I would like to sleep before it."

Bea looked at Beni. Under the watchfulness, there was the first edge of plain tiredness now, adolescent and unguarded. "If you call my father right away, he'll send a car and people and act like I've stolen state secrets."

"If I don't call anyone, that's worse."

"I know." Her jaw tightened. "I know. I just needed one place where nobody would start by arranging me."

Beni went into the hall with his phone and closed the front-room door most of the way behind him. The bulb over the stairs buzzed. He scrolled to Eszter's number and stopped there. Two days ago she had cut him off in the basement kitchen with a face he had not known how to answer, hard not because she was shouting but because she no longer needed anything from him. He had no right to drag her back into his mess. He had even less right to drag Bea into another bad adult choice because his pride hurt.

But the other choices were worse and getting worse by the minute.

He typed, deleted, typed again. Finally he sent: `Bea's with me. She left home. I know I've no right to ask anything. I need to bring her somewhere safe for tonight.`

The message went. No reply came.

He stood there listening to the buses shake the building and the low blur of Bea's voice inside answering something Laci had said. He could not hear the words, only the shapes. He sent a second message before he could talk himself out of it: `I'm coming to the church room unless you tell me not to.`

Still nothing.

When he went back in, Laci was standing at the window with the curtain lifted on two fingers, looking down to the street. Bea sat where he had left her, but her bag was zipped properly now, ready for movement. She read his face too fast.

"We're leaving," she said.

"Yes."

Laci dropped the curtain. "Good."

"Can I stay ten more minutes while he figures out where?" Bea asked.

"No." Laci pushed himself off the sill and went to the hook behind the door, where three umbrellas hung collapsed and leaking onto the tray. He pulled down the black one with only one broken rib and held it out to Beni without quite looking at him. "Take this. Bring it back. If that girl turns up soaked and sneezing in my hallway, I'll kill you from sheer irritation."

Beni took it. "Thanks."

"Don't thank me. I'm protecting my floorboards."

Bea got up and slung the bag over her shoulder. For the first time some awkwardness showed through, not about being there but about taking anything from anyone in it. "I'm sorry," she said to Laci, and this time it sounded less polished.

Laci shrugged, apology too small a coin to bother counting. "Be interesting somewhere else."

The rain had thickened to a fine needling mist. Beni did not take Bea to the bus stop. The thought of sitting with her under fluorescent shelter glass while her phone started lighting up made his skin prickle. He used the last of his prepaid battery to call a minicab office near the station and gave the church off Seven Sisters Road as the destination. The man said ten minutes. It became fourteen.

They waited under the umbrella in front of the off-licence, the pavement slick with old flyers and a burst bin bag. Bea kept one hand in her pocket and the other on the overnight bag strap. She was not shivering yet, but the cold had sharpened her face, drawing the seventeen back into it. A strand of hair had stuck to her lip gloss and she kept blowing it away impatiently.

"What did you pack?" Beni asked, mostly because silence was making him hear all the wrong possibilities.

"Toothbrush. Knickers. A jumper. Phone charger. The ugly trainers I wear in the car if my mother makes me put on shoes that hurt." She looked down at the bag. "I left the dress."

"Good."

"You don't even know what it looked like."

"I know enough."

Headlights slid over the wet road and away again. The minicab still had not come. Bea dug at her thumbnail with the edge of a key. "Do you think I made it worse? Telling Eszter."

Beni answered too fast for it to be generous. "No."

She glanced at him. "You didn't have to say it like that."

"Like what."

"Like you were relieved someone did it for you."

He had no defence that would not sound like more of the same. The wet pavement reflected the green pharmacy cross, breaking it under passing tyres. "You weren't wrong," he said at last.

"I know I wasn't wrong." Bea shifted the bag on her shoulder. "That wasn't the question."

The cab arrived with a heater already blasting and a pine-tree air freshener swinging from the mirror so hard it clicked the windscreen. Beni gave the driver the church again, paid half up front in cash because that was the rule with some offices after midnight, and got in beside Bea in the back. The seat fabric was rough and smelled faintly of cigarettes scrubbed with lemon.

For a few streets neither of them spoke. Tottenham slid past in shuttered chicken shops, cash-and-carry light, buses with two wet passengers on the top deck. Beni watched the route out of habit even while someone else drove. Left at the junction, queue by the lights, don't take the shortcut because the roadworks would still be there. He could not stop his brain doing it. Counting roads felt cleaner than counting blame.

Bea leaned her head back and closed her eyes, giving them one minute of darkness. "If she says no, what then?"

"We'll think again."

"That means you don't know."

"Yes."

She opened her eyes. "Fair."

At the red light near Stamford Hill her phone lit inside the bag pocket, then buzzed hard enough to rattle against something metal. She froze before she touched it. On the second buzz she dragged it out and looked at the screen. The colour left her mouth.

"Your mother?" Beni asked.

"Both."

She thumbed the screen dark again without answering. The phone began vibrating once more almost immediately, the trapped-wasp sound of insistence. This time the preview flashed across the glass before she killed it: `Answer now. If I don't hear your voice in ten minutes, I call the police.` Under it came Nina's name and another message: `Please just tell me you're breathing.`

"You have to tell them you're alive," he said.

"Not yet."

"Bea."

"Not yet." She kept her voice low, but the panic in it was real now, fine as wire. "If I answer my mother she'll cry and hand him the phone. If I answer my father he'll turn tonight into an operation. I just need to get to Eszter before he starts moving pieces."

Beni looked out at the wet row of shops, the halal butcher shut behind its metal grille, the betting shop still lit. The problem had changed shape. It was no longer tomorrow's staircase and photographer. It was police, staff calls, and Oscar Ashdown making lists.

"What does he mean by police?" he asked.

Bea stared at the dark screen in her hand. "He'll ring the school, the friends he approves of, maybe the police if he thinks he can keep it private. He'll call it concern and ruin half the night doing it."

"And your mother?"

"She's sending please answer and where are you and little broken bits of herself. Then she'll put him back on. They're a relay race when there's blood in the water."

The phrase kicked a breath out of him that might have been a laugh on another night. "When we get there, you text," he said. "Straight away."

She shut her eyes once, then opened them. "All right. But only when we're there."

The driver turned the heat higher. Warm air hit their shins and brought the smell of wet wool up from Bea's coat. She folded forward, elbows on her knees, suddenly less composed. Her bag had left a damp stripe on the seat where it rested. Beni could hear her stomach make a small humiliating sound under the engine noise.

"Have you eaten?" he asked.

"At lunch."

"Only lunch?"

"There were canapes in the kitchen when I left, but I wasn't staying for donor hummus."

He took out the last coin-pound piece from his pocket, then another from the change tray built into the door, and when the driver stopped by the lights near the station he got out long enough to buy a paper cup of tea and a wrapped cheese pastry from the all-night kiosk. He came back wet at the shoulders. Bea looked at the food like she did not want to be seen wanting it.

"Take it," he said.

"I'm not going to faint dramatically if that's what you're thinking."

"I wasn't."

"You were a bit."

"Eat."

She burned her mouth on the first bite because the pastry had been sitting under heat too long and gone lava-hot at the middle. She swore, swallowed, and then laughed once at herself, small and actual. That, more than anything, made her look her age. She drank the tea too quickly and wiped her upper lip with the back of her hand.

"Did Eszter stop speaking to you?" she asked after a while.

Beni looked at the wipers working the windscreen ahead. "Yes."

"Because of me?"

"Because of me."

Bea held the cup between both hands, warming them. "I didn't tell her to be kind."

"I know."

"I told her because she was the only person in that house who ever looked at me like I wasn't furniture."

The cab took the bend too fast and the tea slopped against the lid. Beni steadied the cup without thinking. Her fingers were cold through the paper.

"She might send us away," he said.

"Probably."

"Then don't be surprised."

Bea looked out at the rain-streaked window. "I'm not here for grace."

The church sat back from the road behind a low black railing and two plane trees shedding bark in pale strips. A side light burned by the basement steps. The upper windows were dark except for one yellow square near the presbytery. Beni paid the rest of the fare, every note suddenly feeling filthy-soft from other people's hands, and got out with the umbrella and Bea's bag.

The gate squealed when he pushed it. Rainwater had gathered in the broken edges of the steps, and the basement corridor smelled of mop water, radiator dust, and the soup someone had cooked earlier for too many people. A laminated fire notice had curled away from the wall by the door. Bea stood beside him with her shoulders up around her ears and watched him knock.

No reply.

He knocked again, harder this time. The old wood carried the sound down the corridor in a way that made him feel intrusive in his bones.

"Maybe she's asleep," Bea said, quieter now.

"She won't be."

How he knew that he could not have said. Maybe it was only that Eszter was never fully off duty, no matter whose building she was in.

He heard movement at last: a chair leg, a bolt, the quick rubber squeak of slippers on lino. The door opened four inches, chain still on. One dark eye looked through the gap, then widened.

Eszter shut the door, lifted the chain, and opened it properly.

She wore thermal leggings under a long grey T-shirt and an old men's cardigan buttoned wrong, one button off all the way down. Her hair was loose and half dry, a child's pink elastic looped around her wrist for later. There was flour on one forearm. Behind her Beni saw a metal table, a hot plate, and a saucepan with the lid crooked, steam fogging the tiny window above the sink.

When she saw him her face closed. When she saw Bea beside him with the overnight bag and wet school coat, it sharpened into action, the way her whole body did when a job announced itself.

"Tell me you've got a better explanation than the one standing in front of me," she said.

"I left home," Bea said before Beni could answer. "He didn't kidnap me."

Eszter's eyes cut back to Beni. "I wasn't asking her."

"She came to my flat," Beni said. "I couldn't keep her there."

"So you brought her to mine."

"I know what that sounds like."

"Do you." She looked him up and down, rain on his shoulders, umbrella dripping onto the concrete. "That would be a first."

Bea stepped forward an inch, enough to claim the scene back. "If you want to tell me to leave, tell me. Don't do the adult thing where you speak over my head and then act like it's for me."

Eszter's gaze settled on her. "You're soaked at the hem and your lips are blue. Come inside before I decide anything."

She stood back. Bea went in. Beni followed because there was nowhere else for him to stand without becoming a weather problem.

The room was smaller than Laci's front room and cleaner by force. A narrow bed ran along one wall under a folded church blanket. Two plastic crates were stacked beneath the window, one full of clothes, the other of food and cheap toiletries. A kettle sat beside the hot plate. Above the sink a laminated picture of St. Joseph had been blu-tacked to the wall, probably by whoever used the room before. Everything had edges of use and none of it had room to pretend.

Beni felt at once what it cost her to keep it this exact: every crate squared under the window, every mug washed before sleep, no corner left slack enough to invite pity.

Eszter shut the door with her heel and took Bea's bag from her before she could resist. "Shoes off."

"They're fine."

"If they were fine, your socks wouldn't be making that sound."

Bea opened her mouth, then bent to unbuckle one shoe. Her fingers missed the clasp twice. On the third try the whole controlled structure of her seemed to slip half an inch. Her grip failed before the rest of her did. The metal buckle clicked uselessly against her nail.

Eszter saw it. So did Beni.

"Enough," Eszter said. She crouched, slapped Bea's hands lightly away, and undid the buckle herself with two fast movements. "Sit. Both shoes. Beni, don't stand there like a coat rack. Take this thing off her."

Beni moved at once. Bea let him pull the wet coat from her shoulders with stiff, furious compliance. Under it her school jumper was damp at the neck. Eszter set the shoes by the radiator, peeled the wet socks off, and wrapped Bea's feet in a tea towel from the counter because there was nothing else close enough.

Watching Eszter move around Bea with those fast, economical hands made shame rise under Beni's wet shirt. Two nights ago she'd shut him out. Now he had brought a richer version of the Ashdowns' mess straight over her threshold.

"Did anyone hit you?" Eszter asked, already rising, already reaching for mugs.

"No."

"Did anyone touch you?"

"No."

"Good. Then this is only rich-people insanity and we can manage it."

Manage it, she said, but Beni could hear the legal edge scraping under the joke. Bea was seventeen. This was a church basement. One wrong call and the night would harden into statements.

Bea gave a broken little laugh that sounded more like her teeth had knocked together. She sat on the chair now, hands flat on her thighs, rigid in the old school way, waiting to be called in and judged.

Eszter pointed to the bed. "Sit properly. Not on the edge like you're at a private clinic."

Bea obeyed.

The phone started vibrating again from inside the bag Eszter had taken. The sound against the cheap metal table was louder in the small room than it had been in the cab. Bea flinched so hard the tea towel slipped off one foot.

Eszter fished the phone out, looked at the screen, and blew air through her nose. "Mum. Dad. Mum again. Christ."

The sound had already drilled into Beni's molars. He could picture Oscar making his neat midnight list: drivers, school, hospitals, police if it could be done quietly. Every item would land on someone paid less than him.

"Don't answer," Bea said immediately.

"I wasn't planning to take relationship instructions from either of them." Eszter set the phone face down. "But you're sending a message."

"I don't want them here."

"Neither do I. That's why you're sending a message."

Bea's jaw set. "If my father knows I'm alive he'll send someone."

"He already will. The difference is whether he starts with police, staff, and hospitals. I'm not having half of Belgravia climb over cleaners and drivers because you wanted one dramatic night." Eszter put a mug down in front of her with more noise than necessary. "You left because they were using you as decoration. Fine. I believe you. It's still not noble to make poor people do a missing-person shift."

The words hit clean. Bea took them. Her face changed, not into obedience, but into something more honest than that. "I wasn't trying to make work for staff."

"I know. But that is what happens anyway."

Beni kept his mouth shut. The rebuke fit him too neatly. He had already made one woman in this family carry the cost of his silence; tonight he had delivered the bill to another.

For a moment nobody spoke. Water hissed at the edge of the saucepan. The little radiator knocked once like a pipe swallowing air.

Beni stood by the door with the umbrella still in his hand, ridiculous and dripping, and hated how visible his part in all this was. If Bea had not seen him with Nina in the garage, if he had not lied to Eszter, if Eszter had not been driven out of the Ashdown house and into this borrowed basement room, this scene would not exist in this shape. It would be some other damage, maybe, but not this exact one.

Eszter turned to him at last. "Well?"

"She came to me," he said.

"Yes, I heard the plot."

"Laci wouldn't keep her. I couldn't either."

"No, you definitely couldn't."

"I know."

"Stop saying you know. Tonight has too much evidence otherwise."

Bea looked from one to the other with the bleak concentration of someone who had watched adults fail for too long to be shocked by it. "I can leave if this is your hobby."

"Sit down," Eszter said, not unkindly. "My hobby is surviving."

She lifted the saucepan lid. Onion and paprika and lentils came into the room with the steam, humble and immediate. Beni had not realised until then how hungry he was as well. Eszter ladled soup into two mismatched bowls, tore bread with her hands, and shoved one bowl toward Bea and the other toward him without looking at him long enough to make it giftlike.

"Eat," she said. "Talk after."

Bea took the spoon. Her hand was still not steady. She got the first mouthful down anyway. Colour came back to her face in pieces, not all at once.

Eszter sat on the end of the bed opposite her, elbows on knees. "Now. Tell me exactly why you left."

Bea swallowed. "Because if I stayed, I'd have ended up in that dress and in those photographs."

"I know what tomorrow is. I scrubbed candle wax out of silver for it last year. Give me the part underneath."

Bea stared into the bowl. "My father wasn't asking whether I was coming. He was talking about angles. He said if the three of us held still on the stairs long enough the room would stop sniffing around the house. My mother said I owed her one evening of normality after the month she'd had. Like normality comes on a hanger."

Eszter snorted once.

"And I looked at the dress and thought if I put it on tomorrow I'd either smile for them or tell a room full of donors what kind of house they're drinking in." Bea glanced at Beni, then back at the bowl. "About my mother and Beni. About you ending up in a borrowed basement while they still send champagne up three floors. All of it. I left because I didn't trust myself not to do something I couldn't take back."

Eszter's mouth thinned. "Yes. That sounds like a real reason."

"So I packed a bag. That's it. I didn't leave a note because I didn't want to write something I'd have to mean forever. I just left."

"Where from?"

"School first. Then I went home, got the bag, and left again before supper."

"Anyone see you?"

"Probably six people. Nobody who mattered to the version of me they were watching."

Eszter let that sit a second. "You understand this still isn't clever."

"I know."

"Good. Clever is overrated. We need useful now."

She slid the phone back across the table. Bea stared at it, shoulders locked, and did not pick it up.

"Write: I'm safe. I'm not hurt. I'm with Eszter tonight. Don't call the police. I'll ring Mum in five minutes, and Mrs. Keene can collect me in the morning." Eszter ticked each part on her fingers. "No address in the message. No little knife in the wording. No bait."

"You want me to say I'm with you?"

"Yes," Eszter said. "Because you are. And because if I carry the risk, they can know which woman is carrying it."

"If I say morning, they'll start negotiating."

"They can negotiate with my voicemail. Send it."

"My father will hate the don't-call-the-police part."

"Then enjoy that for three seconds and send it."

Bea let out a short breath through her nose, almost a snort. She typed, stopped, deleted something, typed again. Her thumb hovered over send. "Should I send it to both?"

"Yes," Eszter said. "Make them work equally for once."

Bea sent the message to both parents, then turned the phone face down and pushed it away with two fingers. Within ten seconds it buzzed again. She didn't touch it.

Eszter held out her hand. "Now call your mother. Speaker."

"I just sent the message."

"And I just said call."

"I don't want my father on the line."

"Then get to your mother first."

Bea stared at her, then picked up the phone and called. The answer came before the first full ring had died.

"Bea?" Nina's voice came tinny and split by panic. "Jesus Christ, where are you?"

"I'm alive," Bea said. Her knuckles went white around the phone. "I'm not hurt. I'm with Eszter tonight."

Relief broke through the speaker, ugly and wet and furious all at once. "With Eszter where? Your father is about to call the police."

Eszter held out her hand. Bea gave her the phone without argument.

"She's safe," Eszter said. "She's fed, she's dry, and she's staying the night."

"You can't keep her there without telling us where that is."

"I can tell Mrs. Keene where to collect her. Eight-thirty. Church gate off Seven Sisters Road. One car, one adult, no staff circus."

"Oscar will want-"

"Oscar can want. Mrs. Keene can come. If anyone else turns up, Bea stays put."

There was a silence long enough for the radiator to knock once. Then Nina said, smaller, "All right. Mrs. Keene at eight-thirty."

"Good," Eszter said. "Tell your husband not to ring the police. She's accounted for."

Bea took the phone back. "I'm alive," she said, looking at the soup rather than the speaker. "That's all I've got tonight."

Nina's voice came back clearer now, relief already hardening into management. "Thank God. Bea, please don't do this to me tomorrow. Let Mrs. Keene fetch you, come home quietly, and don't make me spend the gala keeping your father from taking it out on the staff."

"That's not my job either," Bea said, and ended the call.

Eszter rose and went to a plastic drawer unit by the bed. She rummaged, produced an oversized charity-run T-shirt with a faded green logo and a pair of thick socks, and handed them to Bea. "Bathroom is through there. The lock sticks. If you break the handle, I'll charge your father personally."

Bea took the clothes. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. You still might snore."

When the bathroom door shut, the room changed shape at once. Not quieter, exactly, but more adult and more dangerous. Beni set the umbrella in the tray by the door. Water ran off it in a thin black line.

Eszter did not lower her voice. She did not need to. "You had no one else."

"No."

"You thought of her father?"

"Yes."

"And?"

"He'd turn it into control."

"Her mother."

"Drama."

"Your cousin."

"He wanted her gone before the tea cooled."

Eszter folded her arms. "And me."

He met her eyes because not doing it would have been cowardice stacked on cowardice. "You were the only person she'd let me bring her to."

"That isn't what I asked."

The truth of it sat between them, ugly and exact. Eszter was witness, judge, and still, because the world was rotten in its usual practical way, the safest room available.

"You didn't deserve this ask," he said.

"No."

"But she needed somewhere."

"Yes."

He waited for the rest, the cutting line he had earned. Eszter gave it to him without hurrying.

"Don't stand there and mistake this for trust," she said. "A girl turned up wet and half frozen. I opened the door for her. Don't confuse that with opening it for you."

"I know."

"Good. At least one sentence tonight can die after being used."

The bathroom tap ran, then banged off. Beni looked at the small room, the bed that was really one person's bed and would now have to become shelter for two, the loaf of bread already cut down by half, the bowl he had almost finished because hunger did not stop for shame. "I can stay outside," he said. "On the hall chair. In case--"

Eszter's face went incredulous. "No."

"If Oscar sends someone--"

"Then a young man in a church basement with a rich girl and no witness makes the story filthier, not cleaner. Use your brain. Here it's a woman and a child for one night. That I can argue. You in the corridor like a tragic extra helps nobody."

He looked away. She was right so fast it hurt.

"You'll go home," she said. "You'll keep your phone on. If she bolts again or vomits or sets this stupid building on fire, I'll call you."

"She won't."

"Excellent. Then you can sleep badly in Tottenham instead of artistically on church furniture."

The bathroom door opened. Bea came out in the oversize T-shirt, which hung to mid-thigh, and the thick socks folded at the ankle. She had washed her face. Without the mascara smudge and school coat she looked younger and stranger at once, a rich girl temporarily stripped of props. She carried her uniform folded over one arm with more care than Beni would have expected.

"Where do I put these?" she asked.

Eszter took the damp clothes and spread them over the radiator and the back of the chair with the ruthless efficiency of someone who had dried too many things in too little space. "There. Sit. Finish the soup."

Bea obeyed, then looked at Beni. "Are you going?"

"Yes."

She nodded once. Relief and guilt crossed her face so quickly they nearly canceled out. "Sorry."

"For what."

"For turning up at your flat."

He could have said several things then, most of them useless. That she had done the right thing. That she had done a foolish thing. That he was sorry too, for larger reasons than doors and taxis and soup. None of it would have helped the next ten minutes. "You're safe here," he said instead.

Eszter shot him a look, sharp enough to cut the sentence back down to size. Bea caught it and dropped her face to the bowl, but one shoulder loosened.

The phone buzzed again. Bea ignored it. Eszter picked it up, read the preview without unlocking, and set it back down. "Mrs. Keene says she'll be at the gate at eight-thirty. Your father says be ready and don't make this worse. So the house is still the house."

Bea made a face into her bowl. "That sounds right."

"You'll sleep. Until eight-thirty you're my responsibility, and after that you go with Mrs. Keene." Eszter made it sound like the next necessary jobs, nothing more, which was maybe why the words calmed the room instead of grandstanding in it. "In the morning you'll wash, you'll eat, and you'll go. No sneaking out at dawn. No dramatic notes. No social-media martyrdom."

"I wasn't going to do social media."

"Good. Nothing becomes noble because strangers like it."

Beni picked up his empty bowl and automatically took it to the sink. Eszter let him rinse it under the cold tap, but only because refusing would have taken longer. The ordinary act of his hands in her small room felt almost obscene in its familiarity.

"Leave the bowl," she said. "You don't live here."

He set it down.

At the door he reached into his pocket for his wallet, then stopped. Money was the wrong shape for this. Whatever he put on the table would feel purchased or contaminated or both. Eszter saw the movement anyway.

"Keep it," she said.

"I wasn't--"

"You were. Keep it."

He did.

Bea had finished the soup. Her shoulders had dropped a fraction. The tea towel still lay crumpled beside the bed where Eszter had wrapped her feet in it. Small domestic wreckage already, proof of a real body passing through. Not a donor photo, not a scandal, not a lesson. Just a wet, angry girl who needed somewhere to sleep.

"I'll keep my phone on," he said.

"You already said that," Eszter replied.

"Right."

He put his hand on the door. Bea looked up once more. "Beni."

He waited.

"I know this doesn't fix anything."

He thought of the garage, of the kitchen after Bea had spoken, of Eszter's face when she understood what kind of money he had pressed at her problems, of all the clean lies he had told because speaking late always felt easier than speaking in time. "No," he said. "It doesn't."

Bea nodded, apparently satisfied by that much honesty. Eszter was already turning down the bed and pulling the church blanket free with sharp economical snaps. Her pink elastic flashed once on her wrist in the yellow light.

Beni stepped out into the corridor. The door stayed open just long enough for Eszter to say, without looking at him, "Bring back Laci's umbrella tomorrow if you want one decent thing not to rot on top of the others."

"I will."

Then she shut the door.

He stood under the buzzing basement light with the damp umbrella in his hand and the smell of lentils and paprika leaking faintly through the wood. Inside, he heard Eszter say, "Move over, your wet hair will soak the pillow," and Bea answer something too soft to catch. It was not forgiveness. It was a lock, a bowl in the sink, a bed being made wider by necessity. He went up the stairs before he could turn any of it into more than that. By morning he'd be back through the Ashdowns' basement door for the gala.
