# Chapter 11

Back on the M4, airport pickups were good until the client got in. For twenty or thirty minutes the car was only a car: leather gone a little cold overnight, heater low, route slip clipped to the vent, the white name board on the passenger seat waiting to become a person. Beni liked that part. Nobody asked for a charger, nobody cried, and nobody decided his silence meant they could pour whatever they liked into it.

He was due at Terminal 5 for a man called Arvind Mehra off the first New York flight. He had signed back into Mercer at five, dragged a clean shirt off the rail, and picked up the route slip. It had the booking reference, the flight number, and the note that the client preferred still water and no radio. Beni had not slept enough to feel hungry. Nina had kept him in the car until after two, talking herself hoarse and then going quiet in the dark like she had spent even that. Coffee sat under his ribs, bitter and flimsy. The shirt on his back had come from the depot rail and still smelled faintly of starch and somebody else's aftershave.

The M4 was wet but not bad. The rain had thinned to mist. Spray lifted from tyres and hung low over the tarmac so that every tail light dragged a red seam through it. Beni kept to the left lane where he could, wipers on intermittent, both hands at ten and two not because Mercer trained that into him but because it kept his shoulders quiet. The satnav spoke once and he muted it. He knew this road: the slow bend by the hotels, the stretch where the barriers crowded close and the lane felt narrower than it was, the overhead signs with the airport glyphs shining green in the wet.

He passed a fuel tanker, then a white van with two ladders tied to the roof. Up ahead, a curtain-sided lorry in the middle lane ran almost level with him for half a minute, its trailer dirty enough to hold finger marks, the company logo half hidden under brown road spray. A torn strip of red tape flapped from the rear hinge and hit the metal in a fast, stupid rhythm. Beni noticed it because he always noticed the stupid small things: tape, cracked indicators, bad retreads, the way some men sat too near the wheel because they were scared of distance.

The lorry's left indicator never came on.

It just came.

It did not happen fast. That was the worst part. It came with the dull confidence of something too large to think it needed permission. The white wall of the trailer moved a foot toward him, then another, the rivet line drifting across his windscreen. Beni hit the horn. The sound was swallowed at once by insulation and rain. He checked his right mirror and saw only the lorry's flank. To his left there was only barrier, hard shoulder barely a strip, and tarmac slick with standing water. The trailer kept coming.

He braked just enough not to lose the back end. The Mercedes shuddered under him, expensive and obedient and nearly useless. The wheel gave a quick dry chatter through his palms as the front tyre touched the ribbed line. He eased left, not yanked, because a yank spun cars and a yank killed people. The trailer was close enough for him to see black grease around a bolt head. Water sheeted off its side onto his windscreen in a hard slap. The heater vent hissed. His chest locked around the sound.

He saw white, a chemical white, and the burnt-plastic stink arrived before the pain. Klara laughed with her lipstick on the filter because she never kept it off the cigarette for long. The road in Hungary opened the wrong way under the headlights. The line in the middle of it vanished by rain and then he was on the wrong side, no, the side was wrong because he had put them there. He saw her bare knee against the gear stick and the headlights where they should not have been. He felt the wheel going light and then too heavy. He heard her say his name once, not frightened yet, more annoyed than frightened, as if he had missed a turn.

The Mercedes found the hard shoulder and ran along it with all four wheels humming over grit. Beni got the hazards on without seeing his own hand move. He brought the car to a stop under a gantry that said Terminals 2, 3, 4 as if those places had anything to do with him. Traffic tore past on his right. Each lorry shoved a pulse of air against the bodywork. The whole car twitched with it.

He sat very still.

The indicator for the hazards clicked. The heater kept breathing. There had been no impact. The bonnet was clean in front of him. The windscreen held rain and the red wash of other people's brake lights. Inside, the car offered up its ordinary inventory: leather, damp wool from his coat on the back hook, coffee in the cup holder, the lemon cleaning product from the depot valeter. Through it all cut the hot dusty tang of deployed airbags from another country and two years ago, so clean and false he almost respected it.

His right foot would not come off the brake.

He tried. The muscle in his thigh worked. The shoe stayed planted as if the pedal had risen through the sole and into bone. He looked down and the foot was his, the trouser leg his, black Mercer cloth with the hem starting to shine. None of it helped. He had the violent certainty that if he lifted his foot the car would already be turning, already spinning, already meeting the white burst that split a life into before and after.

Beni took his hands from the wheel by force. His left palm had left a damp print in the leather. He reached into his coat pocket and closed his fingers around the silver lighter he carried without ever using. The metal was cold and blunt-edged, scarred at one corner. He had no idea why that made anything worse, but it did.

His phone was vibrating in the cup holder. Dispatch was calling. He let it rattle twice before he could pick it up.

"Yeah," he said, and his voice came out from too far away.

The dispatcher wanted the booking status. Beni could not remember the client's name for a second, which frightened him more than the near miss had. Then it came back off the route slip: Mehra, Terminal 5, arriving. He said there had been a lane incident on the M4, no contact, car was fine. He did not say that he was not. The dispatcher asked if he could continue.

Out on the road, every set of headlights seemed to come toward him in the wrong lane before the angle corrected itself. The shoulder under the open sky was too narrow. The barrier post outside his window had a black number on it. He read it three times and kept forgetting what number was after the slash.

"Noor's nearest," the dispatcher said. "Ten minutes. Sit tight."

"I'm all right," Beni said, because that was what he said when there was no useful answer.

"Didn't ask," the dispatcher said, not unkindly, already elsewhere. "Put your hazards on if they aren't on."

He laughed once, a horrible dry sound, and cut the call.

He got the door open and the cold hit him with motorway filth in it, wet diesel, brake heat, the metallic taste of winter rain. His knees held. That annoyed him. If they had folded there would at least have been a shape to the problem. He walked to the front wing and put one hand on it. The paint was beaded with mist. His stomach came up all at once and he bent over the hard shoulder, coughing coffee and acid onto the black grit. The traffic noise did not alter for him. Nothing on the road ever did.

When he wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, he saw that the fingers were shaking so badly he could not line them up with each other.

He sat on the barrier because standing made the tarmac tilt. The guardrail was wet through the seat of his trousers within seconds. Cars passed so near he could feel the air rake his coat against his spine. He kept the lighter in his fist. It warmed there and stayed a piece of old weather.

Noor's car arrived eight minutes later, maybe nine. Beni saw the black Mercedes in the mirror first, Mercer plate bracket, low dipped lights, then the clean deliberate move into the shoulder behind him. Noor did not hurry in the panicked way some men did around roadside trouble. He got out with the driver door half shielding him from the lane, shut it with his hip, looked once up the traffic stream and once back, then came forward. His suit was the same depot black as Beni's, only older in the cloth, the cuffs polished shiny where they rubbed the wheel all day. He had a dented green thermos in one hand because he said motorway coffee tasted of pennies and refused to pay for it anymore.

"Did it hit you?" he said.

Beni shook his head.

"Good. Means I get to be irritated, not busy."

That was Noor's one joke. After it he went practical at once. He crouched to Beni's eye level, not touching him yet. "Look at me."

Beni did.

"Head on the wheel?"

"No."

"Chest?"

"No."

"You blacked out?"

"No."

Noor took that in and looked past him to the car, the tyres, the straight line of the bumper, then back at Beni's hands around the lighter. "All right," he said. "Stand up."

"I can drive."

"No, you can't."

"The car's fine."

"I'm thrilled for the car." Noor straightened. "Stand up anyway."

Beni did because refusing would have required more balance than he had. Noor took the thermos cap off and poured tea into it, dark and sweet-smelling with cardamom. His mother sent him to work with it the way other mothers sent children with scarves, whether they wanted them or not. He held the cap out.

"Drink."

Beni took it. The tea burned his mouth because he swallowed too fast. It tasted of milk cooked a little too long and sugar not quite dissolved. It was so ordinary he nearly cried from it, which was intolerable, so he did not.

Noor watched him swallow. "Now tell me true. If I put you back in that seat this second, what happens?"

The open driver door waited there, wet leather darkened by rain, the wheel bare and ready for his hands. In his head the white trailer still moved toward him with patient certainty. Under that, older and worse, another road opened under rain, another steering wheel, Klara's voice in his ear and the stupid fond way she said his name when she wanted something.

"My hands don't work right," he said.

"Fine. Better answer."

Noor took the route slip from the vent, glanced at it. "Mehra, T5. Flight landed ten early, because of course it did." He looked back at Beni. "Can you sit in the passenger seat without vomiting on the dash?"

"Probably."

"Brilliant. That's all I need from you for now."

He moved around Beni's car with quick exact motions, checking for damage, kicking lightly at a front tyre, wiping road spray from the side mirror with the side of his hand. Then he got into the driver's seat as if it were the simplest thing in the world to take over another man's wheel. Beni stayed where he was.

Noor leaned across and pushed the passenger door open. "Come on, then. Unless you want Highways to stop and ask why you're sitting on the barrier looking haunted."

Beni got in. The interior was still warm from his body and that felt obscene. He held the thermos cap empty in one hand and the lighter in the other until Noor looked down at both and said, "Choose a relic." Beni put the lighter back in his pocket and passed over the cap.

Noor settled the seat in three movements, adjusted the mirrors though Beni had already had them right, and pulled out with a cleaner line than Beni had managed even on his best mornings. Traffic folded around them. Noor drove as if the road had personally offended him years ago and they had since reached a grudging understanding.

Beni kept seeing the lorry come at them in the wing mirror though it was long gone. He sat too straight. His own knees looked borrowed. The name board for Mehra slid a little on the passenger floor mat with each bend and tapped the plastic trim like a metronome.

"Talk to dispatch?" Noor said.

"I did."

"What'd you tell them?"

"Lane incident. No damage."

"Good." Noor checked the mirror, indicator on, off. "They know I'm on it now. I told them you'd had a shock and I was taking the pickup."

Beni turned his head. "Why?"

"Because it's true."

"They'll mark it."

"Everything gets marked. That isn't the interesting part."

They ran the last stretch toward Heathrow in a silence that was not empty. Noor kept one hand loose on the wheel and the other near the indicator stem. Beni tried to make himself useful by watching the road signs and the lane arrows, but each overhead gantry broke into two images for a second before it settled. He gave up and watched Noor's cuff move over the wheel, the shiny cloth catching the grey light each time the car passed under a lamp.

At Terminal 5 short stay, Noor parked on level three between a Volvo estate with two child seats and a black Range Rover that had not seen real dirt in its life. The concrete smelled of damp tyres, cleaning fluid, and the fry oil from somewhere below. Every few seconds a suitcase rattled over the painted lines and someone swore in three languages at once. Planes climbed overhead so low the ceiling seemed to feel it.

Noor cut the engine but left the key in. "You need air, or do you need to stay in here a minute?"

"I don't know."

"Fine. Stay confused in here, then."

He went to arrivals with the white name board, shoulders broad under the thin work coat, thermos tucked under one arm like it belonged in every part of his life. Beni watched him until the automatic doors swallowed him.

The stillness after driving was worse for a minute. The concrete pillar outside held the number C38, and Beni fixed on that until it stopped breaking apart. Around him, Heathrow morning happened without asking him to join it. A toddler wailed. Wheels hit an expansion strip over and over. Somewhere nearby a man laughed the kind of laugh rich men learned in clubs, all mouth and no fatigue. Beni put both palms flat on his thighs and felt the damp cloth cooling there.

When he shut his eyes, he was back in Klara's car so hard it hurt.

It was a silver Opel Astra with biscuit crumbs in the back from her daughter and a cracked saint medal glued to the dash because her mother thought every vehicle needed watching over. A town-mother car built for groceries, school runs, and bad weather, which made the secret life inside it feel cheaper and meaner. The heating only worked on the highest setting, so the windscreen always smelled faintly of hot dust. Klara kept lipstick in the side pocket, cheap red that bled into the little lines around her mouth by evening, and a silver lighter in the cup holder because she was forever losing the disposable ones.

She had been thirty-four, maybe thirty-five. To Beni at seventeen that was nearly another species. She had a husband who did not look at her when he spoke and a girl old enough to leave hair clips under the front seat and ask why Mummy was wearing perfume on a Tuesday. Klara laughed too loudly in small shops. She wore blouses that were one step better than the town required and boots with thin heels that sank into mud outside the school gate. She was never only one thing at a time; she could be maternal and mean, warm and showing off, greedy for touch in the way hungry people were greedy for bread, without apology.

She had liked that Beni listened. At first that was all. He carried crates for a neighbour one Saturday, then he was at her kitchen table because she had given him coffee while the rain passed, then she was touching his wrist to make a joke last longer than the joke needed. By winter she was collecting him in the Astra on roads nobody used after dark. The booster seat stayed in the back under his jacket. Once one of the child's glitter hair clips stuck in the sole of his shoe and came home with him. He kept that too for three weeks before throwing it away.

The day of the crash had been April and filthy with rain, the kind that turned every verge in eastern Hungary to black soup. Klara had told him the children were with her mother. She had driven them to a day-rate room above a bowling bar on the edge of Debrecen because, she said, nobody there cared who was ruining themselves. The room smelled of bleach, old smoke, and the fryer downstairs. She had laughed at the bedspread and then fucked him with the laugh still in her throat, one hand over his mouth because the wall was thin and because that made it hotter for her. Afterward she sat on the sink with her slip around her waist, smoking out the little vent window, and told him she couldn't leave her husband before summer because schools did not break for desire.

Beni had been angry before they even got back to the car. It was the sour, humiliated anger of a boy who still needed the woman he wanted to blame. She kept touching him while she talked, as if touch settled arguments. She had taken two swallows from a bottle of pear palinka she kept in her bag, said her eyes were tired, and dropped the keys into his hand. He had loved that weight too much. He had felt chosen. He had felt trusted. He had got behind the wheel like it meant anything good.

It was a two-lane road outside town, nothing dramatic, fields flat on both sides, the kind of road you could drive half asleep and still talk yourself into thinking you were alive. Rain hit so hard the wipers lost and won and lost again. Klara had kicked off one heel and tucked one leg under herself on the seat. Her belt was on at first. Then she unhooked it because it cut across her blouse and because she wanted to turn toward him properly. He told her to put it back. She laughed and said he sounded older in a car.

They were fighting about London.

She did not mean real London. She meant the version of it she had started saying when she wanted to make the affair feel bigger than hotel sheets and farm roads. She had a cousin in Luton she had not seen in six years. She said they could go there. She said people left every day and the world did not close behind them. She said he was wasted in that town, in that body, in that frightened polite way he had of accepting crumbs like they were wages.

He drove faster because he wanted the fight over and because speed made him feel less like a boy she could put down and pick up again. The road markings were drowned half the time. Water sat on the tarmac in black plates. She kept talking. She kept leaning toward him, her hand on his thigh, then on the wheel for one stupid teasing second, then off again. He told her not to do that. She laughed at the wrong moment, exactly as a truck came through the rain ahead, its headlights high and yellow and impossibly close.

The wrong lane was his.

That was the fact. It stayed that way no matter how often memory tried to drag it loose from him.

He had drifted over while looking at her. He had not looked long, but he had looked long enough.

He jerked the wheel left. The rear of the Astra let go at once on the wet paint. The car slewed, caught, slewed harder. Klara's cigarette flew from her hand and burned a black dot into the seat before the world hit white. The passenger-side airbag burst with a sound like a fist through cloth. Something struck Beni above the brow so hard he saw blue. The car spun off the road and took the ditch sideways with the passenger side first. The tree was thin, not old, almost an insult, but it punched through the door skin as if the car were cardboard.

When the motion stopped, the wipers were still going.

That was the first thing he understood. The wipers kept going, smear, slap, smear. The engine ticked like it wanted another try. The chemical stench of the airbags was thick enough to chew. His own belt had him pinned. His left hand was under him and numb. Klara was making a trapped, animal sound, all air snagging in a body that no longer knew how to use it.

Blood had run from her nose into the lipstick and turned the whole lower half of her face dark and sloppy. The airbag had gone off, but the tree had taken the door and half the space where her ribs should have been. She kept trying to lift her right hand and could not. He said her name. He thinks he said it. He must have. Her eyes found him once with full furious clarity, as if this were still a thing she could blame on him properly. Then they slipped.

He had to kick his own door twice before it opened.

Rain got in at once, cold and clean and useless. He leaned back over her from outside because the passenger side was crushed into the ditch. He told her he was there. He told her the ambulance was coming when he had not called it yet. He called with fingers that kept missing the screen. There was mud on his tongue. A lorry had stopped. Somebody shouted. Everything happened and did not happen. By the time they cut the passenger side wider, she had pissed herself and gone heavy in the face. One of the paramedics looked at him the way people look at boys who have done exactly what everyone warned them about.

In the parking gravel behind the short-stay bay, someone knocked on Noor's window and Beni flinched so hard his knee hit the glove box.

Noor was back. He opened the driver's door and slid in with the smell of rain and airport air on him. "Flight's delayed at the gate. Ten minutes." He set the name board down and looked once at Beni's face. "All right. Now tell me what actually happened on the motorway."

Beni did not answer.

Noor leaned back and let the silence sit, his face plain with attention. "Don't give me this 'I'm all right' shit again. I watched you try to open the passenger door with the thermos cap in your hand because you forgot what hand a door uses. You're not embarrassed enough to lie well."

Beni stared at the windscreen. On the concrete pillar opposite, a child had stuck half a dinosaur sticker under the parking instructions. Only the green tail remained. It shook slightly each time a car passed.

"I crashed once," he said.

"Yeah. I got that far by myself."

"In Hungary."

"Who was in the car?"

Beni swallowed. "A woman."

Noor waited.

"Klara Fenyves," Beni said. Saying the surname in London felt indecent, too whole. "She was married."

Noor let out a breath through his nose. "And?"

"And I was seventeen."

That turned Noor's head, not dramatically, just enough. "Right."

"It was... it wasn't one time." Beni rubbed the scarred corner of the lighter through his pocket lining. "Months."

"Did she die there, or later?"

"There. Maybe on the road. Maybe before the ambulance. I don't know exactly."

He heard himself keep going because the cleaned-up version was suddenly more shameful than the rest.

"I looked at her instead of the road," he said. "Crossed the line. Put her side into a tree."

Noor took that in the same way he took bad traffic reports: quickly, with no appetite for theatre. "Was it your fault?"

He turned to Noor then. "Yes."

"You want a drink?"

"Not me."

"What'd the police say?"

"Wet road. Bad visibility. Young driver. Her belt off did the rest." The words came out with a filth he could taste. "Nobody wanted the married-woman part written down. Her husband least of all."

Noor's jaw moved once. "And every time a lorry comes sideways, you go back there."

"Not every time."

"Today."

"Today." Beni kept his eyes on the green tail of the sticker. "And today the car was empty. Next time it could be a client asleep in the back. It could be Bea after school. I might already be gone before I know it."

Noor reached for the thermos, found it empty, and set it down again. "Does anyone here know?"

"No."

"Does your family know?"

"At home, yes. People knew. Afterward everyone knew soon enough. Only before."

"At Mercer?"

"No."

"If they mark me shaky, I'm off the Ashdown work first," Beni said. "Then the decent shifts. Maybe all of it."

"A girl in Belgravia?"

Beni's head came up too fast.

Noor snorted, not amused. "Relax. I didn't ask which one. I asked whether anyone in this city knows what happens to you behind the wheel."

"No."

For the first time Noor looked genuinely angry, though he kept his voice level. "That's stupid, mate."

Beni had no defense that did not sound like a child protecting a hiding place. It was money, work, shame, and the fact that if he named it, it became something other men could decide against him. He looked back at the green half-sticker on the pillar.

"I know," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yes."

Noor turned in the seat to face him more fully. "No, hear me properly. You can't have a thing like that living in your hands and then just hope it'll be polite forever. This isn't some office panic where you shut the toilet door and wait it out. You're doing seventy in a two-ton car for money."

Beni felt the words land because Noor had stripped them of pity. That helped. Pity would have made him lie. This made him listen.

"I didn't think..." he started, then stopped because the sentence was too stupid to finish.

"Exactly," Noor said. "You didn't think because thinking would've meant telling someone and maybe losing work. So you did what everybody does when they're skint and ashamed. You prayed the problem would stay private."

Beni laughed once without humour. "You're making it sound very ordinary."

"It's ordinary. That's why it kills people."

They sat with that. A plane roared low enough overhead to tremble the mirror glass. Somewhere down the row, a boot slammed and a woman said into her phone that she had told him twice already and was not repeating herself.

Beni took the lighter from his pocket and turned it in his palm. The silver had gone dull except where his thumb had polished one side over time. Noor looked at it.

"That's hers?"

Beni nodded.

"You smoke now?"

"No."

"Then why carry it?"

Beni watched the blunt scar at one corner catch the car park light. "I took it from the cup holder after."

Noor waited again.

"They were cutting the door," Beni said. "There was glass everywhere. Mud. Her bag had split open. Lipstick, tissues, a little packet of raisins for the child, all in the water. The lighter was there by the handbrake. I took it." He shrugged and hated the shrug. "I didn't want to leave with nothing. Which is disgusting."

"It's not disgusting."

"It is."

"No. It's strange. That's different." Noor rubbed his thumb over the seam in the steering wheel. "People do odd things when the world breaks in front of them. My uncle kept the watch from his brother's body for twenty years and never wore it once. Thought if he polished it enough he'd eventually understand why it was still ticking when the man wasn't. Humans are trash at grief. That's not news."

Beni looked down at the lighter. "I didn't even do it like a normal person."

"Who gives a shit about normal?"

"Her daughter was at the funeral."

That shut Noor up for a second, not because he had run out of words but because he knew some facts were heavy enough to deserve the pause. "All right," he said finally. "That's bad."

"She had the same hair clips." Beni heard how crazed it sounded and kept going because stopping now would have been cowardice. "The ones from under the seat. Pink plastic stars. I saw them in the girl's hair and thought, I know those. Like that was the worst thing. Not the coffin. The stupid hair clips."

Noor stared through the windscreen. "Brains are cheap little bastards."

Beni smiled without wanting to. It vanished at once.

"Why tell me now?" Noor said. "Why not two months ago, or never?"

The concrete, the painted arrows, the people dragging hand luggage toward money and return held him for a second. "Because the lorry moved and I wasn't here anymore."

"Fair."

"And because if I'd been with a client..." He stopped.

"You weren't."

"No."

"But you could be next time."

Beni nodded.

Noor drummed his fingers once on the wheel, three quick taps, deciding something. "Here's what happens. I finish this job. You sit there and don't pretend you're helping more than you're helping. After that I take you to the depot, I tell them the truth they need and not the truth they don't. Near miss, driver shaken, shift covered. Tomorrow you're off. If they complain, they complain to me."

"I can't lose the day."

"You already lost it."

"Noor."

"No." Noor's voice sharpened, not loud. "Don't start counting pennies at me while you're still smelling ghosts. You get one day. Maybe that's not medicine, but it's better than putting you back on the M25 and seeing which memory wins."

Beni closed his hand around the lighter again. "I never told anyone here."

"You just did."

The radio crackled with dispatch asking status on Mehra. Noor picked it up before Beni could move.

"Client delayed at gate," Noor said. "We're in position."

Beni heard the word we're and looked away.

When Mehra finally came through arrivals, he was not the clean hard businessman Beni had imagined from the booking notes. He was a tired man in a navy quilted jacket with one shoelace untied, dragging a carry-on by the side handle because the telescopic grip had jammed. A paper conference wristband still circled his right wrist, and under his other arm he carried a duty-free Toblerone big enough to make a child believe in him for at least an hour. Noor took the bag before the man could apologise for its wheel and got him settled in the back with his water and the heater turned one notch up.

Mehra smelled of plane air, wool, and stale deodorant. He asked only once if traffic was all right into town, then looked at his phone and shut his eyes before they reached the spirals out of the terminal. Noor drove. Beni sat in the passenger seat with the empty name board at his feet and watched London rebuild itself lane by lane out of the wet morning.

Nobody spoke for most of the return.

That suited the client. It suited Noor too. Noor's kindness lived in practical things. Once, by Hammersmith, he reached over without looking and nudged the thermos against Beni's knee. There was another half cup in it somehow, sweet and lukewarm now. Beni drank it anyway.

At Pembridge Villas, Mehra woke in the back with the blind daze of a man who had slept in four-minute bursts across an ocean. He thanked Noor, then thanked Beni too, because he had no reason not to believe both men had done the same work. He wrestled the dead-wheeled carry-on onto the pavement, tucked the giant Toblerone under his chin, and went into a white stucco house without seeing either of them clearly.

Noor waited until the door shut. Then he pulled away.

"See?" he said.

"What?"

"World didn't end because somebody else drove."

Beni looked out at the rain-dark railings and the clipped little trees in square grates. "Not for you."

"Don't be precious."

That made Beni laugh properly, once. It hurt the back of his throat and felt better than not laughing.

By the time they reached the depot, the sky had lifted into that flat London grey that never became day so much as a brighter kind of damp. Vans idled by the shutters. Somebody had dropped a sausage roll wrapper in the yard and it was plastered to the concrete by rain. Noor parked, cut the engine, and turned to Beni.

"Tomorrow," he said. "Off."

"All right."

"If you get a flash like that again before then, you don't get heroic. You pull over and you call. Me, dispatch, whoever. I don't care if it's embarrassing."

"All right."

"And whatever rich-person nonsense you've got yourself tangled in, sort it before it sorts you."

Beni stared at him.

Noor lifted one shoulder. "You don't nearly leave the road over an old dead woman and tell me there's nothing current feeding it. I wasn't born yesterday."

Beni should have denied it. Denial was the habit that paid him. But the part of himself that had once relied on silence had already cracked open in the short-stay car park under the planes. Lying now would not have closed it again. It would only have made him smaller inside it.

"I know," he said.

Noor nodded as if that was enough for today. He had heard the fact, set a boundary around it, and left the rest for later. He picked up the thermos, opened his door, and paused. "And Beni."

"Yeah?"

"Bin that shirt. You smell like old coffee and fear."

He got out before Beni could answer.

Beni stayed in the passenger seat another half minute with the yard beyond the windscreen and the empty wheel to his right. The silver lighter sat in his palm. For two years it had been a private object with a private charge, something he could carry like a rotten tooth under the tongue, touching it alone in other people's houses, at red lights, in bed after Nina, in the Mercer locker room while everyone else talked about routes and Arsenal and fuel receipts. Now Noor knew what it was, whose it had been, and why a lorry moving one lane too far could take the blood from Beni's hands in a second.

Practical trouble still waited where he had left it. Laci still wanted money. Nina still existed. The altered logbook still sat where he had hidden it. Sunday off meant Sunday unpaid. But the story was no longer sealed under his skin. Another man could name it now. Another man had looked at the ugliest fact and held his ground.

Beni closed the lighter, though it had never been open, and put it in his pocket. Then he got out of the car and went inside to sign off a day he had not finished and had somehow still survived.
