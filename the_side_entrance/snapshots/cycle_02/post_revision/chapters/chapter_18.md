# Chapter 18

The Mercer suit hung in its plastic cover behind Laci's work jackets, too good for the room and useless in it now that January had come and Mercer had finished with him. Weeks had passed since he'd walked out of the Ashdowns' mews into the rain with no key and no next instruction. Beni went out at dusk in dark trousers with shine at the knees, Noor's spare fleece over a thin jumper, and the cheap black shoes he had once kept for wet weather. Damp plaster sweated through the walls. Old onion oil from too many nights' frying sat in the curtains. Water had dried in crooked lines on the window and started again with the next rain. Laci sat at the table in thermal socks, rubbing white dust from the seam of his thumbnail with the edge of a Metro receipt.

"Night airport?" Laci asked.

"Yeah."

Laci grunted and shoved over a dented red thermos as if generosity were another chore to get through, some cartoon excavator still visible in the scrape. "Take it. Machine coffee there tastes like burned screws."

Beni looked at him.

"Don't make a face," Laci said. "It's tea. I'm not giving you my rakia."

"I'm not making a face."

"You are. Go. If you miss the last bit of A406 before it clogs, you save twenty minutes."

Laci had never before offered him road advice without first attaching a lecture about rent. Beni unscrewed the thermos cap. Strong black tea hit up out of it with lemon peel and too much sugar. The sweetness made his teeth ache before he even drank it.

"I can give you one-fifty Friday," Beni said.

Laci kept scraping the receipt edge under his nail. "Good."

"That still leaves-"

"I know what it leaves." Laci shrugged into a hoodie dark with rain and powdered with plaster. "I'm not a bank statement."

Beni nodded. It was the nearest thing to mercy he was likely to get. He put the thermos in his backpack and went down the stairs past the bicycles with no front wheels and the pram frame somebody had left by the letterboxes in November and never claimed again.

Noor had texted him an address behind the airport hotels, an industrial strip where coach companies, minicab firms, and catering warehouses lived shoulder to shoulder in units with roller shutters and bad security lights. Beni had done airport runs in Mercer cars often enough to know the roads west, but he had done them from the expensive side of the glass: polished dash, silent cabin, bottle water in the door, passengers who acted as if travel existed to carry their inconvenience in velvet. Grease filmed the puddles by the unit, and every passing van pushed diesel and soaked-cardboard stink against the shutters. A blue sign above the office window said SKYLINE AIRPORT CARS in letters two shades brighter than the building deserved.

Noor was outside with a paper cup of tea, still in his Mercer suit. The cuffs had gone shiny from years of steering wheels and door handles. His tie was loose, coat open, eyes tired and alert in the same practical way Beni had always trusted.

"There he is," Noor said. "You look like a sixth-former going to court."

Beni glanced at the fleece. "It's your fault. This is your jacket."

"Then wear it with more confidence." Noor knocked his knuckles against the office glass. "Come on. Nita's in a mood already."

Inside, the office was half the size of the Ashdown pantry. A printer coughed on a shelf by the kettle. Three radios hissed at once. The heater by the wall made more noise than warmth. The woman behind the desk had a chipped navy manicure and one strip of New Year's glitter left stubbornly on her ring fingernail. She was maybe forty, hair pinned up with a pencil, reading glasses hanging on a beaded chain against a sweatshirt that said BRUGES in peeling white letters.

"This is the Hungarian?" she asked Noor.

"He has a name," Noor said.

"So do I, and no one uses it at three in the morning." She held out her hand to Beni. "License."

Her name, according to the badge clipped crooked to her chest, was Nita. Cigarette smoke clung to her cardigan and hair under a losing layer of cheap rose soap. While she copied Beni's details she kept a pile of wrapped mint imperials lined along the desk blotter as if counting them calmed her. Every time the radio cracked too loud she straightened one without looking.

"No points?" she asked.

"No."

"No drama with clients? No shouting back, no driving off, no cleverness?"

Beni shook his head.

"Good. They can be animals. You don't get to be one back." She pushed a laminated airport pickup card across the desk. "You pick up at the lane they give you. Not the lane that seems faster. If police move you, you move. If client says you've kept them waiting half an hour, check the timestamp. They lie from habit. If somebody vomits, bag it and tell me before the smell sets in. I don't negotiate bodily fluids after dawn."

Noor hid a grin in his cup.

Nita looked up at Beni over the top of the glasses now halfway down her nose. "You done airport before?"

"Yes."

"In a nice car?"

"Sometimes."

She snorted. "Forget nice car. This one has heating on one side and a boot that sticks if you flatter it too much."

The key fob had a cracked plastic tag marked 14. Outside, bay fourteen held a silver Skoda Octavia with one hubcap missing, a pine-tree air freshener brown with age, and a driver's seat rubbed shiny at the edges. The dashboard phone mount had been reinforced with black tape. There were no bottled waters, no tissues folded into hotel triangles, no smell of leather polish. Somebody had once burned the passenger seat with a cigarette and dabbed at it with superglue. The car started on the second turn and settled into a tired diesel thrum that came up through the steering wheel.

Noor leaned through the open window before Beni pulled out. "Listen."

Beni waited.

"Do the shift. Don't try to prove anything on the first night. If it's shit, it is shit. You still do the shift."

Beni nodded.

Noor tapped the door twice. "And don't go silent if the airport roads get in your head. Ring me. I mean it."

He meant the lorry. He meant the white line bending wrong under sodium light and the hard shoulder and the airbag memory coming up hot and chemical in Beni's throat. Beni didn't answer because the answer was already in the job. Rent did not care what roads got in his head. He put the car in gear and followed the arrows toward the holding area.

The first fare was a man from Terminal 5 to a hotel near Paddington. He came out in a waxed jacket with a golf bag, talking into a headset about severance packages as if he were bored by the cost of ruining other people's week. His suitcase wheel was wrapped in silver tape. When Beni got out to help with the bags, the man kept talking and let the handle drop so the full weight of it yanked through Beni's shoulder.

"Can you not?" the man said, not to Beni, into the headset. Then to Beni, as if changing channels. "Bit sharper, mate. I'm frozen."

He got in without a hello. Two minutes into the drive he said the car smelled of chips. Five minutes after that he demanded a charger Beni did not have. When they hit roadworks near the flyover he sighed theatrically and said, "Of course," like Beni had arranged the cones himself.

At the hotel awning he left a damp half-moon of mud on the rear mat and went inside still discussing staff reductions. He forgot a black leather glove on the seat. Beni picked it up, soft and expensive, warmer than the car. For a second he thought of putting it in the glove box and letting it go to lost property in the morning. Instead he ran after the man through the revolving door and handed it over. The man took it without breaking his sentence or his pace.

Back in the Skoda, Beni sat with the door shut and the engine idling. The heater blew lukewarm air at his right knee and nothing at his left. On the Mercer app a ride like that would have carried polished silence, a hotel doorman, a tip if the client was drunk enough to confuse gratitude with largesse. Here the meter history lived on a scratched company phone and the job was done when Nita's screen said done. He liked, with an irritation that felt almost shameful, how clean that was. Nobody had asked him to erase a stop or forget a face. Nobody cared what he knew so long as he found the terminal and kept the car moving.

The next hour brought a family with two sleeping boys and a pink hard-shell suitcase plastered with cartoon horse stickers. The mother apologized for the crumbs before the crumbs existed. The father smelled of airport coffee and panic. They were going to an aunt in Harrow because the cheaper hotel had cancelled on them and one boy had started coughing on the plane. The younger child woke once, sat straight up, and said, "Not the fox house," then fell back asleep with his cheek against the car-seat strap. The parents laughed a thin, tired laugh and the mother explained in a whisper that the child had become convinced all London foxes lived in one particular brick house near their aunt's bus stop.

When Beni lifted the suitcase out, the father tried to help and knocked his own shin hard on the curb. He cursed softly, laughed at himself, and paid exact fare plus one pound in coins from a pocket full of receipts. "For the patience," he said. It was one pound in coins, and still more grace than the man with the glove had managed.

Near midnight the radio sent Beni back west empty. Rain had thinned to a shine on the road, enough to hold every lamp in a trembling line. The airport signs began again: Terminal 2, Terminal 3, Cargo, Short Stay, Long Stay. The slip road widened under sodium orange, the white lane markings curving away with that same indifferent geometry that had once broken him open on the hard shoulder. His hands tightened around the wheel before he realized they had. The cheap fleece stuck damp at the back of his neck.

A lorry came up fast on the right, spray needling across the windscreen. For a moment Beni could smell the old airbag powder, bitter and intimate as a tooth drilled at the gum. He could see Klara's red lipstick on the overturned cigarette pack between the seats, wet road in Hungary, her laugh cut off so fast it had left part of itself hanging in the car. Then the lorry passed. It was only a lorry. The Skoda stayed true. The wipers beat a flat metronome over the glass.

He eased off the accelerator by three miles an hour, no more, and kept going.

At one in the morning he picked up a woman from arrivals with a lilac suitcase zipped overfull and tied round once with a luggage strap from another airline. She wore nurse shoes with the backs crushed down and held supermarket roses upside down by the stems, petals bruising against her sleeve.

"North Finchley," she said. Her voice had the slack edge of somebody whose feet had given up two hours earlier.

When Beni pulled away she caught him glancing at the flowers and smiled without embarrassment. "For my mum. She says they die slower if you buy them at the airport because they've already been through customs."

"Is that true?"

"No idea. She reads nonsense on Facebook and believes it harder if the spelling is bad."

By the time he reached North Finchley she was asleep against the window, leaving a round fog mark in the glass. He woke her gently. She fumbled in her bag for the fare, then for a second smaller purse, then for one wrapped cough sweet she pressed into his palm as if that were the proper balance of accounts. "For the road," she said. "Cherry."

He drove back south with the sweet on the dashboard untouched. The night's meanness had not improved, but it had widened. There were people in it who were simply exhausted, people whose tempers came from cancelled flights and sick children and two jobs stitched together without enough sleep in between. The old Mercer world had trained him to think humiliation travelled downward in a smooth chauffeur line: client to driver, rich to poor, silence to the man paid to keep it. Airport nights were messier. Everybody arrived wrung out. Everybody wanted someone else to absorb the spill.

At half two he sat in the holding lane and drank Laci's tea, now metallic with the thermos and still too sweet. The car park lights made every puddle look skinned. Drivers smoked under the shelter and argued in bursts of Punjabi, Polish, Arabic, English, then laughed because the argument had never been about language in the first place. On the company phone Nita sent him the week's terms again, as if reminding the men in cars would make the number larger: full rota, no missed shifts, four hundred and thirty before the tax the company pretended was a favor.

The number came to four hundred and thirty, ninety less than Mercer wages on a good week and far below what Nina could slide across a seat without folding it first. It mattered. It wasn't going to ease anything quickly.

He counted it anyway against what remained of the debt to Laci, against travel, against food, against the new shape of a life that did not include cash envelopes or Belgravia tips or the sick thrill of being summoned by name by a woman in the back seat. The number held. Barely, but it held.

Just before dawn, when the sky over the perimeter road went from black to the color of wet cement, his phone buzzed in the cup holder. It was not dispatch. It was a text from Eszter.

Can you carry boxes and keep your mouth useful? New place. Ten. If you're dead, say so.

He read it twice, then once more, because the screen was greasy with his thumb and he did not trust the first meaning when it came from her. There had been Bea's emergency, the gala, the half hour outside the church-basement room when Eszter had let him know Bea was asleep and alive and nothing else. There had been silence since then, flat and shut as an unlit room, giving him nothing to answer and nowhere to put his hands.

He typed, I'm not dead. Off at eight. Where?

The address came back in under a minute, a road in the north again, not far from the retail park where Beni sometimes bought socks because they were cheaper there after nine. Then another message.

Bring screws if you have them. The landlord thinks one loose hinge is a personality.

By eight fifteen damp wool, airport coffee, and the cherry cough sweet he still had not eaten had worked themselves into the Skoda's fabric. Beni handed the keys back to Nita, who inspected the fuel receipt like a priest checking a confession.

"All right?" she asked.

"Yes."

She looked at him longer than the answer deserved. "You still have both eyes. Good start."

Outside, Noor was leaning against his own car with a bacon roll wrapped in paper gone translucent with grease. He had waited though Beni had not asked him to.

"Well?" Noor said.

"It's bad."

"Obviously."

"The heater works only on one leg."

Noor laughed. "And?"

Beni thought of the printed jobs, the ugly little office, the roads west that had not killed him. "And it's work."

"Yeah." Noor took a bite, chewed, swallowed. "That's the trick of it. Shit work is still work."

Beni told him about the text from Eszter.

Noor's expression changed by a degree, no more. "You going?"

"Yes."

"Then go tired and honest. Tired you can manage. Honest is the hard part."

Beni looked at the roll in Noor's hand. "You wait here every morning for everybody's love life?"

"No. Just yours. It's cheaper than television."

The joke pulled at one corner of Beni's mouth before tiredness dragged it flat again. Noor caught the flicker and left it alone. He only stepped forward and squeezed Beni's shoulder once, a brief driver-to-driver grip, then pointed west with the bacon roll. "Sleep later. Don't say anything stupid because you think it sounds noble."

The new place was above a shuttered barber shop on a side street where the buses hissed every four minutes and the front doors all needed paint. The carpet gave underfoot with a damp chill, and the stairwell still held the barber's talc, old hair tonic, and the hot-metal ghost of clippers cooling below the shutter. Eszter had propped the building door with a sack of potatoes and was waiting at the foot of the stairs in a grey sweatshirt, hair tied up in a green elastic that looked ready to snap. Her forearms were reddened from cold and carrying. There was a smear of white paint high on one sleeve.

"You look terrible," she said.

"I came from work."

"Then work is ugly. Take that end."

She pointed at a flat-pack wardrobe box so long it nearly blocked the hall. Her mouth stayed hard but not shut. For now, that was more than Beni had expected.

They carried the box up two flights sideways because the turn on the landing was too tight. The new flat was one room, a kitchenette behind a half wall, and a bathroom so narrow the sink sat crooked over the toilet cistern as if the plumber had solved the problem by surrendering. Fresh white paint still shone wet on the skirting and had already bubbled over an older nicotine line by the window catch. The radiator clicked but gave only a shallow warmth. A paper calendar was already clipped to the wall by the kitchenette, spring dates circled hard enough to score the plaster. On the floor against the wall stood two saucepans, a kettle, three books in Hungarian, a box of winter clothes, and a rolled child mattress tied with string. Leaning beside it was a small white chest of drawers with a cartoon frog sticker still on the top drawer, one eye peeled away.

Beni set down his end of the wardrobe and looked at the mattress.

Eszter caught the look. "Yes, for Lili. Don't get sentimental. It's thinner than prison bread."

"Does it fit?"

"If she doesn't grow four inches by April, yes."

Below it, a shopping list lay on the counter in two languages, half food and half repair: child pillow, pan with lid, curtain hooks, school-holiday dates, airport fare.

"Where do you want this?" Beni asked.

She pointed with the screwdriver in her hand. "Not there. That's the only place where the drawer can open. Here. No, tilt it first. Christ, did the rich people ever let you touch furniture or only cars?"

"Mostly cars."

"That explains your face."

They worked without grace. The wardrobe instructions had been printed by a sadist who believed one smiling line drawing could replace nouns. The screws came in a bag with three missing. The hinges were not the right size. Eszter had already unpacked plates but not cutlery. She moved fast, furious at useless objects in a way that was not really about the objects. Every few minutes she would stop to line up something small and necessary: soap by the sink, the three mugs on the shelf, Lili's plastic yellow bowl in the only cupboard that did not smell faintly of bleach.

Beni found the loose hinge from her text. The screws had stripped the wood clean. He packed the holes with snapped matchsticks from the stove box and drove in longer screws he had brought from Laci's tools. The door held.

Eszter watched him test it twice. "Good. The landlord kept saying he'd come tomorrow. Tomorrow from men like that means after the ceiling falls."

"What does he want for this?"

She named the rent. It was low by London standards and obscene by any honest measure of the room.

Beni looked around once more. "He gets that for this?"

"He gets it because I need an address where my daughter can sleep without the church ladies listening for sin through the radiator."

That shut him up. Eszter bent to cut open another box. When she straightened she winced and pressed a fist into the small of her back.

"How much did you carry before I came?" he asked.

"Enough to hate the second-hand market forever."

"What else is downstairs?"

"Table. Two chairs. One bag of pans from the Hungarian woman on the next road. She gave me extra jars because her son moved to Bristol and now apparently Bristol means he only eats quinoa."

They went down again. The table was small enough for a kitchen and too big for the room. One chair had been painted dark blue, the other left raw wood, as if the set had split in a divorce and reassembled under protest. As they maneuvered it up the stairs, a door on the first landing opened and an elderly man in a vest looked out with a tabby cat over his shoulder. The cat wore a red collar with a bell that had been wrapped in electrician's tape to silence it.

"Mind the wall," the man said. "Landlord'll blame all of us if you scrape it."

"We're minding it," Eszter said.

The man looked at Beni, at the table, at Eszter again. "That chair's missing a screw in the back. I meant to tell you."

"Of course it is," Eszter said.

He shrugged, not offended. "Cat sat on the bag. I forgot." Then he retreated, the taped bell making a dull useless tap against the cat's chest.

Beni got the table in with less damage than it deserved. When he turned, one side of Eszter's mouth had lifted before she bit it back. That quick rise in her face did more than a thank-you.

"You found a strict palace," he said.

"It has a door that locks from inside and a bathroom that is only mine. Luxury."

He wiped his hands on his trousers. Sawdust, dust, and old paint coated his palms. Rich houses bred a cleaner kind of dirt, polished over before it became shame. This was honest mess, or at least mess that admitted its own source.

"Have you eaten?" Eszter asked.

"Not since midnight."

"Then cut that cabbage."

She set a cheap metal pan on the hob and handed him a knife that dragged a little on the chopping board. The kitchen corner was barely large enough for two, so they worked by passing behind each other sideways, hip to drawer, shoulder to wall. She fried onions in oil, added cabbage, caraway, noodles, half a sausage cut mean and thin. It was poor food and smelled good almost at once. The window fogged from the steam and from the buses breathing outside.

He watched her reach automatically for a fourth plate and correct herself to two. She saw him notice and did not apologize.

"I bought the yellow bowl yesterday," she said, nodding toward the cupboard. "She hates eating from white plates. Says white plates make everything look like dentist food."

"How old is she now?"

"Six and a half. Which is an age designed by the devil. Too big for carrying, too small for sense."

Beni smiled despite being tired enough for his skin to feel full of grit.

"Don't make that face," Eszter said, stirring. "You haven't met six and a half in wet socks because somebody said no to ice cream."

"I've met airport passengers."

"Same height?"

He laughed once, quietly. The sound startled him by existing at all. Eszter heard that too. She turned the heat down and let the pan sit. The room grew fuller with the smell of cooked cabbage, cheap sausage, and damp wool drying slowly on both of them.

They ate at the table before it was fully stable, one leg propped with the folded property section of a Metro. Eszter had not unpacked proper napkins, so they used paper towels from the roll. Outside, the betting-shop numbers kept washing the thin curtain she had clipped in place with laundry pegs.

For a few minutes they spoke only about the flat: which cupboard did not shut, which bus stop was nearest, whether the boiler noise meant danger or only character. It felt almost easy until easy itself became suspicious.

"Bea called me after the gala," Eszter said at last.

Beni put down his fork. "All right."

"She wanted to know if you'd been thrown out into the snow like in some Victorian book."

"There wasn't snow."

"I noticed." Eszter ate another mouthful. "She said her father did not shout. That was worse. She said Nina looked like she might either break a glass or swallow one."

Beni looked at the wobbling table leg. "That sounds right."

"She also said you gave him the book and the cash."

"I did."

Eszter nodded once. There was no forgiveness in it, only receipt of information. "Good."

He waited. When she did not add to it, he said, "I should've done it sooner."

"Yes."

"You don't have to keep agreeing."

"Why not? It saves time."

The directness should have stung. Instead it eased something in him. There was no edge of performance to fight through, no hope disguised as punishment, no invitation to make a better speech. He had earned the simple truth of yes.

He ate another bite because the noodles were there and because she had cooked them. He did not look at her until he could do it without asking for more.

"Thank you for letting me help," he said.

Eszter wiped her fork clean with bread. "I let you carry furniture. Don't write a poem."

He almost answered with something safer, smaller. Tiredness made lies look efficient. It also made them harder to lift. He heard Noor again: don't say anything stupid because you think it sounds noble.

"I didn't come for points," he said.

Eszter raised an eyebrow. "Good, because I don't have any."

He breathed through the urge to stop there. The old instinct was to deliver the acceptable line, the one that sounded ashamed enough to pass inspection, that he was sorry, that he had been weak, that he had made a mess. Those generic things cost him less than the precise version.

"It wasn't only the money," he said.

She did not move. Even her fork stayed in her hand the same way. He knew that stillness from kitchens and arguments and beds. It meant continue or shut up forever.

"It mattered," he said. "The money. Your rent. Mine. All of it. But it wasn't only that." He looked at the yellow bowl in the cupboard because it was easier than looking at her while he made himself ugly on purpose. "When she started asking for me, when dispatch sent me because she said my name, I liked it. I liked being the one called. Not the driver in the pool. Not the spare. Me. The one she sent round to the side door instead of the front."

Eszter's face did not soften. That helped.

"I know how that sounds," he said.

"No," she said. "You know how nice lies sound. Keep going."

He let out air through his nose. The room was too warm at the table and too cold by the window. Cabbage grease and metal from the stripped screw heads filmed his hands.

"I liked the back way into that house," he said. "The mews, the side door, the leather in the back seat creaking while the engine ticked hot and she climbed over it with her heel catching in the rubber mat. I liked that she did it there, in the car I was supposed to keep clean, because it made me feel chosen and filthy at the same time. Like I had a trick. Like I could be invisible and special at the same time. It was rotten. It still felt good."

Eszter drank water before she answered. "Yes. That's closer. Not practical. Not useful. You liked her climbing over the seat in the hidden lane and making the car part of it. You liked being chosen where nobody respectable had to see it."

He looked up. "That's all?"

"What do you want, Beni? A certificate?" She shook her head. "No. But that's true. And it's more useful to me than hearing you say you were helpless. You weren't helpless. You were flattered."

The word landed cleanly. The word was flattered, smaller than desire, uglier than love, harder to romanticize. He could not argue with it without beginning another lie.

"I was," he said.

Eszter pushed her plate back a little. "And the money made it easy to keep telling yourself a story about being practical. Men love practical when it covers appetite. A hidden lane, a hot back seat, and suddenly it's all logistics."

He almost objected at men, because Laci was not like this, Noor was not like this, because general statements always made him itch. Then he remembered Nina's envelopes, his own relief at them, the way he had carried both hunger and self-disgust like they cancelled each other out. They never had.

"I used you to feel clean," he said. The sentence came out flat and unadorned, which made it worse. "Not every minute. But enough."

Eszter's mouth tightened, not in surprise. "Yes."

He stared at his plate. One noodle had stuck to the rim and gone glossy with oil. He thought absurdly of the Ashdown kitchen, white china, the silver warming drawer, how quickly a mess vanished there if somebody was paid enough to see it first.

"I'm sorry," he said.

This time she let the sentence sit.

"I'm not saying it to get back in," he added.

"Good. There isn't a back in."

He nodded. The words hurt less than the space after them. Space was cleaner. You could arrange furniture in space. You could put a child mattress against the wall and know where it ended.

Eszter stood to clear the plates. He stood too on reflex and carried them the three steps to the sink.

"Leave it," she said.

"I can wash."

"You can dry. The hot tap only works if you threaten it first."

They worked shoulder to shoulder again, closer than the room allowed and not close in the old way at all. She washed fast, forearms wet to the elbow. He dried and stacked. The rhythm of it steadied him more than any speech could have. When she passed him the yellow bowl, she dried her thumb on the tea towel before touching it, an unnecessary care that told him exactly how much the object mattered.

"Lili likes foxes?" he asked, because he needed a thing in the world between them.

Eszter snorted. "She likes things that should stay far away and not bite. Foxes, ponies, actors on television. She has decided London is full of all three."

"Will she fly?"

"If the ticket doesn't go mad, yes. My sister might bring her. Or I pay for the assistance service and let some airport woman hate me for paperwork." Eszter set the bowl carefully in the cupboard. "I want spring because winter here is ugly enough for adults. I don't want her first look at London to be these windows and everybody coughing."

Beni thought of the airport roads, the holding lane at two in the morning, the fluorescent arrivals hall with its sticky floors and tired children crying at baggage reclaim. "I can meet you," he said. "If you need. I know the terminals."

Eszter did not answer at once. She wrung out the cloth, hung it over the tap, wiped the counter in three economical strokes. Under stress she arranged things. Her silence had always worked that way, practical before anything else.

"Maybe," she said finally. "If the shift lets you. And if I ask. Don't hear more in it than that."

"I won't."

"And you can text sometimes. About real things. Work. If you're alive. If Bea sets fire to Belgravia. Not at two in the morning because you feel lonely and suddenly remember I speak your language."

"All right."

"That's all I've got." Her voice stayed level. "I can talk to a man who tells me the ugly version. I can't sleep with him because he finally learned to say it."

The plainness of that did more than anger would have done. It left no clean wound to bandage, only a shape to live around. Beni nodded once.

"I know."

"Do you?" She gave a short, tired laugh without humor. "Maybe now."

Her phone buzzed on the windowsill. She glanced at the screen and recognition loosened her face by a degree, as if somebody had reached in and loosened one bolt.

"Lili," she said.

She stayed by the sink and thumbed the voice note open where she stood, letting the child's voice spill between them. She was letting him stand in the room for it. For now that counted as trust.

The child's voice came small and bright through the cheap speaker, Hungarian rushing over itself with the impatience of a six-year-old who believed any pause might lose her the world. Eszter translated the first line automatically under her breath for Beni and then stopped because he was already following.

Mama, Grandma says the rabbit can't come because he smells like soup, but he's coming. Also if London buses have two floors, do foxes use stairs? And I want the bed by the window when I come, not the wall, because I need to see if the moon there is different.

There was a muffled older woman's voice in the background telling her not to shout into the phone. Lili shouted something else anyway, laughter skidding behind it, then the message cut off in a rustle.

Eszter listened to the first part twice more, not because she had missed it but because that was how missing worked. On the second play she laughed once at the rabbit smelling like soup. She rubbed at her mouth immediately after, as if the laugh had escaped some rationing rule.

"She has a rabbit?" Beni asked.

"One ear, no eyes, stuffed with whatever was cheap in 2013." Eszter put the phone down more gently than she had picked it up. "She named him Minister because my ex used to shout at the television."

"Does he smell like soup?"

"He has smelled like everything. Soup would be an improvement."

Beni looked at the rolled mattress, the frog sticker drawer, the window over the bus stop. The room was still too small. The rent was still theft. The boiler still clicked like something thinking mean thoughts in the wall. But the child voice had changed the geometry. The place now had a future weight in it, one pair of small socks and one rotten toy rabbit already occupying air.

Eszter followed his glance. "She'll want the window bed until the first siren. Then she'll want my side and my ribs and half the blanket. She always sleeps with one foot outside like she's testing the room."

The detail went through him more sharply than any plea could have done. He could see that foot outside the blanket. That was the danger of children, even other people's. They became precise too fast.

"What else do you need?" he asked.

She understood the question for what it was and also what it was not. Money hovered around it like steam but he had not said money. Perhaps because he did not have enough to offer. Perhaps because they both knew the old contamination of help.

"A thicker curtain," she said after a moment. "That sign from the betting shop is like living inside a fish tank. Maybe a second saucepan. Maybe a proper pillow. Nothing heroic."

"I can find curtains."

"I know you can. You know routes to sad discount shops better than anyone."

"I know one on the retail park."

"I know. You love a car park more than a human being."

"That's not true."

She gave him a look.

"Not anymore," he said.

That earned him a brief crooked smile, real and gone quickly. "Careful. That almost sounded charming."

He took the rubbish down because it needed taking down. On the landing the old man with the taped cat bell was smoking out the window in slippers. The cat sat on the sill watching buses with the contempt of a landlord.

"Got the chair fixed?" the man asked.

"Not yet."

"I've got screws somewhere. If my daughter hasn't stolen them for art." He squinted at Beni's tired face. "You working nights?"

"Yes."

"Rotten for the stomach." He tapped ash into a mug he had dedicated to the purpose. "My wife did nights thirty years. She still eats breakfast at midnight sometimes. Tell your girl to get thicker curtains. This street does not sleep."

"I will."

When he went back up, Eszter had folded the flat-pack cardboard and leaned it by the door. She had also put half the leftover noodles into a plastic container with a lid that did not quite match. The mismatch felt almost luxurious in its ordinariness.

"For later," she said. "You look hollow."

"I can make my own food."

"I didn't say you can't." She held out the container until he took it. "The spoon goes back to me."

He slipped it into his bag beside the thermos. Laci's sweet tea smell had sunk into everything. For one strange second the bag held his whole present life: borrowed fleece, dented thermos, screws, leftover cabbage noodles, airport card.

At the door he stopped because leaving required an exactness he had once avoided by simply lingering until somebody else defined him. Eszter waited, one hand on the frame.

"If I text," he said, "and you don't answer, I won't keep pushing."

"Good."

"If you need the airport when she comes-"

"I heard you the first time."

He nodded.

She took him in then from the damp hair to the sleepless drag under his eyes, as if checking what remained of the nineteen-year-old under all the service polish and damage. The look tightened something in his chest: care cut down to its hard useful shape, with no forgiveness in it.

"Sleep before you drive tonight if you can," she said. "I'm not having you wrap the car round a lorry because you wanted to be useful twice in one day."

It was care, limited, unsentimental, entirely on her terms. It nearly undid him because it had edges.

"I'll sleep," he said.

"Good. And Beni."

"Yeah?"

"When you text, text like a person. Not like a man leaving receipts on a table."

He let out one breath that almost became another laugh. "All right."

Then he went down the stairs with the container warm through the bag and the smell of cabbage following him into the street.

He slept three hours in the front room with buses shaking the glass and Laci coming in once, looking at him, saying nothing. At dusk he took the Skoda west on a pickup from Victoria to Heathrow, one businessman and two cases, no conversation. Rain had started again, fine enough to seem like the air itself had decided not to dry out. Near Belgravia the traffic slowed. White facades rose clean and blank above the shine of the road. A florist's van blocked half a lane. Ahead on the left the mouth of the Ashdowns' mews opened between brick walls, narrow, hidden, the route by which he had once arrived to keep everything shameful and efficient.

His hand moved a fraction on the wheel before he caught it. It was an old habit, old training. The back way was quicker. The back way kept you out of sight. The back way had been where Nina first climbed over the seat and where he had learned how easy it was to confuse being chosen with being used.

He left the indicator off.

The main road was clogged with buses, cyclists slick in waterproofs, and a delivery van whose rear doors had been tied shut with blue rope. Everybody saw everybody there. There was no service entrance and no mews wall to swallow the car whole. The businessman in the back glanced up from his phone, impatient already for the airport, for whatever small misery waited after that. Beni kept the wheel straight.

The mews slipped past on his left and was gone. He stayed in the lit lane with the rest of London and drove west.
