# Chapter 3

Cold filmed the inside of the windscreen before daylight had done much for the mews behind the Ashdowns' house. Beni sat with the engine off and his coat zipped to the throat, rubbing a clear patch in the glass with the side of his thumb while the stone stayed dark and the bins waited against the wall. It was a little before seven. The car looked expensive even under the weak security light. That was part of the trick. The black paint made a promise the rest of the day would keep cashing: no mess, no smell, no history, no one poor enough to be cold inside it.

The service door opened once for a delivery of flowers. White boxes came through in the arms of a man in a padded vest, then the door shut again and left behind a ribbon of warmer air carrying polish, radiator heat, and something meaty from downstairs. London at that hour smelled of wet stone and bus exhaust. The house smelled of polish over stock and radiator dust, and from downstairs came the sharp knock of a lid on cast iron.

He liked the first minutes of a job best, before anyone had started needing things. There was still sequence then: he picked up, drove, waited where he was told, kept the car clean, and kept his face neutral. The job got worse the more it asked you to stand three feet away with your face arranged until your toes went numb.

At seven ten Bea came down the mews steps by herself, moving fast because the rain had started again and the narrow gap between the houses funneled it straight at her face. She had her blazer on, skirt under a dark raincoat, and a school satchel that probably cost more than his monthly Oyster topped with a ridiculous plastic frog keyring, bright green and one-eyed, bumping against the polished leather. The frog should have looked childish. Instead it made the rest of her look more tired.

She yanked open the rear door and slid in with the rain at her back.

"Morning, Ben."

"Morning."

She tossed the satchel beside her and leaned forward enough for him to see that she had scrubbed off some dark nail polish before school and missed the corners. "If my mother asks, we left at seven twelve exactly."

"Why?"

"Because she likes hearing exact things before coffee. It calms her." Bea sat back. "Can you take Queen's Gate instead of Knightsbridge? They're digging up something again and everyone acts surprised every time."

He pulled out of the mews. "All right."

The wipers squeaked once before settling into rhythm. Belgravia was only half awake: vans unloading vegetables behind white stucco, women in leggings with expensive dogs, a cyclist swearing under his breath at a taxi that nudged too far into the lane. Bea took her phone out, looked at it, put it face down on her knee. She was seventeen in the specific rich-house way that made boredom look trained. Nothing on her face was loose. Even her slouch had control in it.

At the corner she said, "Do you actually like driving, or do people only ask because they can't think of anything less stupid to say to a driver?"

He glanced at her in the mirror. There was no smile on her mouth, but there was one waiting somewhere near it.

"I like driving," he said. "I don't like traffic."

"Reasonable."

They rolled through a light gone amber. A bus breathed diesel along his side. Bea watched the shops go by, still shuttered, wet metal pulled down over luxury. "What about the waiting?"

"No one likes the waiting."

"I thought maybe there was a secret driver hobby. Crosswords. Gambling. Quiet breakdowns."

"Mostly phones."

"That's depressing."

"It's work."

She picked at the frayed edge of her cuff, then stopped when she noticed herself doing it. "You always answer like you're being marked."

"Maybe I am."

That did it. Her mouth moved. It was not a full laugh, but it was enough. "Fair." She looked out again. "Do you ever drive somewhere because you want to, not because someone rich is late for Pilates?"

Back home, yes, he almost said. Wet roads outside town, empty enough that the engine mattered more than the rules. He kept both hands steady on the wheel and gave her the safer piece. "Not here. There's nowhere to stop."

"That sounds grim."

"London is expensive for pleasure."

"That's true." She said it quickly, not like a girl performing insight but like someone who had already tested the sentence against evidence. "My father says the city filters out unserious people. I think it just filters out people with less money than him."

He did not answer. It was not a sentence that belonged to him.

Bea did not seem to need an answer. "Anyway, if I'm five minutes late Mrs. Fenton will act like I've joined a gang, so maybe don't let me become a social experiment."

The school sat behind high brick and a set of black gates shining with rain. Parents were already spilling cars into the street, women under umbrellas, men in coats cut close to the body, a line of drivers who knew how to move three feet at a time without looking irritated. Beni eased in, let Bea out under the awning, and popped the boot when she remembered a folder she had forgotten.

She took it, one hand on the door, rain needling her hair dark at the temples. "You should drive somewhere for yourself once," she said. "Otherwise it's just expensive babysitting."

Then she was gone into the building with the frog knocking against her satchel and the security guard greeting her like another item being checked in.

Beni stayed in the driver's seat a minute after the line moved. He watched the gate swallow girls in navy skirts and clean shoes and knew the day had already begun doing what it would keep doing until dark: brake, creep, brake, his left calf beginning to burn on the clutch.

By nine he was back in Belgravia. Nina came down in a camel coat over gym clothes, dark glasses on though the sky was the color of dishwater. She got into the rear seat smelling of expensive face cream and old wine that had sweated out overnight.

"Sloane Street first," she said. "Then Dr. Weiss at eleven. I'll text if it changes."

He said yes and drove. She spent most of the trip answering messages with one red fingernail and not looking up. At a light she asked if Bea had got to school on time. Before he answered she had already turned back to the phone.

Sloane Street meant waiting in double yellow anxiety with the hazards on, then circling the block because a van had taken the only legal space. Dr. Weiss turned out to be a white clinic behind frosted glass where the receptionist wore the sort of smile that made it clear anyone without an appointment was part of the furniture. Beni waited there forty minutes, engine off, hands tucked under his armpits because he refused to pay nearly five pounds for a coffee and a seat he did not need. He watched a woman in cashmere come out crying behind sunglasses and get into a Bentley driven by a man old enough to be her father or poor enough to be no one's anything.

He could be idle. Back in Tottenham idle meant lying on the mattress in Laci's front room listening to the pipes complain and the buses shake the glass. Here it meant being stranded inside someone else's neighborhood with no good reason to exist in it. Every cafe chair cost money. Every doorway had cameras. Even the benches on the square looked like they would ask what school he had gone to before they let him sit.

At noon Nina told him to bring the car back to the mews and stay close. "I may need you at half one. Or two. It depends how things run." She was already halfway up the stairs before he said all right.

He parked, wrote the time in his notebook, then sat another ten minutes with the car off and the silence clicking around him. The leather had gone cold through his trousers. From upstairs somewhere came a piano scale, wrong twice in the middle, then stopped. A minute later a vacuum started in one of the front rooms. The house did not ever sound empty. Noise only shifted rooms: the broken scale, the vacuum's whine, a bucket tapped once against a doorframe.

He got out because his legs had started to feel borrowed. Rain had blown under the mews arch and left the stone dark as old bruises. When he went through the service door the basement heat hit his face so fast it almost felt personal.

The kitchen was brighter than upstairs, not prettier but honest about what it was for. White tile covered the walls. A scarred wooden table was shoved against the wall. Racks of plates hung above a radiator ticking under the window, and the steel sink was full of rinsed glasses waiting for the dishwasher. Something paprika-heavy had been reheated recently, and the smell clung to the wet air, thick enough to hold. Eszter stood at the counter with her back half turned, spoon in one hand, phone pinned under her chin as she listened to a message and scraped hardened rice from the side of a container.

She looked over when he came in. Her hair was tied back any old way with a black elastic, and there was a line of flour or bleach dusted pale across one forearm. "If you stand there looking hungry, you'll make me look cruel."

"I wasn't looking hungry."

"You were." She ended the message and tucked the phone into the waistband of her skirt. "Sit down."

"I'm fine."

She switched into Hungarian for the last two words, not louder, just firmer. Sit down. The language hit him low and immediate. In London people either heard his accent and rounded their mouths at him or they did not hear it at all. Hungarian from someone his own side of the world was different. It landed without permission.

He took the chair nearest the radiator.

Eszter went to the microwave, opened it, swore softly because the bowl was hotter than expected, and wrapped the bottom in a folded tea towel before carrying it over. The bowl held pork stew, potatoes gone soft at the edges, and paprika oil bright on top. She put down bread after it, not sliced neatly, just cut thick.

"There," she said. "If anyone asks, I'm preserving driver morale."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me before you taste it. It was better yesterday."

He ate because the smell had already undone the point of pretending he could wait until evening. The stew was too hot at first. He burned the roof of his mouth and kept going. The bread had been reheated in foil so the crust gave under his fingers before cracking. He had not realized how empty he was until the first proper mouthful landed.

Eszter leaned against the sink and watched him with the kind of expression that would have been kinder if it were less accurate. "How old are you?"

He swallowed. "Nineteen."

"Christ." She folded her arms. "You look nineteen when you stop trying not to."

"Is that bad?"

"Only if someone is charging by the hour." She reached past him for a jar, unscrewed it, sniffed, and made a face before throwing whatever had gone off into the bin. "I thought you were maybe twenty-three. Very tired twenty-three."

"That sounds better."

"It doesn't. Eat."

He took another spoonful. Heat spread back into his fingers by way of the bowl.

"How long have you been here?" she asked.

"London? Nearly two years."

"And before that?"

"Hungary."

She gave him a flat look. "Really. Incredible. We should get you on the radio."

He looked up despite himself. "Near Debrecen."

"I knew east." She pointed the jar at him like evidence. "You flatten the vowels when you're tired."

"You listen too much."

"Someone has to. In this house people talk all day and say nothing useful." She tightened the lid and lined the jar back up with the others, labels facing out. "Why London?"

The true answer needed a colder room and more time than this. "Money."

"That's not why. That's what for."

"Same thing."

"No. Money is rent. Debt. School shoes. A boiler that dies in January. It's never the why." She nodded at his bowl. "That stew is money. London isn't."

He ate and let the silence do some work for him. He did not dislike silence. He disliked the kind where someone watched him vanish into it. Eszter did the opposite. She kept him there inside it, waiting to see if there was more.

"You always interrogate people over lunch?" he asked.

"Only the evasive ones."

"Then maybe often."

"Very often." A corner of her mouth turned. "The rich lie prettily. The poor lie lazily. I like to know which one I am hearing."

"And me?"

"Too early to say." She pushed away from the sink and went to the fridge. "You drive like someone who prefers roads to rooms. That's one thing. You say thank you too fast. That's another. And your shoes are clean, so either you're disciplined or you're frightened."

"Maybe I just stepped carefully."

"That is frightened with extra steps."

He laughed before he meant to. It came out small and surprised enough that she looked at him properly for the first time since he sat down, as if laughter from him changed the shape of the room a little.

"There," she said. "Better. I was starting to think Mercer trained you by beating."

"Only with invoices."

"Good. I don't date men from accounts."

It was such a quick throwaway line that by the time he had worked out whether it counted as flirting she had already reached for a tea towel and started drying the last of the glasses. The towel moved between her hands with brisk clean force. Her wrists were narrow, but the forearms under them were strong from the kind of work that never showed up in any heroic story about women.

Her phone buzzed against the waistband of her skirt. She checked the screen and rolled her eyes, but the expression softened before it finished crossing her face.

"Who is it?" he asked, then regretted asking because it sounded like a larger right than he had.

Eszter surprised him by answering. "My daughter. Or my mother pretending not to be worried in a voice note and failing." She hit play and set the phone on the table between them.

A child's voice burst into the kitchen in rapid Hungarian, high and indignant. There had been a disaster with yellow tights. Grandma had packed the wrong cardigan. Also the cat behind the bakery had stolen sausage again and was now, in Lili's opinion, too powerful. Behind the girl another woman said something that made the child protest louder, and then both of them started laughing at once.

The message ended with a kiss against the phone speaker so wet and exaggerated it made Eszter snort.

"Six," she said. "Which means every problem is either the end of the world or solved by a biscuit."

"Yellow tights sound serious."

"Apparently they're essential to education." She put the phone away and reached automatically for the teaspoon in his bowl, stealing a potato to test if it was still hot. "Her name's Lili. She currently thinks I left Hungary solely to ruin her wardrobe."

"Does she know you're in London?"

"London? Yes. She thinks it's a place where I work in a palace and occasionally mail stickers." Eszter shrugged with one shoulder. "She's not fully wrong. The stickers matter."

He imagined the voice traveling from a kitchen in Hungary to this one under Belgravia, crossing all the wet and ticket barriers and bad landlords between. It made the room feel narrower and larger both at once.

"You have children?" Eszter asked.

"No."

"Wife?"

"No."

"Girlfriend?"

He wiped the spoon with his bread. "No. I rent space in my cousin's front room and work stupid hours. That's not much of an offer."

"Dog?"

"In that room? It'd have nowhere to turn around."

"Houseplant?"

"I had basil once. The radiator cooked it."

She barked a laugh. "Finally. A human detail."

The swing door opened before he could answer. Nina came in without really entering, one hand already reaching for the fridge. She wore different clothes now, dark trousers and a cream knit, the sort of soft expensive thing that showed every drop of rain and somehow never seemed to absorb any.

"There you are," she said to Beni, as if he had misplaced himself. "I need Bea at three forty-five, then back here. Stay available after that. Oscar may need the car this evening."

"Of course."

Nina took sparkling water, then looked at Eszter. "Could you send up the blue file from the study when you have a second? Not the charity one. The other blue one."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

The answer came out of Eszter clean and quick, so practiced it barely sounded like the same mouth that had just mocked yellow tights and dead plants. Nina gave a distracted nod and went upstairs again with the bottle against her hip.

The kitchen held itself still for a beat after the door shut.

Eszter let out air through her nose. "The other blue one," she said, low. "Very helpful species, the wealthy. Wonderful at naming objects."

Beni smiled into the bowl. "Maybe there are many blue files."

"Then let her suffer and become specific." Eszter peeled away from the counter. "No. If I do that, somehow I am the difficult one." She took up the tea towel again, drier now, more annoyed than before. "You see how it works."

He did see. That was part of what kept him in the house's orbit. He knew the shape of it: eyes sliding past his face, instructions dropped over his shoulder, a hand already reaching for the next thing.

"They don't speak like that to you in the car?" she asked.

"Sometimes worse. There's no kitchen there."

She looked at him for a second, weighing whether that sentence had more under it than he meant to give. "No," she said. "There isn't."

He finished the bowl more slowly, mostly because finishing it too fast would mean standing up and becoming a driver again. The radiator clicked. Somewhere in the corridor a trolley rattled past. Eszter found the blue file on the sideboard with no visible surprise that there were four of them and carried one upstairs, muttering in Hungarian about idiots who organized their lives by color. Beni stayed where he was, hands around the empty bowl to keep the heat.

When she came back down she found him still there and raised an eyebrow. "I charged no rent for the chair."

"I'm going."

"Then go."

He stayed where he was. The hunger had gone. What kept him in the chair was smaller and more dangerous: he wanted the next question.

Eszter saw it before he had a way to hide it. She opened the cupboard above the kettle, took down two mugs, and made a face like someone accepting an inconvenience she had caused herself. "Tea, then. But only because if you wander back into the mews after stew, you'll fall asleep and kill all of Belgravia."

"That would improve parking."

"It would improve rent."

She filled the kettle from the tap, and while it rumbled to life she leaned her hip against the counter and studied him with less irony than before. "What did the girl say to you this morning?"

"Bea?"

"I've met only one girl today and she called from a phone."

He set the bowl down. "She asked if I liked driving."

"And?"

"I said yes."

"That can't be the whole conversation. She's too nosy for that."

"She asked about waiting."

Eszter nodded once, as if Bea being nosy confirmed a theory rather than supplied information. "And what did you say?"

"That no one likes it."

"True."

"She said the city filters out people with less money than her father."

Eszter's eyebrows went up. "That sounds like her. Sharp child. Too sharp for this house."

"Maybe the house made her."

"Maybe." The kettle clicked off. "Or maybe she survived it by paying attention."

She poured, dropped teabags in, left them there longer than the English did. The steam came up damp and tannic. He had not noticed until then how cold the rest of his body still was under the stew. The heat had only reached the obvious places.

"You going home after this?" she asked.

"Depends if Nina calls."

"That means no."

"Probably no."

"And you sit in the car all day for that?"

"Sometimes I walk."

"Where?"

"Around."

"You're impossible."

"You keep talking to me."

"Yes," she said, handing him a mug. "That's my own stupid decision."

Their fingers touched on the handle. Nothing happened except contact and the fact of it. Even that was enough to make him aware of how long it had been since touch in daylight had asked nothing from him.

He took the tea to the table. Eszter sat opposite this time instead of standing over him. The room did not grow less functional because of it, but it changed use. The dishwasher gave a low watery sigh in the corner. Upstairs a door shut, then another, and someone crossed the hall overhead in quick hard soles without slowing.

"What did you do in Hungary?" she asked.

"Before?"

"No, after. Yes, before."

He looked down into the tea. It was strong, dark, and no nonsense, exactly the kind people back home would call weak because it had been made in England. "I worked in my uncle's garage for a while."

"What kind of garage?"

"Outside town. One lift, when it felt cooperative. An inspection pit that filled with brown water every time it rained hard. In winter we put flattened boxes on the concrete or it took the skin off your knees."

Eszter's mouth changed. Less joke now. "Better. That's a place."

He shrugged. "Mostly farm vans. Old Suzukis. A dentist's Audi that turned up every month with a new scrape and the same perfume in it. If an exhaust dropped on Tuesday, we wired it up and told people not to hit third until Friday."

"So you were a mechanic?"

"At first I held the light and fetched parts. Then more." He ran his thumb around the mug handle. "I liked it when the radio was off. You could hear what was wrong before anyone lied about it."

"And then?"

His mouth shut on the answer. Wet dashboard glow came back at once, a lipstick smear on a cigarette filter in the ashtray, the wheel kicking in his hands so hard his wrists had ached for days. Tea jumped over the rim onto his fingers.

Eszter set her mug down. "All right," she said, quieter. "Leave that road alone."

He wiped his hand on his trouser seam. "It's not a good story."

"I didn't ask for good." She nudged the sugar bowl toward him though he had not touched it. "Tell me the part before it went bad."

He looked at her. The room held.

"I liked knowing where to put my hands," he said at last. "You listened first. Then you checked the bolts that looked rusted but weren't. Then the hose some idiot had already patched with tape. Engines made sense if you paid attention."

"That part I understand," she said. "A vacuum at least tells you why it hates you."

He laughed into the mug. She had a point. Even the appliances in rich houses made their grievances known. They whined, clicked, clogged, leaked. They did not look straight through you while relying on you not to fail.

The phone in his pocket buzzed with Nina's name before either of them had to decide what the next question might be. He looked at the screen and felt the room tighten back into what it officially was.

"Go," Eszter said. Not unkindly. Already she was half up, collecting the bowl and mug together in one hand. "The palace is calling."

He stood. "Thank you. For lunch."

"For the food, yes. For the interrogation, no. That was free." She moved to the sink and ran hot water into the bowl. Over the sound she added, "Pick up Bea on time. If the school fines them, they'll dock me and make me sign for it."

"I will."

"I know."

The words followed him back through the corridor more closely than they should have.

At the service door he stopped long enough to wipe his hand on his trousers before calling her back. Nina answered without greeting.

"Bring the car round," she said. "We're going out."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

He put the phone away and stepped back into the mews, already reaching for the Mercedes. Rain drifted under the arch in a fine cold spray and got into his collar. The day had closed over again: keys, timings, mirrors, Nina's urgency laid neatly over his next hours. But the kitchen heat was still in his hands, and when he thought about the drive ahead he caught himself measuring it against the next time he might have a reason to go back downstairs.

That was new enough to feel like trouble.
