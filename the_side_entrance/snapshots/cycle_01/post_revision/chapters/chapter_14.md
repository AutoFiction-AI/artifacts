# Chapter 14

The school let out into sleet so fine it only became weather when it hit glass. Until then it looked like dirty light. Beni sat with the heater low and watched girls in navy coats come through the gate in bursts, expensive and cold in the same way, their shoes dark with the wet. The car smelled of lemon polish and old damp wool. He had been thinking about the basement kitchen since noon: the radiator under the table, the dented kettle, the way Eszter leaned her hip against the counter when she was too tired to hide it. By then he had eighty pounds less in his wallet than the night before. He could still see the envelope going soft where sleet had hit it, Eszter knocking it once back against his coat with the heel of her hand before the stacked edge of twenties showed at the flap. Then she took it by one corner like it might leave grease, and the relief that crossed her face felt dirtier than the cash.

Bea came out late, alone, carrying her blazer instead of wearing it. One lace had slipped out of the top eyelet of her left shoe and dragged black with road grit. There was a fountain-pen stain along the side of her middle finger as if she had spent all afternoon pressing too hard. She saw him and did not raise a hand. She came straight to the car, opened the passenger door, and got in next to him instead of taking the back.

He looked at her once. "Everything all right?"

"Fine."

Her bag was still on the pavement. He leaned across to unlock the rear door, but she did not move.

"Your bag," he said.

She looked through the windscreen as if it belonged to someone else. "You can get it."

He got out into the sleet, took the bag from beside the kerb, and put it in the back. By the time he slid in again the edge of his cuff was wet through. Bea had both hands under her thighs, pinning them there.

"Home?" he asked.

"Obviously."

He pulled away from the kerb. Traffic on the school road was bad in the ordinary afternoon way, mothers stopping where they were not meant to stop, a white van backing half onto the pavement, a bus breathing diesel into the wet. Bea sat with her face turned toward the side window. Now and then she picked at the torn skin beside her thumbnail with the nail of her index finger, fast and neat, like she did not want even that much movement noticed.

At the end of the road she said, "Is Eszter in today?"

The question was so sudden he almost missed the light changing. He took the turn late and felt the tyres complain softly on the paint.

"She was in this morning," he said.

"So yes."

"Probably."

Bea gave a small shrug that was not agreement with anything. "Good."

He took Sloane Street because Eaton Square was choking again. In the mirror he could see the school gate fade off the wet back glass in pale bars. Bea kept her eyes on the window and he kept his on the road, but the car had changed shape with her in the front. Usually she spent the school run in the back seat, shoe against the opposite leather, saying almost nothing until she suddenly said something exact and unfriendly about one of her parents. Today she had put herself next to him like someone who had come to collect a debt.

At Belgravia the house stood in the same clean block of stone and black railings it always did, but the winter cuts were showing if you knew where to look. No fresh arrangement in the lower hall window, only the old lilies opening too far. Fewer delivery crates by the service step. The brass on the mews gate polished to the same shine as before, because rich houses would starve the kitchen before they let the metal look poor.

Bea was out of the car before he had killed the engine. She took her blazer, left the bag, and went through the side entrance without waiting for him.

"Your bag," he called.

She did not turn around. "Bring it."

He took it from the back seat. It was heavier than it looked, books and what felt like trainers, maybe a laptop. When he came in through the service door the house was in the half-hour between school return and evening performance, before coats were dried and before voices from upstairs had settled into dinner tones. Somewhere above him a vacuum cleaner stopped short. The air smelled of radiator heat, polish, and onions gone sweet in butter.

He knew Bea had gone down because the front hall was empty and the service stair door had not latched properly behind her. It stood open on the rubber strip by a thumb's width. He told himself he was only taking the bag after her. Then halfway down he heard his own name.

He stopped so hard the bag swung against his leg.

"It was him," Bea said. Her voice had gone flatter than in the car. "I know what he looks like."

The basement kitchen door was open just enough to show a strip of yellow tile and the side of the old pine table. Eszter's voice came back lower, not soothing, only exact.

"Start at the beginning."

Beni should have gone in. He knew that even as he stayed where he was with the wet bag strap cutting into his palm. He could see Eszter now through the gap, standing by the counter with a tea towel in both hands, twisting and untwisting it by the corners. Bea sat at the table in her shirt sleeves with her chin lifted as if she hated the shape her own mouth would have to make.

"The gallery night," Bea said. "The one where she came home late and Dad was pretending not to wait up."

Eszter did not speak.

"I came back through the mews because I wasn't in the mood for the front door and the hall lamps and my father pretending he'd just happened to be reading in a suit at midnight." Bea rubbed the pen stain on her finger with her thumb and only made it darker. "The garage door wasn't shut all the way. I saw the car in there."

Beni felt the cold from the stair rail through his sleeve.

"I saw my mother's shoe on the floor," Bea said. "Just one. I saw your tie hanging off the handbrake. And I saw enough through the glass to know no one was discussing parking."

Eszter looked at the table, not at Bea. "You saw his face."

"Yes."

"And hers."

Bea gave a humorless little breath. "Unless you've got another tall blond woman in that garage who wears La Mer and lies for sport, yes."

Eszter set the tea towel down. Beni knew that movement. It meant she was making herself cleaner before touching something filthy.

"Why didn't you say anything then?" she asked.

"Because I wasn't going to be the child who tells on her mother for getting fingered in the car."

The words hit the air and stayed there. Beni closed his hand harder on the bag strap. It dug into his skin through the wool of his glove.

Bea was looking at the table too now, breathing through her mouth as if the room smelled wrong. "And because I thought maybe it had been a one-off and I didn't want to hear about adult loneliness for three hours. But then it kept being him."

Eszter's face had not changed. That made it worse.

"How?" she said.

"Timings. Stupid little things." Bea pushed at the mug in front of her without drinking. "My mother's schedule started bending around him. She'd say pilates in Chelsea and he'd still be in the mews after. She'd say lunch in Mayfair and he wouldn't get sent off for me till twenty minutes late, then she'd come downstairs with her lipstick redone and pretend the whole day had been impossible. She never remembers anybody's name downstairs, but she remembered his from the first week. She asked for him in front of dispatch on speaker once. Twice, maybe."

The bag felt heavier by the second. Beni set it very carefully on the stair.

"And?" Eszter said.

Bea lifted one shoulder. "And I live here. I know what the house sounds like when everyone's lying. It's practically the family instrument."

Eszter's mouth moved, not quite a smile and not kind enough to become one. "That's not evidence."

"No," Bea said. "The evidence was later."

She wiped her stained finger on the inside hem of her shirt. It left a faint dark line.

"Last week," she said, "I came down the service stairs because I could hear Mum shouting at someone on the phone upstairs and I wanted toast without getting any of her on me. He was by the bins with you. He gave you an envelope." She nodded toward the stair without seeing Beni there. "You looked like you were trying not to take it and also like you needed whatever was in it badly enough to hate needing it. That was revolting, so I started paying attention to every single thing."

Eszter was very still.

"You think that envelope came from my mother," she said.

"I think," Bea said, "that if someone lies to you for that long, they usually don't stop at one subject."

Beni stepped into the doorway because staying outside had become more cowardly than entering.

Bea looked at him once, with no surprise at all. "There you are."

Eszter turned. Hard recognition crossed her face, as if a sum she had been checking in her head had finally balanced the wrong way.

He put Bea's bag down by the table. "You shouldn't be doing this downstairs."

Bea blinked. "That's really your line?"

"Bea," he said, and the name came out rougher than he meant. "You don't know all of it."

"Don't insult me." Her voice stayed quiet. "I know enough."

Eszter looked from one of them to the other. "Did it happen?"

Beni felt the kitchen grow small around the old smells of fried onion, bleach, and damp wool steaming by the radiator. He could hear the tick of the cheap wall clock over the freezer. He looked at Bea because Eszter was harder to look at and that too was a kind of answer.

Bea said, "Right."

"Beni." Eszter did not raise her voice. "Did it happen."

He put his wet hands flat on the back of the chair nearest the door. The varnish was worn away at the top rail where a hundred other hands had gripped it. "Yes."

Nothing broke. No one shouted. From upstairs came the brief thud of a door and then the house settled again around their silence.

Bea got up so suddenly her chair legs skidded on the tile. She shoved them back with her knee and reached for her bag.

"I wasn't telling for fun," she said, not looking at Beni. She was talking to Eszter, but the edge of it was for both of them. "Dad would turn it into payroll. Mum would make it about whether she's still wanted. I told you because downstairs is the only place in this house where people still talk like things happened in a real order."

Eszter nodded once.

"Go upstairs," she said. "Do your work. And if either of them asks why you were down here, say you wanted toast."

Bea pulled on her blazer without sliding her arms properly into it first. "I'm sorry," she said, and the apology sounded like something she was ashamed to own. Then she looked at Beni. "I'm not doing witness protection for either of you."

She went out past him. On the stair she stopped just long enough to say, without turning around, "I didn't tell him. Or her. Yet."

Then she was gone.

Beni stayed by the chair. Eszter picked up the tea towel again and folded it into a square so precise it looked sarcastic.

"Say something," he said.

"Not while the girl is still on the stairs."

"Eszter."

She pointed with the folded towel toward the door, not at him, at the space beside him. "Go and do your job. You can come back when the house quiets down and tell me exactly how stupid you think I am."

He wanted to cross the room, touch her wrist, force it into the shape of the afternoon before this one. Instead he said, "I never thought that."

She gave him a look so clean it made his neck burn. "Then you weren't thinking at all."

He left because there was nothing else that did not make him smaller.

In the mews the sleet had thickened into rain. The car roof clicked as it cooled. Beni sat in the driver's seat with both hands on the wheel and watched a drop crawl sideways across the passenger window where Bea's shoulder had leaned. He could still hear her voice in the kitchen, not loud, not dramatic, only exact. That was what made it fatal. If she had been hysterical he could have defended himself against that. Facts were harder. They sat there with one shoe on the garage floor and an envelope by the bins and his own silence in the middle of them.

He took his wallet out and looked at the notes still left from Nina's cash, folded by size, damp at one corner from the day it had rained through his coat. There were one hundred and twenty pounds left: too little to fix anything, enough to have poisoned more than one room.

He put it back.

The house sent him nowhere for almost an hour. A delivery van came and went with flowers that looked cheaper than usual but were trying hard not to. A boy from the butcher stood in the rain with two crates balanced on his thigh and swore softly into his scarf when no one buzzed him in fast enough. Beni should have been grateful for the wait because it meant he did not yet have to go downstairs again, but all waiting had shape, and this waiting had Eszter in it. He kept seeing the way she had set the towel down before she asked Bea for sequence and detail, like a surgeon clearing space.

At half six Nina came out by the side entrance in a charcoal coat with the belt hanging untied. She had one earring in and the other in her hand. The missing one made her look younger and more dangerous in the way unfinished rich women sometimes did, as if disorder were another accessory they could afford. She slid into the back seat without looking at him.

"Pimlico first," she said. "Then Sloane Street. Oscar's dining with some carrion banker and I'd rather be dead than sit through it."

Beni pulled out of the mews.

In the mirror he saw her fit the earring into place by touch alone. Her face in the glass was pale with the day's makeup rubbed thin under the eyes. She smelled of cold cream over perfume.

"You look grim," she said after a minute. "Has Mercer finally discovered you have a personality?"

"Long day," he said.

"Everyone's having one." She snapped the compact shut and let it fall into her bag. "My daughter isn't speaking to me, which is either developmental or deserved, and the house now buys coffee that tastes like boiled envelopes. Oscar says we're economizing for a quarter. In this house that means the flowers suffer before the men do."

Beni kept his eyes on the road. Pimlico Road was wet black under the lamps. Cyclists flashed by with plastic over their backpacks and red lights trembling on puddles.

"Did Bea say why?" he asked before he could stop himself.

Nina gave a short laugh. "Why she isn't speaking to me? Because she's seventeen. Because she thinks my existence is an act aimed specifically at her nerves. Because she's not wrong." She leaned her head back. "I assume she'll recover in time for university applications."

Beni said nothing.

At a red light Nina leaned forward a little, close enough for him to smell the mint on her tongue. "Whatever is wrong," she said, "don't bring it into the house in your face. Oscar notices moods when he can't find numbers."

Then she sat back again, and that was all. He drove her to Pimlico, waited, drove her to Sloane Street, waited, drove her home. She made two calls in the back seat and talked about caterers and one donor's impossible husband and whether there was still decent Champagne in the locked cupboard. She kept talking as if the floor under her feet were still level. If she felt any shift at all, she gave him no sign of it and seemed to trust the house to swallow the rest before it reached the drawing room.

Back in the mews, she stayed in the back seat a second longer than necessary after the engine died.

"Tomorrow eight fifteen," she said. "And don't wear that tie. It makes you look like you're going to your own funeral."

She got out and went in through the side door with her coat open to the rain.

Beni sat until the courtesy lights went out. Then he took the tie from his collar, stuffed it into the glove box, and went downstairs.

The kitchen had been cleaned to evening order. The pans were racked. The counters were wiped. The smell now was dishwasher steam, stock, and the bitter edge of over-steeped tea. Eszter stood at the table with her own coat on and a net shopping bag looped over one wrist. Her hair was still tied back but the short pieces at the nape had worked loose in the damp. Beside her on the table sat his flat key on the cheap red plastic ring from the corner locksmith, the one she had once laughed at because it made even a London key look temporary.

"Close the door," she said.

He did.

For a moment neither of them moved. The freezer hummed. Somewhere in the wall a pipe knocked twice and went quiet.

Eszter touched the key ring with one finger and then took her hand away. "How long."

He knew better than to pretend not to understand. "Since the gallery night."

"Not with dates. I'm not the police." Her voice was level and tired and much worse than if she had shouted. "How long while you were with me."

"From before," he said. "Then during."

"During what."

"Us."

She let out a breath through her nose. "You're nineteen, but you're not so nineteen that words don't exist."

"While I was with you."

"Good." She nodded once. "Better."

He stood by the door with his hands empty. He did not sit because sitting would have meant pretending to belong in the room.

"It wasn't all the time," he said. "It wasn't every day."

Eszter looked at him with plain contempt. "What a beautiful defense."

"I'm trying to tell you straight."

"Then do it straight."

He swallowed. The tea smell in the room had gone stale. "She started it. After the gallery."

"Did she hold a gun to your head."

"No."

"Did she climb into bed with you at Tottenham when you were asleep."

"No."

"Then start again."

He rubbed his thumb along the seam of his palm where the steering wheel leather always left a faint tackiness after a long shift. "I went with it. I kept going with it. I should've stopped."

"Before me."

"Yes."

"And after me."

"Yes."

She nodded again. Every answer seemed to make her cleaner, more exact, while he felt himself get blurred around the edges.

"Was it still happening when you came to me on Sunday," she asked, "after the market, after you told me about that woman in Hungary, after you put your mouth everywhere and let me think that whatever was wrong with you had at least one honest corner left."

Beni looked at the table because the key ring's cheap red plastic was easier than her face. "Yes."

Eszter laughed once, a brief snag of sound like a knife catching on bone.

"Fine," she said. "Now the money."

He looked up.

"Don't do that." Her eyes hardened. "The girl did not invent the envelope. Was that money from her."

He should have answered at once. The pause made the room tilt.

"Some of it," he said.

"How much."

"Most."

She put both hands flat on the table. The shopping bag swung off her wrist and bumped her coat. Inside it he could see leeks, a loaf of sliced bread, a tub of cheap yogurt, practical things for one or two days at most.

"You gave me her money."

"I gave you money because you needed it."

"From her."

"I could pay some back from wages."

Eszter stared at him as if he had started speaking through the wrong end of his body. "You think this is about bookkeeping."

"No."

"Then why are you talking like a receipt."

He dragged a hand over his mouth. The skin there still tasted faintly of rain. "I was trying to help."

"No," she said. "You were trying to buy a version of yourself you could stand."

He had no answer fast enough to stop her.

"If you wanted to fuck the lady of the house, that's ugly and stupid and very ordinary," she said. Her voice remained low, but each word arrived with the neat force of a plate set down exactly where it belonged. "I'm not seventeen. I know rich people get bored and poor men get used. I know desire doesn't wait for moral paperwork. But you don't take the money from that lie and put it in my hand like you're saving me."

"I wasn't mocking you."

"You think humiliation only counts if someone laughs?"

He shut his eyes for a second. The kitchen light showed red through the lids. "No."

"I told you things I don't tell men who haven't earned them. About Lili. About my room. About why I left home. I took your key." She touched the key ring again, the smallest movement. "Do you understand what that is in my life. How little room I've got to be stupid."

He saw it then not as a sentence but as objects: her folded clothes in the church-basement room, her daughter's photo tucked into the mirror frame, rent counted to the pound, the envelope from him in among it all with its mouth still open like a smear.

"I know," he said.

"No." She shook her head. "If you knew, you wouldn't have done this part."

He stepped away from the door before he meant to. "Eszter, I wanted to stop. With her. I kept thinking I would, then the cuts came and you were losing the room and I thought if I could fix one thing first"

"Fix." She said the word softly, almost curious. "You fixed me with her money."

"It wasn't like that."

"Wasn't it." She straightened. "Tell me where the room came from then. Tell me what paid for the bread I bought yesterday."

He opened his mouth and closed it. There was no sentence that did not put Nina's hand directly in Eszter's shopping bag.

"Exactly," she said.

The refrigerator motor kicked on behind him. It made the silence feel mechanical.

"I would've helped you anyway," he said finally, hating how thin it sounded.

"With what." Eszter's eyes flashed once. "Your good intentions. Your sad face. Twenty pounds of honesty after the fact."

He looked at the shopping bag again because he could not stop looking at what life cost in her hands. The leeks had dirt still trapped high in the stems.

"I loved being with you," he said.

Her mouth changed at that, but not toward softness. "That may even be true."

"It is."

"Don't." She held up one hand. "Don't give me nice clean sentences now. You don't get to climb out through language."

He swallowed whatever else had been coming.

"Listen to me," she said. "The sex isn't the whole insult. The lie isn't even the whole insult. The worst part is that you stood in this kitchen and watched me thank you for money that came from the woman paying you to keep your mouth shut while you fucked her. You let me feel relieved. Grateful. That's filthier than the rest."

He had nothing to put against that. He remembered the exact moment now: the paper gone damp in his glove, Eszter's first refusal quick and hard, the back of her fingers knocking the envelope against his chest. Then the flap had lifted and shown the stacked edge of twenties. She took it without looking at him and pressed the wet corner flat with her thumb as if tidying it could make the money less intimate. She said thank you too fast, like the word had to get out before it turned into pleading. His relief had come before his shame. That was the part he could not forgive.

"I'm sorry," he said, and heard how inadequate it was even as it left him.

"I know." She sounded almost bored by it. "That's not a tool."

She picked up the key and pushed it across the table. It slid over old knife marks and stopped near his side.

"I'm not coming to Tottenham," she said. "Not tomorrow, not after Christmas, not because you miss me, not because you finally tell me the whole truth when the whole truth is all that's left. You keep your room."

He did not move toward the key.

"Take it," she said.

He took it. The red plastic was warm from her fingers.

"And hear this properly. If I'm on shift here, you don't come downstairs unless the house calls you. You don't come for tea. You don't come because you're lonely. You don't come because you think looking wrecked is a kind of apology. You wait where the other drivers wait."

"Eszter."

"No." She hoisted the shopping bag higher on her wrist. "You had the kitchen because I opened it. That's finished."

He felt that more sharply than he had felt the key. The kitchen had been a room with steam on the windows and food in foil and her voice crossing from Hungarian to English and back. It had been somewhere he could arrive without being announced. Hearing her remove it from him in a sentence made the tiled floor look suddenly official, as if he had been trespassing there all along and only just got told.

"Is there anything," he began, then stopped because he heard himself asking for work.

Eszter saved him from finishing. "There's the girl," she said. "If she needs looking after in a practical way, that's separate. But you and me are done. I don't have enough room in my life for this kind of dirt."

She walked to the door. When she passed him he smelled hand cream under bleach, the ordinary smell of her after a shift, and the familiarity of it was brutal.

At the threshold she stopped.

"One more thing," she said without turning around. "Don't make me comfort you about this."

Then she went out.

Beni stayed in the kitchen until the door swung shut behind her. He could hear her steps on the service stairs, quick and even, then nothing. The freezer hummed. Water ticked somewhere into the trap under the sink. On the counter by the bread bin sat the mug Bea had left earlier, tea skin dried across the top in a pale wrinkled film. Beni looked at it and had the absurd urge to wash it, as if labor done neatly enough might still count for anything.

He put the key in his pocket and left the mug where it was.

Upstairs, the house had turned toward dinner. A radio murmured from a room off the back corridor. Someone laughed once, then stopped as if remembering where they were. Beni went to the service landing by habit more than intention an hour later, after Nina had asked for a car blanket and then changed her mind, after Oscar's assistant had sent down a list for the next day's flowers, after he had stood in the mews long enough for the cold to soak through the soles of his shoes. His body still knew the route to the basement kitchen better than most routes in London: left at the laundry chute, down the narrow stair, hand to the rail where the varnish had worn away.

Tonight the door at the bottom stood shut tight, a rolled tea towel pushed along the gap to keep the heat in.

He stood one step above the landing and listened.

From inside came the soft knock of plates, the hiss of the kettle, the murmur of two women talking too low for words. One of them was Eszter. He knew the weight of her voice even through a door. The other might have been the Thursday temp, the skinny Polish cleaner with violet trainers under her black work trousers and eczema cracking white across two knuckles. Last week she had shown Eszter a photo of her son in a cardboard astronaut helmet while they waited for the oven to heat. Beni had been in the room then with a plate in his hand, not family, not exactly staff, but something blurred and warm in between.

Now he was on the wrong side of the green baize.

The temp opened the door just enough to come through with a crate of folded napkins balanced on her hip. She looked up, saw him on the stair, and shifted the crate higher.

"Sorry," she said automatically, though her face said she already understood the map of the evening. "We're keeping the heat."

The sentence was so small and polite it could have passed for nothing. Beni stepped back to let her pass. She did not hold the door for him. Through the gap he saw the corner of the table, the radiator glowing under it, Eszter's coat on the chair back, and steam rising from a pot. Then the door shut again. The smell of onion and stock came out warm as breath and was gone.

He stayed on the stair with his hand on the worn rail until the wood cooled under his palm.
