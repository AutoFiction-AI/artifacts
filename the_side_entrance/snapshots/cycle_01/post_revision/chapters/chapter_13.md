# Chapter 13

The radiator in the Ashdowns' basement kitchen was on, but only in theory. It clicked and sighed behind the drying aprons and gave off just enough heat to keep the tiles from feeling damp through the soles of Beni's shoes. The room still smelled of coffee, bleach, and yesterday's stock, but the ripe sweetness from the fruit bowl was gone. In its place sat twelve clementines in a neat row on the stainless counter, leaves shriveled to paper at the stems, waiting to be carried upstairs and made to look abundant.

This was how money trouble arrived in a rich house. It did not come as darkness or panic, and no silver went missing. The lights still burned in every hallway. The brass still shone. But there were fewer herbs in the morning delivery, cheaper yogurt decanted into the expensive bowls, and a new rule that only one strip of fluorescent lighting was left on in the mews garage unless a car was actually moving. The house still pretended ease. It just asked the people under it to do more of the pretending.

Mrs. Keene stood by the service door with the invoice clipped to a board, her navy cardigan buttoned wrong at the middle and a fennel seed working from one side of her mouth to the other. She always had something small to chew after ten in the morning, as if appetite needed to be disciplined in public. Today it was fennel. Yesterday it had been mint gum. Her eyes flicked over the crates on the floor.

"Three trays of eggs, not four," she said to the delivery man. "And where are the pomegranates?"

"Order came through for one case only."

"We usually have three."

He gave her the sort of shrug men used when they wanted the paperwork to become somebody else's theology. "Then they've changed their minds."

Eszter, in her grey work T-shirt and black trousers, was checking labels against the old pantry list with a pencil behind her ear. Her hair was tied up hard and high, not a strand loose. She had a dish towel slung through the back of her waistband like a tail. Without looking at Beni she said, in Hungarian, "Apparently winter means they can now survive on one pomegranate."

He set Bea's school bag beside the back stairs and answered in the same language. "Maybe they found religion."

Eszter gave the smallest snort. "No. If it was religion we'd all be eating worse."

She sounded like herself, but not fully. The words landed too cleanly. There was no play after them. She crossed out three things on the pantry sheet and wrote new quantities in the margin. Beni saw the numbers before he saw what they meant: six yogurts instead of twelve, one side of salmon instead of two, no fresh dill at all.

From the corridor came the light, irritated ring of a phone ending and the harder sound of a man's heel turning on stone. Mrs. Keene straightened automatically. Eszter did not straighten. She only moved the pencil from behind her ear to the counter and wiped her fingers on the towel.

Oscar Ashdown came into the kitchen carrying a printed rota and his reading glasses in one hand. He wore a charcoal suit and no overcoat, though it was raining in the mews. His hair at the temples looked silverer in the strip light than it ever did upstairs. Beni had seen him angry only twice, and both times the anger had arrived as precision, not volume. Today he looked almost rested, which was worse.

"Morning," Oscar said.

Mrs. Keene answered at once. "Morning, sir."

Eszter said, "Morning."

Beni was not sure whether he counted as staff for this conversation or furniture. He stayed where he was by the end of the counter, one hand on Bea's bag.

Oscar looked at the crates, then at the invoice. "Good. That's in line with what I asked for."

Mrs. Keene swallowed the fennel seed. "There are a few substitutions."

"There will be more. That's fine." He put the rota down on the counter, flattening it with his palm. "We're tightening the household budget through the gala. Pantry is down fifteen percent from this week. Flowers are reduced except on event days. Duplicate evening cover stops immediately. I don't want any visible drop in standard."

Eszter said, "Visible to whom?"

Mrs. Keene's eyes cut toward her, quick and warning.

Oscar did not raise his voice. "To anyone whose opinion costs money."

For a second the kitchen gave back only the hiss from the kettle. Somewhere above them a door closed softly, thick carpet swallowing the end of it.

Eszter picked up the pencil again. "And invisible standards?"

"You know exactly what I mean."

"I do. I wanted to hear you say it."

Beni felt the cold metal edge of Bea's lunchbox under his hand. The room had narrowed. Mrs. Keene was staring at the rota as if she could enter it by force.

Oscar moved his attention to the page, which was his way of making the page more important than the person. "The live-in room is suspended after Friday. We don't need overnight coverage at current occupancy. Eszter, you'll move to day rota only for now. Monday, Wednesday, Friday until the gala week, then we'll reassess."

Eszter's face did not change. She looked down at the printed lines. "You said that room was part of the arrangement."

"It was. The arrangement has changed."

"To what?"

"To three days' work and no room, for the moment."

Mrs. Keene said quietly, "We can speak after Bea's gone in, Eszter."

Eszter ignored her. "By Friday?"

"By Sunday afternoon," Oscar said. "I'm not putting you on the street. I'm asking for flexibility."

That almost made Beni laugh, and because it almost made him laugh it also made him want to spit. He kept his face still.

Eszter set the pencil down with care. "What a beautiful word."

Oscar adjusted his glasses in his hand. "This doesn't need dramatics."

"No," Eszter said. "Only arithmetic."

He gave her a look that would have made most people back away, as if he were checking whether a door would shut if pushed. Cruelty would have admitted feeling. "You'll have your final overnight payment included. Keene will sort the details."

"And the gala still gets twelve kinds of canape?"

"The gala is not up for discussion."

"Of course it isn't."

Bea's footsteps sounded on the back stairs before anyone could answer. Light, quick, then a pause on the last step while she took in the room. She had her blazer on but no tie, her hair tied badly with a black ribbon that had nearly slipped out. She looked from the small morning delivery to Oscar to Eszter and understood enough to roll her eyes at her father before she looked away again.

"Is there any proper yogurt left," she said, "or are we doing wartime now?"

"Bea," Oscar said.

"I'm asking. Last night the hummus tasted like punishment."

Mrs. Keene moved at once toward the fridge. "There is Greek yogurt, miss."

"The cheap one?"

Eszter said, "It still works if you put it in your mouth."

Bea's mouth twitched. Not quite a smile. "Good to know."

Oscar picked up the rota. "Car in two minutes."

"I can hear the car from here," Bea said. Then she took the yogurt Mrs. Keene handed her, peeled back the lid, and ate one spoonful standing by the sink. Her eyes went back to Eszter for one beat too long. Beni saw it. So did Eszter. Oscar did not, or pretended not to.

Beni took Bea's bag again and headed for the corridor. On the way out he heard Oscar say, to Mrs. Keene, "Please make sure the attic room is clear before the florist storage starts arriving."

He heard the phrase florist storage and understood that there was not even a person in it after all, only square footage.

Outside, the mews smelled of wet brick and diesel. Bea got into the back of the Mercedes and pulled the door shut harder than necessary.

"Did he sack someone," she asked before Beni had even started the engine.

"No."

"That's not a real answer."

Beni checked the mirror. "Seat belt."

She clicked it with enough force to make the buckle snap. "Mum says when he starts talking about efficiencies it means somebody else is paying."

He pulled out of the mews into slow, glazed traffic. Rain had polished the road to the colour of fish skin. "Your school bag's leaking glitter."

"That's because the art department hates us."

That was all she offered. She leaned her forehead against the window and drew a small circle in the condensation with one finger. He drove her to school through Eaton Square and Knightsbridge, passing shop windows already staged for Christmas. Everything glittered harder when the weather was mean.

When he got back to Belgravia, the kitchen had gone quiet in the way kitchens did when anger was being worked off through small necessary tasks. Mrs. Keene was upstairs. Eszter was at the sink scraping labels off old jam jars so they could be reused in the pantry. The hot water had steamed the lower half of the window. Her phone was propped in the corner under the cupboard, playing a child's voice note in thin bursts through the speaker. It was Hungarian, too fast for the cheap phone microphone, and Beni only caught pieces of it: a complaint about maths, a dog barking, then a girl laughing at her own mistake.

Eszter hit pause with her wrist because her hands were wet.

"Lili?" he asked.

"Who else talks like she invented the world."

He stood beside the sink. Up close he could see that the skin over her knuckles had split in two places from cleaning chemicals and dry air. She had rubbed hand cream over it anyway. The smell under the soap was almond.

"What happens now?" he said.

She kept scraping. Paper came away from the jar in wet curls. "Now I work three days unless they decide to discover another emergency. Now I lose the room because apparently peonies need somewhere to sleep before the gala. Now I pay to get to Belgravia for the privilege of cleaning rooms I no longer live in."

"Have you got somewhere?"

"Maybe. Marika from St. Stephen's knows a woman with a church-basement room in Holloway. Clean, strict, no men, no noise, no cooking after ten. Very sexy. She wants two weeks up front because the last girl disappeared with a kettle and forty pounds."

"How much?"

Eszter dried her hands and finally looked at him. Fear made her tidier. Even her eyebrows looked organized. "Two hundred and forty by Friday to hold it. Then Oyster, then the money home. My mother says Lili's winter boots are already pinching. That girl grows out of things like she's doing it for revenge."

Beni knew the exact shape of two hundred and forty pounds in notes. Four fifties, two twenties. Or twelve twenties if that was what the cash machine gave. He also knew what he had in his wallet: sixty-three pounds, a ten for diesel he still needed, and a folded fiver stuck behind his licence for luck he did not believe in.

"I can ask around," he said. It sounded pathetic as soon as it was out.

Eszter tossed the ruined label into the bin. "For a room? Unless you know a lonely duchess in Stamford Hill, don't bother."

"I meant with drivers. Someone might know a place."

"Drivers know places for drivers. I'm not sleeping in a room that smells like Lynx and old socks."

The offer climbed as far as his teeth. He looked at her split knuckles, at the wet jam jars, at the rota with her nights cut out of it, and swallowed it. What he had in his wallet did not cover Holloway. She saw something move in his face and shook her head.

"Don't do the heroic look," she said. "It doesn't suit you either."

"I wasn't."

"You were absolutely about to." She picked up the next jar. "Go on. Nina will ring soon because rich women can smell bad timing better than dogs can smell meat."

That, he thought, was probably true.

He took the empty coffee cup to the sink just to have something to do with his hands. On the counter beside the jars lay the revised rota, face down. He turned it over when Eszter moved to the fridge. The days left were Monday, Wednesday, and Friday, with the nights stripped out cleanly, like a butcher had gone down a chicken and taken the useful parts.

When Nina called twenty minutes later, she did not ask whether he was free. She said, "Bring the Mercedes round. I need Hackney first and then Chambers, and I need you not to discuss either with anyone dull."

He said, "I'm here."

"How lucky for both of us."

* * *

The first box came down from Jonas Hale's studio carried by a young man with a silver hoop through one nostril and a mustard scarf wound around his neck three times although he was sweating. He knocked on the rear passenger window with two fingers, as if the glass might be artistic and fragile, then passed the cardboard box in without meeting Beni's eyes.

"There's another smaller one," he said to Nina. "And a garment bag."

"Of course there is," Nina said.

She had made Beni wait outside in the drizzle for eleven minutes without letting him pull into the loading bay. The studio building rose out of Hackney Wick in wet brick and corrugated metal, all confidence and draft. Someone had spray-painted a bright pink mouth on the roller shutter next door. Nina sat in the back seat with her coat unbuttoned, one hand bare, the other still in its glove, as if dressing completely for the weather had become too much to ask of the day.

The box on her lap was light. When it tilted, Beni saw the top layer: a silk blouse folded badly, a paperback with water damage along the bottom edge, a tangled phone charger, a face cream pot with the lid missing. It was worse than love letters: the small household rubbish of being with someone who had got bored.

The young man came back with the garment bag and the second box. This one was taped shut.

"That's all Jonas asked me to send down," he said.

Nina smiled at him with full social accuracy, the smile that left no legal bruise. "I'm sure he had a very taxing afternoon."

The young man's ears reddened. "He's in the middle of installation."

"I'm sure he's in the middle of something."

He left. Nina stared at the closed studio door until Beni heard the lock click from inside. She laughed once through her nose.

"Drive," she said.

He pulled away from the kerb. The wipers pushed a skin of dirty rain across the windscreen and back again. In the mirror he could see her looking not at the road but at her own reflection laid over it in the glass.

"Lincoln's Inn?" he asked.

"In a minute. Take the long way first."

He took the roads he would take for anyone who wanted to delay arrival without admitting that was what they were doing: west along the canal side where traffic crawled, then down through the City where the rain held people under awnings and made expensive coats look temporary. Nina kept one hand on the edge of the open box, fingers spread over the silk blouse as if pinning something down inside it.

"He couldn't even be bothered to hand me my own things," she said.

Beni said nothing.

"No, don't become even more useful than usual. It makes me murderous."

"Sorry."

"Don't apologize. It's dreary." She shut the box flaps hard enough to bow the cardboard. "Do you know the vulgar thing? I don't even want most of this. The cream's mine. The blouse is not. I wore it to please him and now it looks like somebody else's bad judgment."

He turned onto Farringdon Road. A cyclist hit the roof of the car lightly with the flat of his hand as he squeezed past, angry at a space that had not been his. Beni barely heard it. Nina's voice had gone softer, which with her never meant safer.

"Oscar's done one of his cleansing routines downstairs, hasn't he?" she said.

He did not answer quickly enough.

"Keene looked as if she'd swallowed a drawing pin. That usually means my husband has mistaken subtraction for character."

"They're cutting shifts."

"They're cutting every part of the house that doesn't photograph." She leaned back. "Eszter too, I assume."

His fingers tightened on the steering wheel, enough for the leather seam to print into his palm. He hated how fast she had found the tender place. He hated more that the tenderness existed for her to find.

"You don't have to look so alarmed," she said. "The entire basement knows you're in love with her. Bea probably knows, and Bea notices things chiefly to despise them."

"It's not like that."

"Then you're lying badly. I prefer your usual standard."

He took the corner too cleanly, too fast, then corrected it. The chassis settled under him. He kept his eyes on the lane line ahead, white paint shining with rain.

"What do you need?" he said.

Nina was quiet for a moment. When she spoke again her tone had changed to polished surface with business underneath. "I need Thursday afternoon off the books. Friday morning as well. Possibly Sunday. Chambers, storage, one stop in Notting Hill, and one place in Chelsea Oscar does not need itemized. I could use Mercer and get a different driver every time, but I'd rather not have my life distributed in fragments to men who still call women mad when they mean inconvenient."

"Mercer logs the car."

"Mercer logs what it's told to log."

He said nothing.

"Don't give me the little moral silence now, Ben. You abandoned that months ago."

The words hit cleanly because they were true enough to bruise. He drove through them.

Nina bent, slid her hand into the side pocket of her box, and came up with a cream envelope folded once. It had no monogram and no seal, only weight. She leaned forward between the seats and held it out. Her perfume reached him first, the dry expensive amber of it, then the paper touching the back of his hand because he had not taken it fast enough.

"What's this?"

"An advance."

"For what?"

"For the week. For your discretion. For the fact that poor people are always forced to solve rich people's timing problems." She put the envelope on the centre console instead of back in her lap. "Use the phrase that lets you sleep. Fee. Hazard pay. Seasonal bonus. I don't care."

He glanced down once. It was thick, more than her usual folded twenties.

"I can't take that."

"Of course you can." She gave a short laugh. "You can take money very well. That's part of your charm."

He felt the heat rise up his neck, a dull, angry humiliation. "I mean that much."

"Yes. I know what you meant." Her voice stayed calm. "Take it anyway."

He did not move. The envelope sat between the seats like another passenger.

Nina looked out at the wet traffic, then back at him in the mirror. "Keene told me she has to clear the attic room by Sunday. She wasn't gossiping. She wanted me to intervene, which was touching and idiotic. I'm not going to win a domestic battle with Oscar in the week he's trying to save face for the gala. But I can spare you the expression you're wearing."

"What expression?"

"The one men get when they're counting a woman's problem in banknotes."

He wished she would slap him instead. It would have been cleaner.

"You don't know anything about it," he said.

"I know enough." She nudged the envelope with one finger. Her nail was bare. He noticed that because usually it was painted. "And I know you'll come if I ask. You may as well be paid in a way that does something useful."

"Why?"

"Because I need you, and because I'm not sentimental enough to pretend need is noble." She looked down at the taped box on the seat beside her. "Also because I'm in no mood to watch Oscar's efficiencies turn the whole house Presbyterian."

There it was, one of her stupid good lines, the kind that would have made another man smile. Beni kept his face still.

"Thursday, Friday, maybe Sunday," she said. "After that the gala swallows everything and we can both consider ourselves temporarily released from each other. Doesn't that sound healthy?"

The phrase temporarily released made them sound like reasonable adults ending an arrangement with good administration. As if there had been any arrangement to end except her wanting and him not stopping.

He thought of Eszter in the kitchen with the wet labels curling off jam jars. Two hundred and forty by Friday. Boots for Lili. Oyster fare. He thought of his own wallet, the sixty-three pounds, the ten for diesel, the useless lucky fiver. He thought of Laci's face if rent was late again. He thought of how easy it would be to move numbers around in his head until none of them belonged to the place they came from.

If he took the envelope, it was only for the drives. If he used some of it for Eszter, he could replace it after next week's Mercer wages. If he replaced it before Christmas, then what had really happened? A bridge, that was all. A temporary bridge over a bad week. It was not betrayal if he told Eszter about Nina later, properly, when there wasn't a room and a deadline sitting on the counter between them. No one needed the full truth while the floor was moving. Full truth was a luxury item. He could bring it once the urgent bills were paid.

He heard himself say, "If I take it, it's for the work."

Nina's mouth moved. Not quite a smile. "If that makes you feel cleaner, by all means."

He picked up the envelope and slid it into the inside pocket of his coat. The paper was warm from her hand.

"Good," she said. "Now take me to Lincoln's Inn before my solicitor decides I'm dramatic for being late to my own humiliation."

He drove south and west through traffic thickening into early dark. At the lights near Holborn she opened the first box again and took out the face cream pot, turned it in her fingers, then put it back. By the time he stopped outside chambers, she had her expression set for old carpet, old money, and men who billed in six-minute units.

"Tomorrow at half two," she said before getting out. "And Ben."

He looked back.

"Don't make this tedious by growing a conscience only where money is concerned."

She shut the door without waiting for an answer.

He sat outside the chambers for three minutes after she disappeared inside, engine idling low. Rain ticked on the roof. Somewhere to his left a bus exhaled at the kerb. He took the envelope out and counted in the dim cabin light.

There were six hundred pounds in it: twelve fifties, crisp enough to rasp.

He counted again because the first time had felt like theft and the second time felt like accounting.

* * *

That night in Tottenham, Laci was in the shower singing badly with the radio through the bathroom door, not because he was cheerful but because he was always louder when he was tired. The flat smelled of damp plaster, frying onions from downstairs, and the citrus cleaner somebody had used in the hall to no effect at all. Beni sat on the edge of his foldout bed with the envelope open on the blanket and his own cash laid beside it in a smaller, meaner stack.

He had sixty-three from his wallet and another forty in the tobacco tin under his spare socks. He left the lucky fiver where it was, not from superstition but because some stupid part of him wanted one untouched thing.

He made three piles: Nina's fifties, his twenties and tens, and the money he owed Laci, which at this point was not a pile so much as an accusation extending into the future. The room shuddered once as a bus went by outside.

If he gave Eszter all Nina's notes, even he would be sick looking at them. They were too crisp and too easy, rich money that had never been sweated into shape. So he split them, changed one fifty at the corner shop for smaller notes, bought cigarettes he did not want just to make the exchange look ordinary, then came back and mixed everything together on the bed, with twenties against fifties and tens against twenties, his own worn notes against Nina's clean paper. He told himself he was making the loan honest by dilution.

By the time Laci came out of the shower in his boxers, towelling his hair with one hand, the money was in a plain white envelope from the Chinese takeaway drawer.

Laci nodded at it. "What's that?"

"Nothing."

"That usually means cash."

Beni slid the envelope under his thigh. "Private runs."

Laci snorted. Water still ran down his shoulders and into the waistband of his boxers. He smelled of cheap shower gel and plaster dust that never fully left him. "Then stop staring at it like it called you a cunt."

He went to the kitchenette corner, opened the fridge, and swore at its contents in Hungarian. Beni was grateful. Anger about old cheese and someone else's lager cost less than other kinds.

When Eszter texted just after nine, she did not write anything soft. `Can you come tomorrow after shift? Need help carrying one suitcase.`

He wrote back, `Yes.`

Then, after a minute: `I'll bring tape if you need it.`

Her answer came at once. `Bring shoulders. I have tape.`

The next day the attic room over the Ashdowns' top floor had already begun to stop being hers. That was the ugliest part. The room had always been narrow and mean, a space under the eaves with a single bed, a wardrobe that smelled faintly of cedar blocks, and one sash window that rattled in wind from the square. The radiator banged like a trapped ghost every time it came on. But until now it had contained the shape of Eszter's life in London: two paperbacks in Hungarian, a jar of paprika wrapped in a sock so it would not stain, a photograph of Lili in a yellow raincoat held to the mirror frame by a supermarket receipt, nail scissors, remittance stubs clipped together with a blue hair grip, and nothing decorative except a tea towel from Debrecen with geese stitched on it in red thread and hung over the chair back as if that made the chair less borrowed.

Now half of it was in open bags on the floor.

Eszter was kneeling beside the bed folding T-shirts with the brutal speed of somebody who knew delay only made leaving last longer. She had changed out of her work clothes into leggings and an old sweatshirt with the logo of a cleaning company she no longer worked for. There was dust in the loose hair at the back of her neck.

"You weren't joking about the suitcase," Beni said.

"I'm very funny under pressure. It just doesn't always travel."

He shut the door behind him. The room felt even smaller with both of them in it. Under the sloped ceiling he had to angle one shoulder without thinking. A roll of bin bags, a packet of zip ties, and a bottle of cheap hand cream sat on the bedspread in a tidy line, as if organization itself might count as backup.

"What time do you need to be out?"

"Sunday afternoon. But Keene wants the room stripped tomorrow so the florist can stack boxes in here next week. Imagine dying for lilies and then ending up above a woman from Szeged." She stuffed socks into the side pocket of the suitcase. "Take the blue bag after this. The church room is available from tomorrow night."

"You've got it, then."

"If I turn up with the money and if the woman likes my face." Eszter stood and reached for the wardrobe rail. "Her name is Mrs. Varga, which I told Marika is already suspiciously intimate."

A smile tugged once at his mouth. "Maybe she's family."

"Then she definitely won't give me a discount."

She pulled down two blouses on wire hangers and laid them on the bed. Her movements were efficient, but her mouth had gone thin with tiredness. There were purple half-moons under her eyes. She had put on lip balm at some point and forgotten to rub it in fully; the shine made the split in her lower lip more visible. The room smelled of dust, old radiator paint, and the almond hand cream she used on cracked skin. It tightened something in his chest. Everything here was small enough to carry, which felt like its own insult.

"How much exactly?" he asked.

"Don't start."

"I'm asking."

Eszter turned, one hanger in each hand. "Two forty to hold the room. Another thirty or so in fares until next pay. I can float the rest if I eat like a monk and Lili doesn't suddenly need another winter organ."

"And the money home?"

"That is the rest."

He took the white envelope from inside his coat and put it on the bed between the folded shirts. The mattress gave under it with a tired little dip. Once the paper touched her blanket, the lie stopped being numbers in his head and became an object in her room.

Eszter looked at it, then at him. "No."

"Just listen."

"No."

"Eszter."

"Don't 'Eszter' me like I'm being unreasonable about free money appearing from a nineteen-year-old driver who still owes his cousin rent."

Heat climbed up his neck. Nineteen sounded younger in her mouth, like he'd come upstairs in a borrowed suit to play lender for an afternoon.

There it was, exact as a knife. He could not even resent it because it was true. He kept his hand on the envelope.

"It's not free," he said. "It's a loan."

"From what?"

"Private runs. Nina's got extra things before the gala."

She laughed once, hard and unbelieving. "Yes, and I'm the Queen."

"I'm serious."

"So am I. You don't have three hundred pounds to lend me unless you robbed a bank with a very stupid mask."

"It's from work."

"From Mercer?"

"From Nina."

That made her stop. She did not soften; she just stopped and thought. "What kind of work?"

He kept his face steady and hated that the skill had become easy. "Off-book errands. Same as before. She pays cash when she wants things done quietly."

"Quietly." Eszter looked at the envelope again. "I don't like that woman buying pieces of my week."

"She's not. I'm lending it."

"From her."

"From me. I pay it back when Mercer clears next week."

"And if Mercer doesn't."

"It will."

"You don't know that."

"I know enough."

She folded one of the blouses over the other, buying time by making them align. When she was frightened she reached for straight edges. He had seen that in the kitchen with jars, with cutlery, with a tray of washed grapes she had once sorted by the ones already splitting. Now she squared the blouse cuffs until they matched exactly.

"I don't want to owe Nina Ashdown oxygen," she said.

"Then don't. Owe me for a week and call me an idiot later."

"I already call you an idiot now. That's not incentive."

The word please pressed against his teeth. He would not lay that on the bed beside Nina's money. Instead he opened the envelope and counted onto the bedspread: four fifties, four twenties, two tens. Three hundred, in mixed notes that no longer advertised where they had begun. He pushed them toward her in a small untidy stack.

"Take what you need," he said.

Eszter stared at the money. The room was so quiet he could hear the radiator knock in the wall and the distant vacuum cleaner from somewhere below. She touched the top note with one finger and pulled her hand back.

"You'll need this yourself."

"Not by Friday."

"Laci will still be Laci by Friday."

"He can wait a week."

"Can he?"

"He'll have to."

That landed because it was one of the few hard things Beni could say and make sound true. Eszter sat down on the bed beside the pile of notes and rubbed at the bridge of her nose with thumb and forefinger.

"I hate this," she said.

"I know."

"No, you don't. I hate needing anything from anyone in this city. It makes me feel like my skin belongs to other people."

He sat beside her, careful not to crowd. "Then take the room. Pay me back when you can. Hate that separately."

She looked at him sideways. "That is almost clever."

"Almost."

"Still annoying."

She counted out two hundred and forty with the speed of someone who had done this with worse money in worse rooms. Four fifties. Two twenties. She left the last two twenties and both tens on the bedspread.

"Take the rest back," she said.

"Keep it for fares."

"I said fares were thirty. I'm not suddenly buying truffles because you got dramatic."

"Eszter."

"Beni."

He let it go. She took one of the tens after a second and tucked it under the others. It made two hundred and fifty, enough for the room and a little breathing space, not enough to feel like rescue.

"I'll give it back after Christmas," she said.

"Whenever."

"No. After Christmas. I don't want this sitting between us longer than that."

He heard the phrase and nearly answered too fast. Sitting between us. As if there were only the money there.

She took the notes and slid them into a brown envelope already addressed in her own hand to Mrs. Varga, Holloway Road. On the back she had written `room + key` in block capitals. Then she tucked that envelope into the zip pocket of her passport holder, behind the photograph of Lili in the yellow raincoat. Beni watched Nina's money disappear behind Eszter's daughter's face.

For a second he felt almost relieved. It had become practical now, filed and directed instead of being dirty paper in a man's coat. Room plus key made it look like something solvable.

Eszter let out a breath and looked around the half-emptied room as if seeing it properly for the first time. "All right," she said. "Now lift the suitcase before I decide to burn the house down on principle."

He took the case. Heavy with books and shoes and the dense ordinary things that made a life weigh more than it looked. They carried it down the back stairs together, bumping once at the turn because the wheel caught on the strip of brass. No one saw them until the basement, where Mrs. Keene was pinning new menus to the service board with the focused misery of a woman who knew exactly how many canapes were still required in a crisis.

Her eyes went to the suitcase and away again. "Leave it by the laundry door," she said. Then, lower, to Eszter, "I spoke to Marika. The room is decent."

Eszter nodded. "Thank you."

Mrs. Keene pushed the pin in harder than necessary. "I'm sorry about this."

It was the first thing all day that sounded like apology and not procedure.

They took the blue bag out to Beni's car by the mews. Rain had stopped, but the air still held it. The stone underfoot gave back a weak damp shine. Somewhere nearby somebody was testing Christmas lights and failing; a relay clicked on and off behind a wall.

Eszter stood with her arms folded while he put the bag in the boot. Without the kitchen around her she looked smaller, though he knew she wasn't. Just more exposed. The cold had reddened the tip of her nose.

"Tomorrow after your shift?" he said. "I'll take the rest."

"You don't have to."

"I know."

She nodded once. Then she stepped forward, hooked two fingers into the front of his coat, and pulled him down enough to kiss him. Her mouth was cool from the air, then warmer. She tasted of tea and the mint gum she chewed on long cleaning days. He put his free hand on the side of her neck and kissed her back with more hunger than the mews deserved.

The remaining fifty from the white envelope was still in his inside pocket. When Eszter pressed close, the folded notes made a hard edge against his chest, square and unmistakable. He felt it between them while he kissed her. He could not stop feeling it. Fifty wasn't the rest, and even with her mouth on his he was already counting the other thirty he would have to find before tomorrow. By the time she let him go, the help had already gone where it needed to go, and there was no clean way to tell himself it had not brought something else with it.
