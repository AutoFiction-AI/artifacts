# Chapter 4

Nina came down from the townhouse at twenty past one in a camel coat belted too tight over a cream blouse and narrow black trousers. By the time Beni turned from Chesham Place onto Knightsbridge she was fastening one earring in the back seat and they were already headed from Belgravia to Mount Street through a rain that never committed to falling hard, only kept needling the windscreen until the wipers squeaked at the edges. She smelled of cold air, face cream with a rose-and-wax finish, and the nicotine gum she chewed when she did not want to look like a woman who needed anything.

"Forty minutes," she said, looking at herself in the black glass of her phone instead of at him. "Park somewhere sensible. Bea needs collecting at three forty-five."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

"And if Oscar rings, I'm at lunch."

She gave him a small smile with that, the social one, all agreement and no warmth. It was not a request. It was the line she was drawing on the day, thin as the gold on her watch.

He dropped her near the far end of Mount Street, not directly outside any restaurant. She stepped out without umbrella or hesitation, then bent back down to the open door.

"If I'm delayed, I'll text."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

Her coat swung behind her as she crossed the pavement. She did not look back. At the corner she passed a restaurant awning and kept walking, east instead of west, disappearing between a jeweller and a gallery where three men in navy raincoats were unloading crates that looked more secure than most flats Beni had slept in.

He parked on Duke Street, paid for an hour because paying for ninety minutes felt like admitting the truth of rich people's timings, and let the engine tick down into silence. The Mercedes held the day inside it: damp wool from Nina's coat, a ghost of Bea's strawberry lip balm from the morning school run, his own burnt coffee in the cup holder, the leather warmth left by bodies that never had to take night buses home. A Mercer driver in a BMW two cars back got out to smoke beneath a plane tree and nodded once in the flat way men nodded when conversation would only make the wait feel longer.

Waiting never settled in Beni's body the way driving did. Behind a wheel, every problem came with a lane, a mirror, a pedal. Parked, he became more aware of money: the next rent payment, the part of the nine hundred he still owed Laci, the fact that Nina's cash rides had started to feel less like luck than a second job built out of whatever she did not want written down. He ate the flattened heel of a croissant from the glove box, brushed flakes off his trousers, checked the time twice in a minute without learning anything useful from it.

At fourteen minutes past two her name lit his screen.

He answered before the first vibration had finished. "Mrs. Ashdown."

"Where are you?"

"Duke Street."

He heard a short breath at her end, not relief exactly. "Go back to the house. In my dressing room there's a white Boots bag on the blue chair and a black catalogue with a grey paper band round it. Bring them to Jonas."

There was traffic noise behind her, but not traffic. Something metal clattered. A door rolled somewhere behind her. Then a man's voice sounded too far from the phone to separate into words.

Beni looked through the windscreen at the wet shine on the pavement, the doorman outside the Connaught pretending not to notice drivers who lingered too long, the neat fiction of lunch continuing all around him. "Now?"

"Yes, now." Her voice sharpened, then corrected itself. "Please. I left in a hurry."

He said nothing.

"And use the side entrance," she went on. "Oscar's working from home. Don't ask anyone unnecessary. Just get the things and bring them. Text when you're downstairs."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

The line went dead. She offered no explanation, and he knew none was invited.

He pulled out into traffic and headed back southwest. From Mount Street to the Ashdown house was quick if the lights behaved. From the house to Hackney Wick and back to Belgravia before Bea's pickup was not impossible, but it was not a lunch delay either. From Wilton Crescent to Jonas Hale's studio by the canal and back again was a little under nineteen miles if London kept its hands to itself. London never did. By the time he slid past Hyde Park Corner and took the turn toward Eaton Square, he had already started measuring the extra distance against the afternoon sheet in his head.

The side door of the townhouse opened off the mews, where the bins were hidden in painted cupboards and the cobbles got washed more often than the stairwell in Laci's building ever had. Beni let himself in with the service key Nina had started leaving under a porcelain jardiniere when she wanted him useful and unofficial. The house met him the usual way: polish, lilies, radiator heat, and the smell of food someone else had made but probably would not sit down properly to eat.

He found Eszter in the basement kitchen slicing an apple into eighths over the sink. She had her hair tied back with a black elastic and one of the Ashdowns' tea towels tucked into the waistband of her skirt, a homemade third hand. A little heap of salt sat on the chopping board for the apple slices. Her left thumb was wrapped in a flesh-coloured plaster already going dark at the edge from dishwater.

"You're back early," she said in Hungarian, without surprise. "I thought the lady was lunching with civilization."

"She forgot something."

"Only one thing?" Eszter dusted salt over an apple slice and bit into it. "Restraint. Impressive."

He almost smiled. "Is Mr. Ashdown upstairs?"

"In the study. Talking softly at money." She nodded toward the service stairs. "If you go up, don't drip on the runner. This house always blames the wrong person."

She held out an apple slice to him. He took it because refusing always felt more revealing than eating. The apple was sharp and cold, the salt making it taste like something served on a plate instead of eaten over a sink.

"Second floor?" he asked.

"Her dressing room, yes. Door's open. She was looking for that catalogue this morning and swearing like she'd misplaced a child."

"Thanks."

Eszter's gaze went to his face for half a second longer than needed. Her look was not suspicious, just alert. She missed very little when a house changed temperature.

"Eat later," she said. "You look like you've been parked instead of fed."

He put the apple slice in his mouth and went up.

The service stairs were narrow and hot. On the first floor he could hear Oscar somewhere behind a closed door, speaking in the calm stripped tone men used when the figures were bad enough to make politeness useful. Beni caught only fragments through the thickness of the house.

"...not a question of appetite..."

"...mark it honestly, then..."

"...tomorrow morning, not tonight..."

The words stopped mattering as soon as he reached the second floor and stepped into Nina's part of the house, where everything softened into suede, silk, and low lamp light. Her dressing room was bigger than the front room he slept in at Laci's. Dresses hung in garment bags along one wall. Shoes stood in pairs on lit shelves, the leather and satin colours arranged so carefully they looked obedient. A glass of water sat on the vanity with a crescent of lipstick at the rim. One earring back lay beside it, a gold speck no bigger than a breadcrumb. On a chaise at the far end, an evening shawl had slid half onto the floor and stayed there because nobody in this house was paid to decide whether Nina wanted disorder preserved or removed.

The white Boots bag was on the blue chair exactly where she had said. The black catalogue lay underneath, heavier than it looked, bound with a grey paper band printed in small silver letters. He did not read them. The bag held a blister pack, a prescription box with her surname on the label, and an amber bottle of tablets that clicked against his knuckles when he lifted it. There was also a folded receipt and a lipstick. She had clearly emptied half a pocket while leaving in a hurry and expected the world to carry the pieces after her.

He took both items and left without looking at anything twice.

Back in the kitchen, Eszter was rinsing the knife. She glanced at the bag, the catalogue, then at him.

"Life or death?" she asked.

"Probably not."

"That is where the good jobs live."

He tucked the catalogue under his arm. "I'll be back before Bea."

"You say that like I control time."

"No. Only food."

That earned him a short laugh, surprised out of her more than offered. It followed him up the stairs and out into the mews, a warmer thing than the apple had been.

The drive east tightened around him almost immediately. Knightsbridge stalled. Park Lane was a slow glitter of brake lights and wet taxis. The radio murmured about rail strikes and a minister resigning over something nobody would remember in a month. Beni kept it low and watched the map on the console fatten with traffic colour. He knew faster routes by habit and slower ones by distrust. Today he took Marylebone Road because Euston Road at that hour could eat a school pickup alive, then cut south of Angel and out toward the canal where the city started pretending industry was a style rather than a wage.

By the time he crossed City Road the trip had already added more miles than Nina's lunch was supposed to contain. The number on the dash kept rising in plain digital calm while he sat in traffic beside men in white vans and cyclists with takeaway bags strapped to their backs. Any of them could say exactly where they were going. He was carrying a rich woman's pills and a glossy art catalogue across town so another rich man would not have to wait for either. There was no way to say that aloud without sounding either ridiculous or bought.

He had been to Hackney Wick only twice before, both times dropping people who wanted rough brick and water towers behind whatever glossy version of themselves they were making. Jonas Hale's building sat near a run of studios and breweries in an old warehouse block with a fresh black gate and security cameras small enough to miss unless you were looking for them. Graffiti climbed the lower brickwork. The intercom plate had too many surnames on it and one small gold plaque with HALE taped over the original lettering, temporary and vain at once.

Beni texted the number Nina had used.

Downstairs.

The reply came at once.

Come up. 3rd floor. Don't ring again.

He parked badly on purpose because there was nowhere legal to put the car and no point pretending Hackney Wick would offer a neat Belgravia solution to a Belgravia problem. He took the bag and catalogue and crossed the yard past a pile of broken pallets slick with rain. Somewhere nearby someone was grinding metal. The sound went through the building like dental work.

The freight lift smelled of damp plywood, spray paint, and old piss under citrus cleaner. On the third floor the corridor had been painted white badly enough to keep the industrial bones visible. Jonas's studio door stood open three inches, then wider when Beni knocked with the side of his fist.

Jonas Hale answered in a black T-shirt dusted with plaster and loose charcoal trousers rolled above the ankle. He wore soft suede slippers with one heel flattened underfoot and had a nicotine patch peeling at the top edge of his left arm, lint stuck to the glue where he'd worried it with his fingers. He was younger than Nina by enough to be obvious but old enough to know exactly what use he made of it. The first thing Beni noticed, after the patch, was that Jonas smelled not of paint or cigarettes but of cedar or vetiver hand soap, the kind that lived beside a stone basin in brown glass.

"Yes?" Jonas said.

"Mrs. Ashdown asked me to bring these."

Jonas's gaze dropped to the Boots bag, then the catalogue. Something like contempt passed over his face so quickly it almost qualified as boredom.

"Of course she did."

He stepped back just far enough for Beni to enter the threshold. The studio beyond was huge and cluttered without ever losing the look of a place buffered from consequence. At the Ashdown house, effort disappeared into polish and flowers changed before they drooped. Here effort had been staged as temperament. Plaster dust filmed the concrete floor. Three unfinished sculptures stood under work lights like bandaged bodies. A low cream sofa sagged deep at the middle, paint drying on one arm. Wine bottles crowded a metal sink beside a line of Japanese knives. There were shipping crates stencilled from Basel, a Bluetooth speaker the size of a suitcase, and tulips collapsing elegantly in a chipped champagne bucket. A silk scarf too fine for the room had been kicked halfway under a welding glove. The mess was real enough to smell, but even the disorder had been arranged into a signature.

From deeper in the studio Nina called, "Is that Beni?"

Jonas turned his head only a fraction. "Your supplies have arrived."

She appeared a second later from behind a canvas screen used to divide the sleeping area from the rest of the room. She was barefoot, carrying one heel in her hand and already sliding it on as she crossed the floor. Her blouse had been fastened wrong then corrected; one button still pulled. Her lipstick was blurred at the right corner of her mouth, and there was a pale streak of clay or plaster on the sleeve of her coat where she had brushed against something. She looked at Beni first, not Jonas, the way a person looked at the one thing in a room that might still function.

"Thank God," she said. "I knew you'd sort it."

There was no embarrassment in the words. There was gratitude, yes, but it carried ownership inside it.

He held out the bag and catalogue. "From your dressing room, Mrs. Ashdown."

She set the heel down, opened the bag, and shook two white tablets into her palm from the blister pack with hands steadier than her voice. She swallowed them dry. The muscles in her throat worked twice. For a second her face lost its polish and became simply a face that hurt.

"God, that's vile." She took the catalogue under her arm. "Thank you."

Jonas had already turned away to adjust something on a table, aligning a small plaster maquette by millimetres and giving it more attention than the scene in his studio. "This is absurd," he said, not quite to either of them. "A courier for tablets and an art catalogue. We're one floral arrangement away from a hotel."

Nina's jaw tightened. "Don't be tiresome."

"I'm not being tiresome. I'm observing scale."

Beni kept his eyes on the strip of floor in front of him. He had learned early that rich people preferred witnesses who could be mistaken for furniture, but he had also learned furniture did not usually get spoken at with this much sharpness. That made him too present in the room for his own comfort.

Nina slid the pills back into the bag and held the catalogue against her hip. "Give me five minutes," she said to Beni, making it sound like a timetable adjustment rather than an order. "We'll go straight back."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

Jonas gave a short laugh through his nose. "You see? Infrastructure."

The word landed in the open space between Beni and Nina and stayed there.

He waited outside in the corridor because staying in the studio felt worse. The door remained ajar. Through the gap he heard nothing clear enough to count as content, only the shapes of pressure: Nina's voice stretching longer than usual, Jonas answering briefly, the knock of something set down too hard, then silence that meant more than the sentences had. Once, very clearly, he heard Nina say, "Don't be childish." A few seconds later, lower, he heard the word please. After that he stopped trying to separate one sound from another.

The corridor window looked over the canal and a yard full of stacked materials. A young woman in an oversized waxed jacket smoked beneath the fire escape while staring at her phone, one hand absently rubbing the ear of a small black dog tucked under her arm. The dog wore a mustard knit jumper and looked bored out of its life. A courier dragged a crate across the concrete below, swearing at a stuck wheel. On the opposite roof somebody had planted rosemary in paint tubs. Hackney Wick sold damage back to people as atmosphere. Beni had seen cheaper versions of the same brick and rust in Hungary with nobody taking photographs of them.

After twelve minutes Nina came out with both heels on, her coat closed, and her face reassembled. Only the missing softness around her mouth gave away how recently she had been something other than composed. She no longer carried the catalogue. The Boots bag was folded flat in her hand.

"Let's go."

Beni turned toward the lift. As he reached the button Jonas came to the door behind them. He had found a nail file from somewhere and was working at the edge of one thumbnail with bored concentration, folding even that into the afternoon's art practice.

"Nina," he said, "if you need half of Belgravia to make a visit possible, maybe shorten the visits."

She did not turn. "Grow up."

Then his gaze shifted past her to Beni, landing on him with fresh irritation. The sight of him spoiled the arrangement.

"Does she send all her errands through boys now," he asked, "or are you a special feature?"

Nina stopped then, one heel lifted a fraction off the concrete. Beni could not tell whether she had expected the insult or hoped it would not be spoken in front of him. She still did not look back.

"I drive where I'm told, sir," he said.

Jonas gave a thin smile, not kind enough to be called one. "Yes. That was clear."

The lift arrived with a thud. Nina got in first. Beni followed. As the doors closed he saw Jonas already turn away, rubbing at the nicotine patch again, bored with the damage as soon as he had made it.

In the car Nina did not tell him where to go because there was nowhere else to go. He pulled out from the canal and headed west. For the first few minutes she said nothing. She sat in the rear left seat, though no one else was with them, her usual position when she wanted the car to function as a room rather than a vehicle. She folded the empty Boots bag once, twice, then tore the chemist receipt into strips so small they stuck to her fingers.

At a red light by Old Street she said, "You can put the air up a bit."

He adjusted it.

Another minute passed. The rain thickened, then thinned again. Cyclists slashed between buses. A siren went somewhere north and kept going.

"Did he say anything else?" Nina asked.

Beni watched a white van edge half into his lane. "No, Mrs. Ashdown."

It was not a full lie yet. Jonas had said enough without giving Beni anything useful to report.

She let out a breath and leaned her head back. "He always does that. He acts as if being needed is an offence against good taste."

Beni said nothing.

She looked at her own reflection in the window, saw him there as well, and went on because he did not interrupt. "I forgot the pills because I was already late. Then I forgot the catalogue because I was trying not to look like someone who needed to bring a catalogue at all. Ridiculous. All of it."

She laughed once, with no humour inside it, and rubbed at the heel of her hand where the torn receipt paper had left a grey smudge.

"I shouldn't have gone today," she said. "He told me he was working. That ought to have been enough information for a sane person."

The word sane hung there longer than it wanted to. She seemed to hear it herself and straighten against it.

"Anyway." She pressed two fingers to the bridge of her nose. "Thank you for not making a face."

He caught her eyes in the mirror for a second. "Mrs. Ashdown?"

"Most people do. They either look pleased you're in trouble, or they look disgusted, or they try to help in some eager way that makes everything worse. You just do the thing."

"I just drove."

"Yes," she said. "That's why I asked you."

The car moved again. Her voice had softened, but the softness did not take the hierarchy out of it. If anything it made it more exact. She did not want understanding from him. She wanted function without commentary, a human extension cord she did not have to thank properly because the current was what mattered.

By the time they turned back into Belgravia she had powdered her nose with the mirror in her phone and fixed the lipstick well enough that only someone who had seen the earlier version would know the difference. She folded the empty Boots bag into her handbag, looked once at the house, and said, "If Bea asks, I was at lunch."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

She got out before he could come round to open her door. That, more than anything, told him the pills had not yet fully taken hold.

The mews held the kind of late-afternoon yellow that made even clean stone look tired. Beni parked in the garage and killed the engine. The car gave back the house sounds slowly: a delivery van reversing on the next street, cutlery somewhere inside, a plumber's radio from further down the mews. He checked the dash. The run had added exactly what he had known it would add, plus a little more for the crawl back through Marylebone: enough miles to stand out if anyone took the trouble to line them against Nina's lunch.

He was reaching for the afternoon sheet when Oscar Ashdown appeared in the garage doorway.

Oscar was already in his coat though he was not yet leaving, tie perfect, hair still damp at the temples from whatever quick shower or sink-wash he used to turn one part of the day into another. He held his phone and a slim silver tin in the same hand. Beni had once seen him shake fennel seeds from it after lunch instead of taking coffee with the rest of the table. Now he flipped the tin open with his thumb, neat enough to make chewing look procedural.

"Beni," he said. "One small question."

"Sir."

Oscar tipped a few seeds into his palm but did not eat them yet. He looked at the dashboard, then at the sheet in Beni's hand. He did not look suspicious exactly. He looked exact.

"Nina told me she was at lunch on Mount Street and then straight home for Bea." His tone remained courteous enough that a stupid man could have mistaken it for ease. "The mileage is high for that. Did she add a stop?"

There was time, in the space after the question, to tell the truth in a thin practical version. Mrs. Ashdown asked me to collect forgotten medication and a catalogue and take them across town to Jonas Hale's studio. He could imagine the sentence cleanly because roads always arranged themselves in order for him. That did not make the sentence survivable.

He thought of Nina in the studio swallowing tablets dry because she could not bear to ask Jonas for water. He thought of Jonas saying infrastructure and making the word sound like a joke. He thought of the house around them, the service stairs, Eszter's apple slice salted against the sink, Bea needing collecting in less than half an hour, the nine hundred pounds still split over his month like a threat. All of it sat there in the pause with Oscar waiting politely for numbers to explain themselves.

"There was traffic, sir," he said. His voice sounded steady enough to belong to another driver. "Mrs. Ashdown asked me to keep moving rather than sit outside. Then she needed a pharmacy stop before we came back."

Oscar watched him. "A pharmacy where?"

Beni had passed three Boots and one independent chemist on the return and could have named each frontage by colour. He picked the one nearest the lie's shape.

"Sloane Avenue, sir."

Oscar finally put the fennel seeds in his mouth. He chewed once, twice. "That still seems ambitious."

"Park Lane was blocked and Knightsbridge hardly moved. I had to loop up through Marylebone."

Oscar's gaze stayed on him long enough for Beni to understand that politeness could be a form of pressure more efficient than anger. If Oscar had raised his voice, Beni might have felt only insult. This way he felt the calculation. The man was weighing not just whether the story was true but whether it was useful to let it remain true for now.

"I see," Oscar said at last.

He closed the tin. "Next time the route changes materially, let the office know. Untidy numbers make people curious, and curiosity is usually expensive."

"Yes, sir."

Oscar gave a short nod, half thanks, half dismissal, and left the garage. His shoes made almost no sound on the concrete.

Beni stayed where he was. The car had cooled enough that the leather no longer held the shape of Nina's body. In the rearview mirror he could see the empty back seat, the pale crease where her coat had pressed into it, one tiny strip of receipt paper caught in the floor mat like fish skin. He reached back, picked it up, and rubbed it between finger and thumb until it softened.

The lie came in three pieces: traffic, pharmacy, Marylebone.

They were three clean pieces, easy enough to repeat. It was a story built from road names and errands, the kind of story a driver could tell because drivers were expected to know how cities wasted time. He had carried richer people's excuses before in silence: late train, wrong entrance, bad phone signal, she felt ill. He had let them settle around him and had not called that lying because nothing had been asked directly of his mouth.

This was different. Oscar had asked. Beni had answered. The falsehood had crossed his tongue with the same plainness as any route instruction. What made his stomach go light was not that he had protected Nina. He had been protecting Nina since the day she first told dispatch she wanted him by name. The change was smaller and worse. He had heard himself do it and recognized how usable the sound was.

He folded the strip of chemist receipt once more and put it in the bin by the workbench. Then he sat back in the driver's seat with both hands on the wheel and waited for the taste of the lie to leave his mouth. It stayed there, chalk-dry and ordinary. By the time he started the engine again, it was no longer only Nina's.
