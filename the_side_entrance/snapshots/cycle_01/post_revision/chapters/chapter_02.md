# Chapter 2

The job sheet said `ASHDOWN, BELGRAVIA, 11:30, BY REQUEST` in the dispatcher's block capitals. Mercer had put him back on the Ashdown account by Tuesday as if the Saturday night drive had been an ordinary success instead of the sort that kept humming in his nerves two days later. Beni looked at the last two words a second longer than he needed to. Requested meant tipped well sometimes. It also meant remembered.

Rain had dried to a greasy shine on the roads but not on the parked cars. The S-Class smelled of leather gone slightly sour from other people's coats, stale perfume deep in the seams, and the faint hot-plastic breath of the dash vents. He liked all of it. Inside a good car the world reduced itself to useful things: mirrors, blind spots, lanes that opened or closed, the soft resistance of the wheel when he corrected too fast. People were worse because they wanted to be handled without admitting it.

Belgravia in daylight looked less beautiful than expensive: white stucco, black railings, windows washed to a cold shine. The Ashdown townhouse sat in one of the terraces like a mouth set against being photographed badly. He did not stop at the front. The text from the house had sent him round to the mews behind, where the real work arrived: florist van, laundry sacks, two crates of mineral water, a man in a navy gilet swearing softly because one of the bins had split at the bottom and leaked coffee grounds into the wet.

The garage door was already up. Heat came out carrying polish, radiator dust, and something rich from downstairs kitchens, onions maybe, or stock. The house breathed from the back like a body that had to keep its face clean while its organs did the dirty part.

He had just killed the engine when a woman from the house office came down the side steps with a clipboard. She wore trainers under a black skirt and kept pushing her glasses back up with the heel of her hand because both palms were occupied by paper. "Mrs. Ashdown will be two minutes," she said. "Please keep the car ready."

"Yes."

She was gone before he had properly answered. Thirty seconds later movement smeared across the frosted pane: a tray with coffee cups went up; a school blazer came down over someone's arm in clear plastic; a man in shirtsleeves crossed the hall behind the glass, paused to check a watch, and disappeared. Nothing about it looked frantic. Everything about it was timed. The whole place moved like a schedule nobody had written for guests to see.

Nina Ashdown came out without hurry, though hurry had clearly arranged her. Tall boots, dark blond hair smoothed into place, a cream silk blouse under a coat the colour of wet camel hair. In daylight she looked less drunk than expensive and more tired than either. She slid into the rear seat and closed the door herself.

"Good morning, Beni."

"Good morning, Mrs. Ashdown."

She gave a small smile at the formal answer, not because she found it funny but because it confirmed something she already thought about him. "We'll stay here for a minute. I don't actually need to go anywhere yet."

He turned slightly in his seat. "Of course."

From the mirror he saw her taking off one glove finger by finger. Her hands were well kept, though the skin around one nail had been picked raw. She set the gloves beside her and looked out through the rain-dulled glass at the mews wall rather than at him.

"You drove very well on Saturday," she said. "More importantly, you did not feel compelled to become conversational."

Beni said nothing. Silence was safer ground than gratitude.

"Mercer sends me boys who think eye contact is a form of service," she went on. "Or they try to sound invisible and end up sounding sulky. You, at least, understand the distinction."

"Thank you."

She tapped one nail against the phone in her lap. "I want to use you directly. Sometimes for ordinary things. School pickups if the regular arrangement changes. Daytime errands. Late collections. Sometimes simply to wait while I'm somewhere I would rather not have entered into a route summary."

He watched a delivery cyclist pedal past the alley mouth and vanish. "You mean outside Mercer bookings."

"Not entirely outside. I am not an idiot." The line came out cool, almost bored, then she softened it a fraction. "Mercer can continue to know that a car is available to this house. Mercer doesn't need to know every address attached to my day."

Beni kept his hands resting on his thighs so she would not see him count. Mercer wages were due on Friday. He had eighty-six pounds in his wallet after topping up his Oyster and buying groceries that did not look like groceries because he'd stripped them of price stickers before putting them in Laci's fridge. One hundred and forty-three pounds sat in his account, and that was if the gas direct debit had not landed early. Laci wanted the rent debt reduced before the weather turned cold enough to make every shower and radiator argument sharper. Nine hundred pounds sat over Beni's sleep like a plank.

"What would the arrangement be?" he asked.

Now she looked at him properly in the mirror. Her face sharpened when practicalities entered. Whatever had happened in Hackney Wick on Saturday night had not made her messy. If anything it had made her more exact. "Two hundred a week in cash for being reachable and discreet. If I keep you half the night or send you somewhere absurd, more than that. If Mercer has you on another client, I'm capable of waiting an hour like a grown woman. What I'm not interested in is commentary, moral concern, or the possibility that Oscar's office learns my movements because a dispatcher enjoys a spreadsheet."

The number landed in him with humiliating force. Two hundred was not fantasy money or rich-people nonsense. It was food, travel, and a slice off the debt. It was Laci not standing in the doorway with his hand on the curtain rail and saying this was the week Beni found somewhere else to sleep.

"And if someone asks?" Beni said.

"Then you were where I told you to be." She leaned back. "This is not a temporary emergency, Beni. I need the condition of discretion, not the occasional favour. If that worries you, say so now and I will find someone stupider."

The mildness of it made the pressure cleaner. She was offering him money and work, yes, but also telling him the price was not negotiable because the price was his shape. He knew the shape already. He had built a life inside it: quiet, useful, absent when required, present when summoned.

"I can do it," he said.

"Good."

She opened her handbag and took out an envelope, plain white, unsealed. She had written `B` on the front in a quick slant that still looked as if she had been educated somewhere expensive. "For this week. We can see whether we suit one another."

He reached back and took it. The paper was thick. There were four fifties inside. The feel of the notes through the envelope was almost indecent.

"Keep your phone on," she said. "And don't save me under my full name if you're one of those men who lets people look through his messages."

"No."

That smile came again, thinner this time. "I should hope not."

A latch clicked behind him. A voice from inside said something about twelve-fifteen and Bea's tutor. Nina put one glove back on. By the time she looked at him again she had become the version of herself the front rooms used.

"Take me to Motcomb Street first," she said. "Then wait. I may need to go east later, and I don't want the house calling Mercer if I change my mind."

He started the engine. As he pulled out of the mews she was already texting, head bent over the pale blue light, arranging the next part of the day with the face of a woman who expected the day to arrange itself around her and got angry only when it did not.

He dropped her, waited, moved the car twice for deliveries, took her to a gallery office in Mayfair, then back toward Belgravia for lunch she did not eat in the car. The envelope stayed in his inside pocket, flat against his ribs. Each time he shifted in the seat he felt the thickness of it and then felt ashamed of feeling it.

By the time he got back to Tottenham the sky had gone the colour of dishwater. The windows on the estate held a wet shine that made even the broken frames look sharp. He tapped his card for bread, eggs, and the cheapest sliced ham from the corner shop, then stood outside a cashpoint under a leaking awning and checked his balance before taking anything out.

He withdrew sixty anyway. The machine made that dry mechanical cough before the notes came. At the shop next door he spent ten of it topping up his Oyster because not topping it up was how a small emergency turned into a public humiliation at seven in the morning. That left him three hundred and thirty-six pounds in cash if he counted Nina's envelope, and counting Nina's envelope made him feel as if he had already agreed to more than driving.

The flat smelled of damp plaster and old frying oil. Somebody upstairs was dragging furniture or pretending to kill it. A bus went past on the high road and the vibration shivered the thin glass in the front room where Beni slept behind a curtain that did not quite close. Laci's work boots were by the radiator, toes white with dried dust. His hi-vis jacket hung from the chair back. That meant he was home early, which was never good.

Laci came out of the kitchenette rubbing at one knee through his work trousers. He was only thirty-two but the skin around his eyes had collapsed into the tired lines of an older man. Plaster dust lived in his hair and in the seam of one ear no matter how hard he showered. He had a cheap menthol heat patch stuck to the side of his neck and a supermarket receipt folded into the pocket of his T-shirt, ready to be used as proof in some future argument about what things cost now.

"You're late," he said in Hungarian.

"I was working."

"Good. Then maybe today you brought money instead of another story."

Beni put the bread and eggs down and took the notes from his wallet first, then the envelope from inside his jacket. He disliked the small greed that sharpened Laci's attention when cash came into view. He disliked even more that he understood it.

"I've got some," he said.

"Some." Laci gave a short laugh with no amusement in it. "You owe me nine hundred. The gas bill came. The landlord wants the leak looked at and naturally that means me, not him. Some is a word for olives, Bence. Not for rent."

Beni counted out the money on the table: four fifties and six twenties. The pile came to three hundred and twenty. Then he stopped because the money left in his wallet was suddenly also his week: travel, food, whatever stupid emergency London shook loose next.

Laci did not touch it at once. He counted with his eyes first, then with the thick, square fingertips of a man who spent all day carrying bags that split his skin. "Where did this come from?"

"Work."

"Mercer pays you that in magic cash envelopes now?"

"Extra work."

Laci looked up. "Legal extra work?"

Beni said nothing.

"Ah." Laci gathered the notes into one stack and tapped them against the table edge until they aligned. That little neatness made the whole thing worse. Anger Beni understood. Carefulness meant Laci had already been worried long before he spoke. "You think I'm asking because I'm the police? I'm asking because if you get thrown out of one more thing and come back here with your sad face, I still have to listen to you breathe behind that curtain."

"I'm not getting thrown out."

"No? You couldn't keep the warehouse job. You couldn't keep the minicab place because they wanted their own cousin in the car. Now you have the rich people. Fine. Keep the rich people. But keep them properly." Laci tucked the notes into his pocket and winced when he bent, the knee again. "This brings you down to five eighty. That's still five eighty. Do you understand numbers less well in England?"

"I understand."

"Do you?" Laci opened the fridge and stared into it as if the contents had personally betrayed him. "Because every time I ask, you say I know, I know, and then I find the shampoo bottle full of water because somebody is stretching soap for one more day."

Beni looked at the diluted shampoo on the sill by the sink and wished, stupidly, that Laci had hit him instead. The shame would have been shorter.

"I'll have more next week," he said.

"You'd better." Laci took out a lager, changed his mind, put it back. "And don't bring me trouble wrapped in a nice coat. Rich people trouble is still trouble. It just smells of better detergent."

He went into the bathroom and shut the door with his usual carefulness, not a slam. Laci rarely slammed things. He got meaner by inches, not explosions. That was harder to dodge because it meant he stayed tired enough to be right.

Beni stood at the table with sixteen pounds in his wallet and the corner-shop bag going damp under the eggs. Five hundred and eighty still owing. He said the number to himself once because hearing it in a room made it harder to pretend it belonged to the future. Outside, another bus dragged a long shiver through the window. He thought of the Ashdown envelope against his chest that afternoon, the easy weight of it, and hated that relief had come first and questions second.

He fried two eggs in a pan that had never quite lost the taste of onions and ate standing up. Afterward he lay on the narrow mattress behind the curtain with his phone on the pillow beside him. He meant only to rest his eyes before the next day's early shift. Instead he stared at the crack in the ceiling and waited to see whether Nina would use the number already.

At 9:42 p.m. the screen lit.

Tomorrow 10:30. Mews entrance. Keep the day clear after lunch if you can.

There was no name and no sign-off.

He typed `Yes, Mrs. Ashdown`, deleted it, typed `Yes`, deleted that too, then settled on `Understood.` It sounded stiff even to him. He nearly changed it again, but the message already showed the blue ticks before he could decide.

The typing dots appeared, vanished, appeared again.

Good.

That was all. Still, sleep came badly. The flat held too much noise inside its walls: taps knocking on and off, someone's television bleeding game-show applause through plaster, Laci coughing from the sofa once at midnight and once at three. In between those sounds Beni lay awake inside the thin new fact of being reachable.

The next morning the house took him in through the back as if he had already been there for months.

The mews had a different smell at half ten: wet cardboard, diesel, coffee grounds, lilies from the florist buckets. A delivery van blocked half the lane while two men unloaded arrangements larger than children. The side door stood open on its hydraulic arm. Someone had propped it with a sack of potatoes because the closer kept dragging it shut. Beyond it a narrow corridor ran under the house in clean cream paint, pipes hidden badly behind boxing. People changed shoes there and lowered their voices there. One staircase went up toward the rooms that mattered and another dropped toward the rooms that kept them supplied.

No one greeted him like a guest. That was the point. A woman with a Hoover hose looped over one shoulder lifted her chin toward a peg board where the day's schedules sat clipped in plastic sleeves. A man in a dark suit came out of a ground-floor office carrying two phones and a dry-cleaning ticket between his teeth. He passed Beni without rudeness or warmth and said to somebody behind him, "No, Bea at eight-fifteen, not eight-twenty, because the traffic on Eaton Terrace doubles after that." Then he disappeared upstairs, still talking.

The sheet with Beni's name on it had times typed in narrow columns.

10:30 driver to remain on-site

12:10 Mrs A. to Cadogan Place

13:00 wait

15:40 contingency

Nothing after that, which meant everything after that was probably Nina.

He spent twenty minutes in the garage wiping rain pearls from the bonnet that did not need wiping. At eleven the school blazer from yesterday reappeared on another hanger. At eleven-fifteen two trays went up. At eleven-twenty a woman in heels came down the front stairs laughing into her phone, then stopped laughing before she crossed the hall where the staff could hear. The house did not feel alive in the ordinary way. It felt serviced. The air heated at the correct hour, the silver brightened on schedule, and family members arrived at doors exactly when other people had made arrival possible.

By noon his stomach had started talking. He had not brought enough with him, only a cereal bar gone soft in his pocket. One of the men from the florist team backed through the garage carrying green water in a plastic bucket and nearly clipped his sleeve. Beni stepped aside and ended up by the basement door. Heat came through it. So did the smell of frying butter, bleach, and onions. Someone below laughed once, abruptly, then stopped.

He should have stayed where drivers were meant to stay. Instead he went down.

The kitchen ceiling was low enough to make the pipes look deliberate. One wall held a row of labelled shelves; another held hanging pans bright as mirrors in the middle and blackened at the edges where no polish reached. A radio the size of a brick whispered bad pop under a cloth. At the main table a woman was slicing crusts off a loaf with the speed of someone who had done it angry, tired, and late often enough to need no attention for it.

She was compact, broad through the forearms, her hair twisted up with a pencil because her elastic had snapped. Three spare bands sat around her wrist beside the red line where a glove cuff had pressed all morning. She wore men's football socks under soft clogs, the kind choice made when you worked on cold stone all day and had stopped caring whether anybody found it graceful. Without looking up she said, in English, "If you're lost, the undertaker is next door."

Beni stopped in the doorway. "Sorry."

Now she looked. Her face changed at the sound of the word, not much, just enough to sharpen.

"You're Hungarian," she said in Hungarian.

The relief of hearing it landed so fast it almost felt like a mistake. "Yes."

"That bad?" She set the knife down. "One word and already you sound guilty."

"I didn't know there was anyone here."

"There's always someone from somewhere in a house like this." She went back to the bread. "Mostly we're in the basement where the decorative people don't have to think about us. Come in or go away. Standing in the door makes a draft."

He came in.

"Driver?" she asked.

"Yes."

"I know. You have that waiting look." She nodded at the chair opposite the breadboard. "Sit. Unless they feed chauffeurs upstairs now and nobody told me."

"It's fine."

His stomach had been clawing at him since noon, but sitting down for food in somebody else's kitchen felt worse than hunger. Hunger was private. Being seen with it wasn't.

"That's not what I asked."

He sat.

She cut one heel from the loaf, spread butter on it so thick it shone, and pushed the plate toward him. Beside it she put half a hard-boiled egg with paprika on the yolk and a mug of tea already sugared. "Eat that before I remember I dislike strangers."

The butter sat on the bread in a yellow thickness that would have started a fight in Tottenham. His first impulse was to refuse it, out of manners or shame or whatever stupid name pride took when you were hungry.

"Thank you."

"You're welcome. If it helps, the bread was going stale and the egg is from a tray Bea decided was ethically upsetting yesterday and therefore would not touch."

He took the mug first because his hands needed something to do. Heat bit his fingertips through the ceramic. The tea was too hot and too sweet, and the sugar seemed to wake his body one organ at a time. It was also the best thing he had tasted that day.

"I'm Eszter," she said. "Housekeeping, laundry, emergency child logistics, occasional florist murder when they bring lilies covered in pollen."

"Beni."

"I guessed you weren't Stephen."

He laughed before he meant to. It came out rough from disuse. Eszter glanced at him once, quick, as if confirming he was capable of it.

"How long have you been in London?" she asked.

"Nearly two years."

"And already this face?" She clicked her tongue. "Terrible. Another six months and you'll be discussing council tax like you were born in Enfield."

"I don't live in Enfield."

"No? Then forgive me. Tottenham has made you elegant."

Her tone stayed brisk, almost careless, but she kept doing three things at once while she talked: slicing fruit for a tray, checking something on the hob, folding sandwich paper into exact squares. Nothing about her invited pity or ornamental interest. She was simply competent in a way that made the room seem less temporary.

From somewhere overhead a bell buzzed once.

Eszter rolled her eyes toward the ceiling. "Coffee." She got up, poured from a silver pot into cups already lined on a tray, then stopped to point a spoon at him. "Don't disappear. If Mrs. Ashdown asks where the driver is, I'm not losing ten minutes because you felt shy."

"I won't."

She carried the tray out with one hand flat under it and the other still peeling a clementine she must have started during some previous job. That, more than anything, told him how long she'd been doing this sort of work: she ate in movement, in corners, in the seconds nobody could invoice.

Alone, he finished the bread more slowly than hunger required. The kitchen sounded different without speech. Pipes ticked. The fridge hummed. Feet overhead crossed rooms thickly carpeted enough that the steps arrived softened, class translated into acoustics. On the shelf by the radio sat three takeaway soy sauce packets, a child's plastic hair clip shaped like a strawberry, and a packet of headache tablets held closed with a clothes peg. It looked like a life in fragments, not a stage set.

Eszter came back two minutes later.

"Still here," she said. "Good. Some of them melt if you leave them unsupervised."

"Do you know all the drivers?"

"Enough of them." She rinsed her hands and dried them on her apron. "Most keep their mouths shut, which is sensible. Some overdo it and become furniture. Furniture gets kicked."

He looked at the typed schedule pinned by the door. "And this house?"

"What about it?"

"It runs like a hotel."

"Hotels are friendlier." She leaned one hip against the counter. "This is a family home, which means the feelings are more expensive and the instructions less clear. Mr. Ashdown's office likes times and columns and things initialled. Mrs. Ashdown likes changes. Bea likes warning. The flowers like water. The rest of us improvise."

"You're making it sound easy."

"I'm making it sound like work." She folded her arms. "Why are you looking at everything as if you need to memorize it for an exam?"

He looked down at the tea. "Habit."

"Bad habit."

"Useful one."

"Sometimes." She picked up the child's strawberry clip from the shelf, turned it over in her fingers, then set it back. "Sometimes it's just a way not to answer."

His phone vibrated on the table. He looked before he could stop himself.

7 tonight. Use the side entrance if front is busy.

Again there was no name, and there was no question.

Eszter saw only the change in his face, which was enough. "Ah," she said. "So you're not a one-off."

"I don't know."

"You do. You just don't want to say it in front of another Hungarian in case it sounds more true."

He put the phone face down. "Mrs. Ashdown needs extra driving."

"Of course she does." There was no judgment in it yet, only recognition. "Well, if she's using you direct, charge properly and keep receipts in your own head. Rich people become innocent very quickly when money changes shape."

That line stayed with him because of how matter-of-factly she said it. She wasn't warning him or prophesying. She was talking about plumbing.

"You work here full time?" he asked.

"Near enough." She smiled without warmth this time. "Enough to know the boiler hates rain, the daughter hides books in the linen cupboard, and the man from the wine supplier pinches the cheese straws if nobody watches him. You learn a house from what goes missing."

Upstairs another bell sounded, two short bursts.

Eszter pushed off the counter. "That's lunch. Your Mrs. Ashdown will want the car in ten minutes."

"She's not my Mrs. Ashdown."

"No?" She was already halfway to the tray station. "Good. Keep that clear and life will hurt you a little less."

He stood because sitting any longer would have looked like lingering, and lingering would have meant admitting that this was the first ordinary conversation he had had in days that did not end in payment or complaint. Eszter reached past him for a stack of plates. He smelled soap on her wrist and frying butter on her sleeve.

"Next time," she said, not looking at him, "bring actual food. Drivers always arrive hungry and then pretend they aren't. It's boring."

"All right."

"And if you say thank you like an English schoolboy again, I'll charge you for the tea."

This time he smiled properly. "All right."

She flicked her fingers at the door, dismissing him back into the machinery.

Upstairs the corridor had filled again. Someone was taking a garment bag toward the front rooms. The office door stood open and he saw, just for a second, Oscar Ashdown at the desk inside, silver at the temples, reading from two pages laid side by side while he ate almonds from a glass jar and moved only his eyes. The sight lasted no more than a second before the door swung in, but it fixed the order of the place. Nina's secrecy might bend the day. It still bent inside a structure her husband paid to keep invisible.

At 12:09 heels clicked on the service stairs and Nina came down already fastening one earring, having left the other for the hall mirror. "Cadogan Place," she said. "And tonight, seven. You saw that?"

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

She glanced past him at the corridor, checking who might be within earshot, then lowered her voice without softening it. "Good. Keep it this way. If the house asks, you're available to the house. If Mercer asks, you're on my account. That's how this works."

This wasn't just today or just this drive. This was how the work would run now.

"Understood," he said.

She got into the back, and he shut the door on the scent of her perfume and the colder house air behind it.

Driving out of the mews, he caught sight of the basement window for an instant: steam on the inside of the glass, somebody moving behind it, then the turn into traffic took it away. In his jacket pocket the phone sat warm from his body. In Tottenham Laci had already folded yesterday's money into the rent debt and left five hundred and eighty as the number still waiting. In Belgravia there was a service corridor, a typed schedule, and a woman downstairs who had spoken to him as if he existed in full sentences. When he turned onto Sloane Street, he knew the answer he had given Nina that morning had already stopped being provisional.
