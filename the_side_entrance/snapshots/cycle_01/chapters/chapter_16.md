# Chapter 16

By ten in the morning the front door of the Ashdowns' house looked ready for a painting and was useless for anything real. The brass had been buffed, the black steps washed, the wreath fixed square between the lamps. No one with weight in their hands came through it. The actual day was happening in the mews and the basement: florist vans breathing diesel into wet brick, crates of rented glass knocking together on the cobbles, a catering boy in a white jacket smoking under the arch with one cuff still basted in blue thread. Everything that would be called effortless by seven o'clock arrived in cardboard and went downstairs first.

Beni had parked the Mercedes half a street over because the mews was already jammed with a refrigerated van, two flower lorries, and a small transit from the event company whose driver kept apologizing in Irish while blocking everybody anyway. Rain had not stopped since dawn. It lived in the cloth of his coat and in the hems of the temp staff waiting by the service gate with garment bags under plastic covers. The house smelled stronger than usual when he came in through the basement door: polish and lilies and radiator heat, yes, but also wet wool, cut stems, tape, coffee grounds, bleach, and the hot metallic breath of catering ovens brought in for the evening.

Mrs. Keene stood at the junction where the service corridor met the kitchen passage and the back stairs, holding a laminated floor plan so damp at the edges it had started to peel. She had a cough sweet parked in one cheek and a yellow pencil tucked through the bun at the back of her head, point outward like a tiny weapon. When she saw Beni she did not say good morning. She said, "Thank Christ. Take those champagne cases to the south pantry, then get the garment rails out of the garage. And if anyone from Delacourt asks where the ice sculpture goes, tell them no one in this house has ever wanted an ice sculpture and send them to me."

"Morning to you too," Beni said.

She gave him the look she used for remarks that failed to improve logistics. "Move."

He moved. The champagne came in wooden boxes with rope handles that bit his palms through the damp. He took them one by one past the laundry alcove, the humming wine fridge, the folded trestle tables waiting like bodies at a station. On the service stairs above him temp waiters were learning the house by error: one came down backwards with a stack of charger plates, another froze flat against the wall to let a florist pass with a cage of bare branches wired with tiny lights. The branches shed drops onto the runner and left dark little wounds on the fabric.

At the kitchen door he almost stopped from habit, then did not. The door was open anyway. He saw enough in one glance: steel counters loaded with canape trays under cling film, boxes of herbs on the floor, a rented salamander grill giving off a hard baking heat. Eszter was at the centre of it in a black crew-neck top and the same work trousers she always wore, but with a short white apron tied over them for service. Somebody had pinned her hair up fast and badly; one dark strand kept coming loose above her ear and she kept blowing it away instead of touching it with her hands. On her wrist, under the elastic bite of a hair band, was a cheap plastic bracelet with yellow beads spelling LILI. He had never seen it on her at work before.

She was showing two waitresses how to carry smoked-salmon spoons without tipping the creme fraiche off them. "Not over people's shoulders," she said. "You come in lower. Otherwise somebody's tuxedo gets fish on it and suddenly the end of the world is happening."

One of the waitresses, a girl with bitten burgundy nails and a silver stud through one eyebrow, laughed because she was nervous. "Right."

Eszter turned, saw Beni in the doorway, and let her eyes rest on him for no longer than the tray in his hands required.

"South pantry," she said. "Not here. If Keene sees bottles in my walkway she'll start speaking in tongues."

"I'm going."

"Then go."

Nothing in her voice was for him. That was almost easier than the chapter before last, when anger had still wanted an answer. This was work. Work was clean, even when people were not.

He took the boxes on. The south pantry, usually a sober little room for spare wine and table linen, had become an organ under strain. Cases of champagne were stacked by label. Silver tubs waited on the floor with their blue plastic still half torn off. A boy from the hire company, narrow-faced and red-handed from polishing, was counting flutes into rows of twelve while whispering football scores to himself. Every time he reached thirty-six he tapped the bridge of his nose with his little finger as if that kept the number in place.

"Need room there, mate," Beni said.

The boy looked up. "Sorry. I've got four hundred and eighty and they all think they're precious."

He shifted fast enough and Beni wedged the champagne into the gap by the wall. Four hundred and eighty flutes. Enough mouths to make a house look successful for three hours.

All morning the mews kept filling and clearing, filling and clearing. Florists brought in trees with their roots wrapped in hessian and wire. A woman from the rental company arrived with chair covers no one had ordered and cried in the corridor because the return paperwork meant she'd pay for the taxi herself. Mrs. Keene took the forms from her, found the dispatch number, and fixed it while insulting everyone involved under her breath with the precision of a woman who had done this for fifteen years and no longer mistook competence for affection.

Oscar crossed the basement twice before noon. He wore trousers and shirt sleeves, cuff links still undone, jacket over one arm. Without the full suit he looked younger only in the sense that a machine looks younger with one panel off. He had a folded run sheet in his hand and kept opening and flattening it against whatever surface was nearest: counter, wine crate, wall. He asked for timings, counts, contingencies. He never raised his voice. He did not need to. Every question implied somebody else's failure in advance.

"The donor cars start at six forty-five," he said to Mrs. Keene while Beni took a rail of pressed coats out through the garage. "I want the first tray live in the front hall at six forty-seven, not before. Early reads desperate."

"Yes, sir."

"And the portrait lighting?"

"Tested again at four."

"Good."

His eyes slid to Beni. "You'll keep the Mercedes ready in the mews until Nina comes down. Once the guests begin, no dead space at the front. If a car idles where it can be seen, move it."

"Yes, sir."

"And if Bea refuses the photographer again, tell Keene, not me. I won't have a scene on the pavement."

The phrasing landed harder than if he had said daughter. Beni almost looked up, almost asked where she had been since last night, how that had been handled, whether anybody in this house had used the words thank you or where were you or are you all right. But Oscar was already reading the next line of paper. Family and staff existed for him in columns today: arrival, placement, problem, solution.

In the garage the garment rails rattled over the concrete as Beni dragged them into the corridor. The smell down there always held old rubber and cold petrol, but today somebody had added steamers. Their hiss made the space feel like a launderette in a rich dream. A seamstress from Knightsbridge alterations had taken over the far wall with irons, white cloths, and two evening gowns hanging from hooks on the pipes. She wore half-moon reading glasses on a chain and bright red trainers under her black skirt. Every time she passed one gown with the steamer she muttered to it in Romanian as if coaxing a difficult child through an exam.

"Don't let anyone smoke in here," she said when Beni came by. "These fabrics burn faster than sinners."

"No one smokes in here."

She snorted. "Men always say that about places they've never watched properly."

By one o'clock his shirt was sticking between his shoulders. He had driven nowhere except twice around the block to clear space for delivery vans, but he felt as tired as if he'd done airport runs all morning. Service work in a rich house was often like that: no distance, no destination, just constant small lifting and being in the way until suddenly you were the solution to something not your own.

He was taking a box of votive candles up from the cellar when his phone buzzed once in the inside pocket of his coat. Not Mercer dispatch. Nina.

Garage. Now.

He stood at the turn of the service stairs with the candle box against his thigh. From above came the hard bright chatter of hired women arranging flowers in tall glass cylinders. From below came Eszter's voice saying somebody had put the vegetarian tartlets next to the duck and if that happened again she'd feed the whole tray to them personally. Mrs. Keene was asking for six more black napkins. The house needed hands on every floor.

His phone buzzed again.

Now, Ben.

He set the candles on the stair, went down two flights, cut through the laundry, and crossed the garage with his pulse doing something stupid and adolescent in his neck. That room had been where the thing with Nina stopped pretending it belonged to language. Even empty, it remembered.

The Mercedes waited under the strip light, polished for the evening, black enough to take the garage into itself. Nina was in the back seat with the door on the driver's side open, one heel on the floor, the other foot tucked under her on the leather. She wore a pale slip under a robe the colour of watered wine, and someone upstairs had done half her face for the gala and not the other half. One eye was lined, the other bare. It made her look both younger and more dangerous, as if different women had started claiming her and neither had finished.

On the seat beside her lay two passports, a leather folder, and a pair of long earrings tangled together like hooks.

"Close the door," she said.

He did.

"Sit down."

"I can't."

"You can. You're not carrying Christ."

He got into the front, not the back. The car still held last night's leather polish and the faint powdery perfume from whatever she had been sprayed with upstairs. Rain tapped the garage roof in a thin metallic scatter. Through the windscreen he could see only the shut garage door and, above it, the strip of wired bulbs somebody had tested for the mews arch.

Nina picked up one passport, turned it over in her hand, then the other. Her nails were bare again. He had started noticing when they were bare because it usually meant she had been chewing at the edges.

"I'm going to Paris after tonight," she said.

"For how long?"

"Long enough." She looked at the passports, not at him. "Maybe Christmas. Maybe spring. Maybe until Oscar has learned the restorative pleasures of missing me. The apartment on Rue de Verneuil is free until April, and after that I can take something else if I choose to stay."

Beni said nothing.

"Don't make that face. It's Paris, not exile in a salt mine."

"I didn't make a face."

"You did, but never mind." She finally looked up. "I want you to come."

The garage gave back the sentence in a softer shape. He thought first of the obvious practical thing, because practical things were easier to hold. "For driving."

"Among other uses."

It should have made him angry. It did, a little. But under the anger came the image so fast it embarrassed him: a flat with dry windows, his own key, a bathroom where the towel stayed warm after you hung it up, no cousin's boots under the bed, no Tube calculation every time he wanted to cross the city. He thought of his foldout mattress in Tottenham, the spring that caught his hip, the window that sweated all winter onto the sill. He thought of Laci counting what he owed as if each note had already left his own pocket and only Beni's incompetence delayed the transfer.

Nina watched him take the picture in. She had always been quick with appetite in other people.

"You'll have a room," she said. "A proper one. Not a chair and a prayer in some damp north-London hovel. There'll be work because I won't suddenly stop needing to be driven, and there are three women I know already who complain incessantly about their useless chauffeurs. You speak decent French for a start, which puts you ahead of most English men. I can pay you better than Mercer. If we stay past April, I can have papers done sensibly through a service company and save everyone the melodrama."

She touched the passports again, aligning them exactly with the leather folder. Control through objects. He had seen it often enough to know what it meant. Under the poise there was panic moving furniture around.

"Why me?" he asked, and hated how young it sounded.

"Because I know you." She smiled without warmth. "Because you know where all the embarrassing pieces are buried. Because you can keep your mouth shut without making it a theological problem. Because you don't bore me. Pick whichever answer flatters you least."

He looked away to the rearview mirror, which showed them both badly: him in his damp shirt and work suit, her half-made face and robe, the passports bright as teeth between them.

"And if I don't want to be buried with the embarrassing pieces?"

"Then you have mistaken me for a sentimentalist." Her tone stayed light, but the words did not. "Ben, don't be childish. I'm offering you a life that is visibly better than the one you have."

From somewhere overhead came the muffled thunder of something heavy rolling across floorboards. The house was still moving. He pictured Mrs. Keene asking where the candles were, Eszter sending a temp back for more pickled onions, some donor's stupid dietary requirement becoming urgent in a pantry. Down here, cut off in the garage, the offer sat in the warm air like a second body.

Nina leaned forward, forearms on the front seat. "Listen to me. No one in Paris will care who you were here. No one will ask whether you came through the front door or the side, whether some girl in a kitchen stopped speaking to you, whether your employer's daughter has become tiresome. You can have a decent coat, a decent wage, a better city, and fewer witnesses. That is not nothing."

No one will ask. Fewer witnesses.

It was tempting because it was true. A city large enough to rinse him clean by indifference. No Oscar. No Mercer logs. No Laci. No church-basement room where he had stood last night with Bea's overnight bag against his shin while Eszter made a child tea and did not look at him unless she had to. He could leave the whole London arrangement behind and call it survival.

Then the house thudded again above them and he thought, stupidly, of the service corridor on the second floor where the wallpaper had worn dark at shoulder height from staff carrying trays. Paris would have those as well. Better painted maybe. Wider. The same rubbed strip where people like him passed without leaving their names.

"What would I be there?" he asked.

Nina blinked once, thrown more by the wording than by the question itself. "What do you mean?"

"I mean in Paris. What would I be there?"

She gave a short impatient laugh. "Employed. Comfortable, if you let yourself be. Useful. Why does everyone under forty insist on turning everything into an identity crisis?"

"Useful to what."

"To me, obviously. At first. And then to yourself, if you have any ambition beyond martyring yourself for people who can't even afford your loyalty." She sat back. "You'd drive. You'd help with the apartment. You'd manage errands. You'd keep certain things off paper when they belong off paper. You're very good at lives that need discretion."

There it was. Not even dressed up.

He looked at his hands on the steering wheel. They were clean because the house had forced him into cleaner tasks today, but the rope from the champagne cases had left pink grooves across the base of his fingers. Useful. Drive. Help with the apartment. Keep certain things off paper. In London he did it through the mews, the garage, the altered logbook, the waiting silence in parked cars. In Paris he would do it in another language with better coffee and a river people photographed more respectfully. That was all.

The shame of wanting it did not vanish just because he understood it. He still wanted the dry room, the money, the heat, the ease of not counting every Oyster top-up against food. He wanted the kind of life where the emergency amount in his wallet could stay an amount instead of dissolving into fares and rent gaps and other people's boots. Nina had known that before he did. That was part of her talent.

"I can't leave tonight," he said.

"I didn't ask you to leave mid-canape." She sounded annoyed now, which meant she had heard the answer forming and disliked waiting for it. "After the gala. Tomorrow, or Monday at the latest. I can book the train this afternoon if I must. I assumed speed would appeal to you."

"Why so fast?"

"Because delay gives everyone time to perform principles. Because Oscar will be unwatchable after tonight. Because I would rather be gone before this house starts discussing itself in that ghastly controlled voice rich people use for disaster. Must I really narrate every motivation as if you're my analyst?"

He almost said yes. Instead he said, "I need to think."

Nina studied him. Half her mouth had lipstick; half did not. The effect was oddly childish and made the next line rougher.

"No," she said. "You need to recognize luck before it gets embarrassed and leaves."

He met her eyes in the mirror then, properly. For a second he could see the version of it she meant to sell: train doors shutting on this house, on London, on everybody who had used him and everybody he had lied to, a new city where he could be kept without being called kept. The idea went through him with almost physical relief. That was what made it dangerous. Not fantasy. Plausibility.

Then he heard the radio crackle on the dash from the house set someone had left in the cup holder.

"Beni?" Mrs. Keene's voice, thin with static. "If you're still alive, where are the candles?"

Nina rolled her eyes toward the roof. "You see? Even Providence is middle management."

He opened the driver's door. Cold damp air rushed in around his knees. "I've got to go."

"Tonight," she said behind him. "Not here. Not now. But before the speeches I want an answer."

He got out and shut the door softly. Through the darkened glass he saw her tip her head back against the leather and close the made-up eye first, then the other.

The candles were still on the stair where he had abandoned them. He carried them up. Mrs. Keene took one look at his face and, to her credit, asked nothing. In this house everyone senior knew the value of other people's silence as long as it kept the timetable moving.

The afternoon compressed. Rain thinned to a fine mist and the house grew hotter, louder, more arranged. The service corridor outside the drawing room filled with black coats on staff pegs, radio chargers, spare votive lighters, tape, three hidden boxes of supermarket mince pies decanted into a Fortnum tin in case anybody downstairs needed to eat without offending the concept of elegance. Beni drove once to collect an extra run of ice from the hotel supplier in Knightsbridge and once more to move Oscar's second car to a nearby private garage because the mews had become all loading zone and no exit. The city beyond Belgravia looked ordinary and mean in comparison, buses wet and overfull, Deliveroo riders hunched under boxy bags. Every time he came back through the service entrance the house swallowed him again and handed him another small necessary thing.

At four thirty the first musicians arrived through the back gate because the cello case was too large for the side stairs and no one wanted marks on the wall. The cellist was a woman in her fifties with nicotine fingers and a string of tiny silver fish around her neck. She asked where she could boil water for her thermos and, when Mrs. Keene pointed her toward the staff kettle, said, "Lovely, because if I drink donor tea I start resenting the dead." Then she vanished into the morning room with the other two players and began tuning in long private notes that made the walls feel expensive.

Beni saw Bea for the first time just after five.

She came down the back stairs in a black dress that looked too adult in the cut and too honest in the way she wore it, as if she'd been dressed by committee and rejected the committee by posture alone. Her hair had been blown smooth, but she had tied the old frayed black school ribbon around her wrist instead of leaving it in her hair. At the heel of one foot a blister plaster showed above the strap of her shoe. Mrs. Keene was behind her carrying a steamed shawl and the expression of a woman escorting unstable glass.

Bea stopped when she saw Beni by the service table with the guest place cards.

"You look thrilled," she said.

"You look tired."

"That's because I've spent the day being upholstered."

Mrs. Keene said, "Miss Bea."

"What? It's accurate."

Her face was pale under the makeup, but not frightened. Worse. Resigned in the flat adolescent way that made adults think compliance had been achieved when really it was only being stored for later. Beni wanted to ask if she'd eaten, if she'd slept after the church room, if Eszter had managed to keep the whole thing from blowing the house apart by breakfast. None of those belonged to this corridor with place cards and a tray of canapes sweating under cling film.

So he said, "Do your shoes fit."

Bea blinked, almost smiled, then looked down. "Not morally."

"There's a chair in the pantry if you need five minutes before photos."

"That's the most romantic thing anybody's said to me all month."

Mrs. Keene made a noise in her throat. "The photographer is waiting."

Bea turned to go, then looked back once. "Eszter says if I faint on purpose she'll know."

"Then don't faint."

"I wasn't planning anything so gauche."

She went on. The ribbon on her wrist trailed like she'd forgotten it was there.

At six Oscar changed into black tie and became, somehow, even more managerial. He did not look handsome exactly. He looked settled into the costume people expected money to wear. He had a printed guest list in one hand and his cuff links closed now, both perfect. Only once, when a florist misread the plan and sent the large white arrangement to the library instead of the front hall, did he show the strain underneath. He took off his glasses, pinched the bridge of his nose, then put them back on and said, very softly, "If the flowers are wrong by the front door the entire evening begins as apology. Move them."

Everyone moved them.

The thing Beni hated most about watching rich households under pressure was how often the language of theatre turned out to be the language of fact. Place. Entrance. Backdrop. Hold. Delay. Reveal. The family was not outside the machinery. They were the central props that cost the most to damage. Oscar stood in the front drawing room with the photographer and the event manager, placing bodies in air.

"Not there," he said to the photographer. "If Nina stands under that painting she'll look shorter."

"We'll adjust."

"No, let's avoid adjusting. Bea half a step left. There. Keep the piano in frame, not the drinks station. If my mother arrives before the speech, she goes into the blue room first. I don't want her cornering Nina in public."

He turned when Beni came to the door.

"Good. At six forty-eight you bring Nina round to the front once the first two donor cars have cleared. Not before. If she isn't ready, you wait in the mews and keep the engine out of earshot. At seven-oh-three Bea comes down for photographs. At seven-ten she's in the morning room until the chairman arrives. At seven-fifteen, if the family sequence is stable, we do the portrait. Stable, Beni. I'm not improvising around adolescent moods with forty people watching."

He spoke as if reading route instructions for cargo. Beni heard the daughter's name, the wife's name, the time codes, the car positions. No difference in structure.

"Yes, sir," he said.

Oscar checked the list. "And if Nina asks where I've gone, you tell her I'm receiving guests. If she asks twice, you tell Keene."

"Yes, sir."

"Good."

That was all. Beni was already dismissed, already part of the apparatus again.

He took the service stairs down because the main hall was no longer a place to walk through unless you belonged to the show. Halfway down he stopped at the landing window that looked into the mews. The light outside had gone fully blue. Wet brick shone under the strings of bulbs. A van from the florist was still parked crooked by the bins because the driver had lost his keys and was searching every jacket pocket with greater and greater despair. From the basement kitchen came the smell of butter warming, then the quick clean cut of citrus as somebody dressed oysters. From upstairs floated the first proper notes of the cello, low and polished and meant to make people feel their own importance as if it had a soundtrack.

Paris, he thought, would have a front door too. It would have a back one. Nina might call the flat elegant and his room generous and the work civilized, but the actual shape of it was already clear. Wait here. Bring this. Not yet. Keep that off paper. Be useful. Be quiet. Know the embarrassing pieces. No one will ask who you were. The sentence that had sounded like mercy in the garage now sat in him like a hand on the back of his neck.

He could still feel the pull of it. That was the worst part. If the offer had been purely insulting, refusal would have been cheap. But the room in Tottenham was still damp. Laci would still want money. Mercer would still be Mercer. Eszter still might never touch him again except by accident over a tray. Saying no to Nina would not open some moral clean air. It would only leave him with the life he already had and the cost of having refused a softer one.

He stood there long enough for the landing radiator to click on by his shin.

Then he understood that the softness was the trap. Not because Paris was false. It might be exactly as comfortable as she promised. That did not make it different in the way that mattered. He had already spent too much of his life learning how to move through other people's side doors without taking up room. He knew what that training did. It taught you to call disappearance skill. It made gratitude out of being hidden well.

Downstairs a radio snapped alive. "Front gate open in two. Car on approach."

Beni straightened, one hand still on the paint-chipped sill. He did not say the answer out loud because there was no one there to hear it and because saying it might have made it feel theatrical. He only let it settle into the place where he usually kept route numbers and night addresses.

He would not go to Paris.

Not after the speeches. Not on Monday. Not into a better room built around the same arrangement.

The first donor bell sounded upstairs, neat and resonant through the house. In the mews somebody swore with helpless fury about the missing van keys. Beni went down the rest of the stairs, crossed the service corridor, and took the Mercedes keys from the hook board. When Mrs. Keene's voice came over the radio asking for the car at the front, he answered at once and kept the no to himself.
