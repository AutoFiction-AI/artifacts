# Chapter 8

Beni put eight pounds on the meter before he changed his shirt. He did it in coins because that was what he had in the mug by the bed, and because feeding the machine one pound at a time let him feel, for a minute, like heat was something he had chosen instead of something he chased from room to room. The radiator clicked, then hissed. The front room warmed by degrees. It was not enough to matter to London, but it might matter to a woman coming over after work in damp sleeves.

He stripped the bed and made it again with the less-grey sheet. He folded the other one and pushed it behind the armchair with two old Mercers shirts and a towel that never finished drying. The flat still smelled of damp plaster, frying oil from downstairs, and whatever bleach Laci had spilled on his boots the night before. The window held a skin of rain. Each time a bus went down the High Road, the loose pane gave one thin rattle in the frame and then settled.

He should have liked the room better for being empty. Laci was on a late job in Wood Green, or at the pub after it, or both. His boots were gone from the hall. The television was dark. No lager cans sweated on the sill. But privacy only made the place look smaller. The bed took most of the floor. His jacket hung from the back of the chair with Nina's cash still in the inside pocket, folded once, too much money for such a cheap piece of cloth. He turned the jacket around so the pocket faced the wall.

When Eszter knocked, it was with two quick taps of her knuckles, not waiting for the bell that had not worked in months. He opened the door before she could do it again.

She came in with a supermarket carrier looped over her wrist and a small blue Nivea tin already in her hand. Before she kissed him she flipped the lid with her thumb, dug a little cream out with her nail, and rubbed it into the raw places at the base of her fingers.

"Your stairs still smell like boiled socks," she said.

"That means it's home."

"That means some man in this building should be arrested."

He laughed, which relieved him more than he wanted to show. Her hair was tied back badly, as if she had redone it in a staff loo mirror and did not care if a few pieces stayed loose. She had a dark coat damp at the shoulders and trainers with one lace replaced by a strip of black cord. She looked tired, but not the kind of tired rich women wore to make a room rearrange itself around them. She looked work-tired, and her mouth was still quick.

He took her coat. She took the room in with one fast glance that managed to be honest without being cruel.

"You made the bed," she said.

"I do know how."

"I didn't say you didn't. I said you made it like your landlord was coming to inspect your morals."

"Maybe my morals are very tidy."

"Then they're not yours."

She held up the carrier bag. Inside were a roast chicken in a cloudy plastic box, two white rolls, a jar of pickled yellow peppers wrapped in yesterday's Metro, and two cans of cheap lager.

"I wasn't cooking," she said. "If you want courtship, find a woman with more time."

"This is already courtship. You brought peppers."

"I brought peppers because English roast chicken tastes like someone told it bad news."

He took the bag from her and set it on the bed. She sat cross-legged against the wall without asking, as if she had always known this was where the room expected people to sit. He liked that. He liked, too much, how quickly she made the place stop looking ashamed of itself.

They ate with their fingers because there were only two clean forks and he did not want to leave her alone in the room while he checked whether Laci had washed up or only rearranged dirt. The chicken was still a little warm at the breastbone and already greasy-cold at the legs. Eszter peeled the plastic skin off one side and dropped it onto the lid with disgust.

"See?" she said. "Depressed bird."

"Maybe it worked a double shift."

"Then I respect it. I still don't want the skin."

She tore off a wing and handed it to him. He opened the jar of peppers. Vinegar and paprika hit the air hard enough to beat back the damp for a minute. Outside, a siren ran along the High Road and thinned into distance.

"What was for dinner upstairs?" he asked.

"Sea bass that nobody liked and potatoes arranged like architecture. Bea ate half a piece of toast and left for studying. Nina didn't come down. Oscar came in late and asked if there was lemon. There was lemon. It became a whole event."

"How many lemons?"

"Two. Sliced. As if I had personally hidden the rest of the citrus from him."

She mimed his voice so dryly that he nearly choked on bread.

"Cruel," he said.

"Accurate."

He watched her drink from the can. She wiped the foam from her top lip with the back of her hand, then reached for another piece of chicken as if appetite did not have to apologize to anyone. That, too, he liked. With Nina everything had happened inside a car that smelled of leather and expensive perfume and being careful. Here there was grease on the blanket, pickle brine on his thumb, her knee warm against his thigh. Nothing in the room pretended not to be there.

"You look wrecked," she said.

"I worked."

"Everybody works. You look like somebody ironed you while you were still inside the shirt."

"That's a good service. Mercer should offer it."

"No. They'd charge the clients and still make you do it."

He smiled. She kept looking at him.

"What?" he said.

"Nothing dramatic. You're just doing that thing again."

"What thing?"

"The one where you answer the first question and hide behind it."

He pulled a piece of bread apart to give his hands something exact. "I said I worked."

"Yes, and if I asked what color the sky was you'd tell me it was above us."

He looked down. The bread had gone gummy where the chicken fat touched it.

Eszter leaned back against the wall and bumped his shin with her foot. "I'm not interrogating you. Eat."

"I'm eating."

"See? Again."

He wanted to tell her that the day had not even been bad by recent standards, only long and full of being useful. He wanted to say Nina had been cool with him in the morning, which had been worse than if she had been cruel. He wanted to say the extra cash sat in his jacket pocket like something hot. Instead he lifted the jar of peppers.

"These help," he said.

"Beni."

"What?"

"If you don't want to talk, don't talk. But don't act like bad peppers are a full personality."

He laughed despite himself. "That's unfair. They're excellent peppers."

"Good. Then marry them."

They kept eating. It should have turned awkward then and somehow did not. She told him about a woman in Belgravia who had ordered a cleaner back to re-fold a blanket over a chair because the first fold looked "distracted." Beni told her about a Mercer client who once made him drive three extra streets because he did not want anyone seeing him get out outside a dermatologist's office. She switched into Hungarian to imitate the woman's horrible English vowels; he answered in the same language, and the room loosened around it. The jokes got meaner and funnier there. He did not have to place each word so carefully before it left his mouth.

When the food was half gone and both lager cans were empty, she licked pepper brine from her finger and said, "Tell me about Tottenham, then. Properly. Not driver version."

"What does that mean?"

"Driver version is mileage and traffic. Properly is where you buy cigarettes when you don't want the man at the corner shop asking why you look tired."

"That man asks anyway."

"Then he's doing his duty."

He shifted back against the wall. The bed springs gave one rusty complaint.

"Tottenham smells worse in rain," he said after a minute. "Everything gets pushed up together. Frying oil, wet cardboard, diesel, that sweet shit smell from the fried-chicken place by Seven Sisters when they burn the old grease. The buses sound different when the road's wet. Heavier. They don't hiss, they shove. If you miss the last one after two, you walk with all the other idiots pretending it isn't far."

"Better."

"The pigeons are violent."

"That's London."

"No, these are specific pigeons. These ones would take a baby's tooth if it looked edible."

"Very nice. Tourism board will call."

"The guy at the off-license has a cat with one ear. The cat's friendlier. In summer the lads stand outside the betting shop in socks and slides like they're on a beach holiday in hell. One window in the building opposite has had Christmas lights up for two years. Blue ones. They make the whole room look like a fish tank."

"There," she said softly. "That's a place."

He shrugged. He had not known he was holding something back until the words started moving and did not hurt.

"You?" he said. "Debrecen."

She grinned and folded one leg under herself. "Wind. Flat stupid wind because there is nothing to stop it and no hill to blame. The tram bells by the station. Those ugly concrete underpasses where everybody says not to go and everybody goes anyway. In winter the women on the number one tram all smell of cold wool and hair spray. In summer everything looks dusty even when it's clean. The town square pretends it's grand. It isn't grand. It just has room."

"And the food?"

"Better than here, obviously."

"Obviously."

"At the station you could get langos so hot the cheese slid off and burned your wrist if you were greedy. Which I was. The old women at the market sold sour cherries in plastic bowls and stared at your shoes to see if your life was failing. If I walk through Tottenham market and somebody stares at my shoes, I assume he wants my phone."

"Maybe he wants both."

"Then London wins."

He watched her while she talked. She used her hands more when she spoke about home, even now, even lightly. Her fingers still looked sore despite the cream rubbed into them. There was a pale crescent scar near one knuckle, probably from a knife, probably from work, probably from one fast stupid second long ago that no one else would remember. He wanted to put his mouth on that scar, which embarrassed him by being both tender and hungry.

"You do that thing when you listen," she said.

"What thing?"

"You go very still like you're hiding under the table."

"Maybe I'm listening."

"Maybe. Or maybe you're halfway out the window."

He wiped his hand on the Metro page and read the same headline twice without seeing it.

"I'm here," he said.

Eszter nodded once. "Then be here."

She did not make it heavier than that. She took the chicken carcass back into its box, screwed the lid on the pepper jar, and moved the rubbish into the carrier bag with quick neat motions. Her hands got more precise when she was irritated. She was not angry yet. She was just done with doing both parts of a moment. He knew enough now to see that.

He reached for her wrist before she could sit back fully. "Stay."

"I'm staying."

"No, I meant..."

"I know what you meant."

She let him pull her closer. Her mouth tasted of lager and the sharp vinegar from the peppers. He kissed her once carefully, then again without the care, and that was better. She climbed over his legs to get nearer and the room made its small noises around them: bedsprings, radiator tick, the bus window's weak rattle. None of it ruined anything. Her hand went to the back of his neck and stayed there with a firmness that felt less like permission than like a decision.

When she kissed him she did not do it to be adored. She did it to get closer faster. He liked that too. He liked the weight of her when she half sat on his thigh, liked the practical way she took his hand and put it at her waist instead of waiting for him to guess correctly. Her jumper came off over her head with a static crackle. Under it she wore a plain dark bra, one strap twisted, the hook at the back bent enough to scrape his knuckle when he found it.

"Cheap bastard thing," she muttered.

"Me?"

"The bra. Though let's keep options open."

He laughed into her shoulder. She smelled of detergent, skin, and the ghost of whatever hand soap they used in the Ashdown staff bathroom. Nothing about it was perfumed or staged. He kissed the place where shoulder became chest. Her skin warmed under his mouth.

She touched the scar at his brow with one thumb. "This?"

"Bike."

"You were a terrible child, then."

"I was excellent. The bicycle was weak."

"Of course."

He slid his hand up her back and felt muscle under skin, the small damp warmth at the base of her spine. When he kissed her again she opened for him at once, with no rich-person calculation about timing or effect, and his body answered before he could think himself out of it. Want was the easiest part. Want was always the easiest part as long as nobody asked him to put words around it.

They shifted down awkwardly because the wall was there and the bed was narrow and one of his feet was still half trapped under the blanket. She laughed when he nearly elbowed himself in the face.

"Very elegant," she said.

"I trained for this."

"At Mercer?"

"Strict course. Advanced falling."

"I can tell."

She lay back against the pillows, hair half out now, and pulled him down with her. He kissed her throat. Her hand moved under his shirt and up his spine. He had spent so much of the last week being touched in ways that turned him into equipment that this almost hurt. It was not because it was gentler. It wasn't gentler, exactly. It was mutual in a way that left him nowhere to hide.

He pushed his palm under the back of her bra. Her skin was hot there. She made a low sound in her throat, impatient more than shy, and he felt the sound in his stomach. When he kissed lower she let her head tip back against the wall with a soft thud.

"Careful," she said, half laughing. "If I die in your bed, your cousin will be unbearable."

"He'd ask if I still had rent."

"He'd ask before the ambulance."

"Probably."

Her knee opened against his hip. He slid his hand under the waistband of her tights and then under her underwear and found her already slick. The discovery hit him with a hard, almost stupid rush of relief, as if proof could simplify anything. She caught her breath and pressed into his hand once.

"Beni," she said.

He lifted his head. Her face had changed. It was not colder or afraid. It was just direct.

"What?" he asked.

"What do you actually want from me?"

The room did not move. That was the first thing. The bus outside kept going, the radiator kept ticking, rain kept needling the pane, but inside the question everything held still, as if the whole front room had braced.

He knew what answer would work badly and what answer would work worse. He could say this, and sound like a boy. He could say you, and sound lazier than he felt. He could say I don't know, which would be true in a coward's shape. His hand was still between her legs. His mouth was on the warm skin above her bra. Want had been simple ten seconds earlier. Now it had to pass through his throat and come out attached to something larger than the bed, and the bed was suddenly all he could think about: the damp wall behind it, the chair with his jacket turned to hide cash he had not earned cleanly, the meter eating up the last of his coins so the room could feel less poor for two hours.

"Eszter..." he said.

"Don't say my name like you're buying time."

He shut his eyes. Her body under him stayed open, waiting, and that made it worse. She was not asking for forever. He knew she was not. She was asking him not to arrive in pieces. She was asking him not to use heat as a substitute for being there. The answer should have been small enough to say.

"I want you here," he said finally.

She watched him. "Here tonight?"

"Yes."

"And outside tonight?"

His hand had gone still. He made himself move it again, because stopping felt like another failure, but the movement was wrong now, mechanical, exactly the wrong kind of careful. She caught his wrist and held it, not hard.

"That's not what I mean," she said.

He nodded once as if that could count for language.

"Then say what you mean."

He looked at the damp stain on the ceiling above her shoulder, the one shaped like something spilled and never scrubbed up. In the dark edge of it he could see road maps, borders, any line that let him think in distances instead of this. His body was betraying him in real time. He could feel himself softening against her thigh, not all at once, not theatrically, just enough that shame arrived before there was time to disguise it.

She felt it too. Her face did not change much. That made him more ashamed than if she had laughed.

"Fuck," he said quietly.

"Hey." She pushed herself up on one elbow. "I'm not taking marks."

"I know."

"Do you? Because you look like you're about to jump out the window."

He rolled off her and sat against the wall with his knees up, breathing too carefully. The room felt overfull: chicken skin cooling in the plastic box, vinegar from the peppers, her jumper half on the floor, the sour heat from the radiator, his own skin suddenly cold where sweat touched air. He dragged both hands over his face and came away smelling pickle brine and her.

"Sorry," he said.

"Yes, obviously." She sat up too and fixed the twisted bra strap under her blouse without hurrying. "That's not the useful part."

He wanted to explain that with Nina the problem had not been words because words had not mattered. The body had been enough there, or less than enough, and that had been the whole point: he could leave the rest of himself out of it and still be wanted for something. He wanted to explain that Eszter asking him a plain question felt less like accusation than like being handed a tool and finding no hand free to hold it. None of that would survive his mouth. It would come out self-pitying, or vague, or young in the worst way.

"I don't know how to answer right," he said.

"There isn't a right answer." Her voice had gone flatter now, cleaner. "There's only whether you're here with me or hiding in your own neck."

He let out a breath that scraped. "I'm here."

"No." She shook her head. "Your body's here. Then I ask one real thing and you go somewhere else. I can feel it happen."

He said nothing.

"I'm not asking for a wedding," she went on. "I'm not even asking for next month. I'm asking what this is when it isn't kissing in a stairwell or me feeding you in a kitchen. Because if it's only when you're hungry or lonely or hard, fine. Maybe that's enough for one night. But then say that, and I decide if I want it too."

He stared at the carrier bag by the bed. The Metro date showed through a grease mark: Wednesday. He had folded the paper around the jar without looking at the page. Some minister was smiling over some disaster, and the smile meant nothing.

"It's not only that," he said.

"Good. Then what else?"

He heard himself swallow. The radiator knocked twice. Down on the street somebody shouted and another voice answered, cheerful and drunk.

"When you're not in the kitchen," he said slowly, "I notice that first."

She did not interrupt.

"I wait for you there. Even when I don't need food. Even when I should go. If I hear you talking before I come in, I..." He stopped, then forced himself on. "I listen outside the door for a second like an idiot. On the road, if something stupid happens, I think about how I'd tell you. And then I don't tell you because I don't know how without sounding..." He shrugged once. "Like this."

Her face eased, but only by a little. "Better."

He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Very flattering."

"Don't be a prick. I'm helping you."

"Sorry."

"Stop apologizing for whole paragraphs. It wastes time."

He looked at her then. She had pulled her blouse closed but had not buttoned it. The bent bra hook still sat wrong under the fabric. Her hair was falling out around her face. One of her knees had a faint patch of flour or dust on the tights. She looked solid and tired and unsentimental enough to survive the truth, if he were brave enough to have one.

"I wanted you to come," he said.

"I know. I came because I wanted to."

"I know that too."

"Good. Then don't make me do all the speaking for both of us."

He rubbed his thumb along the seam of the blanket. The cloth was pilled rough as old felt. "I thought if I said the wrong thing you'd go."

"And instead you said almost nothing and nearly left without moving."

He winced.

"That's what I mean," she said, not unkindly. "You think disappearing is safer because it feels polite from inside your own head. It doesn't feel polite out here. It feels like being shut in a room with someone who keeps turning the light off."

That landed so exactly he had to look away.

She stretched for the lager bag, found nothing in it, and clicked her tongue. "Do you have water?"

He got up at once, grateful for the job. In the kitchen he filled the plastic bottle from the tap, listening for Laci's key in the front door though he knew it was still early. The mirror over the sink showed a face too young for the kind of shame it was trying to carry with dignity. He came back before he could think long enough to make himself clumsy again.

Eszter took the bottle, drank, and handed it back. "Thank you."

That simple thank you made him feel worse than the rest of it.

"I didn't mean..." He stopped. Started again. "I wasn't trying to do that to you."

"I know. Intent isn't magic." She tucked one foot under herself and reached for her jumper, then changed her mind and left it where it was. "Listen. I like you. Quite a lot, which is inconvenient because you're nineteen and sometimes you behave exactly like it."

"I'm twenty in January."

"Marvelous. Then in January maybe you'll speak in full sentences."

He laughed before he could help it. She smiled, but only with one side of her mouth.

"I'm serious," she said. "Awkward is fine. New is fine. Even bad is fine, for a while. But blank isn't fine. If you don't know, say you don't know. If you want sex, say sex. If you want me, say me. If you get scared, say scared. Don't go dead and call it being careful."

He sat back down beside her without touching her yet. "All right."

"No. Not all right. Do you understand?"

"Yes."

"Say it like a person."

He looked at his own hands. "I understand."

"Good."

They sat in the warm, ugly room with the remains of the chicken between them and the rain working steadily at the window. He felt emptied out and overexposed at the same time. Still, the pressure in his chest had changed shape. It was not relief. Relief would have been simpler. It was closer to the pain in a hand after it came back from numbness.

After a minute she nudged his shoulder with hers.

"Also," she said, "for the record, you could have just said you wanted me to take my tights off and keep being nice to you. I'm not against simple requests."

He looked at her.

"What?"

"Nothing."

"Liar."

"A little."

"I noticed."

He smiled. She picked at the blanket where a thread had come loose and wound it once around her finger.

"Are you staying?" he asked.

"I said I was."

"I meant staying staying."

"Until your cousin starts pounding on the door or your meter dies. Whichever romance arrives first."

"The meter has eight pounds."

"Look at you. A provider."

"Don't mock my infrastructure."

"Never. It's all we have."

She lay down again, this time on top of the blanket, and patted the space beside her. He followed more carefully than before. When he turned toward her she took his wrist and set his hand flat on her stomach under the blouse, not lower, not yet. It was a reset. He understood that and was grateful for the exactness of it.

Her body rose and fell under his palm. Outside, another bus shoved through the wet road. Somewhere in the building a child cried once and was quieted. The room had gone from charged to ordinary so quickly it almost hurt, but he knew better now than to mistake ordinary for nothing. Ordinary was what people had to build on purpose.

"In Debrecen," she said into the dimness, "my aunt used to say men from flat places are bad liars because there is nowhere for the lie to hide."

"Is that true?"

"No. Men lie everywhere. She just liked sayings."

"We have old women for that too."

"I'm sure."

He traced one small circle over her blouse without meaning to. She caught his fingers before he could turn the touch restless again.

"I'm staying because I want to," she said. "Not because you earned some reward for admitting one thing."

"I know."

"Good. And next time, if you start disappearing while I'm in your bed, I put my bra back on and I go home. Understand?"

"Yes."

"And if there's something else going on with you, some real thing, don't hand me your silence like it's good manners."

He felt the words go through him and lodge. On the chair his jacket hung turned toward the wall, Nina's money still in the hidden pocket, paper weight dragging the cloth out of shape. He lay still beside Eszter and listened to the radiator spend his coins a little at a time.

"I understand," he said.
