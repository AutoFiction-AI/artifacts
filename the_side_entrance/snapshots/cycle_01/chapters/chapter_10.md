# Chapter 10

At 2:58 the Mercer depot smelled of burnt coffee, wet nylon, and the cold coins drivers emptied from cup holders before handing keys back. Beni had one heel up on the leg of the plastic chair, eating the end of a petrol-station sandwich he did not want, when Nina called his private mobile instead of dispatch.

He looked at her name for one ring too long before he answered.

"Where are you?" she said.

"Depot."

"Good. Come to Hackney Wick. The studio on White Post Lane. Now."

Her voice was too even. It had the scraped-flat sound she used with caterers and women on charity boards when she was furious enough to be elegant about it. Behind her he heard music cut off mid-bar and a metal door slamming somewhere farther inside the building.

"Are you all right?"

There was a pause. Then she said, "No. Get the car."

The line went dead.

Beni folded the sandwich back into its paper and dropped it in the bin. Noor's warning from yesterday still sat in him like a stone under the ribs. Rich people's secrets are never worth a poor man's paperwork. He took the Ashdown Mercedes anyway, because saying no to Nina at nearly three in the morning did not feel like a choice he possessed. He wrote nothing on the movement board. The hook clacked empty behind him when he lifted the key.

Rain had been falling for hours without force, the kind that never looked dramatic from indoors and yet got into every seam. By the time he brought the car east past the dark offices at Old Street and the wet arches near Bethnal Green, the windows had furred over at the corners. The wipers made a patient metronome across the glass. London at that hour looked less asleep than badly hidden. Office towers kept whole lit floors for no one. Men in aprons hosed sour foam out of restaurant doorways. A woman in trainers smoked under the awning of a hotel that cost more for one night than Beni paid Laci for two weeks on the floor space in Tottenham. Delivery riders hunched under insulated boxes bright as toys, waiting for people richer than them to keep not cooking.

Hackney Wick arrived in pieces: shuttered breweries, canal water black under security lamps, brick made expensive by being near artists and by being old in the correct way. Jonas's studio sat in a yard behind a metal gate that had failed to latch. Beni nosed the Mercedes in past two white vans and saw Nina before he saw Jonas.

She was standing under the strip light by the entrance, coat hanging open over a dark silk dress, one bare leg wet from the knee down where the rain had blown at her. She had kicked off one shoe and was holding it by the strap like she had forgotten what it was for. Her other hand was wrapped in a dish towel that had soaked through at the center to a black red. Jonas stood just inside the doorway in paint-marked work trousers and a charcoal T-shirt, rubbing one foot up the back of his other calf as if the concrete floor was colder than he had expected. There was gray clay under one thumbnail. He kept twisting the tea towel in his hands even though it was already twisted to rope.

"She cut herself on a glass," he said when Beni got out. "It needs looking at."

Nina turned her head toward him without looking at him. "Do not brief the staff, Jonas."

"For fuck's sake, Nina."

"That isn't even inventive from you."

Beni moved between them before the argument found shape. Up close the towel around Nina's hand smelled of whiskey, iron, and the clean lemon soap people used when they had never had to buy the cheap kind in bulk. Blood had run over the heel of her palm and dried in the fine lines of her wrist. Her mascara had held. Her mouth had not. It looked rubbed raw, as if she had been biting the inside of it.

"You should go to hospital," Beni said.

"I should absolutely not go to hospital."

"It might need stitches."

"Then it can remain artistically unfinished."

Jonas shut his eyes for a second. "You see? This is what I mean."

She laughed once. It was a bright little sound, almost girlish, and it made the back of Beni's neck tighten harder than shouting would have. "No, darling. What you mean is what you always mean. You mean I've become inconvenient."

"I mean it's three in the morning and you've smashed a tumbler because I said I was done."

"You said other things."

"Because we were speaking."

The blood had started fresh under the towel. Beni opened the rear door and said, more firmly than he usually spoke to her, "Sit down, please."

Nina looked at him then, really looked, and for a moment he thought she might slap him or kiss him or laugh in his face. Instead she climbed in with the broken shoe still in her good hand and sat sideways on the leather like someone boarding a train she fully intended to complain about. Jonas took half a step forward.

"Take her somewhere she'll get that cleaned properly."

"I'm not going with you," Nina said, not to Beni but still looking at Jonas. "Let the record show that you have found your boundary at last."

Jonas's mouth tightened. He was handsome in the thin-skinned way of men who had spent enough years being told so that annoyance came to them with a mirror already inside it. "You're drunk and bleeding."

"And you are so brave when a woman is already leaving."

Beni shut the door before Jonas could answer. He did not want the next line, from either of them. He went around to the driver's seat, put the car in drive, and took them out into the rain.

For two streets she said nothing. He could hear only the blower, the tires on the wet road, and the faint dry crackle of the towel when she adjusted her grip on it. In the mirror he saw her lean back, close her eyes, then open them again immediately as if the dark behind them was worse.

"There is a first-aid kit under your seat," he said.

"Of course there is."

"If you let me stop, I can wrap it."

"Drive first."

"Mrs. Ashdown."

"Don't do that."

He waited.

"Not tonight," she said. "Just Nina, if we're still pretending names matter."

He took the A12 west because at that hour it was the cleanest line out, though clean in London only meant fewer chances to sit at lights beside drunk men tapping on the glass. Rain silvered the barriers. The Olympic Park slid by to their right in dead gray metal and sleeping cranes. On a billboard over the carriageway, a development by the river promised studios from £2,150 pcm in letters big enough to be seen from a speeding car. Beni read the number twice without meaning to. More than half his monthly pay for one room and a view of water cold enough to kill you.

At the lights before Bow Roundabout he pulled the hazard switch on and turned half around.

"Give me your hand."

"We're in traffic."

"We're at red."

She made a face at him like a displeased schoolgirl and then held the injured hand out. The towel peeled away with a wet reluctance. The cut ran diagonally across the base of her palm, not deep enough to see anything white, deep enough to keep opening every time she flexed. Blood welled fresh and fat. Beni took saline from the kit, tore it with his teeth, and washed the grit away while the light changed and the man behind them honked once, offended by a world that had not organized itself around him quickly enough.

Nina sucked air between her teeth. "That stings."

"Good."

She gave him a quick look, almost offended and almost amused. "You've been spending time with Eszter."

His fingers paused for half a second. He was glad the light had gone green and the car behind them had moved off, because the man would have seen him sitting there like an idiot with his body turned toward the back seat, one hand around Nina's wrist as if he had forgotten who was employed by whom.

"Pressure," he said.

He folded gauze thick, set it over the cut, and wrapped her hand with the white bandage from the kit. Her skin was warm from drink and shock. The tendons in her wrist shifted under his thumb. He could smell the whiskey on her breath, the sharper green scent of her perfume gone sour with rain, and under both of those the clean iron smell of blood. His body answered before his mind did. He hated that the answer was so old and so immediate.

"Can you move your fingers?"

She did, slow and obedient for once. "There. A triumph of roadside medicine."

"If it keeps bleeding, we stop."

"If it keeps bleeding, you'll drive faster."

He pulled back into the lane. The city tightened around them as they came through Mile End and Whitechapel and then loosened again into the broad damp shine of roads built for finance men to leave after midnight. When he reached Tower Hill she leaned forward.

"Take the river."

"Where do you want to go?"

"Along it. I don't care. West first."

"Home eventually?"

"Eventually everything. West first."

He brought the Mercedes down onto the Embankment and the river appeared to the left, black and broad and carrying strips of torn light. The city made more sense to him from a driver's seat than it ever did on foot. The lane markings held. The bridges arrived in sequence. Buses breathed at stops and moved on. Cyclists in black sprayed water from their wheels. River-facing flats rose in smooth glass stacks with one kitchen lit here, one blue television there, whole dark elevations bought as investment and left to stare at weather. A sales office near St Katharine Docks glowed with a model apartment no one had cooked in and a sign that said from £3.1 million. Down by Blackfriars a man in a suit slept sitting upright on a bench under a shelter ad for luxury retirement living. London knew how to place one insult beside another and call it a skyline.

Nina had stopped pressing the bandage. That worried him more than the blood.

"What happened?" he asked.

She watched his eyes in the mirror. "You mean what was the line? There was a line. Men like Jonas always believe history occurs because they have spoken. Before that there is only atmosphere."

He said nothing.

"He ended it. Properly, this time. No pauses for dignity, no absurd promise to call when things were less complicated, no little speech about timing. He was almost admirable in it. He's been practicing, I think."

At Temple the road narrowed around roadworks and Beni had to drop speed. A taxi cut across him with no signal. He braked, swore softly in Hungarian, and Nina laughed again, this time with actual amusement.

"There you are."

"What?"

"A personality. I was beginning to think you only had shoulders and hands."

He kept his eyes on the lane. "He told you not to come back?"

"He told me he couldn't keep fitting me around the rest of his life. That I had become all appetite and administration. Imagine. Administration. As if I were a filing system he had once found erotic."

She leaned her head back. The strip of city light crossing her face showed where rain had marked her foundation in two pale tracks. "He said he wanted less consequence. That was the actual phrase. Less consequence. He is forty pounds of damp emotional laundry and a trust fund, and suddenly he wants less consequence."

"How much had you drunk?"

"Enough for him to use it."

"How much?"

"Whiskey. Then his natural wine, which tasted like a horse had died in a plum."

Beni almost smiled. He did not let it happen where she could hear it.

"Did he hit you?" he asked.

She opened her eyes. "No."

"Did you hit him?"

"No. I am not sixteen." She shifted, then corrected herself. "I threw the tumbler at the wall. It missed the wall in the sense that walls are not made of glass."

He took them over Waterloo Bridge, turned south, then west again because she had not given him any destination he could complete. The river stayed beside them, sometimes close enough to smell its cold metal, sometimes lost behind hotels, office blocks, and apartment buildings with concierge desks brighter than hospitals. At the Savoy a man in a coat that cost more than the car insurance stood under the awning smoking while a porter held an umbrella over him as if rain had been invented specifically for his inconvenience.

"He said something worse before I threw it," Nina said.

"What?"

She worried the edge of the bandage with one thumbnail. He could see her trying not to pull it loose. It was the first scared thing she had done all night.

"He said he was tired of being cast in someone else's emergency."

Beni's hands stayed steady on the wheel, but inside him something shifted and found purchase. She had called him from the middle of hers anyway.

"Maybe he meant tonight," he said.

"He meant me."

They passed the London Eye half extinguished, its great circle a ghost over the wet road. Men in orange waterproofs were washing yesterday's filth off the pavement with hoses. The water ran toward the drains in black ribbons. A homeless woman's blankets under the bridge had been wrapped in blue tarpaulin so careful it looked almost domestic.

"Do you know what is vulgar about being forty-one?" Nina asked.

He thought of several answers and kept them all.

"Everyone congratulates you on how little damage it shows. As if the achievement were not having been alive. You become this before-and-after comparison with no before visible. Men your own age want peace. Younger men want novelty with references. Your husband wants optics. Everyone is delighted when you are appetizing and self-containing. They become very injured if appetite leaks."

Her voice had lost the hard polish from the phone call. Now it moved in longer lines, spilling and correcting itself, the way expensive wine loosened her without making her sloppy until all at once it did. Beni had heard versions of this register in back seats, outside restaurants, on the way home from dinners where rich women discovered the evening had not repaired whatever the day had done to them. With Nina it was different because she knew exactly what she was performing and still could not stop.

"Oscar likes me best at tables," she said. "Not in private. Certainly not in arguments. At tables I know where my hands go and which story about Bea is harmless and which donor's wife hates whom. Jonas liked me in hotel rooms and in cabs and at his openings, when I made him look chosen by a woman who understood things. But the moment I wanted an actual hour from him that wasn't arranged around his appetite, I became oppressive. Middle-aged. Embarrassingly available."

"You have a daughter. A house. A husband."

"Thank you, Beni. Do continue the inventory of my prison."

He felt the rebuke and the perversity of the heat that came with it. He knew that tone now, the one that made contempt sound like private attention. Six weeks ago he would have heard only the attention.

"I mean people see those first," he said.

"Yes. Exactly." She leaned forward between the seats until he could smell her hair, damp at the ends. "That is exactly what I mean. They see arrangement. They see frame. They see what can be used to explain why I should be grateful and contained. Jonas used to say I made him feel looked after. As if I were some gracious hotel with opinions."

She settled back again. A few cars ahead, a minicab drifted half into the bus lane and then corrected. The river on their left widened and darkened near Chelsea, beyond it whole terraces stood in white stone patience, each window curtained against a life someone else would have called fortunate.

"Pull over a second," Nina said.

He glanced at her. "Why?"

"I want a cigarette."

"You don't smoke."

"Tonight I very much do."

He found a legal bay on Cheyne Walk where no one was likely to care for ten minutes and stopped under a plane tree dripping old rain. The river wall was slick. Across the water the power station's chimneys stood ghost-pale in the thinning dark. Nina opened her bag one-handed, swore softly, and let it spill into the seat beside her: lipstick, card case, a crushed packet of mints, one small perfume bottle, receipts folded into soft white knots, and finally a cigarette case in black enamel.

"You've had those how long?" Beni asked.

"Since a summer in Antibes when I was nineteen and feral."

"Do you know where Antibes is from Tottenham?" he said before he could stop himself.

That made her laugh properly. She bent over the laugh, forehead briefly against the back of his seat. "There he is again. God, don't become funny now. I've got enough to miss already."

She put the cigarette between her lips and held it there, then realized her injured hand made the lighter awkward. Beni reached automatically into his coat pocket for the cheap black lighter he carried for clients who lied about not smoking. The plastic was warm from his body. For one stupid second he almost put it away again. Then he turned in his seat and lit Nina's cigarette with a flick practiced long before London.

She drew in, the tip flaring orange, and looked at the lighter. "That's not yours by class."

"No."

"Was it a gift?"

He shut it and put it back in his pocket. "Smoke or don't."

She exhaled through the crack in the open window. Rain smell, river smell, cigarette smoke, warm leather, blood. It made the car intimate in a way he did not trust. A red double-decker went by nearly empty, top deck lit like an aquarium for one man asleep against the glass.

"He said I make everything heavier," Nina said.

"Did you?"

"Of course I did. What else am I for?" She smiled without humor. "I'm a mother. I'm a wife in technical dispute. I'm a donor list, a seating chart, a car account, a body that has already been interpreted by other people for twenty years. If I love anyone, it arrives with furniture."

She tapped ash into the empty water bottle from the door pocket because even drunk she would not drop it on the leather. That small neatness made him sadder than the blood had.

"Why'd you call me?" he asked.

"Because you answer."

"You could have called anyone."

"No." She looked out at the river. "That's the whole point. I couldn't."

He waited, because waiting was the work and because some part of him still wanted what might come next if he left enough space for it.

"Oscar would turn this into liability by breakfast," she said. "A doctor would make notes. A friend would make it about whether I'm humiliated enough yet. Jonas had finished being noble for the evening. Bea is a child and also far too observant to be used for this. You..." She tipped ash again, careful as a hostess over a linen cloth. "You're good in disasters."

"I drive."

"You contain."

The word sat between them.

Beni looked out through the steamed windshield at the river wall, the slick pavement, the basement light burning in a house where someone had already started breakfast for someone else. He could feel the line drawing tighter, the one between being seen and being used. It was always there with Nina. Sometimes sex blurred it. Sometimes money did. Tonight nothing was blurred enough.

"If you want home, I take you home," he said. "If you want a clinic, I take you there. If you want me to wait until morning, I wait. But don't talk like I'm furniture."

For the first time all night she went still.

The cigarette hovered at her mouth and did not move. Then she lowered it and turned toward him fully, one bare foot tucked under her on the seat, the broken shoe hanging from two fingers. Up close she looked older than she did in daylight and younger than she did at dinners. He could see both women at once and understood how exhausting that must be.

"You're not furniture," she said quietly. "Furniture is expensive and stupid. You're..." She searched for the word, and the searching was its own cruelty because it meant she thought there must be one that would flatter him. "You're a relief."

He did not answer.

"Do you know how rare that is?" she said. "A man who does not immediately begin negotiating. You don't ask for declarations. You don't ask where this is going. You don't pout if I disappear for three days. You don't make me soothe you because I've left. You don't even ask for the truth unless you need it for the route."

The rain ticked in the branches above the car. Somewhere along the embankment a fox barked, sharp and ridiculous and lonely. Beni felt the old reflex rise in him, the one that stepped aside from pain and called the movement practicality. He knew how to let a sentence pass over him. He had done it in two languages for years.

Nina mistook his silence for permission and went on.

"That isn't an insult, Beni. God, don't look like that. It's what makes you safe." Her bandaged hand lifted a little, as if offering him a principle. "You never ask for anything. That is a gift. You have no idea what a gift."

He looked at her in the mirror.

At first he felt only the simple hot sting of it, the way anyone would at being praised for the damage they had spent so long arranging into usefulness. Then something colder arrived behind the sting. He saw, with a clarity so clean it almost calmed him, that she did not mean he was discreet or kind or loyal. She meant he was built for absence. She meant he could be put where need went when need had no respectable address. She meant the worst thing about him had been convenient to her.

Outside, beyond the river wall, a houseboat window lit and a man in a vest stood brushing his teeth over a sink the size of a shoebox. He looked less trapped than Beni felt.

"Drive," Nina said, softer now. "Please."

He put the car back in gear.

They crossed Albert Bridge, its lights trembling in the wet, and came east on the far side of the river before looping back again because he was not ready to point the car toward Belgravia while her words were still warm in the air. The city thinned toward dawn without growing kinder. Street cleaners replaced drunks. Bakery vans replaced black cabs outside clubs. The newspaper bundles outside stations grew under plastic wrap like meat waiting for knives. In the windows of riverside gyms no one had entered yet, televisions already played financial news to empty treadmills.

Nina smoked half the cigarette and then could not stand it, crushing it carefully into the water bottle as if even disgust should leave a neat residue. She spoke less after that. When she did, the sentences came loose and oddly factual.

She told him Jonas had said he was tired of hiding her and tired of being hidden by her in return. She told him she had once mistaken that for seriousness, the hiding, when really it had only been convenience in better clothes. She told him Oscar had not touched her in months except once at a dinner to move a strand of hair from her necklace because photographs were being taken. She told him she had looked at herself in Jonas's studio bathroom tonight and seen a woman expensive enough to display and foolish enough to beg. Once she said, very flatly, "I could have cut deeper if I'd wanted to," and Beni's hands locked harder around the wheel until the leather seam bit into his palm. After that he did not take the car above forty.

He answered when answer was required and no more. He said "Hold the bandage higher" and "Drink some water" and "No, I'm not driving faster on wet roads," the last one making her snort in a way that might have been gratitude. Mostly he drove. That was the only skill he had that did not humiliate him while he used it.

Near Westminster the sky lifted by one shade. Not light yet, only a thinning, but the city felt it. Commuter buses began to fill at the lower decks. A woman in running gear waited at a crossing with the defeated face of someone who had promised herself discipline before coffee. A row of townhouses near Pimlico stood wrapped in scaffolding printed with a fake image of the facades beneath, wealth covering its repairs with a photograph of itself.

Nina had gone quiet enough that he checked the mirror twice to make sure quiet was all it was. She was awake, watching the river through half-lidded eyes. The fury had drained from her face. What remained looked worse. Not sorrow exactly. Exposure.

"Take me home," she said at last.

He turned inland.

Belgravia at dawn always looked indecent to him, as if the district had arranged to have its rubbish removed before daylight could accuse it of being inhabited. The square garden railings shone black with rain. Window boxes hung trimmed even in November. A florist's van was already unloading pale roses into a side entrance that smelled of bleach and money. When he pulled into the mews behind the Ashdown house, the paving stones reflected the garage lamps in one long expensive ribbon.

He got out first and came around to open her door. She sat a moment without moving, gathering herself with the discipline of someone who had built a whole adulthood out of entering rooms correctly. The bandage on her hand had spotted through at the center, a rust-dark bloom no wider than a coin.

"You'll need that looked at in the morning," he said.

"Yes."

"Actually looked at."

"You're becoming very authoritarian for nineteen."

"I'm serious."

"I know."

She put her good hand on the edge of the door and began to stand. Then she stopped and looked up at him from the seat, her hair roughened by the night's damp, one foot still bare on the black rubber mat. In that moment she looked more human than he had ever seen her and no less dangerous for it.

"I did mean it," she said. "About you being safe."

Beni held the door and said nothing.

"Not because you're nothing," she added, hearing at least part of the wound and deciding, as she always did, that explanation could repair what power had done. "Because you don't crowd. Because you let things be what they are."

He thought of the logbook with his altered numbers. He thought of Jonas at the door with clay under his nail and relief already beginning in his face. He thought of Nina's blood drying into white gauze bought in bulk by a company that billed discretion by the hour. Let things be what they are. As if not asking were wisdom. As if silence were not sometimes only fear with good posture.

"Go inside, Nina," he said.

Her mouth changed. Not quite a smile. Not quite defeat. She stepped out, balancing the broken shoe by its strap, and passed close enough that the fabric of her coat brushed his wrist. The contact lit his skin stupidly, treacherously. She felt it or guessed it; he could not tell. She touched the knot of his tie once with two fingers, straightening nothing.

"Sleep a little," she said. "You look wrecked."

Then she went through the side door and vanished into the house that always seemed to swallow evidence by breakfast.

Beni stood in the mews until the automatic light timed out and the garage front went dim. Rain tapped somewhere in the guttering. A plane crossed low and unseen above the city. He got back in the driver's seat because there was nowhere else for him to put what the night had become.

The car smelled of cigarette smoke now, and blood under perfume, and the mechanical warmth of expensive leather being asked to hold too much human mess without showing it. On the rear seat a mint had rolled from Nina's bag and come to rest in the crease by the armrest. He picked it up, looked at the clean silver wrapper, and set it in the cup holder instead of throwing it away.

His phone showed 5:21. In two hours the house would wake. Someone would bring up coffee. Someone would lay out Bea's breakfast. Someone would remove the towel from whatever sink Nina had dropped it in. If she chose, she could make the night into nothing more than a difficult mood and a small domestic accident. Jonas would sleep. Oscar would read numbers. The city would begin charging for itself in daylight rates.

Beni started the engine and sat with it running.

Safe, she had said, as if that were praise.

Useful was what she had meant. Useful because he did not ask. Useful because he had trained every wanting thing in himself to stand still until called. Useful because a man who could disappear that thoroughly could be mistaken for a man who consented to it.

He put the car in reverse, backed carefully out into the wet mews, and drove toward the river again because home was too far, the depot too bright, and he could not yet bear the thought of any place where someone might speak to him as if the night had only been work.
