# Chapter 10

The bell found Jin at the cistern. He was filling the last of the kitchen jugs, the water cold against his wrists where it splashed over the stone lip, and for a count of three he let the sound mean nothing — just another instruction, another hour marked — before the weight of it settled. The naming bell. Different from the meal bell only in Fasa's way of striking it: three measured beats, a pause, three more.

He lifted the jug by both handles and walked it to the kitchen overhang, where Enn Tesh was banking the afternoon fire. She looked up at him and then past him, toward the main hall, and he recognized the expression on her face — the weariness of a woman who had been named enough times to resent the ritual without being able to refuse it. She had flour on her forearms from the evening dough, and she folded them against her chest as if she could keep the kitchen's warmth from leaking out while she was gone.

"Leave it there," she said, meaning the jug. "I'll pour when we're done."

Jin set it on the stone shelf and wiped his hands on his trousers. The water left dark marks on his thighs. He could hear the families moving now, the compound contracting around the bell's summons: Venn's voice directing the Kondos, a child's sandal scuffing flagstone, Nessa Okoye pulling Tomas by the hand while she settled baby Ada against her shoulder with the other arm. Thirteen days of this, and the movements had become practiced. People found their places without being told.

Melie was already inside. He could see her from the hall's entrance — cross-legged in the children's cluster near the left wall, her shoulder touching Kiri Tesh's, both of them still holding the chalked stones they'd been playing with in the courtyard. She hadn't noticed him yet. She was whispering something to Kiri, her mouth close to the older girl's ear, and Kiri was smiling.

She was so easy here. That was the thing Jin couldn't stop noticing and couldn't stop fearing. Melie, who had barely spoken for the first two weeks after her mother left, who had stopped asking for stories at bedtime, who had become careful with Jin in a way no six-year-old should be careful with her father — that Melie had dissolved somewhere during the first week at the shrine and been replaced by this one, this child who laughed at Pik's jokes and climbed into Suna Mera's lap without asking and had once, three days ago, called Bett Dao by her first name without any honorific, as though Bett were family.

Jin took his place at the back right of the hall, where the Parras always stood. Not a family section — there were only two of them, so they occupied the narrow gap between the Teshes and the stone wall, a margin that suited Jin's preference for being peripheral. The stone was cool through his shirt where he leaned against it. The oil lamps had been lit despite the daylight still showing through the hall's open side, and the combined glow gave everyone's skin a warm cast that felt dishonest, as if the light were trying to make this kinder than it was.

Fasa entered from the custodians' walkway. She wore the same cotton shawl she always wore for the naming — gray, undecorated, wrapped over her left shoulder in a way that gave her silhouette a formal asymmetry. She stood at the center of the hall's curved front wall, where the stone floor was slightly raised, and waited. She always waited. She didn't call for silence; she just stopped, and the room quieted around her the way water settles after a stone drops through it.

Olet was not present. He attended some namings and not others, and Jin had stopped trying to predict the pattern. Today there were four custodians in the hall — Fasa and three assistants, one of whom Jin knew only as the young man who refilled the oil lamps every evening and had a habit of humming under his breath while he worked, a thin tune that sounded like a prayer but might have been a pop song from before the crisis. He stood behind Fasa now with his hands clasped, his humming stilled, his face settled into the deliberate blankness all the custodians wore during the naming.

"The naming," Fasa said. Her voice carried without effort. "We are here to name."

The formula. Jin knew it now. First the formula, then each family in turn, then questions if Fasa had them, then whatever came after. The first naming had taken less than an hour. The subsequent ones had stretched longer as the manifestations accumulated and the questions grew pointed.

Fasa began with the Kondos, as she usually did — the largest family went first, either by custom or because Venn's impatience was easiest to manage when he was allowed to go early. Venn stood. He was wearing the same dark button-up he'd worn to every naming, the collar soft from washing in the cistern, and he declared the twelve Kondos without consulting anyone, each name a point of certainty driven into the room: his wife Tila, his son Arash, his sister Habel and Habel's husband Olu, their daughter Ori, aunt Mava, and the rest. A roll call that Venn performed as if repetition could make the family more solid, as if saying the names louder would stop whatever was loosening them from the inside.

Fasa asked Venn a single question about Ori's fear of water, whether it had continued. Venn said it had not. Habel, standing behind him, looked at the floor. Her fingers moved against her own wrist — rubbing, as if checking a pulse that wasn't there.

The Okoyes went next. Bram stood and spoke clearly, the way he always did, his teacher's diction turning the naming into something that sounded almost like a lesson. Five people. He named Nessa, Leni, Tomas, Ada. Fasa had no questions. Leni sat behind her parents with her arms crossed and a face that Jin would have read as boredom in any other context but that he had learned, over thirteen days, was her way of watching everything while pretending to watch nothing.

The Meras. Suna stood. She was holding Asha, who had her face buried in Suna's neck, and she shifted the child's weight to her left hip before speaking. She declared the six Meras — herself, Deshi, Peyam, Rimi, Kael, Asha. No mention of a husband. She never mentioned a husband. Jin noticed this the way you notice the shape of your own wound in someone else's body — the absence in her declaration was the same shape as the absence in his.

Deshi sat behind Suna with her hands folded in her lap, her back very straight, her face composed into the expression she reserved for namings: watchful, assessing, yielding nothing. Peyam had his eyes half-closed. He might have been praying. He might have been asleep. Jin had never been certain which.

The Teshes, the Daos, the Leos — each family standing and declaring and sitting down again, the ritual proceeding with the steady weight of a wheel turning. Gitta Tesh spoke slowly, as she always did, each word arriving with a pause before and after it, and Jin noticed that her hands were steady today. They had not been steady at the last naming. Hala Dao spoke for the Daos, her voice carrying the practiced efficiency of a woman who had assumed responsibility for more people than she'd planned on, and Bett sat beside her without adding anything, her eyes on some middle distance Jin recognized — the focal length of someone who had decided, in advance, exactly how much of herself she would bring to this room. Cade Leo declared the Leos simply — Cade, Wynn, Pik, Vera — and his mother Vera sat behind him with her hand on Pik's back, the child leaning into her with the unselfconscious trust of a seven-year-old who had not yet learned that trust was something people questioned.

Then Fasa looked at Jin.

The transition was nothing — just her gaze shifting — but his body knew before he did. His jaw locked. The muscles along the left side of his face tightened the way they always did before a hard consonant, that fraction of a second where his mouth refused to open, and he had to wait it out, push past it, the word coming through like a door forced against a swollen frame.

"Jin Parra," Fasa said. "You and Melie."

He stood away from the wall. His hands went to his sides. He was aware of the stone behind him, the lip of it pressing against his lower back where he'd been leaning, and he missed it — the wall's support — as soon as he stepped forward.

"Two," he said. "Jin and Melie Parra. Father and daughter."

He waited for the formula to release him, the way it had before — two people, the smallest family, nothing to interrogate because there was nothing to see. But Fasa didn't nod. She didn't look away. She stood very still at the center of the hall, her hands clasped at her waist in the pose she used when the naming was about to ask more than someone wanted to give.

"You declared, at the first naming, that your wife was deceased."

It wasn't a question. Jin felt the words land on his skin like the cistern water — cold, specific, from a source he couldn't stop. He heard a small sound from the children's cluster — Melie shifting, or Kiri, or one of the other children — and the sound drove a spike of focus through his skull.

"Yes," he said.

"The naming has noted a connection between Melie and the Dao family." Fasa's voice was steady, measured, the cadence of someone who had been asking hard questions for twenty years and had learned to remove urgency from her delivery so that the questions themselves could carry the weight. "A response in Melie's body that belongs to the Dao line, not to yours. This is connected to your declaration. Do you understand?"

"I understand what you're saying."

"Then I would like to ask about Melie's mother. When did she die?"

The room was very quiet. Jin could hear the flood below the shrine — that constant rush, deeper today because of last night's rain, a sound he could usually fold into the background but that was now pressing against the walls of the hall like something waiting to get in.

He'd prepared for this. Not carefully — he didn't have the kind of mind that prepared carefully, that built narrative architecture and checked it for consistency — but in the rough way of a man who knows he'll be asked and has a sentence ready. He had a sentence.

"Last year," he said. "In the winter."

"Last winter." Fasa's voice was patient. "Which month?"

He couldn't name a month. The fiction of it — that he had a wife who died in a named month of a named year — buckled under the need to say something true enough to pass. "Late winter," he said. "I don't — the cold months. Before the planting."

"Before the planting. What illness took her?"

He had said illness to someone — he couldn't remember who, maybe Bett, maybe one of the other mothers who had asked about Melie's mama in the way women asked about absent mothers, gently, assuming the worst. He had said illness and illness was what he had.

"A fever," he said. "She got sick and she didn't get better."

"A fever." Fasa repeated it without inflection. "She was young. Was she treated?"

"There wasn't — we don't have—" He stopped. The jaw-lock again, harder this time. He could feel the room's attention on him like heat from the oil lamps magnified, the weight of forty-odd people watching a man stand in lamplight and speak about his dead wife, and he hated them for watching and hated himself for standing here and hated the word *fever* for being so thin, so obviously the first word a liar grabs when he needs a cause of death.

"There wasn't a doctor," he said. "Not where we lived."

Fasa paused. The pause was its own instrument — a space she opened and then stood in, letting the room's silence do work that her voice did not. When she spoke again her tone had not changed, but the question carried something new beneath it, a pressure Jin recognized as the moment when a ritual stopped being form and became excavation.

"Where is she buried, Jin?"

"At home. In our village."

"Your home village." Fasa's hands had not moved from her waist. Her face had not moved from its ceremonial composure. But her voice leaned into the next words with the slight emphasis of someone placing a stone on a scale. "And you are traveling away from your home village. With Melie. During the crisis."

She didn't finish it as a question. She didn't need to. The direction was the crack and everyone in the room could see it: a man whose wife was buried at home, traveling away from home, away from the grave, with a six-year-old, during a political crisis that made the roads dangerous and the journey senseless unless there was something at the other end more powerful than what he'd left behind.

Jin's hands were shaking. He put them behind his back, pressing his knuckles against his spine, and the posture looked like a man trying to appear controlled but was really a man trying to hold on to the bones in his own body.

"I'm taking Melie to her mother's family," he said.

"Her mother's family. They're expecting you?"

"Yes."

"They know about their daughter's death?"

"Yes."

"When did you tell them? By letter? By messenger?"

He hadn't told anyone anything because there was no death to report. The fiction was now three layers deep — the death, the illness, the notification of family — and each layer was thinner than the last, and Fasa was standing there with her liturgist's patience and her ritual attention and she was going to keep asking until the whole structure fell through.

"I sent word," Jin said. His voice sounded wrong to him — flat, detached, as if it were coming from the wall behind him rather than from his own chest.

"When?"

"After she — after." He swallowed. "A few months."

"A few months after the death." Fasa's sentences had the cadence of repetition, of confirmation, but they were not confirmation — they were mirrors held up to his words so that the room could see what he had actually said. "Before the planting, or after?"

He couldn't remember what he'd said. Late winter, before the planting — that was the death. So sending word a few months later would be spring, early summer. But he'd told Bett — he was almost certain he'd told Bett, in the days after the allergic reaction, when she was asking what Melie had eaten at home — that they'd left home recently, weeks ago, not months. If the death was late winter and he'd left home weeks ago, then he'd spent most of a year at home after his wife died before leaving. Which was plausible. But the letter would have arrived at her family months before Jin himself set out, and they would have had time to send something back — a reply, a message, anything — and Jin had nothing, no letter, no response, because none of it had happened.

"I don't remember the exact—" He stopped. The jaw-lock seized him worse than it had in months, the consonant jammed behind his teeth like something physical, and the silence in the hall thickened around his failure to produce the word, and he heard — he was certain he heard — Melie say something from the children's cluster, not a word, just a breath, the small inhalation of a six-year-old whose attention had shifted from the game in front of her to her father standing rigid in the lamplight.

"Is there family on her side?" Fasa asked. "Parents, siblings, anyone who might help us understand the connection between—"

"I've answered your questions."

The words came out louder than he intended. Not a shout — Jin didn't shout — but louder, the volume of a man whose control was a physical object he had been gripping and the grip was failing.

"The naming asks—" Fasa began.

"I know what the naming asks." His hands came out from behind his back. They were still shaking. He looked at them and then at Fasa and then at the room — the ring of families, the oil lamps, the stone floor with its worn places where generations of travelers had stood and been named and been found acceptable or insufficient — and he understood that he was trapped in the exact space between his two impossibilities. He could not tell the truth. He could not sustain the lie. There was nothing else available to him except leaving.

"I said she died," he said. "She died. That's what happened."

The room was absolutely still. Jin could feel each person in it — their heat, their attention, the weight of their watching — and he could feel, behind all of them, the flood's roar from the valley below, and the oil lamps' tiny sounds, the wicks burning, the soft hiss of flame against liquid. Somewhere to his left, in the Okoye section, Leni was watching him with her arms no longer crossed. Somewhere behind Fasa, the young custodian who hummed had gone very still. Bett Dao, in the Dao section, had her eyes on him — steady, not pitying, not hostile, just present — and Jin couldn't bear that either, the gaze of the woman who had saved his daughter, watching him fail to speak about the woman who had walked away from her.

He could tell the truth. He could say it: *she left. She walked out of our house on a Tuesday morning while Melie was eating breakfast and she didn't come back. She's alive. She's somewhere. I told Melie heaven because what word do you give a six-year-old for a mother who chose to leave?*

But Melie was sitting fifteen feet away with her chalked stones in her lap, and if Jin said it here, said it in front of all these people and a six-year-old who believed her mother was with the angels, it would be worse than the lie. The lie at least gave Melie a mother she could love without complication. The truth would give her a mother who didn't want her.

"I won't answer any more questions," Jin said.

Fasa looked at him. Her face held its composure, but something moved behind it — a recognition, maybe, that she was standing at the edge of something she couldn't push past without breaking it. Her mouth opened and then closed. For a moment she looked less like a custodian conducting a rite and more like a woman who had just understood, too late, the cost of what she'd been pressing against.

"The naming notes your—"

"I'm done."

Jin stepped out of his place by the wall and walked toward the hall's open side. The space between the families' sections was narrow — he passed the Meras, passed Suna's face turned toward him with an expression he wouldn't let himself read, passed Deshi's sharp eyes and Peyam's closed ones — and then the air changed as he reached the open side of the hall, where the stone floor met the sky and the valley fell away below and the flood was visible, brown and endless, covering the roads.

Behind him, Melie's voice.

"Papa?"

Small. Not frightened — not yet — but confused, the confusion of a child whose father had just done something outside the architecture of her world. He was supposed to stand in his place and say the words and sit down. He was not supposed to walk away.

He didn't stop. He heard Suna say something low — to Melie, he thought, or to the room, it didn't matter — and then the sound of the hall receded as he stepped onto the courtyard flagstones and kept walking, past the kitchen overhang where Enn's fire was still smoking, past the cistern where he'd filled the water jugs an hour ago, past the terraced garden with its herbs going leggy in the monsoon heat, to the outer wall of the compound where the stone was rough-cut and body-warm from the afternoon sun and there was nothing beyond it but the hill's slope and the flood below and the gray-green sky.

***

He sat down with his back against the wall. The stone pressed against his spine through his shirt. He put his hands on his knees and looked at them — his hands, the dark water-marks still on his trousers from the cistern — and he tried to think about anything other than Melie's voice saying his name and the sound of it landing in the silence he'd made by leaving.

*Papa?*

He pressed his palms flat against his kneecaps. The fabric was damp. The air smelled like rain and wood smoke and the mineral tang of wet stone, and from inside the hall he could hear the naming continue — Fasa's voice, muted by distance, asking someone else's family their questions, the ritual proceeding as if his refusal were a stone thrown into a river, a disruption the current absorbed without changing course.

The shame was easier to feel out here, away from the walls, where there was nothing between him and the valley but the hill's slope and the darkening air. Inside, the shame had been public — witnessed, measured, turned over by forty-odd pairs of eyes. Out here it was just his. It sat in his chest like the water in the cistern — still, cold, with a weight he could feel pressing against his ribs when he breathed.

He thought about the first time he'd said it. Not to the naming — before that, months before. To the woman at the transit office where he'd applied for the travel documents. *Marital status.* He'd looked at the form and the word *married* was there and the word *widowed* was there and neither was true, but *widowed* was closer, because she was gone, because she might as well be dead, because *abandoned* was not a category the form offered and *separated* would have required an address for her and he had no address because she had given him nothing. Not a note, not a reason, not even the courtesy of a fight she could have won.

So he'd checked *widowed.* And the woman at the transit office hadn't looked up, hadn't asked, had stamped his papers and handed them back. That was the first lie. Every one since had been a child of it — quick, easy, each one costing less than the last until the cost had become invisible, until *she died* was as natural in his mouth as *good morning* or *please* or *thank you for letting us stay.*

The lie had worked because nobody asked follow-up questions. A dead wife was a closed door. People looked at you with a softness that was its own kind of shield, and they didn't push, didn't probe, didn't need the details of a grief they could imagine well enough on their own. The lie relied on decency — on the fact that most people would rather assume the worst than demand proof of it — and until Fasa, until this place with its naming and its ritual patience, that decency had held.

Now it hadn't.

He thought about the morning she left. He thought about it most days, but here, with the stone warm against his back and the compound's noise distant and the flood rushing below, the memory had the quality of something being pressed through a sieve — only the hardest parts getting through.

The shoes. She had put on her shoes. That was the part that kept him, because putting on shoes was nothing, was ordinary, was what you did before you walked to the market or the neighbor's or the well. She had been wearing the brown sandals with the strap that needed fixing, and she had bent to buckle them at the door, and Melie had been at the table eating rice with her fingers because Jin hadn't washed the spoons yet, and Jin had been standing at the stove watching the kettle and he hadn't said anything because there was nothing to say about a woman putting on her shoes.

She'd straightened up. She'd looked at Melie — not at Jin, at Melie — and the look was the thing Jin couldn't parse, even now, even after months of parsing. It wasn't guilt. It wasn't grief. It was something closer to a decision that had already been made, a door that was already closed, and the woman on the other side of it was just going through the physical motions of walking out. She looked at Melie the way you look at something you've already lost.

She said, "I'll be back."

She wasn't.

Melie had finished her rice and asked where mama was and Jin had said *out* and then *she went to the market* and then, later, when the market answer stopped working, *she had to go somewhere far away* and then, later still, when Melie's questions grew more specific and her eyes grew more careful, *heaven, baby, mama went to heaven, she's watching you from there.*

Heaven was a better country than the truth. In heaven, mama loved you. In heaven, mama hadn't chosen the brown sandals with the broken strap and the door and the road over the girl at the table with rice on her fingers.

The naming bell rang — three beats, the closing signal — and Jin let his head fall back against the wall and closed his eyes.

He didn't know how long he sat there. The light shifted. The compound's noise changed register — naming-formal to evening-casual, the bell's authority replaced by the smaller sounds of families returning to their sections, children being collected, the kitchen fire being rebuilt for the evening meal. He heard Hala calling for Sem. He heard Venn saying something to Tila, too low to make out, in the tone he used when he wanted something done without being seen wanting it.

He didn't hear Melie.

Suna had her. He was sure of this without being told — Suna, who had three children of her own and a capacity for absorbing other people's emergencies that Jin recognized as the competence of a woman managing her own collapse one task at a time, had taken Melie to the Mera section and was probably feeding her and probably not explaining anything, because Suna understood — he was becoming sure of this too, though he couldn't have said why — the architecture of a lie built to protect a child from a truth that would do nothing but hurt.

The sky above the valley was turning the color of a bruise — purple-gray, the monsoon sunset that wasn't really a sunset, just the light failing through cloud cover until everything went flat and then dark. Jin watched it happen. The shame sat behind everything — behind the warmth leaving the wall at his back, behind the insects starting their evening pulse, behind his closed eyes — with the patience of something that knew it could outlast him.

The worst part wasn't that they'd caught him. The worst part was that everyone in the hall would now be constructing their own version of what he was hiding, and most of their versions would be worse than the truth. They'd think he'd done something to her. They'd think he was running from a crime, or a debt, or a violence he'd committed and couldn't face. They'd look at Melie and wonder if she was safe with him, and the wondering would be reasonable, because a man who lied about his wife's death was a man whose other claims were now suspect, including the most basic one: that he was a fit father.

The truth was just sad. His wife had left. No kidnapping, no murder, no dramatic departure. She had buckled her sandals and walked through the door and the door had closed and that was it. Three months of managing Melie alone. Three months of the specific daily grief that was not dramatic enough to inspire sympathy and not small enough to dismiss — the grief of being chosen against, of being the thing someone looked at and decided to leave behind.

He heard footsteps on the flagstones. Not a child's step — heavier, deliberate, the walk of a tall man who was choosing to announce himself. Jin opened his eyes.

Bram Okoye stood a few meters away, his glasses catching the last of the failing light. He was wearing a sweater his wife had patched at one elbow — green fabric against brown, the seam slightly puckered where the needle had caught — and the patch was the kind of detail Jin normally wouldn't notice but that, in this moment, with his defenses down to nothing, registered with an absurd clarity: someone had fixed this. Someone had looked at the torn elbow and decided it was worth mending, and the person wearing it had let them.

Bram didn't say anything. He walked to the wall, a body's width from where Jin sat, and lowered himself to the ground with the care of a man whose knees objected to cold stone. He leaned his back against the wall. He stretched his legs out. His hands settled in his lap.

Then he reached into his sweater pocket and produced a cigarette — a single cigarette, slightly bent, the paper softened from being carried for weeks — and a match. He struck the match against the wall behind him. The sound was sharp and small in the quiet. He lit the cigarette, took a drag, and held it out to Jin without looking at him.

Jin took it.

The tobacco was stale. It tasted like dust and old paper and the ghost of whatever it had been before the crisis, before the roads, before the shrine. Jin pulled the smoke into his lungs and held it and felt the familiar small violence of it — the burn, the tightening, the body's brief refusal followed by its acceptance — and when he exhaled, the smoke dissolved into the evening air as though it had never existed.

He passed the cigarette back. Bram took it. They smoked in turns, not speaking, the cigarette traveling between them in a rhythm that required no negotiation.

The flood sounded different from out here — not the muffled roar that reached the main hall but something fuller, more textured, the sound of water moving through the valley with purpose. Jin could hear individual currents within the rush, variations in speed and depth that the compound's stone walls had been compressing into a single noise. Somewhere below them, a tree or a piece of road infrastructure was being worked at by the current — a low grinding sound, intermittent, patient.

Bram spoke once. "Nessa's got Ada," he said. "She'll be a while." It was nothing — just a man explaining why he was sitting outside instead of inside, why he had time to waste on a wall with a man he barely knew. But it carried a second weight inside it, quiet as the green patch on his elbow: *I'm not here because I have to be. I chose to sit here.*

Jin didn't answer. He didn't need to. The cigarette went back and forth until it was too short to hold, and Bram pinched it out against the stone and put the stub in his pocket — a teacher's habit, Jin thought, or a father's, someone who had learned to clean up after himself in places that weren't his.

They sat. The light finished failing. The valley went dark below them and the compound's oil lamps came on, their warm glow reaching the courtyard in patches, and Jin could hear the evening meal beginning inside — the clatter of wooden bowls, children being told to sit, Deshi's voice rising above the others with a complaint about the flatbread that was also, in its way, a proof that the world was continuing, that the daily things were still happening regardless of what had broken in the naming hall.

Melie would be eating. Suna would have checked the food — for cashew, for tree nuts, for anything that might trigger the allergy that wasn't supposed to be Melie's but was. Bett would be watching too. Two women watching his daughter eat because he was out here on a wall, unable to go in, unable to face the room he'd walked out of and the forty-odd people in it who now knew, if not what he was hiding, at least that he was hiding something.

"Thank you," Jin said.

He wasn't sure what he was thanking Bram for — the cigarette, the silence, the decision to sit down in the first place — but the words came out before he could examine them, and they were true in a way that caught him off guard, because gratitude had not been available to him for months and its sudden presence felt like warmth returning to something he'd thought was numb.

Bram looked at him for the first time. In the near-dark, Jin couldn't read his expression clearly, but something in the angle of Bram's head — a slight incline, the adjustment of a man who had spent years listening to people say things that mattered — told Jin that the thanks had landed.

"Yeah," Bram said.

They sat together against the wall as the dark settled in and the compound moved through its evening and the flood kept on below them, patient, constant, the sound of water waiting for something that hadn't happened yet. After a while the custodians' closing bell rang — the signal for the oil lamps to be covered, for the families to return to their sleeping sections, for the day to end — and Bram stood, brushed the stone dust from his trousers, and went inside without ceremony, without looking back, the way you walk when you have done the thing you came to do and there is nothing left to add to it.

Jin stayed.

He stayed until the lamps went out and the compound fell into the breathing quiet of sleep and the only light was the cloud-filtered moon on the flood below and the only sound was the water and the insects and, from somewhere inside the stone walls, a child coughing once in her sleep and then settling.

He would have to go inside. He would have to find Melie and carry her to their mat and lie beside her and listen to her breathe and figure out what he was going to do about the fact that his story was now cracked open in front of everyone and he still couldn't tell the truth — not yet, not here, not with her face turned up to his and the word *papa* in her mouth like a name that meant everything he was afraid he wasn't.

He stood. His back ached where the stone had pressed. His shirt was damp with dew or mist or the monsoon air's perpetual wetness, and his knees were stiff, and the compound was dark, and the only direction available to him was in.

He went inside.
