# Chapter 1

The rain found the seams in Suna's shawl before they were halfway up the switchback. Kael walked ahead, his sandals slapping the stone steps with a deliberateness she recognized as bravery — eight years old, performing steadiness for his sister's benefit. Behind her, Deshi climbed with one hand on Peyam's elbow, breathing audibly between the gusts, and Suna could hear the older woman's complaints gathering like weather of their own: the mud, the steepness, the weight of the bags they'd pulled from the truck before abandoning it where the water covered the wheels.

Asha rode on Suna's hip. Four years old and too heavy for this, but the child had stopped walking twenty minutes ago, and there was no argument Suna could win against a four-year-old's exhaustion that wouldn't cost more time than carrying her. Asha's fingers found the wet collar of Suna's blouse and rubbed the fabric between thumb and forefinger, the continuous investigative motion that was her default relationship with the physical world. She touched everything — buttons, bark, the grain of wooden spoons, the hems of strangers' clothing when Suna wasn't quick enough to redirect her hands. Now she was touching the rain, palm open to it, collecting what she could in the cup of her hand and licking her fingers.

"Rimi," Suna said. "Stay with Kael."

"I'm with Kael."

"Where I can see you."

Rimi adjusted her position one step closer to her brother without further comment. She was twelve, angular, already showing her father's long face, and Suna would have been grateful for the obedience except that obedience from Rimi always meant the girl was too frightened to argue.

The shrine appeared through the rain as they came around the last bend — stone walls the color of wet iron, a covered walkway extending from the entrance, and behind it the larger shapes of the compound on the hilltop. There were people under the overhang already, too many to be the shrine's own. Suna counted: a knot of bodies near the entrance, bags and bundles piled against the wall, a child's voice bright and complaining above the rain. Other families had arrived first.

Peyam said something to Deshi that Suna didn't catch. Deshi's response carried: "Of course there are others. Where else would they go?"

Where else. Below them, where the seven roads should have converged at the valley floor, there was only water. Brown, silt-heavy, moving with a weight that made Suna think not of rivers but of something being poured — the valley filling like a basin. She could see the roof of a truck at the waterline, just the ridged metal and a side mirror catching what remained of the afternoon light, and past it the dark shape of a road sign half-submerged. The water had no edges she could find. It extended into the treeline on both sides and vanished around the far bend where the northern road should have been, and the sound of it climbed the hill in a continuous, pressured rush that sat beneath the rain like a second weather, lower and steadier.

"Mama," Asha said, and pressed her wet palm flat against Suna's cheek. The gesture was neither kiss nor comfort — it was Asha's way of making contact, of confirming that the surface she was touching was real and warm and responsive.

"I know," Suna said. "We're almost there."

A man stood at the entrance to the covered walkway. He was lean, mid-sixties, with the unhurried posture of someone who had been standing in that position for some time and would continue standing in it for as long as the situation required. He watched them approach without coming forward to help, without calling out. When they reached the overhang and the rain stopped hitting them, the relief was almost physical — the world narrowing from open hillside to the close stone smell of the shrine and the press of other bodies underneath the roof.

"How many in your family?" the man said.

Suna set Asha down. Her back sang with the release. "Six. Three children."

"Names."

She gave them. Mera. Suna. Rimi. Kael. Asha. Deshi, who was her mother-in-law. Peyam, who was Deshi's brother. The man listened without writing anything, his eyes moving from face to face as she spoke each name, confirming what he saw.

"Your destination, before the road closed."

"Gelden," Suna said. "My husband went ahead. We're meeting him." The lie came out the way it always did, with the flatness of something rehearsed past the point of effort. She had said it so many times that it occupied the space where the truth should have been, smooth and without edges.

The man nodded. He did not ask about her husband, did not note the absence. "I am Olet. I am the head custodian of this shrine. You will be given a place to sleep and food. There are rules. You will hear them when everyone has arrived."

"How many are here?" Suna asked.

"You make seven families."

Seven. Suna looked past Olet into the covered walkway and through to the main hall beyond. The hall was stone — floor, walls, and a vaulted ceiling blackened by decades of lamp smoke. It was large enough to hold everyone, but the largeness was the compressed kind, a space that could fit the bodies but not the distances they wanted between them. On the far side, a wide arch opened onto the valley, and through it the flood was visible in full: the brown water filling the valley floor, smooth and unhurried, the submerged roads marked only by the crowns of trees that lined them. The sound of the water carried through the arch with a low, steady insistence that would, Suna realized, be constant. It would not stop when the rain stopped. It would be there at night when the voices quieted. It was the sound of the thing that was keeping them here.

Oil lamps burned along the walls, already lit against the failing afternoon. A woman with angular features and roughened hands was placing them in iron brackets, moving through the hall with the efficient, unself-conscious rhythm of someone performing a task she had performed a thousand times. She glanced at Suna as she passed — a brief, assessing look, not unfriendly, not welcoming — and continued to the next bracket.

People sat on the stone floor or stood along the walls, their belongings in wet heaps around them. Suna began to count, not the total — Olet had said seven families — but the composition. How many children, how many elderly, how many men. It was what she always did in a new room, the inventory that told her where she stood.

The largest group occupied the far end of the hall. At least ten people, maybe twelve, their bags and bundles claiming territory in the way of a family accustomed to taking up space. A man built like a wall — broad across the shoulders, thick-necked, his gray-streaked hair wet against his scalp — stood at their center speaking to a woman who was folding a length of embroidered cloth with the practiced motions of someone rescuing something valuable from the rain. Wedding cloth. Suna recognized the pattern, the heavy border stitching. This family had been going somewhere specific and festive, and the flood had intercepted them. Beside the big man, an elderly woman sat on a bundle with her hand against her left ear, rubbing it slowly in a circular motion — the absentminded, continuous gesture of a lifelong habit. A young man in his late twenties stood near the elderly woman, holding a bag in each hand, his face less set than his father's, more open to the room.

Near the entrance, where the stone floor was wettest, a young man sat with his back against the wall and a child asleep across his lap. He was thin, younger than Suna, with the dark half-moons of exhaustion beneath his eyes and a posture that made his body a kind of shelter around the sleeping girl. He had set his single bag between his feet and his hand rested on the child's back, rising and falling with her breathing. He was looking at the floor between his knees, his jaw held at an angle that suggested he was working to keep his mouth shut, and Suna felt the quick, involuntary arithmetic of another parent's assessment: one adult, one child, no partner visible, the girl maybe six. She looked away before he caught her looking.

"Suna." Deshi had found a dry section of floor near the wall and was lowering herself with one hand braced against the stone. Her wet sari hem dragged behind her, and she looked at it with the same expression she used for a pan left too long on the fire — a judgment so habitual it required no words. Peyam sat beside her, slowly, his knees giving their report. He was seventy-two and walked with the careful economy of a man who knew his body's budget and allocated accordingly. He reached into his shirt pocket and drew out a string of wooden prayer beads, running his thumb across the first bead as though checking it was still there after the climb.

"Rimi, Kael, come sit," Suna said. "Asha, don't — Asha."

Asha had already crossed three meters of wet stone floor and was reaching for the embroidered wedding cloth that the woman in the large family was folding. Her small hand extended toward the heavy border stitching with the unerring instinct of a child drawn to the most expensive fabric in any room.

"Asha." Suna crossed the distance and caught her daughter's wrist. "That's not ours."

The woman looked up with an expression that was more weary than annoyed. She had the face of someone managing twelve people through a disaster and who had already stopped sorting which annoyances came from inside her family and which from outside. "She's fine," the woman said, but moved the cloth to a higher bundle.

"Sorry," Suna said, and carried Asha back to their section of wall. Asha went without fuss. Her fingers had already found the next thing — the rough texture of the stone wall behind their bags, which she stroked with the flat of her hand, her face concentrated, absorptive, as though the wall were telling her something through her palm.

A tall man in glasses arrived carrying a baby against his chest in a cloth sling, his free hand on the shoulder of a teenage girl whose braids dripped water onto the stone floor. The girl was doing something with her face that she probably thought was indifference — a slack-jawed, half-lidded look — but the way she pressed against her father's side said otherwise. Behind them came a woman, shorter, rounder, holding the hand of a ten-year-old boy who walked close enough to be her shadow. The man said something to Olet that Suna didn't hear, but his voice had the modulated authority of someone accustomed to being listened to, the careful emphasis of a teacher or a speaker.

Two sisters — they had to be sisters, the same broad foreheads, the same way of carrying their weight — came up the walkway with three children between them. The younger sister's face gave away nothing, a blankness so complete that Suna recognized it as something chosen. The older one positioned herself slightly forward, a hand on each of her two children's shoulders, steering them toward the hall with the body language of a woman accustomed to occupying the space between her family and whatever came next.

The last family arrived slowly, because the oldest member could not be hurried. She was small, white-haired, perhaps seventy, and she climbed the final steps with a hand on her son's arm and a deliberateness that was not frailty but precision — each foot placed where she wanted it before she committed her weight. A teenage boy hovered behind her, tall for his age, dark-haired, watching the old woman's progress with a readiness that said he would catch her if she stumbled, though she showed no sign of stumbling. Behind them a woman in her forties and a girl of about eight, the girl looking back down the switchback at the water below with the frank amazement of a child who had not yet learned to be afraid of large things.

By the time the last family was under the overhang, the main hall held forty-three people and the particular density of sound that many bodies produce in a stone room — children's voices, adult murmuring, the rustle and thump of bags being moved, the thin persistent cry of the baby in the sling threading through the rest. And beneath it all, channeled up through the open arch, the flood. Its steady, unhurried rush.

Olet waited until it was clear no one else was coming up the road. Then he stood at the center of the main hall and spoke.

He did not raise his voice. He did not wait for quiet. He spoke into the noise of forty-three damp, uncomfortable, frightened people and the noise lowered itself around him, not silenced but diminished, the way weather sometimes abates without anyone deciding it should.

"This is a transit shrine," he said. "It sits at the meeting of seven roads and has served travelers for longer than records count. You are welcome here. You will be fed from our stores. You will be given places to sleep. In return, you will follow the shrine's schedule. Meals are communal. Labor is shared. There is a daily gathering, which the custodians lead. You will be told more tomorrow."

"How long?" the man in glasses — Bram, Suna would learn — asked. "How long until the road's passable?"

Olet turned to him without hurrying. "The road opens when the water drops. The water drops when the rain stops. The rain will stop when it stops."

A sound from the large family's end of the hall — not quite a laugh, more the exhale of a man who had expected precisely this kind of answer and was too tired to be angry about it. Venn Kondo stood with his arms folded across his broad chest, his wet wedding shirt clinging to his shoulders, and Suna could see the effort he was making not to speak first. He was accustomed to speaking first. His mouth worked briefly, as though testing and then discarding a sentence.

"Now," Olet said. "Sleeping quarters."

He had a system. Suna could see it in the way he moved through the hall — not consulting anyone, not asking for preferences, simply pointing to sections of the floor and the adjacent stone rooms and assigning families to them with the institutional certainty of a man distributing rations. This is your space. That is theirs.

"The Kondo family. The large room on the south side."

Venn moved immediately, but paused at the doorway and turned back. "My family is twelve," he said. "My aunt is seventy-three. There are children who need—"

"It is the largest room," Olet said.

"I can see what it is. I'm asking whether there's—"

"It is the largest room available, and it has been assigned to the largest family." Olet's voice did not change in pitch or pace. He was not arguing with Venn Kondo. He was not acknowledging that an argument was taking place. He was stating a fact about a room, and the fact was finished.

Venn held the doorway for a beat. Suna watched his shoulders shift — the physical recalculation of a man who was used to negotiating from strength and had just discovered that the other party was not negotiating at all. Then Tila, his wife, said something low from behind him, her hand on his back, and Venn turned into the room. The young man — their son — held the door for the elderly aunt, who passed through and touched his forearm as she went, a gesture of thanks or habit or both.

"The Okoye family. The northeast corner of the main hall."

Bram's hands moved — a small, unconscious rotation at the wrists that Suna would come to recognize as his thinking gesture, the motion his body made when an argument was assembling itself. His wife adjusted the baby in her arms and looked at the corner Olet had indicated. It was open to the hall on two sides, semi-private at best. Bram said nothing. He took his son's shoulder and steered the family toward the corner, the teenage girl trailing after them with her jaw set in an expression she must have thought was boredom.

"The Mera family. The room beside the kitchen."

Deshi spoke immediately. "Beside the kitchen? We'll smell cooking all night. Is there nothing—"

"It is what is available," Olet said. Not with emphasis. Not with apology. He said it the way he might have said the stone was stone, or the rain was rain.

Deshi's mouth thinned. She looked at Suna as though Suna were responsible for both the assignment and the custodian's tone. Suna picked up their bags.

"The Parra family. The alcove by the east wall."

The thin young man — Jin — stood without speaking and lifted his daughter onto his hip in one motion that said habit. The girl was awake now, her face pressed into his neck, her arms locked around him with the fierce concentration of a child who had decided that this particular adult was the only fixed point in the room. Jin collected his single bag and walked to the alcove Olet had indicated, a recessed space in the stone wall barely deep enough for two mats. He set the girl down, arranged his bag behind her as a backrest, and sat beside her. He did not look at anyone. He did not complain about the size or the placement. He took what was given with the absolute absence of objection that is its own kind of wall.

"The Tesh family. The west annex."

The old woman — Gitta — rose with the same measured precision she'd used on the stairs. Her son offered his arm. She took it without acknowledgment, as though the arm were a railing, a feature of the architecture rather than a person's gesture. The daughter-in-law gathered the younger girl by the hand. The teenage boy — Fen — picked up the heaviest bag and followed his grandmother, matching his pace to hers without being asked.

"The Dao family. The room past the cistern."

The two sisters. The older one nodded and began to move, her children close. The younger one stood for a moment longer than the rest, her gaze settling on the open arch and the valley beyond. The brown water was darker now as the light failed, and the sound of it had taken on the quality it would have all night — a low, continuous pressure that was not loud but was never absent. Then she turned and followed her sister without having spoken a word.

"The Leo family. The alcove near the prayer courtyard."

The anxious man — Cade — exchanged a glance with his partner, a quick look that carried a question and its answer in the space of a second. His partner touched the boy's shoulder. His mother, a woman in her sixties with a canvas bag and the expression of someone who would form her own opinion in her own time, followed. They moved together with the careful coordination of a family that had practiced being unremarkable.

"These are your spaces," Olet said. "You may arrange them as you wish. The kitchen area is communal. Water is from the cistern. The prayer courtyard is open to all. The custodians' quarters are not. If you need something, ask."

"Is there a radio?" Bram said from his corner. "We haven't had news in two days."

"There is a radio. It is in the custodians' quarters. We will share what news comes."

"Can we hear it ourselves?"

Olet paused — not the pause of a man considering a request, but the pause of a man allowing a request to finish before declining it. "The custodians will share what news comes," he repeated, in the same tone and at the same pace, as though he were reading the same line from the same text.

Bram's hands rotated again at the wrists. His daughter was watching this with the particular attention of someone cataloguing an adult's failures, and she caught Suna looking at her and dropped her gaze so fast it was itself an admission: the girl saw things and did not want to be seen seeing them.

Suna carried their bags into the room beside the kitchen. It was small. The stone floor was dry but cold, and the single window — barely more than a slot in the wall — opened onto the kitchen overhang, from which she could already smell ash and old cooking fires.

"Six of us," Deshi said from the doorway. "In this."

"We'll manage," Suna said. She was laying out mats — thin, woven, serviceable — from a stack the custodians had placed in the hall. Three for the children, one for Deshi, one for Peyam, one for herself. The room was perhaps four meters by three. She could touch both walls if she stood in the center and stretched her arms.

"The large family has a room three times this size."

"They're twelve people."

"We're six. This isn't proportional."

Suna didn't answer. She was counting: mats, blankets, their food supply from the truck — two bags of rice, dried lentils, salt, the tin of cooking oil that had leaked in the bag and now smelled rancid. Not much. If the custodians fed them, it wouldn't matter. If they didn't, it would matter quickly.

"Peyam needs space for his knees," Deshi said. "He can't sleep with his legs bent. You know that."

"I know." Suna positioned Peyam's mat against the far wall, where he could extend his legs without anyone stepping over them. She would take the mat nearest the door. The children between her and Deshi. This was the arrangement. Suna looked at it and saw the next days — the six of them in this room, the stone walls close, the kitchen smell, the sound of the flood outside the window, and no end she could name.

***

By nightfall, the rain had not stopped. It arrived in long, rolling waves — ten minutes of hammering followed by a lull that was never quite silence, then the hammering again. The shrine groaned with it, or the wind groaned through the shrine's stone openings, a low sound that wound through the rooms and corridors and made the oil lamps pull toward the walls.

Suna fed her children from their own supplies — rice cooked in the communal kitchen, lentils, a scrap of pickle that Deshi had packed and now parceled out in slivers, setting each one on the rice with the deliberate precision of someone distributing medicine. The kitchen was the most exposed part of the compound, an outdoor stone counter and fire pit covered by a deep overhang that kept the rain off but not the wind. Suna cooked beside the older Dao sister — Hala, she learned from the woman's daughter, who wandered over to ask Kael if he wanted to see a beetle. Hala worked with the steady competence of someone who could cook for ten in any conditions and had done it often enough that rain and strange kitchens were background noise. They did not speak much, but they shared the fire and the counter space with the wordless negotiation of women accustomed to working in kitchens not their own: a glance to claim a burner, a nod to share the salt, the brief choreography of two bodies avoiding each other's elbows. Hala's hands were large and certain, and she sliced an onion with the heel of her palm guiding the knife — a technique Suna recognized from her own mother, from another region entirely, and the recognition landed in her body before her mind caught up with it.

In the prayer courtyard, which was open to the sky and therefore open to the rain, Peyam stood barefoot on the wet stone and prayed. Suna could see him from the kitchen overhang — a thin, elderly figure with his hands together and his eyes closed and the rain running down his face without interrupting him. He had not asked whether prayer was permitted. He had not asked where to pray. He had found the courtyard, removed his sandals, and begun. His prayer beads hung from his right hand, clicking softly against each other, a small, regular sound that the rain could not quite erase. When he finished, he stood for a moment with his face turned up to the sky, and then he put his sandals on and walked back inside with the careful steps of a man who had just concluded a private piece of business and was ready for the next thing.

After dinner — if you could call it dinner, families eating in clusters in a stone room by lamplight while the rain beat on the world — Suna washed the children's faces and hands with water from the cistern. Kael submitted to it. Rimi did it herself, turning her back to the room for privacy, which she'd started doing six months ago. Asha turned the washing into exploration, rubbing her wet fingers against Suna's wrist, pressing her palm against the cool stone rim of the cistern basin, tasting the water — "Don't drink that, Asha" — and then, as Suna was wringing out the cloth, reaching up and touching the face of a boy who was waiting behind them.

The boy was Kondo. Nine or ten, round-cheeked, with a smudge of something on his collar that he'd been trying to rub out with his thumb. He looked at Asha's hand on his cheek with surprise but not displeasure, the way you'd react to a cat that had walked up and placed its paw on your knee.

"Sorry," Suna said, and caught Asha's wrist. "She does that."

"It's fine," the boy said. He touched his own cheek where Asha's hand had been, briefly, as though checking what she might have found there, and went back to his family's section of the compound.

Asha watched him go with her hand still open, her fingers slightly curled, as though she were holding the impression of his skin in her palm.

Their room was lamplight and stone and the close fact of six people in a space built for fewer. Peyam had not moved since lying down after his prayer. His beads clicked once, softly, and then he was still, asleep with the promptness of a man who had been falling asleep in strange places for seven decades and no longer required comfort to do it. Deshi was on her mat, her back to the room, though Suna could tell by the quality of her breathing that she was not asleep. She was composing tomorrow's complaints, or she was thinking about her son, or she was doing the same arithmetic Suna was doing — supplies, days, the distance between here and anywhere else — and coming to the same numbers.

Suna settled the children. Kael on his back, knees up, the way he'd slept since he was three. Rimi on her side, facing the wall, her breathing deliberately regulated — the twelve-year-old's performance of sleep for the benefit of the room. Asha last. She lay on her mat between Suna and Deshi, and her hands continued to move even as her eyes closed — fingers tracing the edge of the mat, the seam of the blanket, the loose weave of the cotton. She found the hem of Suna's sleeve and rubbed it between thumb and forefinger, that same slow investigation. Her breathing lengthened. Her fingers slowed. But they did not stop. Even at the lip of sleep, Asha was reading the world through her skin, taking the measure of each surface, each texture, each boundary between one material and the next. It was how she learned things. It was how she knew what she knew, through the pads of her fingers, through the palms of her hands, through the patient, unstoppable curiosity of a child whose body was a kind of open question.

Suna lay on her back and looked at the stone ceiling and did not sleep.

She could hear the compound settling around her. A man's voice, low and insistent, from the direction of the Kondo room — Venn, establishing something, commanding something, the bass note of a patriarch who could not stop managing even at the end of a day that had defeated him. The baby crying, thin and persistent, the kind of cry that was not hunger or pain but the pure bewilderment of an infant in a place that smelled wrong. Melie Parra's father murmuring something to his daughter in the alcove, too low to hear, the sound of his voice like a hand held out in the dark. The older Tesh woman coughing once, dry and careful. Someone walking on the stone floor, barefoot, the soft pad of footsteps going somewhere private.

And beneath all of it, the flood. The valley's steady rush, climbing the hill, filling the silence the way water fills a basin — from the bottom, from the edges, with a patience that had nothing to do with intent and everything to do with weight.

"Mama." Kael, already half asleep, his voice dropped to the pitch he used only at the edge of consciousness — when his bravery wore off and he was just a boy in a strange room and the rain was louder than anything he'd slept through before. "When are we going to Papa?"

"When the road opens."

"When does the road open?"

"When the water goes down."

"When does—"

"Sleep, Kael."

He slept. Children could do that — accept an insufficient answer and let it carry them under. Rimi, beside him, was still awake. Suna could tell by the held quality of her stillness, the deliberate steadiness of her breathing. Twelve years old and already performing rest for the benefit of the people around her. She had her father's long face and, Suna was beginning to fear, her father's talent for silence that meant something other than calm.

Suna pressed her hand flat against the stone floor beside her mat. It was cold. The cold entered her palm and climbed her wrist, and she held it there, the solidity of it, the factual indifference of the stone to everything she was carrying.

In the morning, or in two days, or whenever the rain stopped and the water dropped and the roads emerged from the flood like a landscape returning after anesthesia, she would take her children to Gelden. She would arrive at her husband's family's house and explain. That was the plan. That had been the plan since he left.

Since he left. Not since he went ahead. Since he left.

The distinction lived in Suna's body the way a swallowed coin might — hard, foreign, impossible to digest, impossible to pass. He had not gone ahead to prepare the house, to find work, to make things ready for them. He had packed a bag while the children were at school and Deshi was at the market and Peyam was praying, and he had taken his good shoes and the money from the tin above the stove and left a note that said *I'm sorry, I can't do this,* and Suna had found the note and read it and folded it into a square the size of a stamp and put it in her pocket and gone to pick up the children from school. She had made dinner. She had told Deshi that evening that her son had gone ahead to Gelden to prepare. Deshi had looked at her with an expression Suna could not decode — surprise, or suspicion, or the particular stillness of a woman who already knew and was deciding how to hold the knowledge — and said, "Without telling me?"

"It was sudden," Suna had said. "He wanted to get ahead of the rains."

Three months. Three months of sudden. Three months of Kael asking when they were going to Papa and Suna saying soon, and Rimi watching her with those assessing eyes that were her father's eyes in her father's face, and Deshi's questions arriving like weather — indirect, persistent, unanswerable. Three months of performing a marriage that was not a marriage, of pointing toward a destination where no one was waiting, of drawing the shape of a family around a space where a man had been and was not.

And now this. The flood, the shrine, forty-two strangers and a stone room and a custodian who assigned sleeping quarters with the finality of a man who had seen a hundred families arrive at his door and knew that none of them would be the same when they left. She was stuck on a hilltop with her three children and her mother-in-law and a lie that grew heavier with each repetition, and at the other end of the road — whenever the road surfaced — there was no husband waiting. There was a house belonging to people she had met twice, who would look at her standing in their doorway with three children and their bags and know by the shape of her arrival that their son had done what he'd done.

The panic came the way it always did — not as a wave but as a list. What she owed. What she couldn't pay. The rice in the bag, enough for perhaps four days if she stretched it. Kael's sandals wearing through at the soles. Rimi's silence, which was getting longer. Asha's hands, always moving, always reaching, the four-year-old's body open to everything in a world that was not safe to be open to. Peyam's knees. Deshi's questions. The lie about the husband. The money running out. The road underwater. The note in her pocket — she still had it, folded into its stamp-sized square, in the inner pocket of the bag where she kept the children's things, and sometimes she touched it the way Peyam touched his first prayer bead, compulsively, to confirm it was still there, that the thing she refused to look at was still a fact she would eventually have to answer.

From somewhere in the compound, a woman was humming. The melody was unfamiliar, low, circling back on itself the way lullabies do — built not for arrival but for return, the same phrase offered again and again until the child surrendered to it. It was not for Suna. It was for some other woman's child in some other family's room. But Suna lay in the dark and listened to it, and felt, beneath the panic and the lists and the weight of the lie she'd repeated so many times it had almost replaced the thing it covered, the involuntary warmth of being held inside a building full of people who were also afraid, also carrying things, also trying to get their children to sleep in a place that was not home and might not let them leave.

Asha's hand found hers. Small, warm, slightly damp. The child's fingers closed around two of Suna's fingers and held them — not gripping, just holding, with that gentle, total attention she gave to every surface, as if Suna's hand were another texture to be read, another piece of the world to be absorbed and known.

The rain hammered the shrine. The flood talked in the valley below. Suna closed her eyes and did not sleep, and outside the night grew heavy with water and the roads stayed gone.
