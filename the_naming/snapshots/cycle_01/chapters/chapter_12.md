# Chapter 12

Fasa had been watching for fifteen days, and the pattern would not resolve.

She stood at the edge of the main hall where the stone floor ended and the open air began, looking down at the floodwater. Brown, steady, patient as doctrine. It had swallowed the lower switchbacks of the road weeks ago, and each morning she looked for the waterline and found it unchanged — maybe a handspan lower, maybe not, maybe she was imagining the recession because she wanted to see it. The custodians' gauging stone, a post driven into the hillside at the third bend, still showed water up to the fourth mark. In a normal monsoon year the road would have been passable again within ten days. It had been fifteen.

She turned from the view. The compound was mid-afternoon still — the heat pressing everyone into shade, the children's voices muffled, baby Ada's crying arriving from somewhere in the sleeping quarters and then cutting off, soothed or exhausted. In the kitchen area, Hala Dao and Tila Kondo were preparing the evening meal. Fasa could hear the flat slap of dough being worked against stone. The smoke from the cooking fire drifted up past the overhang in a thin column that the wind sheared sideways.

Six connections. She ticked them in her mind the way she had been ticking them for days, the way she ran through the naming's records in her prayers each morning. Asha Mera carrying Mava Kondo's gesture and vocabulary. Melie Parra's body claiming the Dao family's allergy. Tomas Okoye acquiring Jin Parra's speech hesitation. Kiri Tesh humming a Mera lullaby from a region she had never visited. Deshi Mera and Hala Dao making the same flatbread with the same hands. And whatever was forming between Arash Kondo and Bett Dao — not a manifestation but a gravitational thing, a pull toward each other that Fasa had watched tighten over the past week like rope under load.

Six connections across six families. And the Leos, quiet in their corner, untouched.

Fasa didn't know what to make of the Leos' exemption. She had studied them — the careful way Cade positioned himself between Pik and the naming, the way Vera Leo sat at the back of the hall during evening assemblies with her sewing in her lap, eyes sharp above the needlework, the way Wynn spoke only when spoken to. They were a family organized around a vulnerability they did not name. In twenty years, the naming had always reached every group the shrine held. She had never known it to leave a family untouched.

Unless it was waiting.

She pulled herself back from the thought. That was Olet's way of thinking, not hers. Olet believed the shrine had intention. Fasa believed in patterns — observed, tracked, honoured through repetition. She had been doing this long enough to know the difference between mysticism and practice, even when the practice produced things she could not explain.

But the pattern was not resolving. The connections she could see pointed toward something, some shape beneath the visible data, and she could not find what it was. The manifest traits were too varied to be explained by a single mechanism: a dead woman's gesture, a family allergy, a speech impediment, a song from another region. What connected the connections?

She needed to talk to someone who would push back.

---

Suna Mera was scrubbing rice in a clay bowl when Fasa found her in the kitchen area. Her hands worked the grain with the automatic rhythm of someone who had washed rice ten thousand times, the water clouding and clearing as she poured it off and refilled from the jug. Her sleeves were pushed past her elbows, and the scar on her left wrist caught the afternoon light.

"I'd like to talk to you," Fasa said. "And to Bram, if he's willing."

Suna's hands stopped. She looked up, and the expression on her face was the one Fasa had come to recognize over two weeks — competent assessment, the rapid calculation of cost and obligation. "About?"

"The shrine. Its history. Why the naming exists."

Suna rinsed her hands and dried them on her thigh. "You've never offered that before."

"You've never asked."

"Because you told us it wasn't optional. There didn't seem to be much point asking why."

Fasa took that without flinching. It was fair. "I'm asking now if you'd hear it. Both of you."

Suna studied her for a long moment. Then she said, "Bram's in the courtyard with Tomas."

---

They gathered in the prayer courtyard twenty minutes later. Bram arrived with the look of a man who expected to be argued with and had prepared accordingly — his glasses pushed up, his posture slightly forward, his hands already moving before he spoke. Suna sat on the low stone wall that bordered the courtyard, her back to the open valley. The afternoon light was thick and yellow, the kind that preceded evening rain.

Fasa sat across from them on the flagstones. The stone was warm under her legs. She could hear the flood below — the constant rush that everyone at the shrine had stopped noticing but that she, who had lived here for twenty years, had never stopped hearing. It was louder now than it should have been. That was a fact, not an interpretation.

"This shrine sits where seven roads meet," she began. "You've seen them — or you've seen where they were, before the water. The roads come from seven directions, through seven different valleys, connecting regions that are otherwise separated by the mountains. Travelers heading north use the eastern road. Travelers heading to the coast use the western. The roads were laid by different peoples, in different centuries, for different purposes. But they all pass through this point. This convergence."

She paused. The rhythm of telling was familiar — she had told this story to dozens of groups over twenty years — but each telling was shaped by the listeners, and these two were sharper than most.

"The shrine was built here because of the convergence. Not for worship, originally. For shelter. A waystation. Travelers stopped here for a night, sometimes two, before taking the road down and continuing. And the custodians maintained it — kept the water supply, kept the lamps burning, kept the path cleared."

"And conducted the naming," Bram said. His voice had the measured quality of a man assembling a counterargument in real time.

"Not at first." Fasa let the correction sit. "The naming came later. It came because of what the custodians observed."

Suna shifted on the wall. Her legs dangled. Her feet didn't quite reach the ground. "Observed how?"

"Over generations. Families stopped here — different families, from different roads, heading to different places. They shared meals, shared sleeping quarters, shared the daily business of being strangers forced together for a night or two. And the custodians noticed that connections formed. Not friendships — connections. Shared words in dialects that shouldn't overlap. Children who played the same games from different regions. Physical resemblances between strangers. Techniques — cooking, weaving, prayer postures — that matched across families who had never met."

She watched their faces. Suna's was still and assessing. Bram's was becoming harder.

"The custodians began to track these connections," Fasa continued. "They asked families to declare themselves — who they were, where they came from, who belonged to whom. And they kept records. Not written records, not at first. Oral records, passed from one generation of custodians to the next. And over time, the declarations became the naming."

"A ritual that evolved from observation," Bram said.

"Yes."

"Convenient."

Fasa looked at him. "What do you mean?"

Bram leaned forward, elbows on his knees. His hands were moving — the teacher's habit, shaping the argument physically as he spoke. "I mean that every institution has an origin story that makes its power look natural. The naming didn't just evolve. Someone decided to make participation compulsory. Someone decided to tie it to food access. Someone decided that the custodians had the authority to question families about their composition and demand that they answer. Those aren't observations, Fasa. Those are policy decisions dressed up as tradition."

Fasa felt the familiar pull toward her ceremonial register — the balanced sentences, the measured rebuttal. She held herself back from it. Bram's argument was not wrong, and meeting it with liturgy would only confirm his suspicion.

"You're right," she said. "Participation is compulsory. The food condition is coercive. I'm not going to pretend otherwise."

Bram's hands stopped. He hadn't expected concession.

"But the compulsion and the observation are separate things," Fasa said. "The naming is compulsory because the shrine needs it to function. Families arrive and leave. If the declarations are voluntary, most families decline. The connections go unrecorded, and the next generation of custodians has nothing to build on. The compulsion is institutional. I won't defend it as anything else."

"Then what are you defending?" Suna asked. Her voice was flat and direct, the way she sounded when she had run out of patience with abstraction.

Fasa looked at her. "The observation itself. What happens when strangers are held together long enough to see the connections between them. That part isn't institutional. That part is real."

Suna's jaw tightened. "I don't need you to tell me the connections are real. My daughter rubs her ear the way a seventy-three-year-old woman does. My mother's lullaby is coming out of a Tesh child's mouth. I know they're real. What I want to know is what they mean."

"I don't know what they mean," Fasa said.

The words came out plainer than she had intended. She heard herself say them and felt something shift inside her — not physically, not the way the shrine sometimes seemed to settle and resettle on its foundations in the rain, but internally, the thing she stood on when she conducted the naming. Twenty years of practice and she could not explain what was happening in this compound. She could name it. She could track it. She could not explain it.

"In twenty years," she said, more carefully now, "I've seen the naming produce connections. Shared words, shared gestures, shared physical traits. I've seen families arrive as strangers and leave understanding something about each other that neither family could have known. I've kept the records. I've watched the patterns. But this group —" She stopped. Started again. "This group has produced more connections, stronger connections, faster connections than any I've seen. I don't know why."

Suna was watching her with a new expression — not sympathy, exactly, but the recognition of someone telling the truth about the limits of their knowledge. It was the specific look of a practical woman reassessing an authority figure who had just admitted uncertainty.

"So you're saying the naming isn't a theory," Bram said. "It's a method."

"Yes."

"A method of forced disclosure that has, by coincidence, produced results that justify the forcing."

Fasa felt a smile pull at her mouth, despite herself. Bram had the teacher's gift for restatement — he could make your own position sound more coherent than you had stated it and simultaneously reveal its weaknesses. "I know how it sounds."

"It sounds like every other institution that claims its authority is natural," Bram said. "The state tells us our identity papers reflect who we really are. You tell us the naming reflects who we really are. The mechanism is different. The logic is the same."

"The state hasn't made your children manifest each other's traits," Suna said quietly.

Bram turned to her. Something passed between them — not disagreement, but the recognition that their objections came from different places. Bram's was political. Suna's was practical. Both were legitimate, and they pointed in different directions.

Fasa watched them and felt a sudden gratitude that she had chosen these two. They were not making this easy, and she did not want it easy. She wanted it tested.

"I'm not asking you to believe the naming explains what's happening," Fasa said. She spoke slowly, placing each word the way she placed ritual words — not for effect but because the weight was real. "I'm not asking you to believe the connections are ancestral, or spiritual, or meaningful in any particular way. I'm not asking you to accept the custodians' authority or the shrine's history as proof of anything."

She paused. The flood sounded below them. The afternoon light was beginning to thicken toward gold.

"I'm asking you to name. To say who you are, who you carry, what you've lost. Not because I can explain why it matters, but because I've watched what happens when people do."

Suna stared at her. Her hands were on her knees, the fingers slightly spread, the burn scar pale against her skin. "That's a hell of a thing to ask when you've just admitted you don't know what you're doing."

"Yes," Fasa said. "It is."

---

The rain came in the evening, as it had every evening since the families arrived. Fasa stood under the overhang at the edge of the kitchen area and watched it fall across the courtyard in heavy silver curtains, striking the flagstones and bouncing back up in small white explosions. The cooking fire had been covered. The evening meal was done. Most of the families were in the main hall or in their sleeping quarters, settling children for the night.

Suna and Bram had gone back to their families without giving Fasa an answer. She had not expected one. The conversation had done what she needed it to do — it had placed the naming's history in front of the two people whose objections she most respected, and she had heard those objections without collapsing. Suna's pragmatism and Bram's politics were the two challenges the naming had always faced, and Fasa had met them the only way she could: by telling the truth about the limits of her knowledge and asking anyway.

She did not know if it was enough.

Olet found her there. He came from the custodians' quarters with the rain beading on his shaved head and his tunic darkened at the shoulders. He stood beside her under the overhang and looked out at the courtyard the way he looked at everything — without visible reaction, without hurry, as though the rain were a document he was reading at his own pace.

"You told them the history," he said.

"Yes."

"And?"

"Bram thinks we're the state in miniature. Suna thinks we're honest about not knowing and that makes us more troubling, not less."

"They are both correct."

Fasa looked at him. In twenty years of working beside Olet, she had learned that his agreements were never simple. When he said someone was correct, it meant he was about to add something that made correctness insufficient.

"The road is still flooded," he said.

"I know."

"It has been fifteen days. In the last thirty years, the longest the monsoon flooding has kept the road closed was eleven days. That was the year the upstream dam cracked and released the reservoir. There has been no dam failure this year. The seasonal rainfall has been heavy but not exceptional. The road should have reopened by the tenth day."

Fasa waited. She knew what he was going to say. She had known for days that he was working toward it, and she had been avoiding the conversation because hearing it spoken aloud would make it real in a way that her own half-formed suspicion did not.

"The road does not reopen until the naming is done," Olet said.

He said it the way he said everything — without emphasis, without plea, without visible investment in whether she accepted it. A statement. The shrine's position, delivered by the shrine's representative, with the same flat authority with which he assigned sleeping quarters and rang the bell for meals.

"You've said things like that before," Fasa said. "That the naming finds what it finds. That the shrine holds what it holds. I took those as metaphor."

"I do not use metaphor."

She turned to face him fully. The rain was loud enough that no one more than a few meters away could hear them. His face was what it always was — still, weathered, the face of a man who had stopped performing decades ago and now simply occupied whatever expression arrived. He was not trying to convince her. He was informing her.

"You're telling me the flood is the shrine holding them."

"I am telling you that the road has never reopened before the naming is complete. Not in thirty years. Not in my predecessor's time. Not in the records that were passed to me."

"The records are oral. They're not —"

"They are what we have." He paused. The rain shifted, heavier now, drumming on the overhang above them. "Fasa. You have been here for twenty years. You have conducted the naming with every group that has stayed longer than two nights. You have seen what it finds. You have never been able to explain it. This is not new."

"This is different."

"Yes. This group is different. The connections are faster and deeper than any you have seen. And the road is staying closed longer than it should." He looked at the rain. "I do not ask you to believe the shrine has intention. I am telling you what I have observed."

The words landed with the specific weight of an Olet statement — unadorned, unrepeatable, not offered for discussion. Fasa felt the same interior lurch she had felt that afternoon with Suna and Bram, the thing she stood on giving way. But this was larger. Suna and Bram had challenged her practice. Olet's claim reached beneath her practice to what the practice rested on.

If the naming was a method — observation, declaration, pattern-tracking — then it was hers. She had spent twenty years refining it, believing in it, carrying it forward. But if the shrine held people, if the road closed and opened according to whether the naming had found what it needed to find, then the method was a surface, and beneath it was something she had not reckoned with.

"You should have told me this earlier," she said.

"You were not ready to hear it."

She almost laughed. "And now?"

"Now you have told them the truth about what you know and what you do not know. That is the correct preparation for hearing this."

"Preparation." She tasted the word. "You managed me."

"I waited."

It was the same thing, and Olet knew it, and the slight elongation of his pause before the next sentence was the closest she would ever get to an acknowledgment.

They stood together in the rain sound for a while. The courtyard flagstones were black and shining. Water pooled in the grooves between stones and ran in thin channels toward the drainage cut at the courtyard's edge. The lamp in the main hall threw a long yellow shape across the wet ground.

"I want to tell Suna and Bram what you've told me," Fasa said.

"That is your decision."

"If we're asking them to name — to really name, not just the obligatory declarations — they deserve to know the full claim."

Olet said nothing, which was his form of agreement.

---

She found them after the rain slowed.

Suna was in the sleeping quarters, settling Asha and Kael for the night. Fasa waited at the entrance until Suna looked up, read her face, and came out into the corridor, wiping her hands on her wrap.

"What now?" Suna said.

"There's something else."

"There always is."

Bram was closer than she expected — he was leaning against the wall near the main hall entrance, watching the last of the rain, Tomas asleep against Nessa's shoulder just inside the doorway. Fasa caught his eye and he came out, and the three of them stood in the corridor where the lamplight didn't quite reach and the stone walls carried the damp cold of the evening.

Fasa told them what Olet had said. She told it straight — the flood's duration, the historical record, the claim that the road did not open until the naming was complete. She kept her voice even. She did not add her own interpretations or Olet's framing. She gave them the data and the conclusion separately, the way Olet had given them to her, and let them hear the gap between the two.

Bram was the first to speak. "That's insane."

"I know how it sounds."

"No, I'm not — I'm not saying it's irrational. I'm saying it's insane. You're telling me the flood is — what? Held in place? Sustained? By the shrine?"

"I'm telling you the flood has lasted longer than seasonal flooding should, and that Olet believes the road will not reopen until the naming is complete."

"And what do you believe?" Suna asked.

Fasa met her eyes. "I believe the flood is lasting longer than it should. I don't have an explanation."

Suna looked at Bram. He was rubbing his temples with both hands, the glasses pushed up onto his forehead. "This is — Fasa, you understand what this does to the political dimension. If the custodians believe the flood is supernatural, if they believe the naming controls the road —"

"It doesn't change anything practical," Fasa said. "The food condition is the same. The naming is the same. Whether Olet's claim is true or not, the road is still flooded and we're still here."

"It changes everything," Bram said. "Because if the custodians believe their ritual controls physical reality, then they're not running an institution. They're running a — I don't even know what to call it. A cosmology. A belief system that requires our participation to function."

"Yes," Fasa said.

"And you're — what? Presenting this neutrally? As if it's reasonable?"

"I'm presenting it because you deserve to know the full scope of what you're participating in. I told you this afternoon that I'm asking you to name, not to believe. That hasn't changed. But I thought you should know what the head custodian believes about why you're still here."

Suna had been quiet through Bram's objection. Now she spoke, and her voice had the flat careful quality it took on when she was managing something she could not yet categorize. "How long has the road been closed?"

"Fifteen days."

"And the longest it's stayed closed in thirty years, from normal flooding?"

"Eleven days. And that was with a dam failure upstream."

"So we're four days past the worst-case normal."

"Yes."

Suna looked out toward the courtyard. The rain had thinned to a drizzle, and through the drizzle the valley was visible — a dark mass of water below the hill, catching the last gray light. She looked at it for a long time.

"I don't believe the shrine is holding us," she said. "I don't believe a building has intention. But the water's still there, and it shouldn't be."

"Yes," Fasa said.

"And none of us can leave until it isn't."

"Yes."

Suna turned back to her. The competent assessment was still there, but it was layered now over something harder to read — not fear, not belief, but the expression of a woman confronting a situation that did not yield to practical analysis. "Fine," she said. "I don't believe it. But I heard it."

Bram said nothing for a long moment. Then he took his glasses off his forehead and put them back on, and his eyes behind the lenses were tired and angry and something else — something that looked almost like the beginning of uncertainty, which on Bram was more alarming than anger.

"The naming tomorrow," he said. "What are you going to ask?"

"What I always ask. Who you are. Who you carry."

"And if we can't answer?"

"Then the naming waits."

He looked at her, and she looked back, and the corridor was quiet except for the drip of water from the overhang and the distant constant sound of the flood.

---

Fasa did not sleep well that night.

She lay on her mat in the custodians' quarters and listened to the rain stop and start and stop again, and her thoughts moved in the patterns they always moved in — circling, connecting, searching for the shape beneath the visible. The six connections played through her mind like beads on a cord, each one specific and undeniable: a gesture, an allergy, a speech pattern, a song, a cooking technique, an affinity. Each one crossing family lines, joining people who had no reason to be joined. And each one — she could almost see this now, or thought she could — rooted in something missing. Mava's dead sister. Bett's drowned son. Jin's absent wife. The Mera lullaby from a dead grandmother. Nessa's lost baby, the one she had told Suna about in whispered confidence.

Not genealogy. Not ancestry. Something else.

She could not see the full shape yet. It was like watching a constellation form — she could identify individual stars and she could sense that they were arranged, but the lines between them had not yet appeared. She would have to sit with it longer. The naming would have to ask more, and the families would have to give more, and she was afraid of what that giving would cost them.

She rose early, before the bell, and went to the cistern to wash her face. The stone basin was cold, the water colder. She cupped it in her hands and pressed it to her eyes, and the shock was grounding — the body's reminder that the world was solid, that stone was stone, that water was water, whatever Olet believed about the flood.

The compound was stirring. She could hear the assistant custodians starting the morning fire. Someone — Peyam, she thought, by the cadence — was murmuring prayers from the far side of the courtyard. The light was gray and even, the cloud cover unbroken.

She was crossing the courtyard when she saw Vera Leo.

Vera was sitting on the low wall at the courtyard's eastern edge, the same wall where Suna had sat the afternoon before. She had a piece of fabric in her lap — one of Pik's shirts, the sleeve torn at the seam — and she was stitching it with the small deliberate movements of a woman who had been mending clothes her entire life. Her needle case was tucked into the waistband of her wrap, a battered leather thing with thinning stitching of its own, the kind of object that gets passed along rather than bought. Her gray hair was braided tight against her scalp in a style Fasa associated with the western roads — the regions past the second mountain pass, where the air was drier and the customs ran differently.

Pik was sitting beside her, his legs dangling over the wall's edge, his feet bare and swinging. He was seven, small for his age, and like most of the younger children at the shrine he had adapted to the confinement faster than his parents — the compound was his world now, its edges and corners mapped and claimed. He was talking to Vera in the half-attentive way children talk when they want company but not response, a running commentary about something he had seen one of the Kondo children do with a beetle.

Fasa almost walked past. She was heading to the kitchen to check the morning preparations, and Vera Leo was one of the quieter residents — a woman who contributed to communal tasks without complaint, sat at the back during the naming, and maintained the careful neutrality of someone who understood that the shrine's attention was easier to bear when you did not attract it. Fasa had noticed this about Vera: she was watching. She had been watching since the first day, with the eyes of a woman who had raised a family through difficulty and recognized the signs. She had opinions she kept to herself, but Fasa had caught fragments — Vera murmuring to Cade about the food stores, Vera studying Fasa herself during the naming with an expression that was neither hostile nor trusting but weighed each word as it arrived.

Fasa was about to pass when she noticed Pik's hands.

He was still talking — something about the beetle's shell, how it shone — but his hands were not illustrating the story. His hands were clasped in his lap, and his thumbs were circling. Slowly. Right thumb over left in a small, steady orbit, the way a rosary moves through fingers, the way a prayer wheel turns.

Fasa stopped.

The gesture was exact. Not approximate, not a child's clumsy version of an adult's habit. The thumbs circled at the same speed, in the same direction, with the same slight pause at the top of each rotation that Fasa had watched Gitta Tesh make during every naming session, every evening prayer, every moment of quiet when the old woman's hands settled in her lap and her thumbs began their slow, circling devotion.

Pik was not looking at his hands. He was looking at Vera, telling her about the beetle. His hands were doing the gesture the way a body breathes — without instruction, without awareness.

Vera had not looked up from her mending. She was stitching the seam with her eyes on the needle. But as Fasa watched, Vera's needle slowed, and her gaze traveled down from the fabric to Pik's hands, and she went still.

The stillness lasted three seconds, maybe four. Long enough for Fasa to see Vera's fingers tighten on the needle. Long enough for the mending to be forgotten. Vera looked at Pik's thumbs circling in that slow, precise orbit, and then she looked up and found Fasa standing in the courtyard, watching.

Their eyes met. Vera's face did something complicated — recognition, fear, a flash of the protectiveness that Fasa had seen in Cade but that in Vera was older and harder-won, refined by decades of guarding a family that the world did not always make room for. Vera's mouth opened and closed without sound.

Pik kept talking. His thumbs kept circling.

Fasa crossed the courtyard and sat down on the wall beside Vera, leaving Pik between them. She did not speak immediately. The morning was gray and the courtyard smelled of rain-washed stone and cooking smoke, and somewhere on the other side of the compound Deshi Mera was calling one of the children in for breakfast, her voice carrying that particular tone of domestic command that could cross any distance.

"How long has he been doing that?" Fasa asked, keeping her voice low and even.

Vera's eyes were on Pik's hands. "I noticed it yesterday," she said. Her voice was quiet and measured — the voice of a woman accustomed to deciding exactly how much she revealed. "He was sitting with Sem and Raya after the meal. I thought he was fidgeting. He does that — he plays with his hands when he's bored. But this wasn't fidgeting."

"No," Fasa said. "It isn't."

"It's exactly what she does," Vera said. "The old woman. Tesh. I've watched her — I watch everyone here, I can't help it, it's what I do when I'm frightened." She said this last part without self-consciousness, a plain admission delivered in the same measured tone. "She does this in the naming. Every time. Her thumbs go around like that, the same speed, the same little pause at the top. And now Pik's doing it."

"Yes."

Vera set down the mending. Her hands, freed from the task, gripped the edge of the stone wall. Her knuckles were thick with the kind of joint swelling that comes from years of handwork — sewing, scrubbing, the long domestic inventory that never ends and is never acknowledged. "He doesn't know her," Vera said. "He's never spoken to her. He sits with the other children, the younger ones, but Gitta Tesh isn't — they haven't interacted. There's no reason for this."

"No," Fasa agreed. "There isn't."

"Then what is it?"

Fasa looked at Pik. He had stopped talking about the beetle and was watching a moth that had landed on the wall beside him, its wings the dull color of the stone. His thumbs still circled. The motion was steady, a small engine running without fuel.

"I don't know," Fasa said. She was tired of saying it. She had said it to Suna and Bram and to herself and now to Vera, and each time it was true and each time it cost her something — a piece of the certainty she had built her practice on, chipped away. "The other families' children have shown similar things. Traits that belong to other people, other families. This is the seventh."

Vera's head turned sharply. "Seventh?"

"The seventh connection. Your family was the only one not yet involved."

Something moved across Vera's face — the specific combination of dread and a strange, bitter relief that Fasa had seen before in parents and grandparents at the shrine, the look of someone who had been waiting for bad news and found that the waiting was worse than the arrival. "So we're in it now," Vera said. "Whatever it is."

"Yes."

Pik looked up from the moth. "Nan, can I go find Kael?"

Vera looked at him. Her eyes went to his hands, still clasped, the thumbs still moving. She reached out and covered them with her own — gently, not stopping the motion but holding it, the way you might hold a pulse to feel its rhythm without interrupting it. "Go," she said. "Stay where I can see you."

He slid off the wall and went, bare feet on the wet stone, and the courtyard was suddenly larger without him in it.

Vera watched him go. Then she turned to Fasa. "I need to tell Cade."

"Yes."

"He'll — this is going to —" She stopped. Started again, choosing different words, discarding the uncontrolled ones. "Pik is adopted. You know that. Everyone here knows that, or they guessed. Cade and Wynn got him from an institution when he was two. The records were unclear. We don't know where he came from. We don't know who his people were."

"I know," Fasa said.

"So when you say a connection, when you say he's carrying something from this woman — from Gitta Tesh — does that mean he's connected to her? To her family? Because he doesn't have a family." Her voice hardened on the last word, not with anger but with the force of a claim being planted. "He has us."

Fasa heard the precision of it — he has us — and the fear beneath it, the fear of a grandmother who had watched her son build a family outside every traditional structure and who knew how easily that construction could be disassembled by someone with the authority to question its legitimacy. The naming was exactly that kind of authority. Fasa could not pretend otherwise.

"The naming doesn't unmake families," Fasa said. "It tracks connections. It doesn't erase the ones that already exist."

"You'll forgive me if I don't take that on faith," Vera said. "Given what the naming has done to the people in this compound over the past two weeks."

Fasa had no answer for that. The naming had cracked Jin's story open. It had forced Mava's grief into the room. It had made Bett name a dead child she had carried in silence for seven years. The naming's record was not gentle, and Fasa knew it.

"I understand," Fasa said. And she did. "Tell Cade. Talk to Wynn. I won't raise it in the naming until you're ready."

Vera picked up her mending. Her hands were not steady, but she threaded the needle through the torn seam and pulled, and the motion was the same deliberate motion Fasa had watched her make every morning since the families arrived — the hands of a woman who knew that fabric tears and can be repaired, that the mending is never finished, that you keep stitching because the alternative is to let everything come apart.

"One more thing," Vera said, not looking up from the needle. "The woman — Gitta Tesh. Does she know?"

"Not yet."

"Don't tell her. Not before I've talked to my family." She pulled the thread taut and snapped it with her teeth. "We get to be frightened first. She can wait."

Fasa nodded. She rose from the wall and left Vera to her mending, crossing the courtyard toward the kitchen where the morning fire was catching and the smoke was beginning to climb. Behind her, Pik had found Kael Mera near the cistern and the two boys were crouching over something on the wet ground — a puddle, a bug, some small fascination that belonged entirely to them. Pik's hands were at his sides now. But Fasa knew they would clasp again, and the thumbs would circle, and the last family would be drawn into whatever the naming was building, and the pattern would grow larger without resolving, and the flood would remain.

In the main hall, the bell would ring soon. The day's naming would begin. Every family would declare itself again, and one more connection would be waiting to be spoken. Seven families. Seven roads. The pattern pressed against the edges of what Fasa knew, asking to be named, and she could feel it — in the warm stone under her feet, in the sound of the water that would not recede, in the smell of smoke that had seeped into everything they wore and everything they were — she could feel it asking, and she did not yet know the answer.
