# Chapter 18

The light was what Suna noticed first — not the bell, which Fasa rang at the usual hour, the same flat bronze note that had opened every naming since they arrived — but the light that came through the main hall's open side and struck the stone floor in a yellow-white band that none of them had seen in three weeks. The sun was up. It had been up yesterday too, and the day before, since the rain stopped on the evening she and Jin sat in the courtyard and told each other the same lie in reverse. But the light still startled her. It fell across the hall at a steep morning angle, and where it hit the stone it showed every scuff and water-stain of their occupation — the path worn between the entrance and the sleeping quarters, the grey smudge where the cooking fire's smoke had drifted in, the faint greasy mark near the wall where Peyam sat and ate his meals. Three weeks of living pressed into the floor.

She had Asha on her hip. The child was heavy now in a way that meant she'd need to be put down before long, but Suna wanted the weight of her — the specific gravity of a four-year-old's body against her side, the warmth of Asha's forehead against her neck. Asha's left hand was resting on Suna's collarbone, the fingers still, not rubbing her ear for once. Beside them Kael stood with his arms crossed in an imitation of Bram Okoye that he probably didn't know he was doing. Rimi was behind Deshi, close enough that her shoulder nearly touched the old woman's back. Peyam had taken his usual place near the wall, prayer beads still in his hand from the morning's devotion, his pipe tucked into his belt though there was nowhere to smoke it in the hall.

The families filed in. Suna counted them the way she counted her own children — automatically, a reflex she had developed in the first days when she needed to know how many people stood between her and the entrance. The Okoyes came in together, Bram with his glasses pushed up on his forehead, Nessa carrying Ada against her chest, Tomas walking slightly apart from Leni in the way of siblings who have recently argued. The Teshes: Miro carrying a folded blanket under his arm as though he expected to need it, Enn beside him, Kiri trailing behind, Gitta already seated on the stone bench against the far wall with Fen standing at her shoulder. Gitta wore the same grey shawl she had worn every day, pulled tight across her chest despite the warmth. Her hands were still.

The Daos entered from the kitchen side — Hala first, her posture protective as always, angled slightly so that her body screened Bett behind her. Kal walked with Sem, the old man's hand on the boy's shoulder in a way that was more about his knees than about affection, though maybe it was both. Raya had Nori by the hand. Bett came last, her face showing nothing.

The Leos were already seated. Cade and Wynn on the bench near the entrance, Pik between them, Vera on Cade's other side with her knitting in her lap — she'd been working on the same grey rectangle for days, something too small for a blanket and too large for a scarf, and Suna had never asked what it was. Pik was sitting on his hands the way he did when the prayer gesture came on and he was trying to be still.

The Kondos were the last to enter, and they entered as they did everything — as a group, with Venn at the center and Tila half a step behind him. There were twelve of them. Mava walked with Habel, Ori close to her mother's side. Arash was at the edge of the group, not outside it exactly, but positioned the way a person stands near a doorway they are thinking about walking through. He wore the same shirt he'd worn for three days, and the collar was frayed on one side where he'd been pulling at it. Suna had noticed this two mornings ago and hadn't thought about it since, and now she noticed it again.

Fasa stood at the hall's center, not behind the stone ledge where she usually conducted the naming but out from behind it, level with the families, on the same floor. Olet was near the open side, leaning against the stone railing, the valley behind him. From where Suna stood she could see past his shoulder to the view below: the switchback road emerging from the flood's brown margin, the lower bends still glazed with mud but visible, solid ground where for three weeks there had been only water. The waterline had dropped overnight. She could see the stone retaining wall at the third switchback, and clumps of grass and debris caught against it like a tideline.

"This is the last naming," Fasa said.

Her voice was different — not the ritual cadence, not the measured pauses of a woman who spoke liturgy daily, but plainer, as if she had set something down.

"Each family will declare. You know what the naming asks." She paused, and the pause was not ceremonial. "It asks for what is true."

The hall was quiet. Forty-three people and five custodians, and the only sound was Ada's soft breathing against Nessa's chest and the distant, diminished rush of the water below — quieter than it had been, retreating.

Suna felt Deshi's gaze on her back — not a look she could see but a pressure she could feel, the specific weight of her mother-in-law's attention, sharpened by what Deshi suspected or knew or had always known and never said. Suna had told Deshi last night — not in the naming, not in front of the hall, but in the sleeping quarters, after the children were down, in the half-dark with Peyam's snoring as cover. She had said: I'm going to tell the truth tomorrow. About Sev. Deshi had not asked what truth. She had smoothed the edge of her sleeping mat with the flat of her hand, the gesture she used when she was working something out, and she had said nothing. The nothing was louder than anything Deshi had said to her in three weeks, louder than the criticism about the rice and the sleeping arrangements and the way Suna folded the children's clothes. It was the silence of a woman who had already built this room in her mind and was now standing in it.

Suna stepped forward.

She shifted Asha to her other hip. The child's hand came up to Suna's collar, fingers finding the seam the way she found it every time, the small repetitive comfort of touching a thing that held together.

"We are the Mera family," Suna said. Her voice carried. She had not planned it to carry — she had expected to have to push, the way she pushed when she called the children in from the courtyard — but the stone walls did the work for her. "Deshi Mera, my mother-in-law. Peyam Mera, her brother. My children: Rimi, who is twelve. Kael, who is eight. Asha, who is four."

She paused. The weight of the next sentence was physical, sitting in her chest like a swallowed stone. It was not the words — the words were simple. She had rehearsed them in the dark, whispering to the ceiling while Asha slept against her side. The weight was in the room's reception of them, in Deshi's breathing behind her, in Rimi's face, which Suna could not see because Rimi was behind Deshi and Suna had placed her there deliberately so that she would not have to watch her daughter's expression while she said it.

"Sev Mera. My husband. Deshi's son." She said it to the hall, to the stone floor, to the view of the receding water. "He left us. Three months before we came here. He didn't go ahead. He didn't die. He left."

The silence after was not silence — it was the sound of forty people choosing not to speak, which had its own texture, its own particular quality of held breath and swallowed response. Suna felt the sweat on her palm where it pressed against Asha's back. She felt the stone floor through the soles of her shoes, warmed now by the sun that was still moving its band of light across the hall.

Behind her, Deshi closed her eyes.

Suna saw it only because she turned — she had to turn, she could not stand there facing the room and not know what was happening behind her. And Deshi's eyes were closed, and her mouth was set in the line that Suna had learned to read over thirteen years of marriage: not anger, not grief, not the sharp blade of judgment, just the closing of a door she had been holding open for three months, the acknowledgment that the thing on the other side of it was real.

Peyam's beads had stopped moving. He looked at Suna, and in his face — his old, lined, patient face with the pipe-smoke creases around his mouth — there was something she did not expect, not surprise and not sympathy but something closer to the expression of a man who has been watching a load carried poorly and is relieved to see it finally set down right.

Suna sat. She put Asha on the stone floor beside her, and Asha immediately crawled into Kael's lap, and Kael held her with the automatic competence of an eight-year-old brother who had been doing this since she was born. Rimi had not moved from behind Deshi. Suna did not turn to look at her again.

---

Jin stood next. He stood the way he always did — as if standing cost him something, a slight unfolding from his usual hunch, his shoulders pulling back with visible effort. Melie was beside him, holding his hand. She had been told. Suna knew this because Jin had come to her last night, after Suna's conversation with Deshi, and said only: I told her. His face was raw in the lamplight, and his eyes were red, and Suna had not asked him what Melie said because the answer was in his hand — the way he held it slightly open, as though the grip of a six-year-old's fingers had left a tenderness there that he was still carrying.

"Jin Parra," he said. The jaw caught on the first consonant, the slight lock and release that Suna had watched Tomas Okoye develop over the past two weeks, identical, borrowed, impossible. "My daughter. Melie. She's six."

He stopped. The silence stretched. Suna could see his throat working, the swallow that preceded speech the way a breath precedes a dive.

"Her mother's alive," Jin said. "She isn't with us. She left."

It was three sentences, and that was all. He sat down so quickly that Melie stumbled beside him, and his hand found hers again before she'd settled, and she held on with both of hers, her face turned into his arm, not crying, not looking at anyone, just holding on.

Suna watched Jin's free hand — the one Melie wasn't holding — press flat against the stone bench beside his leg. The fingers spread and then curled, and then spread again. She recognized the gesture because she had just done something similar, the body releasing what the mouth had held.

---

Bram stood. He adjusted his glasses, a small precise gesture that Suna had come to understand as preparation — the way a teacher checks his tools before beginning. Nessa remained seated, Ada in her arms, but she shifted her weight forward in the way of someone ready to stand if needed.

"The Okoye family," Bram said. His voice had the teacher's steadiness, the measured pace of a man accustomed to saying important things to people who needed to understand them. "I am Bram. My wife, Nessa. Our daughter Leni, who is fifteen. Our son Tomas, who is ten. Our daughter Ada, who is one."

He paused. It was a different kind of pause from Jin's — not the struggle to speak but the deliberate gathering of a sentence he had built carefully and now needed to deliver whole.

"Before Tomas," he said, "there was Efe." He did not look at Nessa. He looked at the room, his eyes moving across the families with the specific attention of a man who is asking to be heard, not pitied. "He was our son. He lived for three months. He died."

Nessa's voice came from behind him, quiet but distinct — the voice of a woman who had lived with a name inside her for more than ten years and was now saying it where other people could hear.

"Efe," she said — just the name, once — and then she pressed her mouth against Ada's hair and went quiet.

Tomas was staring at his parents. Suna watched his face — a ten-year-old's face, still soft enough to show everything it felt, and what it felt was the specific confusion of learning that the map you thought you knew had a room in it you'd never been shown. His jaw did the catch. Jin's catch. The borrowed hesitation that had no business in a ten-year-old's speech but was there anyway, settled into the bone and muscle of his face as if it had always lived there. He looked at Leni, and Leni — who had been standing with her arms folded and her face carefully blank in the way Suna recognized as her version of armor — put her hand on his shoulder, not gently but firmly, the grip of a sister who was also hearing this for the first time and needed to hold on to something solid.

---

Gitta rose. She used the stone bench to steady herself, one hand flat against the surface, and then she straightened and stood without aid, her back rigid, her shawl pulled tight. Miro was beside her but she did not lean on him. Enn sat with her hands knotted in her lap, her face composed in the careful way of a woman who has already cried about this and is determined not to cry about it again in public.

Gitta's voice was slow. Each word arrived at a distance from the last, weighed and released, the cadence of a woman who had spent sixteen years editing every sentence before it left her mouth.

"The Tesh family," she said. "My son, Miro. His wife, Enn. Their daughter Kiri, who is eight." She paused. The pause was not hesitation. It was architecture — the space between the part of the declaration that was simple and the part that was not. "My grandson, Fen. He is sixteen."

She drew a breath. Suna could hear it across the hall — the slight whistle of air through old teeth, the effort of a woman who was about to say a thing she had already said days ago but which required the same force each time, as if the truth had not gotten lighter with repetition.

"Fen is not my blood," Gitta said. "I found him on the road. He was an infant. His family was dead — a woman, a man, an older child. They had been dead for some time. The infant was alive. I took him. I gave him the name Fen Tesh, and I told my son he was a relative's child, and that was a lie, and I told it for sixteen years."

She did not sit. She stood in the hall's yellow-white light, small and thin and seventy years old, her hands at her sides. She had not asked for forgiveness when she confessed in the hall days ago, and she did not ask now. The naming asked for the truth. She had given it.

Fen stood. He unfolded himself from the bench beside Gitta — taller than her by a full head, dark-haired, his face showing nothing and everything, the particular blankness of a teenager who has decided what he is going to say and is going to say it regardless of what his body does while he says it.

"I am her grandson," Fen said.

It was four words. He sat back down, and his shoulder settled against Gitta's side, and the old woman did not move toward him and did not move away. They sat like that — touching at the shoulder, looking forward, two people who had agreed on what they were to each other and did not need the agreement to be louder than this.

Miro's face was tight. He had known for days, had absorbed the blow in the hall when Gitta confessed, had stood and sat again and not spoken. Now he sat with his forearms on his thighs and his hands clasped between his knees, and Suna recognized the posture — it was the posture of a man doing heavy work inside where no one could see it. Enn's hand moved to his back. She spread her fingers against his shirt, between his shoulder blades, and held them there.

---

Cade stood next, and Wynn stood with him — shoulder to shoulder, a practised formation, the alignment of two people who had learned to present a united front. Cade's face was calm in a way that Suna could see had been built, assembled that morning out of whatever materials he had. Wynn's hand was on Cade's forearm, not gripping, just resting there.

"Cade Leo," he said. "My partner, Wynn. My mother, Vera." He looked down at Pik, who was sitting between Vera and the bench's stone arm, his legs swinging, his right hand moving in the slow circling pattern — Gitta's prayer gesture, thumbs turning over each other, a motion he had never been taught by anyone in his family. "My son, Pik. He's seven."

He did not say adopted. He did not mention the institution, the paperwork, the records that might or might not have been accurate. Suna watched Cade's mouth and saw what he was not saying — the whole careful architecture of explanation and justification that he had prepared and then, in the moment, refused to deliver. He said: my son. And then he sat down.

Vera Leo had noticed Pik's hand. She had been watching it all morning — Suna had seen her eyes tracking the movement the way you track a bird you can't quite identify, with attention that has not yet decided whether to be worried or amazed. Now Vera reached over and took Pik's hand in hers. She didn't press his fingers flat. She didn't still the circling thumbs. She held his hand with the gesture still moving inside her grip — contained but not stopped, like holding a small animal whose heartbeat you can feel against your palm. Pik looked up at her, and Vera looked back, and the grey knitting in her lap slid an inch toward the floor, and she let it go.

---

Hala stood. She was taller than Bett and broader, and she stood with the posture of a woman who had been stepping between her sister and the world for years and did not intend to stop now. Kal was behind her, leaning on Sem's shoulder, his face set in the permanent squint of a man who had spent decades in weather and whose eyes no longer fully opened.

"The Dao family," Hala said. Her voice was clear, matter-of-fact, a voice accustomed to organizing. "I am Hala. My son, Sem, eleven. My daughter, Raya, thirteen. My father, Kal." She paused. "My sister, Bett. And Nori, who is seven. Nori is Bett's daughter."

Hala sat. The declaration had been simple, practical, the facts delivered with the economy of a woman who trusted facts. But the naming was not done with the Daos, and Hala knew it, and everyone in the hall knew it, and the quality of the silence that followed had the texture of a room waiting for something it has already been promised.

Bett stood.

She was smaller than Hala, compact, her face showing the closed watchfulness Suna had come to recognize as Bett's permanent weather. She wore a cotton shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbows, and her forearms were brown and scratched from the terraced garden where she'd been working the day before, pulling weeds from between the herb rows with the focused energy of a woman who needed her hands to be busy.

"Ren," Bett said.

The name landed in the hall the way a stone lands in still water — a single sharp point of contact, and then the rings moving outward, and Suna could feel them, the way each person in the room absorbed the sound and adjusted to it.

"My son," Bett said. "He drowned when he was three. Seven years ago." Her voice had dropped, not in grief but in the specific economy of a woman who had learned that the most important things required the fewest words. "Nori came after. She isn't his replacement. She's my daughter."

Bett sat. She did not look at Hala. She did not look at anyone. Suna watched her hands settle on her knees — steady, the scratched forearms resting on the cotton of her trousers, the fingers quiet. Across the hall, Ori Kondo pressed closer to Habel, the child who had screamed at pooling rainwater three days ago, the child whose terror had belonged to a boy she had never met. Ori did not know why she was afraid of water. She knew only that she was, and that the fear had come from somewhere outside her body, and that the woman who had just sat down was connected to it in a way no one could explain.

---

Venn stood last.

He stood the way he always stood — broad-shouldered, thick-necked, his chin lifted slightly, the posture of a man who spoke for twelve people and expected the room to organize itself around his voice. Tila was beside him. She had dressed carefully this morning — Suna had noticed it at the cistern, the pressed blouse, the hair combed and pinned, the effort of a woman who understood that this day mattered and had expressed that understanding through the only vocabulary available to her at five in the morning with limited water and no mirror.

"We are the Kondos," Venn said. His voice had the weight of formal declaration, the same weight it had carried at the first naming, three weeks ago, when he had stood in this hall and named his family with the pride of a man presenting his life's work to strangers. "We are twelve. Tila, my wife. Habel, my sister. Mava, our aunt. Ori, Habel's daughter." He named the others — the cousins, the nieces, the edges of the family that Suna had learned to recognize over three weeks but had never fully mapped. Each name delivered with the same even weight, each one a brick in the structure Venn had spent his life building.

"Arash," Venn said. "My son."

The name hung. Venn had reached it in the list and it had come out with the same momentum as the others, and now the momentum had stopped, because Arash was not standing with the Kondos. He was standing alone, three feet from the family group, close enough to be part of it and far enough that the gap was visible.

Arash spoke. He did not step forward or raise his voice or make any of the gestures Suna associated with public declaration. He spoke the way you speak when you have made a decision that will cost you everything you assumed about your own future and have discovered that the decision, once made, is quieter than you expected.

"I'm not going to the wedding," Arash said. "I won't continue."

Venn stared. His face was the face from the courtyard confrontation with Bett — the same tight jaw, the same flare in the nostrils — but his body was not moving forward this time. He was standing still. The stillness was worse than the shouting. Suna had seen Venn shout, had watched him cross the hall with his voice raised and his finger pointed, and the shouting was something the room could absorb, could react to, could brace against. The stillness was different. It was the sound of a structure being recalculated from the inside, a man discovering that one of the walls he'd built was load-bearing and it had just been removed.

Mava, seated on the bench behind Venn, reached one hand toward Arash. Her arm extended and hung in the air between them — the same arm whose gesture Asha Mera had borrowed, the slow ear-rubbing motion that had started everything. Her hand did not reach him. She held it out for a moment and then brought it back to her lap, and the gesture was complete in its incompletion, an offer that did not need to be accepted to have been made.

Tila put her hand flat on her own knee. She looked at the stone floor between her feet. Her face did not change expression because the expression she had assembled that morning — the pressed blouse, the pinned hair, the composure — was built to hold, and it held. But her hand on her knee was pressing down, the fingers white.

Venn finished. He named the remaining Kondos — the ones who were staying, the ones who would walk with him down the road to the wedding that his son would not attend. His voice did not waver. He recited the names as if nothing had interrupted, as if the family were still twelve, as if the list itself could hold its shape regardless of what the people on it chose to do with their lives. He sat. His hands went flat on his thighs, and he did not look at Arash, and Arash did not look at him, and between them was the particular silence of two men who loved each other and could not, at this moment, find a form for it that either of them could use.

---

Fasa waited. She stood in the center of the hall, in the band of sunlight that had moved since the naming began and now fell across her hands, and she waited until the room's silence had settled from the active silence of people holding their breath to the quieter silence of people who have heard what they needed to hear.

"Is the naming complete?" she asked.

She asked it to the room, not to Olet, not to any family, but to the air and the stone and the forty-three people who had been strangers three weeks ago and were not strangers now.

No one answered. The question sat in the hall for a long moment, and in that moment Suna became aware of every sound the room contained — Asha's breathing against Kael's shirt, the distant and diminished rush of water from the valley below, a bird somewhere outside the hall that she had not heard before because it had been drowned by rain for three weeks, the creak of the stone bench as someone shifted weight.

Olet spoke from his place at the railing. He had not moved during the entire naming. He stood where he had stood from the beginning, leaning against the stone with the valley behind him, and his voice was the same voice it always was — measured, unhurried, carrying no more weight than it ever carried and no less.

"The naming is complete," Olet said. "The road is open."

The road is open. Three words. Suna looked past him to the valley and saw what she had been seeing since she entered the hall but had not yet allowed herself to understand as fact: the switchback path descending the hill, muddy and littered with debris, but passable. Brown silt on the stones. Tufts of uprooted grass caught against the retaining walls. The waterline a hundred meters below where it had been when they arrived, pulled back to the valley floor like a sheet drawn down from a bed, leaving the damp, stained evidence of where it had lain.

They could leave. The thought arrived with a simplicity that felt almost absurd after everything — the namings, the manifestations, the shouting and the silence and the confessions extracted and volunteered. They could walk down the hill and onto the road and continue to wherever they had been going when the flood stopped them. The thought should have been relief, and part of it was, but the relief was layered over something else, something Suna couldn't name and didn't try to. She had spent three weeks managing collapse one task at a time, and now the tasks were different, and she was already cataloguing them: pack the bags, fill the water skins, check Asha's shoes, make sure Kael had eaten. The list was shorter than usual. The list was almost done.

Fasa looked at Olet, and Olet looked back, and between them passed something Suna could not read — an acknowledgment, a completion, the end of a process they had been conducting together for twenty years and which had, this time, asked more of everyone than it ever had before. Fasa's face was tired, not with the tiredness of a performance completed but with the tiredness of someone who has been holding a thing open for three weeks and can now let it close, and the closing takes a different kind of effort than the holding.

The families began to stand. The naming was over. There was no ceremony for the ending — no prayer, no benediction, no formal release. Fasa stepped back from the center of the hall, and Olet remained at the railing, and the families rose from the benches and the floor and began to move with the specific restlessness of people who have somewhere to go and have been prevented from going there and are now allowed.

Asha pulled at Suna's hand. Suna lifted her.

---

The compound emptied in less than an hour. Three weeks of settling — the sleeping mats pressed into shape by bodies, the cooking arrangements negotiated and renegotiated, the children's worn paths between hall and courtyard and cistern, the nail where Bram hung his jacket, the corner where Mava sat in the afternoons with her back against the warm stone — undone with the brisk efficiency of people who had been waiting to move.

Suna packed the Mera family's things. There was not much. They had arrived carrying what they could, and they were leaving with less — some of it traded for food during the first week, some of it simply worn past use. The bags smelled of cooking smoke and damp stone and the particular sweetness of mildew that had colonized everything during the monsoon's worst days. She rolled the sleeping mats and tied them with the cord Peyam had braided from kitchen twine during the long afternoons, his fingers working the pattern while he watched the rain. Rimi packed the children's things without being asked. Kael carried Asha's mat, and Asha carried nothing, and this was correct — Asha was four, and four-year-olds carried nothing down mountains except themselves.

Deshi packed her own bag. She had not spoken since the naming. She moved with the precise, deliberate economy she always moved with — the economy of a woman who had been managing a household for four decades and could pack a bag the way she could make flatbread, by muscle memory, without thought. But her face was different. The sharpness that Suna had come to expect — the constant evaluative edge, the readiness to judge — was gone, or not gone but set aside, the way you set aside a tool you've been using so long it has worn a groove in your hand. In its place was something quieter — not softness, because Deshi was not soft, but something more like the expression of a person who has been looking at a picture from a fixed angle for years and has just shifted two steps to the left and found that the picture is different from there and she is not sure yet whether different is better or worse.

Peyam packed slowly. His knees. He tucked the prayer beads into his shirt pocket and then took them out again and wound them around his wrist, and then unwound them and put them back in his pocket. He did this three times, and Suna watched without comment, because she understood that the repetition was not indecision — it was the slow leave-taking of a man who had prayed in a specific corner of a specific courtyard for three weeks and had come to feel that the prayers he said there were heard differently than the prayers he would say on the road.

Jin appeared at the edge of the Mera family's space. He had Melie by the hand. Their bag was already on his back — a single bag for two people, cinched tight, the straps adjusted for his narrow shoulders. Melie's face was swollen in the way that children's faces are swollen after crying that happened hours ago and has since been smoothed over with water and distraction but which the skin still holds. She was looking at Rimi.

"We'll walk with you," Jin said. He didn't frame it as a question, but it was — the slight upward tilt at the end, the way his eyes moved from Suna to Deshi and back, the hesitation that was not his speech hesitation but the hesitation of a man who has never asked to be included in a family and does not know the protocol.

"We're heading south-southwest," Suna said. This was practical, not emotional, and the practicality was the point — it was the language they shared, the language of directions and provisions and logistics, and by speaking it she was saying yes without the weight of saying yes. "We've got enough water for the descent."

"I've got extra," Jin said. He touched the water skin on his belt. And that was it — the negotiation complete, the terms settled, two families becoming a walking party through the exchange of compass points and water quantities. Melie let go of Jin's hand and went to stand beside Rimi, and Rimi looked at Suna with the expression of a twelve-year-old who understands that something important has just happened and is reserving her opinion about it.

Suna looked across the courtyard. The compound was alive with departure — bags being shouldered, children being counted, the three assistant custodians standing near the kitchen, watching with the neutral expressions of people who had seen this before. Families that had fought and wept and confessed in the same hall were now navigating around each other in the courtyard with the polite efficiency of strangers at a market, and there was something strange about this, something Suna noticed without being able to articulate: the intimacy of the past three weeks was already retracting, pulling back like the waterline, leaving its mark but withdrawing.

The Teshes were among the first down the road. Gitta walked slowly, her feet careful on the muddy stones, but she did not stop and she did not lean on anyone. Fen carried both bags — his own and hers — slung across his shoulders, and he adjusted his pace to hers without visible effort, the way water adjusts to the shape of whatever holds it. Miro walked ahead with the heaviest pack, Kiri on his shoulders, and Enn walked beside him with her arm through his. At the first switchback, where the road turned and the shrine became visible above them as a whole for the first time — stone against sky, the open side of the hall like an eye — Enn turned and looked back. The rest of her family did not.

The Leos went next. They walked as a closed unit — Vera and Pik in front, Pik's hand in Vera's, the grey knitting tucked under Vera's arm. Cade and Wynn behind, their bags distributed evenly between them in the way of people who had worked out the mathematics of shared weight a long time ago. Pik walked with a slight bounce in his step, the bounce of a seven-year-old for whom a journey is still an adventure and a muddy road is the best kind, and as they rounded the first switchback his free hand came up and his thumbs began their slow circling, the gesture that was not his and was not Gitta's and was his anyway, and Vera's hand tightened around his other hand, and they kept walking.

The Kondos moved like a caravan. They always had — twelve people travelling together required formation, and Venn had drilled the formation into them over years of road travel. He led. Tila was behind him. Habel walked with Ori, and Mava walked with one of the older cousins, her arm through the younger woman's for stability on the steep descent. They were eleven. The formation had adjusted for the absence — closed ranks, tightened gaps, the automatic reconfiguration of a group that has lost a member and cannot stop moving long enough to grieve the loss.

Arash was not with them.

He had walked across the courtyard while the Kondos were packing, and he had stopped near the Dao family's bags, and he had stood there — not speaking, not helping, just standing the way he had stood in the hall — present, visible, his decision made and made again with each second he did not walk back to his father's side.

Bett had looked at him once, briefly, her face showing nothing that Suna could read from this distance. Then she had picked up Nori and settled the child on her hip — Nori, seven years old and too big for it, her legs dangling past Bett's waist — and she had walked toward the road, and Arash had fallen into step beside her, not touching, not speaking, walking in the same direction at the same pace, which was, for now, enough.

Hala walked ahead of them with Sem and Raya. Kal walked behind, his hand on Sem's shoulder again, and he was humming — a low, tuneless sound, the sound of an old man whose body was protesting the descent and who was managing the protest with the only tool available, which was noise. Suna watched them navigate the first switchback and then the second, the family strung along the road like beads on a thread, with Arash among them now, walking where he had chosen to walk, his frayed collar bright in the morning sun.

Behind her, Venn's caravan reached the first turn. Suna watched his back — the broad shoulders, the lifted chin, the posture of a man who leads by going first and does not look to see who follows. He did not look back. The road curved and his body curved with it, and then he was below the retaining wall and out of sight, and Tila looked back once — quick, her eyes finding the spot where Arash had been — and then she turned forward and followed her husband down.

Suna turned to her own family. Deshi was ready, her bag on her back, Peyam beside her with his prayer beads finally settled in his pocket. Rimi had Asha by the hand, and Melie was beside Rimi, and Kael was already three steps down the road, stopped, waiting, his face turned up toward his mother with the impatience of a child who did not understand why adults took so long to leave places.

Jin was beside her. His bag was on his back and Melie's small water skin was clipped to his belt and he smelled, as they all did, of smoke and stone and the particular sweetness of three weeks' close living. He did not say anything. He stood beside her the way Bram had once sat beside him — present, not demanding, the kindness of company that asks for nothing back.

Suna looked up at the shrine. Fasa and Olet stood at the compound's upper entrance, near the covered walkway that led to the custodians' quarters. They did not wave. They did not call out. They stood and watched the families leave the way they had watched them arrive — with the patient attention of people who lived in a place that filled and emptied on a schedule that was not theirs to set. Fasa's hands were at her sides. Olet leaned against the stone. The three assistants had gone back inside.

The shrine looked smaller from the road than it had felt from within. Stone walls, a stone roof, the open side of the hall facing the valley. A building where people stopped on their way to somewhere else, and where something happened to them while they were stopped, and where the happening left marks that the stone would hold and the people would carry. It was already receding — not physically, not yet, they were only a few meters down — but in the way that places recede when you are leaving them, the way the specific pressure of a room releases when you step outside and realize that the air has been different all along.

The road descended in switchbacks, and at the bottom, where the seven roads met, the mud was thick and dark and the debris of the flood lay in heaps against the stone markers — branches, cloth, a sandal, the unidentifiable remains of things the water had carried and discarded. The convergence was a wide, flat space, stone-paved, with each road leading away in a different direction, and the families paused there the way travelers pause at crossroads, briefly, adjusting their loads and looking at the paths ahead.

There were no formal goodbyes. Some families exchanged words — Hala clasped Nessa's hand, Bram nodded at Cade, Deshi and Mava looked at each other for a long moment with the particular attention of two old women who had recognized something in each other that their families would not understand. Kiri Tesh waved at Asha Mera from the eastern road, and Asha waved back with the earnest full-arm wave of a four-year-old, and then the Teshes rounded the first bend and were gone.

The roads diverged. The families took them, one by one and two by two, until the convergence was empty and the hill behind them was just a hill with a building on top, and ahead of them the roads led to the places they had been going when the flood stopped them — weddings, funerals, new cities, old homes, the ordinary destinations that had been waiting, that were still waiting, that would receive them as they were now rather than as they had been.

Suna walked. Deshi was beside her, and Peyam beside Deshi, and Jin behind them, and the children were ahead. The children were always ahead. Rimi and Melie walked together, Melie's hand finding Rimi's the way it had found Jin's, the way Asha's hand found collars and seams, the reflex of a child who held on to what was near. Kael was in front, his pace fast, his face forward. Asha walked on her own feet in the mud, her shoes sinking with each step, each step pulled free with the small sucking sound of wet earth releasing what it had held.

The children walked ahead of the adults. They carried less — some of them carried nothing. Their footprints in the mud were small and evenly spaced, and they did not look back at the shrine on the hill, because the shrine was behind them and the road was in front of them and they were young enough that the direction they faced was the only direction that existed. The adults followed. They carried what they had brought and what they had been given and the names they had finally said, and the road was long and muddy and descending, and the water had left its mark on everything, and the sun was up, and they walked.
