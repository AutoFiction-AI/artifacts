# Chapter 9

Fasa reached the kitchen before the bell stopped ringing. She had learned, across twenty naming cycles, that the work rotations told her more than the naming itself — the naming showed her what people were willing to declare, but the kitchen showed her what their hands knew.

The cooking area was a stone ledge under an overhang on the compound's western side, open to the courtyard and the smell of wet hillside beyond. The fire pit held last night's coals, and the air beneath the overhang was layered — ash at the bottom, green mineral rain above, and the persistent wood-smoke that had worked its way into everyone's hair and clothing by the end of the first week. Fasa stirred the coals and fed them dried grass until flame caught. The wet season made everything slow. Even fire needed coaxing.

Suna Mera arrived first among the assigned families, carrying the morning's water in a clay jug balanced on her hip. She set it down without greeting, but the absence of greeting was itself familiar by now — they had reached the stage of proximity where not every arrival required acknowledgment. Deshi followed a few paces behind, her posture evaluative before she'd even looked at the cooking area, the lean angle of her shoulders conveying judgment about things she hadn't yet seen.

"The coals are still damp," Deshi said. She knelt beside the fire pit and held her palm over the heat, testing. "You need harder wood underneath. Softwood burns wet."

Fasa did not point out that she had been building fires at this shrine for two decades. She moved the clay jug to the flat stone where the cutting board lived and began slicing onions from the garden. Twenty years had taught her when to assert authority and when to let a woman who needed control find it. The kitchen was Deshi's territory, even someone else's kitchen.

Hala Dao came next, with Sem behind her carrying a basket of the dried pulse stores Olet had released from the custodians' pantry that morning. Hala was broader than Deshi, sturdier in the way she occupied space, and she entered the kitchen with the specific confidence of a woman who had fed more people on less. She wore a cotton scarf over her hair that she'd rewashed so many times at the cistern it had faded from blue to the color of overcast sky. She assessed the fire, the jug, the onions, and Deshi's kneeling posture in a single sweep.

"Morning," she said to Fasa. Then, to Deshi: "The wood bin behind the prayer courtyard has harder pieces. Sem, go."

Sem went. He was eleven, dark-eyed, quiet in a way that suggested he was accustomed to running errands without needing them explained. Hala watched him go with the automatic tracking of a mother who counted her children without deciding to.

Deshi looked up from the fire. "I was about to send for the same thing."

"Then we agree," Hala said, and knelt on the opposite side of the fire pit.

Fasa listened to the silence that followed — two women who had just, in three sentences, established that neither would yield the kitchen to the other. She continued slicing onions. The knife was dull; the shrine's tools needed sharpening every few days in the humidity, and no one had done it recently. She could feel the blade dragging rather than cutting, the fibers of the onion separating raggedly under the steel. Her eyes stung. She let them.

The Teshes arrived last. Miro first, a tall man of forty-five who moved with the careful deliberation of someone aware he took up more space than he wanted to and slightly apologetic about it. His hands were large and he used them gently, setting down the flour sack with the concentration of a man placing something breakable. Enn followed, her hair wet from the morning rain, wiping her face with the back of her wrist. She had the look of a woman who had been arguing with someone before she arrived — a tightness around her mouth that she was trying to smooth away.

Gitta came behind them, slower. She carried nothing — Miro had taken her share. She stood at the edge of the overhang where the rain dripped from the stone lip and watched the others settle into the kitchen's geography before choosing her own position. She lowered herself onto the flat stone by the secondary cutting area, folded her hands in her lap, and waited to be needed. Her stillness had a different quality from Suna's stillness — Suna went still to manage crisis, but Gitta went still to disappear, and the discipline of it was practiced enough that Fasa had to remind herself to keep watching.

"We've got lentils, some rice, dried peppers, onions, and turmeric," Suna said, inventorying the stores. "And the flour. That's it from the pantry. We can pull herbs from the garden if someone wants to go down."

"Flatbread," Deshi said. It was not a suggestion. "And a dal. We can stretch the lentils if we cook them long enough."

"Dal needs more spice than turmeric," Hala said.

"Then we use what we have. It'll be bland. People will eat it."

"My father brought cumin seed in his pack," Hala said. "And black pepper. He won't mind."

Deshi paused. The pause was evaluative, and Fasa read it clearly: Deshi was deciding whether to accept help from a woman she hadn't yet ranked in her private hierarchy of domestic competence. Whatever the internal calculus produced, the answer was practical.

"Good. Cumin first, then we'll see."

Enn offered to sort the lentils. She sat beside Gitta and began picking through the dried pulse on a tin plate, discarding stones and shriveled husks with a deftness that had its own kind of authority — her fingers moved quickly, two hands working in complementary arcs, and Fasa could see she had done this thousands of times, that the sorting was not a task she performed but a motion her hands had memorized. Gitta joined her without being asked, and for a few minutes the two women worked in silence, the small clicking of lentils against tin the only sound between them.

Miro took a knife and began cutting onions alongside Fasa. His cuts were slow, methodical, each slice the same width as the last. He did not try to make conversation. Fasa appreciated this. She had noticed that Miro Tesh was a man who understood the value of doing a task without narrating it — a quality she associated with people who had spent years alongside a dominant personality and learned that silence was its own contribution. His mother was that personality, though Gitta's dominance expressed itself through quiet rather than volume, through the particular gravity of a woman whose silence made rooms rearrange around it.

Suna measured rice into the largest pot. She rinsed it three times at the cistern — Fasa heard the water running and then Suna's footsteps on the wet stone, returning — and set the pot over the fire that was now, with Sem's harder wood, burning clean and steady. The rain continued, lighter than yesterday, tapping on the overhang in the irregular rhythm that had become the compound's background music.

The flour came out in a shared bowl. Deshi took half and began mixing it with water, her hands working the dough with an assurance that bordered on aggression — each fold sharp, the heel of her palm pressing with the specific force of decades. Hala took the other half. She mixed her dough more slowly, adding water in careful increments, and when she began to fold, she used two hands where Deshi used one, turning the dough over itself in a motion so similar to Deshi's that Fasa's own hands stopped moving on the cutting board.

They were making the same bread.

The technique was not identical — Deshi folded three times to Hala's two, Deshi used less water, Hala oiled her hands and Deshi floured hers — but the fundamental motion was the same. The press-fold-turn, the way they tested thickness by pushing two fingers into the center of the round and reading the give beneath, the rotational shaping that brought the dough from lump to disc with a specific wrist motion that no one taught because it was the kind of knowledge that passed from one pair of hands to another across a kitchen, not across a page. Neither woman had noticed. They were each absorbed in the rhythm their hands knew, the unconscious mechanics of a task performed so many times it had ceased to be learned and become simply known.

Suna saw it. She was kneeling by the fire, feeding it a piece of the harder wood, and her gaze moved from Deshi's hands to Hala's and back. She caught Fasa's eye across the cutting board. Neither of them spoke.

Fasa watched for another full minute before Hala glanced sideways and went still. She stared at Deshi's hands — at the three-fold motion, at the heel-press, at the two-finger test — and something moved across her face that was not surprise, exactly, but the particular disorientation of seeing your own muscle memory in a stranger's body.

"Your fold," she said.

Deshi, feeling the attention, looked up. Then she looked at her own hands, as though seeing them for the first time in years. Then she looked at Hala's dough, at the two-fold rounds forming under Hala's oiled palms.

A silence opened between them that was different from the competitive silence of the morning. This one had texture and weight, and Fasa could feel both women reaching for an explanation that would make the sameness ordinary.

"Where'd you learn?" Deshi asked.

"My mother. And hers before that."

"Same." Deshi turned her dough over, pressed it flat. "Three folds, though. Makes the layers softer."

"Two's enough if your flour is fine-ground."

"It's not fine-ground here."

"No," Hala said. She looked at the coarse communal flour on the stone. "It isn't."

Hala added a third fold. The concession was small and undramatic — one extra turn of the wrist, one adjustment to a lifelong habit — but Fasa heard it for what it was: one woman revising her technique because another woman's point was good. Deshi did not acknowledge the concession, which was its own form of respect — the respect of assuming competence rather than praising it.

They worked side by side. The flatbreads went onto the hot stone over the fire, and the compound filled with the smell of bread cooking — yeasty, charred at the edges where the dough pressed against the stone, the particular smell that is the same everywhere bread is made over open heat. Fasa breathed it and felt something shift in her chest that she would not have named if asked. She had been conducting namings for twenty years. She had watched families resist, fight, weep, confess, and leave. She had seen the naming produce truths that healed and truths that damaged and occasionally, rarely, something that felt like genuine recognition between strangers. She had never seen it produce flatbread.

But flatbread was not the naming's work, and she knew this. The naming had pushed these families together; the kitchen rotation had put Deshi and Hala at the same fire. The sameness of their technique was not a manifestation — it was culture, the deep grammar of food preparation passed between hands rather than words, alive in the muscles of women who had never met because the knowledge itself was older than either of them.

Still. She watched them, and she allowed herself the private pleasure of watching.

Gitta's voice interrupted the pleasure. "I can make something with the lentils."

Fasa turned. Gitta had finished sorting and sat with the clean pulse in a clay bowl balanced on her thin knees. Her hands were folded over the bowl's rim. Her tone was mild — the elderly-polite register Fasa had catalogued as Gitta's public mode from the first day.

"What did you have in mind?" Suna asked.

"There is a way to prepare them," Gitta said. "You crush the lentils first — not to powder, but rough, so the edges break. Then you toast the cumin in oil until it smokes. The turmeric goes in at the same time, not after. Then the crushed lentils and water, and you cook it thick. Not thin like a dal. Thick enough that it holds the shape of the spoon when you lift it."

She spoke with the precision of a woman reciting from memory, each instruction landing in its exact sequence like beads on a string. Fasa had heard her speak this way before — at the first naming, declaring Fen as her grandson without a flicker of hesitation. The same careful placement. The same certainty.

"That sounds like something my father makes," Hala said. "A tadka, but thicker."

"It is not the same," Gitta said.

Then she stopped. Her hands tightened on the bowl's rim.

"Gitta?" Enn said from beside her.

"I have never made this." Gitta's voice had not changed in volume, but its quality had shifted — the certainty cracked, and what showed through the crack was a bewilderment so raw that Fasa felt her own hands go still on the cumin she was grinding. "I know every step. I can taste it, the way it should taste. But I have never made it."

The kitchen went quiet. Not the charged silence of the naming hall, not the silence that preceded confrontation — smaller than that, more private, the silence of six women and one man recognizing that something strange had happened in a place where strange things had been happening for days. Deshi looked at Gitta. Hala looked at Gitta. Suna's hands stopped moving on the pot she was adjusting over the fire. Miro, who had finished the onions and stood with the knife loose in his hand, turned toward his mother with an expression Fasa could not fully read — concern, yes, but something beneath it, the first fracture of a son seeing that his mother's composure might be a thing she built rather than a thing she was.

Gitta recovered. It took three seconds, and Fasa counted them because counting was what she did when something mattered. Gitta's hands loosened on the bowl. Her face smoothed, each line settling back into the arrangement Fasa had come to recognize as Gitta's public architecture.

"Perhaps I watched someone make it, years ago," she said. "These things stay in the hands."

"They do," Fasa said. She said it because the silence needed filling, and because she wanted Gitta to know that her statement had been heard and would not be challenged in this room. Not now. Not yet.

Gitta made the lentil dish. She made it with the focused precision of a woman following internal instructions that arrived from a source she could not locate, and Fasa watched her hands — the specific way she crushed the lentils between her palms rather than with a stone, the exact moment she judged the oil hot enough by holding her open hand over the pan and reading the heat, the gesture with which she stirred the thickening mixture: slow, counterclockwise, three turns and a pause. Each movement had the quality of the deeply known, and the known had the quality of the uncanny, because knowledge this specific did not come from nowhere.

Twenty years of the naming, and Fasa had never seen it touch an adult. The children were porous — everyone said so, as though children's permeability to outside influence were a natural law that required no explanation. But Gitta was seventy. Her hands had belonged to the same body for seven decades. Whatever knowledge was moving through them now had not entered through the ordinary channels of learning, watching, doing.

Fasa ground pepper into the dal and kept her face arranged. She would bring this to Olet later — not as interpretation, not yet, but as observation. Gitta Tesh knows a recipe she has never made. The manifestations may not have a lower age limit. They may not have any limit at all.

For now, the food cooked. The flatbreads piled up on a wooden board, each one slightly different — Deshi's tighter and more layered, Hala's flatter with a golden char from the slightly hotter side of the stone, the ones Suna made thicker and less even but perfectly good. The dal thickened. Gitta's lentil dish sat in a smaller vessel near the edge of the fire, its surface dark with toasted spice, filling the overhang with a fragrance that did not belong to any of the three families who had contributed ingredients. The rain had eased to a fine mist, and the kitchen felt, for the first time since they'd all arrived at the shrine, like something other than a site of obligation.

---

The custodians' bell called the compound to eat. Fasa rang it herself — she preferred the bell to her voice for routine summons, saving the particular resonance of her speaking voice for the naming, where it served a different purpose.

They gathered in the main hall, forty-three people and the custodians arranging themselves on the stone floor in a configuration that had, over twelve days, become habitual without anyone deciding it should be. The families still sat in their clusters, but the clusters had loosened. Suna's children sat at the Mera family's edge rather than its center — Rimi by the wall where the light from the open side reached, Kael close to his grandmother. Arash Kondo sat a full body-length from his father, and neither acknowledged the distance. The Leo family — Cade and Wynn and Pik and Vera — occupied their corner with the settled air of people who had made a small territory and intended to keep it: a folded blanket on the stone, a tin cup Pik used for water placed at the blanket's edge like a claim marker.

But today the food was the room's center of gravity. The pots and the flatbread board arrived carried by the women who had made them, and when the food was set down on the flat stones in the middle of the hall, the room shifted register. The smell alone did it — the yeast and char of bread cooked on stone, the earthy thickness of dal, and Gitta's lentil dish with its dark toasted-spice fragrance cutting through the smoke-saturated air of the hall like something from a kitchen none of them could place. People leaned forward. Nessa Okoye, with baby Ada balanced on one hip, reached for a flatbread with her free hand and tore it in half with a motion practiced beyond thought, passing the larger piece to Tomas without looking at him.

Kal Dao had brought the cumin and pepper he'd contributed. He was Hala and Bett's father, sixty-eight, with a narrow face and the cautious bearing of a man who had once been taller and had not yet adjusted to the loss. He wore a wool vest with a broken clasp that he kept reaching for and adjusting, pinching the fabric closed over his chest in a gesture so habitual that Fasa suspected the clasp had been broken for months or longer, and the reaching had outlived the repair. He settled beside Peyam Mera as though the seat had been reserved, and Peyam shifted to make room without either man acknowledging the arrangement as deliberate.

The children ate in their clump. This had been the pattern since the first week — the play group that formed during the day carried its own gravity into mealtimes, pulling children out of family orbits and into a loose pile on the stone floor that organized itself by affinity rather than blood. Asha sat pressed against Melie Parra's side, their legs touching. Ori Kondo sat cross-legged with her bowl on one knee. Kiri Tesh was behind Rimi Mera, working on a braid in Rimi's hair with one hand while holding a piece of flatbread in her teeth with the other, tearing off bites between sections. Pik Leo watched the braiding with the serious attention of a seven-year-old encountering a skill he hadn't considered possible. Nori Dao sat beside Asha with her chin nearly in her bowl, eating with the focused hunger of a child who did not yet understand that food could be conversation.

Sem and Raya Dao sat at the group's perimeter — thirteen and eleven, old enough to feel self-conscious about the cross-family mixing but not old enough to walk away from it entirely. Fen Tesh sat further back, near the wall, eating slowly with his shoulders rounded. Sixteen was too old for the children's clump and too young for the adults' weary accommodation, and Fen inhabited the gap with the particular discomfort of a teenager who had not yet decided where his body belonged.

Leni Okoye sat with her parents and chewed her flatbread and watched the children's group with an expression Fasa recognized — the wary attention of someone who had recently understood that observing the world closely was both a skill and a kind of distance from it.

People ate. The dal was bland, as Deshi had warned, but the flatbread was good — warm and layered, with enough char at the edges to give it texture — and Gitta's lentil dish was something else entirely. Dense, fragrant, with a complexity of flavor that drew second tastes from people who had been eating without interest for days. Jin Parra lifted his spoon and looked at it, surprised, the way a person registers an unexpected kindness from a direction they weren't watching. Bram Okoye took a second helping and said, quietly, to the room at large, that it was the best thing he'd had since they arrived.

Enn tasted her mother-in-law's dish and turned to Gitta. "Where is this recipe from?"

"I am not sure," Gitta said. She said it with the deliberateness she brought to everything, each word placed rather than spoken, and the care made the uncertainty heavier than a shrug would have been. Enn looked at her a moment longer, waiting for more, and when more didn't come she turned back to her bowl.

Mava Kondo began telling a story. The children had asked for it — or rather, Asha had climbed into Mava's proximity with the imperious confidence of a four-year-old and said the word "tell," and the other children had arranged themselves in a ragged semicircle that constituted a request no one with a story in them could refuse. Mava settled her back against the wall, adjusted her shawl over her shoulders, and spoke.

The story was old. Fasa could hear its age in the rhythm — the repetitions that belonged to oral tradition rather than written composition, the formulaic openings that marked a tale passed through generations of telling. A family of birds lived on one side of a flooding river. Another family of birds lived on the other side. A storm tore the nests of both families loose from their trees and carried them downstream, tumbling and breaking apart, until the tangled remains of both nests caught in the branches of a single tree at the river's bend. The birds could not separate what was left — the twigs and grass and feathers had woven together too tightly — and so they rebuilt around each other, and the new nest was larger and stranger than either family's had been, and it held twice as many eggs.

Mava told it with the unhurried cadence of a woman who had told this story many times, to many children, in many rooms. Her voice dropped on the repetitions and rose on the moments of change. Her dialect surfaced in the telling — old-language phrasings that Fasa recognized but could not place exactly, the vowels rounder and the consonants softer than the common speech they all shared, as though the story itself demanded a different mouth. The children listened with the particular stillness that meant a story was working on them. Even Fen, from his position by the wall, had stopped eating.

The adults listened too, though most of them pretended they were doing something else. Bram's head was tilted, his glasses catching the light from the open side of the hall. Nessa had stopped bouncing Ada. Suna was looking at her own hands on her bowl as though the story were playing on a screen only she could see. Venn Kondo sat with his bowl balanced on his knees and his eyes on his aunt, and his face for once was not the patriarch's wall — it was a man hearing a story he had heard as a child, told in a voice that had always told it, and the sound of that voice had reached past the defenses he maintained and found the part of him that remembered being small enough to sit in the circle and believe that a nest could grow large enough to hold everyone.

Fasa felt the story land in the room. Not dramatically — no one wept, no one drew the obvious parallel to their own situation — but the quality of attention shifted, the way air shifts before weather changes. Forty-three people and the custodians in a stone hall on a hilltop above a flood, eating flatbread made by two women who used the same fold, listening to an old woman tell a story about nests tangling together in a storm. The rain had stopped while Mava was speaking. Fasa had not noticed when.

Through the hall's open side, she could see the courtyard, and on the courtyard steps she could see Peyam and Kal. They had taken their bowls outside and eaten there, and now Peyam's pipe was out — his personal possession, the clay pipe with a bowl darkened by years of use that he kept wrapped in cloth in his pack. Kal sat beside him, his broken-clasped vest open in the humidity, and they passed the pipe between them with the wordless choreography of two men who had learned each other's rhythm without deciding to. Smoke rose from the pipe in a thin line that the mist smeared before it reached the overhang.

They were not talking about the manifestations or the naming or the flood. Fasa could hear fragments of their conversation through the open side of the hall — roads, weather, the quality of this year's monsoon compared to past years. Practical talk, unhurried, the kind of exchange that had no destination and required no conclusion.

"Rains were heavier last year in our district," Kal said. His voice was lower than Peyam's, rougher, with a rasp that might have been tobacco or might have been the grain of how he'd always spoken. "And the year before. The whole lowland flooded out."

"Always heavier in the east."

"Depends where you draw the line for east."

Peyam took the pipe back and considered this. "East is east," he said. "A man knows it when he looks at it."

"That's what a western man would say."

Peyam's silence was amused. The pipe passed. The rain had stopped and the light had gone silver where the clouds thinned over the valley, and the two old men sat in the silver light with smoke between them and nothing urgent to say.

Fasa watched them and thought: this is what I have given twenty years for. Not the manifestations — those were the shrine's mystery, and she had learned to hold mystery without needing to resolve it, the way she held water in her cupped hands and accepted that some would escape between her fingers. Not the naming's forced declarations — those were the pressure, the mechanism that cracked people open, and she believed in them the way she believed in the necessity of questions even when the answers caused pain. What she had given her life for was this: the possibility that the pressure might sometimes produce something unforced. That the coercion — and it was coercion, she was not naive about the structure she served — might create conditions in which something genuine could take root. That the naming, for all its invasiveness, might leave behind a residue of the real.

She sat with her empty bowl on her knees and watched the hall. Suna was helping Deshi collect the flatbread board, and their hands met over the wood and neither pulled away — a small negotiation of proximity that would have been unthinkable on day one. Hala was talking to Bett in the Dao family's corner, and Bett's face was less guarded than Fasa had seen it, the careful economy of her expression softened by something as ordinary as a good meal and a few minutes of peace. Arash Kondo had moved closer to the children's group and was listening to the last threads of Mava's story with an expression that made him look younger than his twenty-seven years, the tension he carried between his father's expectations and his own unnamed wants temporarily released. Wynn Leo, quiet Wynn who rarely spoke in group settings, had begun humming while washing one of the communal pots at the cistern's edge — a low, tuneless sound, domestic and unselfconscious, the sound of a man performing a task without resentment. Vera Leo sat beside Cade with her thick fingers laced over the folded blanket they'd claimed as their territory, and her posture was the posture of a woman who had decided, for this afternoon at least, that being in this place with these people was not entirely a punishment.

The children were still in their clump. Asha had fallen asleep against Melie, her mouth slightly open, one hand curled in the fabric of Melie's shirt. Jin saw this from across the hall and did not get up to retrieve her. He sat with his hands around his empty bowl, watching his daughter sleep against another man's child in a pile of children from five different families, and he let it be. Fasa noticed that. She noticed that his face, which had carried the rigid blankness of a man holding himself together through refusal to feel, had eased into something more like exhaustion — which was, in its own way, a form of surrender, because exhaustion meant he had stopped clenching and let the weight settle where it would.

The afternoon stretched. The mist thickened and thinned outside the hall's open side, and the valley below remained drowned — brown water where the road should have been, the lower switchbacks submerged, the sound of the flood a constant rush beneath the silence of the hilltop. But the silence itself felt different today. It was not the fraught silence of families avoiding each other's gaze, or the tense silence that preceded a naming. It was the silence of people at rest after a meal, bodies warm and full, the communal quiet that settles when forty-three people have eaten the same bread and tasted the same food and listened to the same story about birds whose nests tangled in a flood.

Fasa sat with all of it. The warmth, the fullness, the ordinary noise of a child sleeping and an old man humming and two elders sharing smoke on the steps. The compound functioning, for a few hours, as something that resembled a community rather than a collection of people held in place by rising water and institutional authority.

She knew what tomorrow held. The naming would resume. The questions she had put to Jin — the questions that had cracked his story about his wife in front of forty-two witnesses three days ago, that had sent him walking out of the circle while Melie called after him — still hung in the compound's air. She would have to decide whether to press or to let the fracture settle, and either choice carried consequences she could feel but not yet calculate. Venn's resistance had not softened; if anything, the manifestations involving his family — Asha's gesture, Ori's terror at pooling water — had hardened him further, and Fasa knew that rigid things broke rather than bent when enough force was applied. And Gitta. Gitta's hands making food she'd never made, Gitta's face when she realized what she was doing, the three-second recovery that was itself a kind of confession — Fasa had filed this alongside every other small rupture, and the file was getting heavy.

But not now. Now the rain had paused and the courtyard was silver and Peyam was passing his pipe to Kal and Mava's story still hung in the hall's air and the children slept in a pile that did not respect the boundaries their parents maintained. Gitta's lentil dish — the dish she had never learned, the dish she knew from a source she could not name — sat in its pot with a wooden spoon resting against the rim, and someone would eat the rest at dinner, and the pot would be washed at the cistern and returned to its shelf and the day would turn toward evening. The warmth was real. Fasa had spent enough of her life reading people to know the difference between performed ease and genuine rest, and what she saw in this hall was rest — imperfect, temporary, achieved within a structure none of them had chosen and most of them resented — but rest.

She rinsed her bowl at the cistern and stood for a moment with wet hands in the returning mist, watching the clouds close over the thin place where the light had come through. The stone was cool under her feet. Water dripped from the overhang onto her wrist, and she let it run down to her elbow without wiping it away. The valley below was still drowned. The road was still gone. And behind her, in the hall she had served for twenty years, forty-three people who had not chosen each other sat together in the quiet that followed a meal, and some of them — not all, but enough — had let the quiet mean something.

It would not hold. The naming always asked more than people wanted to give, and the asking had not finished. But Fasa stood at the cistern with water on her hands and allowed herself, for the space of a few breaths, to believe that what she had seen today was not the naming's product or the shrine's design but something the families had made themselves — out of flour and oil and fire, out of an old woman's story and an old man's pipe, out of the simple human preference for warmth over cold and company over solitude. She allowed herself this, and then she dried her hands on her clothing and went back inside to begin preparing for tomorrow.
