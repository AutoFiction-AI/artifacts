# Chapter 1

The rain found the seams in Suna's shawl before they were halfway up the switchback. Kael walked ahead, his sandals slapping the stone steps with a determination she recognized as bravery; he was eight and trying to look steady for his sisters. Behind her, Deshi climbed with one hand on Peyam's elbow, breathing hard between gusts, and Suna could hear the older woman's complaints building: the mud, the steepness, the weight of the bags they had dragged from the truck before leaving it where the water covered the wheels.

Asha rode on Suna's hip. Four years old and too heavy for this, but the child had stopped walking twenty minutes ago, and there was no fight Suna could win against that kind of exhaustion. Asha's fingers found the wet collar of Suna's blouse and rubbed the fabric between thumb and forefinger. She touched everything. Buttons, bark, wooden spoons, the hems of strangers' clothes when Suna was not fast enough to pull her back. Now she touched the rain, palm open, catching what she could and licking it from her skin.

"Rimi," Suna said. "Stay with Kael."

"I'm with Kael."

"Where I can see you."

Rimi moved one step closer to her brother. She was twelve, angular, already carrying her father's long face, and Suna would have been grateful for the obedience if it had not meant the girl was too frightened to argue.

The shrine came through the rain at the last bend: stone walls the color of wet iron, a covered walkway jutting from the entrance, and behind it the larger compound on the hilltop. Bodies already crowded the overhang. Bags leaned against the wall. A child complained in a bright, relentless voice. Other families had reached shelter first.

Peyam said something to Deshi that the wind took. Deshi answered loudly enough for everyone near them to hear. "Of course there are others. Where else would they go?"

Where else. Below them, where the seven roads should have met, there was only water. Brown and silt-heavy, pushing through the valley with enough force to erase its own edges. Suna could see the roof of a truck at the waterline, a side mirror catching the last dirty light, and a road sign half under. The flood slid into the trees on both sides and vanished around the far bend. Its sound climbed the hill under the rain, steady and pressurized, another weather laid beneath the first.

"Mama," Asha said, and pressed her wet palm to Suna's cheek.

"I know," Suna said. "We're here."

A lean man stood at the mouth of the walkway, rain darkening his shoulders. He did not wave them in. He did not step forward to take a bag. He simply waited until they crossed under the roof and the rain stopped striking their faces.

"How many in the family?" he asked.

Suna set Asha down. Her back flared with relief. "Six. Family name Mera."

"Given names."

"Suna. Rimi. Kael. Asha. Deshi. Peyam."

His eyes moved across them as she spoke, landing on each face in turn. "Your destination before the road closed."

"Gelden. My husband went ahead. We're meeting him." The lie came out flat. She had said it too many times to hear the seam in it.

"I am Olet. Head custodian of this shrine. You will have a place to sleep and food. There are rules. You will hear them when everyone has arrived."

"How many are here?"

"You make seven families."

Seven. Suna looked past him into the hall. Stone floor. Stone walls. A ceiling smoked dark by old lamps. Large enough for the bodies, not for the distance they would want between them. On the far side, a wide arch opened over the valley, and the flood filled it like a second wall. Tree crowns marked the buried roads. The sound came through the arch and settled into the hall. It would be there when the rain eased. It would be there after dark. It was the sound of being kept.

Oil lamps already burned along the walls. An angular woman with rough hands moved from bracket to bracket, tending them with quick economy. She gave Suna one short, measuring look and kept moving. Two younger custodians stacked mats against the west wall and took wet bundles from the newest arrivals without speaking.

Suna counted anyway. Not total. Composition. Children. Elders. Men. Who was sick, who was tired, who had the look of someone who would start trouble when the room got tighter.

Olet kept the threshold moving. The same three questions went to each family. The broad man at the south wall answered "Kondo" with clipped impatience and sent a younger man back into the rain for another sack before Olet had reached the destination question. The thin young father by the entrance said "Parra" so quietly Olet made him repeat it. A man in glasses came up with a baby strapped to his chest, gave "Okoye," and then tried to ask how long the road would stay under water. Olet took the name, not the question.

The biggest family had taken the south side. The broad man with gray at his temples ordered children and sacks out of a puddle while his wife shook rain from an embroidered wedding cloth and folded it over her forearms to save the border work. Near her, an elderly aunt sat on a bundle and rubbed her left ear with her palm in slow circles. A younger man kept one hand at the old woman's shoulder and the other on the stack of bags, ready to move either.

Near the entrance, the Parra man lowered a sleeping girl from his back and settled on the floor with her across his lap. He had one bag. He set it between his feet and curved his body around the child until the rest of the hall had to go around him.

"Suna." Deshi had found a dry strip of floor and was easing herself down with one hand braced against the stone. Peyam bent beside her, careful with his knees, and drew his prayer beads from his pocket as soon as he sat.

"Rimi, Kael, come here," Suna said. "Asha, don't."

Too late. Asha had crossed the wet stones and laid her hand on the wedding cloth, fingers spreading into the heavy embroidery.

The woman holding it looked up first. Her face was tired past annoyance. "Easy, little one."

"That's not ours," Suna said, catching Asha's wrist.

"It's all right," the woman said, but she lifted the cloth higher, out of reach. The broad man glanced over, decided it was not worth his voice, and went back to counting children.

The Okoye family moved in only after Olet had finished with them. The father kept the baby strapped to his chest, the mother pulled a ten-year-old boy through the doorway before he could stop to stare at the flood, and the teenage girl took in the room in one fast sweep and immediately arranged her face into boredom.

The next arrivals came close enough together that the walkway clogged again. One woman kept three children moving with a hand between shoulder blades while the other stopped once beneath the arch and listened to the flood as if it had spoken. Behind them, a man kept the two boys tucked close to his knees while the oldest girl drifted a step ahead and back again. After them came a careful man with his partner, an older woman carrying a canvas bag, and a boy kept so near the adults that his shoulder stayed against somebody's hip. They entered with the concentrated quiet of people used to being noticed for the wrong reasons.

The last family took the longest because the oldest woman would not be hurried. Small, white-haired, exact, she placed each foot and then trusted it. Her son stayed beside her without crowding. A teenage boy hovered behind them with both hands half raised. The girl behind him kept turning to look down the switchback at the drowned road.

By the time the old woman crossed under the roof, the hall held forty-three stranded travelers, their wet bags, their children, their impatience, and the steady rush from the valley below.

***

Olet waited until the path stayed empty. Then he walked to the center of the hall and spoke.

He did not raise his voice. He spoke into the noise and the noise dropped around him.

"This is a transit shrine. It sits at the meeting of seven roads and has served travelers longer than records count. You are welcome here. You will be fed from our stores. You will be given places to sleep. In return, you will follow the shrine's schedule. Meals are communal. Labor is shared. There is a daily gathering. You will hear more tomorrow."

"If the road's under till morning, that's one kind of problem," the man in glasses said. "If it's longer, people need to plan differently. Which is it?"

Olet turned to him. "The road opens when the water drops. The water drops when the rain stops. The rain will stop when it stops."

From the south side came a hard breath that almost passed for a laugh. The broad man in the wedding shirt had folded his arms across his chest and was staring at Olet with the patience already gone out of him.

"Sleeping quarters," Olet said.

He had a system. Suna saw that at once. He did not ask what anyone wanted. He pointed. Families moved.

"The Kondo family. The large room on the south side."

The broad man moved first. At the doorway he stopped and turned back. "My family is twelve. My aunt is seventy-three. We don't put our elders in corners and tell children to make do around them. If this is the Kondo room, I want to hear what provision you're making for us."

"It is the largest room."

"I heard 'largest.' I asked whether the shrine had another arrangement for a family our size."

"It is the largest room available, and it has been assigned to the largest family."

The man held there a moment longer. His wife touched the middle of his back. He went in. The younger man took the old aunt's elbow and helped her over the threshold.

"The Okoye family. The northeast corner of the main hall."

The man in glasses made a small turn of both hands, a private argument gathering itself. His wife looked at the corner and then at the baby. Open on two sides. No door. No wall worth the name. Still, he took the boy's shoulder and led them there. The teenage girl trailed after them with that same practiced indifference, now tighter around the mouth.

"The Mera family. The room beside the kitchen."

"Beside the kitchen?" Deshi said. "So our blankets take smoke all night and by morning everything we own smells like old onions and ash. That's the room?"

"It is what is available."

Deshi looked at Suna as if the answer had come from her. Suna picked up their bags.

"The Parra family. The alcove by the east wall."

The thin young man stood, shifted his daughter onto his hip, and went there without a word. The alcove was barely deep enough for two mats. He set his bag behind the girl as a backstop and sat with one knee up, closing the open side with his body.

"The Tesh family. The west annex."

The exact old woman rose. Her son offered his arm. She took it and moved off at her same measured pace. The teenage boy grabbed the heaviest bag before anyone asked him to.

"The Dao family. The room past the cistern."

The older of the two women counted heads under her breath, touched the younger one's elbow, and steered the children down the passage while the man kept the two boys close beside him. The younger woman gave the flood one last look and followed.

"The Leo family. The alcove near the prayer courtyard."

The careful man did not move immediately. "Is there a curtain there?"

"No."

The man glanced at his partner, at the older woman, at the boy beside them. Then he nodded once and led them toward the courtyard anyway.

"These are your spaces," Olet said. "Arrange them as you wish. The kitchen area is communal. Water is from the cistern. The prayer courtyard is open to all. The custodians' quarters are not. If you need something, ask."

"If the only radio is in your quarters, then the news only comes through you," the man in glasses said from his corner. "That matters when people are deciding what they're walking into."

"There is a radio. It is in the custodians' quarters. We will share what news comes."

"Can we hear it ourselves, then, so we're deciding from the same facts?"

Olet let the question finish. "The custodians will share what news comes."

The man's hands turned again. His daughter watched the exchange with bright, merciless attention. When she caught Suna looking, she dropped her gaze.

Suna carried their bags into the kitchen room. Small. Dry. Cold stone underfoot. A slot of a window opening onto the kitchen overhang, already full of ash smell and old smoke.

"Six of us," Deshi said from the doorway. "In this. You'd call a pantry crowded before you'd call this a room."

"We'll manage." Suna laid out mats from the stack in the hall. Three for the children. One for Deshi. One for Peyam. One for herself. The room was four meters by three, perhaps. With her arms spread she could nearly touch both walls.

"The large family has a room three times this size. They can turn around in there without stepping on somebody's hands."

"They're twelve."

"We're six. That's not a sleeping arrangement. That's storage."

Suna kept counting. Mats. Blankets. Two bags of rice. Dried lentils. Salt. The tin of oil that had leaked in the truck and turned the inside of one bag rancid. If the custodians fed them, it would not matter. If they did not, it would matter fast.

"Peyam needs space for his knees," Deshi said. "He's not a folded blanket you can shove on a shelf. You know that."

"I know." Suna set Peyam's mat along the far wall, where he could stretch out without anyone stepping over him. She took the mat nearest the door. The children would go between her and Deshi. The arrangement settled over the room at once. Six bodies. Kitchen smell. Flood through the window. No date on the end of it.

***

By nightfall the rain still had not let go. It came in long runs, hammering the roof until the lamps thinned, then easing just enough to remind them what quiet was before starting again. The shrine answered with groans from its beams and corridors.

Suna fed her children from their own supplies: rice cooked in the communal kitchen, lentils, a scrap of pickle that Deshi cut into slivers and placed on each mound of rice with medicinal care. The kitchen was the most exposed part of the compound, a stone counter and fire pit under a deep overhang that blocked the worst of the rain but none of the wind. Suna cooked beside Hala from the Dao family; she learned the name from the thirteen-year-old girl who came over to ask Kael if he wanted to see a beetle drowning in a bucket lid. Hala worked fast and without wasted motion. She had done this in bad weather before. They did not talk much, but they made room for each other with glances and brief touches of the hand. When Hala split an onion with the heel of her palm guiding the knife, Suna felt a jolt of recognition. Her own mother had cut onions that way.

In the prayer courtyard, open to the sky, Peyam stood barefoot on the wet stone and prayed. Suna could see him from the kitchen overhang: thin back straight, beads hanging from one hand, rain running down his face. He had not asked whether prayer was allowed. He had found the courtyard, taken off his sandals, and begun. When he finished, he lifted his face into the rain for one quiet breath, then put his sandals back on and came inside.

After dinner, if you could call it that, Suna washed the children's faces and hands at the cistern. Kael submitted. Rimi turned her back to the room and did it herself, guarding the small privacy she had begun claiming six months ago. Asha made the washing into another investigation. She rubbed Suna's wet wrist. She pressed her palm to the cool stone rim. She tasted the water.

"Don't drink that, Asha."

Suna was wringing out the cloth when Asha reached past her and touched the face of a boy waiting his turn.

He belonged to the wedding family, nine or ten perhaps, round-cheeked, still rubbing at a mark on his collar. He blinked in surprise and then stood still for it.

"Sorry," Suna said, catching Asha's wrist. "She does that."

"It's fine." The boy touched his own cheek once, curious now, and went back to his people.

Asha watched him leave with her hand still open, fingers curled around nothing.

Their room was lamplight, stone, and six people packed tighter than sleep allowed. Peyam fell asleep almost as soon as he lay down. His beads clicked once and then stopped. Deshi turned her back to the room, though Suna could hear from her breathing that she was still awake.

Suna settled the children. Kael on his back, knees up, his usual position since he was three. Rimi on her side facing the wall, breathing too evenly. Asha last. Even with her eyes closed, her hands kept moving over the mat, the blanket seam, the weave of the cotton. She found Suna's sleeve and worried the hem between thumb and forefinger until her breathing lengthened. The fingers slowed, paused, then searched once more before finally going still.

Suna lay on her back and did not sleep.

The compound was louder now that everyone was trying to be quiet. The wedding patriarch talking low and hard in his room. The baby crying because the air, the stone, the people, all of it smelled wrong. Melie's father murmuring to her in the east alcove. The older Tesh woman coughing once. Bare feet moving through the corridor.

And underneath everything, the flood, filling the silences from below with patient weight.

"Mama." Kael was nearly asleep, voice gone small. "When are we going to Papa?"

"When the road opens."

"When does the road open?"

"When the water goes down."

"When does the—"

"Sleep, Kael."

He did. Children could do that. They could take an answer with holes in it and drift anyway. Rimi stayed awake beside him. Suna could tell from the held steadiness of her breathing. Twelve years old and already rehearsing calm for the people around her. She had her father's long face and, Suna feared, his talent for silence that meant nothing good.

Suna pressed her hand to the floor beside the mat. Cold stone. Clean indifference. She kept her palm there until the cold climbed her wrist.

In the morning, or in two days, or whenever the rain stopped and the roads came back out of the flood, she would take the children to Gelden. She would go to her husband's family and explain. That was the plan. It had been the plan since he left.

Not since he went ahead. Since he left.

The fact sat in her body like swallowed metal. He had not gone to prepare the house or find work. He had packed a bag while the children were at school and Deshi was at the market and Peyam was praying. He had taken his good shoes and the money from the tin above the stove. He had left a note that said *I'm sorry, I can't do this.* Suna had read it, folded it down to a square no bigger than a stamp, put it in her pocket, and gone to collect the children from school. She had cooked dinner. That evening she had told Deshi that her son had gone ahead to Gelden to prepare. Deshi had looked at her, unreadable, and said, "Without telling me?"

"It was sudden," Suna had answered. "He wanted to get ahead of the rains."

Three months. Three months of sudden. Three months of Kael asking when they were going to Papa and Suna saying soon. Three months of Rimi watching her with that old, evaluating face. Three months of Deshi's questions wearing at the walls of the lie. She had kept a marriage standing by habit and grammar. She had pointed the children toward a man who was nowhere at the end of the road.

Now the road itself was gone. The shrine held thirty-seven strangers around her, plus her own five. Olet had given them a room the size of an animal stall and spoken with the finality of a man who had watched too many families come apart indoors. At the far end of this journey, whenever the flood released it, no husband would be waiting. There would only be his family's house and their faces when they understood what he had done.

Panic never came as a wave. It came as a list. What she owed. What she could not pay. Rice for maybe four days if she stretched it. Kael's sandals thinning at the sole. Rimi's silence getting longer. Asha on other people's things, other people's faces. Peyam's knees. Deshi's questions. The lie about the husband. The money running out. The road under water. The note in the bag. She still carried it in the inner pocket with the children's things, and sometimes she touched the folded square the way Peyam touched his first bead, not from hope, just to confirm the damage was still there.

Somewhere in the compound, a woman started humming. The melody was low and repetitive, turning back on itself until it settled into the air. It was for another child in another room. Even so, Suna listened. Beneath the panic, beneath the lie worn smooth by use, another feeling rose and embarrassed her with its force: relief. Not safety. Nothing that generous. But shelter. Walls. Other adults awake in the dark, listening for their own children. For one night, at least, she was held inside the same fear as everyone else.

Asha's hand found hers. Small, warm, damp from sleep. The child's fingers closed around two of Suna's and rested there, reading even this.

The rain hammered the shrine. The flood kept talking in the valley. Suna closed her eyes and did not sleep, and outside the roads stayed gone.
