# Chapter 17

Her father's mat was already empty, folded at the corner. Leni registered that before she registered the quiet.

Ada was asleep on Nessa's chest, breathing in the tiny percussive rhythm that had become the Okoye section's metronome. Tomas was curled on his side, one arm thrown over his face. Nothing unusual. But the air was different — thinner somehow, or clearer, and the compound sounded like someone had lifted a blanket off the world.

She sat up and pulled her braids over her shoulder. The stone sill under the sleeping quarter's narrow window was dry. She pressed her palm against it and felt heat, actual sun-stored heat, where for weeks there had been only the damp cool of perpetual wet.

The rain had stopped.

She'd known this was coming. Last night the dripping from the overhang had slowed and then quit, and she'd lain there listening to the new quiet push into the spaces where water sounds had lived for nineteen days. But sleep had softened the strangeness, and now the absence hit her freshly. She could hear a bird. She hadn't heard a bird in almost three weeks. The note was small and repetitive and impossibly clear, as though the creature had been waiting behind the weather this whole time.

Through the window she could see the sky — not the low grey compress of the monsoon but actual sky, a pale washed blue already warm at the edges where the sun was climbing.

Her father was in the courtyard. She could see him through the doorway, standing near the cistern with his hands in his pockets and his head tilted back, looking upward with an expression she couldn't read. He looked thinner than when they'd arrived. They all did. Three weeks of custodian rations and communal cooking had sharpened every adult's face into something closer to the bone. But on her father it read as drawn rather than lean, as though the weight he'd lost had come from somewhere internal.

She bit a hangnail and went to find breakfast.

The kitchen was already busy. Deshi was stirring something in the big pot with the kind of aggressive competence that dared anyone to offer help. Hala was laying out flatbread on the stone shelf, and two of the Kondo women — Tila and Habel — were sorting the last of the dried lentils, picking out small stones with practiced fingers. Tila wore a cotton headscarf she'd rewashed so many times it had gone transparent at the temples, and she hummed something tuneless and private as she worked. The cooking smoke was the same — that pervasive wood-char smell that had seeped into every garment and every mat — but without the rain's mineral undertone, it smelled sharper, almost acrid.

Leni took a piece of flatbread and leaned against the stone wall and chewed and watched. She was good at watching. She'd always been good at it, but three weeks in the compound had turned the habit into a survival reflex; she clocked lies now by who kept their hands busy and who stared too hard at the floor. She saw things. She couldn't stop.

Like: Suna was sitting cross-legged near the kitchen edge, braiding Asha's hair, and her hands were steady but her face wasn't. She had the over-braced look of somebody who'd made up her mind in the dark — or last night, after the evening meal, when she and Jin had been sitting together in the prayer courtyard for what must have been an hour. Leni had seen them from the sleeping quarters' doorway, two shapes on the stone bench with their shoulders almost but not quite touching, speaking in voices too low to carry. She hadn't tried to listen. She'd been doing too much of that already.

The bell rang.

Not the naming bell — a shorter, sharper pattern that Olet used for general assembly. The sound cut through the new quiet with a metallic authority that made Leni's teeth ache. Around the kitchen, hands paused. Deshi's spoon hovered over the pot. Habel looked up from her lentils.

They gathered in the main hall, all of them, the way they always did when the bell demanded it — families clustering together by reflex, the children herded to the front, the custodians standing near the hall's open side where the valley spread below. And Leni saw it then, what the clear sky had been trying to tell her all morning: the water was lower. Not dramatically, not a sudden biblical recession, but the brown surface that had swallowed the road and the valley floor for nearly three weeks had pulled back. She could see the first switchback. Mud-slicked and littered with debris, but visible — actual road surface, grey under the new sun.

Olet stood with his hands clasped at his waist, unhurried as always, as though the news he carried were no different from a change in the meal rotation. Fasa was behind him, and Leni thought she looked tired. Not sleepy — depleted. Whatever private reckoning the custodians had been conducting in their quarters, it had left Fasa wrung out.

"The radio received a broadcast this morning," Olet said. His voice was the same: level, measured, the institutional calm that had unsettled Leni since the first day. "Road crews are clearing the route from the lowlands. The pass should be open within two days."

The room inhaled. Not a gasp — the dry, collective pull of breath people make when hope barges in before they're ready to trust it, reshaping posture and expression as it went. Leni saw her mother pull Ada closer. She saw Venn straighten, his broad shoulders squaring, his chin lifting with the specific relief of a man who had been enduring something beneath him. She saw Mava's hand go to her ear, the old circular rub, and then drop. She saw Jin look at Melie, who was sitting between the Okoye and Dao children with her legs crossed and her hands busy with a string game Raya had taught her, unaware that the sentence just spoken had changed everything.

"Two days," Venn said. He didn't ask it. He confirmed it, his voice carrying the weight of a man already planning the journey to his son's wedding.

"If the crews maintain pace." Olet paused — the long, unhurried pause that Leni had learned was not hesitation but emphasis. "The road will be passable. Whether you walk it depends on the naming."

The silence that followed was a different texture than the morning's. This one had edges.

"The naming must be completed before any family departs the shrine," Fasa said. She spoke carefully, as though placing each word on a surface that might not hold. "This has always been the condition. It does not change because the road opens."

"You can't be serious." That was Bram — her father, already leaning forward, already building. Leni watched his hands come out of his pockets. "We've been here three weeks. We've done the naming. Multiple times. Your condition has been met."

"The naming has been attempted," Olet said. "It has not been completed."

"And whose standard determines that? Yours?" Bram's voice was sharper now, the teacher's diction cutting through. "You've moved the goalposts every time we've complied. First it was declaration. Then it was standing together. Then it was the losses. What's the completion criterion — when you say so?"

Olet regarded him with the same steady, impervious calm he'd maintained through every challenge. "When each family has declared truthfully."

The word threaded the room, tugging people out of whatever posture they'd arranged for themselves — Suna's sudden stillness, Jin's hand going to the back of his neck, Gitta's quiet nod that was almost invisible and entirely unsurprised.

"We leave in two days," Venn said, rising. "We do whatever you require. Tomorrow morning, first light. My family will declare exactly as they declared the first time, and then we walk."

"With respect," Fasa began, but Venn was already talking over her.

"We've been patient. We've complied. We've eaten your food and slept on your floors and tolerated —" He caught himself, the thick muscles in his neck working. "We're leaving. Name the conditions and we'll meet them, but we are leaving."

Leni looked at Arash. He was standing behind his father, slightly to the side, in the gap between the Kondo cluster and the rest of the room. His face kept trying on the controlled blankness Leni had been seeing more and more since the night she'd caught him in the courtyard with Bett, and it never quite took. Bett was across the hall, seated with Hala and Nori, and she wasn't looking at Arash. She was looking at her own hands.

---

The debate happened after the morning meal, which was itself a strange, compressed affair — everyone eating fast, as though the food had changed flavor now that departure was possible. Leni ate her lentils and watched the room rearrange itself. Alliances that had formed over three weeks were suddenly being tested against the original question: who are you, and who are you leaving with?

They gathered again in the main hall. This time there were no sleeping mats to sit on — the morning light poured through the open side, warm and foreign, and people stood or crouched on the stone floor in clusters that mapped their positions before anyone spoke.

Her father started. Of course he did.

"I want to be clear about what's happening." Bram had moved to the center of the hall, not because he was performing but because that was where the argument needed to be. His glasses caught the light and his hands were already moving, shaping the logic in the air. "For three weeks, we've been held here. The flood was real, yes. The political crisis is real. But the naming — the condition that we can't leave until we've named ourselves to the custodians' satisfaction — that was always coercion. We said so at the time. The food-access restriction confirmed it. And now the road is opening." He looked around the room. "We don't owe them a final performance."

"It's not a performance." Suna's voice came from the left side of the hall where she was standing with her children around her — Rimi beside her, Kael against the wall, Asha on Deshi's hip. She spoke without heat but with a directness that Leni had watched harden over three weeks. "The first naming was the performance. We all stood there and said whatever would get us through it. I know I did."

Bram turned to her. "That's exactly my point. The coercion —"

"I'm not talking about coercion, Bram. I'm talking about what I said." She paused. Asha was tugging at Deshi's collar, and Deshi shifted her without breaking her attention from Suna. "I lied in the first naming. About my husband. I said he was ahead of us, that we'd meet him at the next town. He left. Three months before we arrived here, he packed a bag and left."

The room went quiet in a specific way — not shocked, Leni thought, but confirmed. As though several people had suspected this and were now receiving permission to know it.

Bram opened a hand toward her, teacher-gentle now instead of argumentative. "You shouldn't have to say that to get down a road."

"No," Suna said. "I shouldn't. I'm still going to say it."

"I'm going to amend my declaration," Suna continued. "Not because the custodians require it. Because I've been carrying it for three months and three weeks on top of that, and it's heavier than the truth. So I'll say it tomorrow, in the naming, in front of everyone. And that's not compliance." She looked at Bram. "That's my decision."

Leni watched her father absorb this. His argument about coercion had assumed a unified front — all families refusing together, the way he'd tried in the food-stores standoff. But Suna wasn't refusing. She wasn't complying either. She was taking the whole argument out of his neat categories and making it personal.

"Suna's right that we all lied," Nessa said quietly. She was holding Ada against her shoulder, bouncing her with the automatic rhythm of a woman who'd been soothing babies for fifteen years. Her free hand found Bram's elbow. "Or at least we all left things out."

Bram looked at her. "This isn't the same."

Nessa's fingers tightened on his sleeve. "It is if we want tomorrow to mean anything."

Leni saw her father's face change. Not much — a small tightening around the eyes, a shift in how he held his weight. Whatever Nessa meant by we all left things out, it carried a private freight that Leni couldn't parse. She filed it away.

"The question isn't whether to do the naming." Venn was still standing, his arms crossed, his voice cutting across the murmur that had followed Suna's admission. "The question is how fast. We declare tomorrow. We leave tomorrow. Anyone who wants to amend whatever they said before, fine. But we don't drag this out. My son has a wedding."

"Your son's wedding isn't the only consideration here." Hala's voice came from the Dao corner, and it carried a different register than Venn's bark or Bram's argument — steadier, lower, with the particular authority of someone who had been listening and was now done listening. Hala was standing with her arms at her sides, her solid frame planted, her chin up. She had a bruise on her forearm from catching the corner of the cooking pot two days ago, and she didn't seem to notice it. "Nobody's saying we should stay longer than we have to. But if we're going to stand up there and declare, we should at least be honest about what's happened."

"What's happened," Venn repeated flatly.

"Ori," Hala said. "Ori, who screamed at rainwater."

"She had a bad night."

Hala took one step forward. "She recoiled from a puddle like it was pulling her under. Don't call that a bad night."

Arash moved as if he might speak. Venn's hand shot back without his even turning his head and closed around his son's forearm for a second before letting go.

"Ori," Hala said again, looking at Venn now instead of Bett. "Who never screamed at water in her life until she came here and started carrying the fear of a boy who drowned. My nephew." She looked at Bett, who gave a single small nod. "Tomas, who talks with a stutter he didn't have three weeks ago. Asha, who rubs her ear like a woman she'd never met. Kiri, who sings a song from a region she's never been to. Pik, who prays with his hands like Gitta." She turned back to Venn. "You want to declare the same thing you declared the first time? Go ahead. But you'll be lying, and everybody in this room will know it."

Venn's face darkened. His jaw worked, the way it always did when he was being challenged — the physical machinery of pride grinding against the pragmatic recognition that the room was not on his side. "What happened to our children doesn't mean —"

"It means something." Hala's voice didn't rise. It dropped, and the drop carried more force than shouting. "I don't know what it means. Nobody does. Maybe the custodians are right and maybe they're wrong and maybe it doesn't matter. But the connections are real. My sister's dead son is alive in your niece's body. That's not a theory. That's Ori screaming at a puddle."

The room was very still. Leni could hear the bird again — the same persistent note from the morning, thin and bright against the thick silence. She could hear Asha making a small, contented sound against Deshi's neck. She could hear her own pulse in her ears, which was stupid, which was exactly the kind of body-inventory she'd mock in a novel but which was happening anyway.

Cade Leo spoke then. He'd been standing near the back wall with Wynn beside him and Vera slightly behind — the three of them forming the same protective triangle they always formed around Pik, who was sitting on the floor near Melie, his small hands still in his lap. Cade was not a loud man. Leni had spent three weeks observing him and what she'd mostly observed was vigilance — a constant low-grade alertness about Pik, about the naming, about how other families would categorize his family. He wore the same thin cotton shirt he'd been rewashing since they arrived, the collar starting to fray, and when he stepped forward his movement was careful, as though he were navigating a room full of breakable things.

"What does the naming mean for a child whose origins are unknown?" He directed the question at Fasa, but the whole room heard it. "Pik is my son. Wynn's son. Vera's grandson. That's not a declaration we're prepared to amend. But he came to us from an institution with records we couldn't verify, and now he's —" He stopped. Wynn put a hand on his back. "He's doing Gitta's prayer gesture. He's never seen her pray. We keep him away from the naming. He started doing it in his sleep."

"Exactly," Bram said, turning half toward him. "That's what coercive systems do. They take uncertainty and —"

Cade didn't even look at him. "Bram, I'm not asking for a theory. I'm asking if this rite can be used against my boy."

Gitta, who had been sitting near the Tesh corner with her particular elderly stillness, turned her head toward Pik. The movement was slow and deliberate, the way everything Gitta did was slow and deliberate. Leni thought about what this woman had carried — sixteen years of knowing that Fen was not her blood, sixteen years of perfect composure, and then the confession that had cracked the compound open. Gitta's truth was already told. She had nothing left to declare except the truth she'd already spoken, and that exemption gave her a strange calm that Leni found almost enviable.

"The boy is yours," Gitta said. Her voice was the same unhurried, careful instrument it always was, each word arriving as though it had been considered and approved before departure. "The naming does not take him from you. Nothing takes a child from the person who chose to raise him." She paused. Fen, sitting beside her with his long legs drawn up and his dark hair falling across his forehead, did not move. "I know this."

Cade's mouth twitched once and his eyes went bright — gratitude and grief and the specific relief of a man whose worst fear had been spoken to directly. He nodded and stepped back. Wynn's hand stayed on his back. Vera, standing behind them both, had closed her eyes when Gitta spoke. She was a woman who wore her worry openly — three weeks of it had deepened the lines bracketing her mouth — and when she opened her eyes again she looked at Gitta with a recognition that wasn't quite gratitude but belonged to the same family. One grandmother recognizing what another had done.

Tomas tugged at Leni's sleeve. She looked down and found her brother staring up at her, his face tight with the effort of not speaking, because when he spoke now the words caught on the same consonants that caught in Jin Parra's mouth, and the catching frightened him. He'd stopped talking in group settings entirely — ten years old, sharp and talkative by nature, reduced to tugging sleeves and pointing. She put her hand on his head and felt the familiar heat of his scalp through his short hair, and something in her chest compressed.

"So what are we actually deciding?" Miro Tesh stood up, and Leni realized she'd never heard him speak in a full assembly before. He was a quiet man — broad, balding, with oil-stained hands from repairing one of the kitchen's broken stools — who'd spent three weeks absorbing the discovery that his mother had lied about his son's origins. His voice was rough, as though it hadn't been used at this volume in a while. "Are we deciding whether to do the naming? Because it sounds like most people are going to do it regardless."

"Some of us object on principle," Bram said, but the fight was leaving his voice. Leni heard it — the downward shift, the concession hidden inside the precision.

"You can object on principle and still stand up and say what's true," Suna said. "Those aren't contradictions."

Bram looked at her. Then he looked at Nessa, who was watching him with the look she used when she needed him to say the hard thing and was promising, silently, not to leave him alone in it.

"I'm not saying don't do it." Bram took his glasses off and cleaned them on his shirt, a gesture Leni recognized as his stalling mechanism. "I'm saying remember what it is. It's not a sacrament. It's not magic. It's a ritual maintained by two people who control our food supply and have told us, in so many words, that the flood won't recede until we play along. We can choose to speak truthfully and still acknowledge that the choice was coerced."

"The rain stopped," Peyam said.

Everyone turned. Peyam had been sitting on the floor near the back, his thin legs folded, his hands resting on his knees. Leni had always thought of him as furniture — one of those old men who existed at the periphery of their families, praying and dozing and taking up very little space. But his voice, when it came, was surprisingly clear and carried an old man's unselfconscious authority. He was looking at the open side of the hall, at the valley and the visible road below and the sky that had emptied itself of clouds for the first time since they'd arrived.

"The rain stopped last night," he said again. "And this morning the radio says the road is clearing. Maybe that is coincidence." He adjusted his seating on the stone floor, shifting his weight off one hip with a wince that spoke of arthritis. "Maybe."

Olet, who had been standing at the hall's edge throughout, said nothing. His silence was its own statement. Leni wished, not for the first time, that she could read the man. Three weeks, and he remained opaque to her in a way that felt deliberate — not a person hiding but a person who simply did not offer the readable surfaces that other people leaked constantly.

"Fine." Venn's voice cut through. He'd been conferring with Tila in a whispered exchange, and now he turned back to the room with the manner of a man issuing a ruling. "We do it tomorrow. My family will declare. But I want it understood: we're complying under duress. And when the road opens, we walk."

"Nobody's stopping you," Hala said.

"Nobody could," Venn said, and the raw honesty of it — the sheer patriarchal certainty — was almost impressive. Almost.

Deshi made a sound from the Mera corner — not quite a word, more the exhalation of a woman who had been holding her opinion with visible effort. She was still holding Asha, who had fallen asleep against her shoulder, and she spoke over the child's head with the quiet devastation she could deploy more effectively than shouting.

"My son left his wife and his children," she said. It was the first time Leni had heard Deshi acknowledge it aloud, and from the rigid set of Suna's shoulders, it might have been the first time Suna had heard it too. "I did not raise him to be that kind of man. But that is what he is. And if this naming wants me to say that in front of strangers, I will say it, because it is true, and because someone in this family should have said it three months ago." She looked at Suna — not with the adversarial sharpness Leni had seen for three weeks, but with the raw, uncomfortable look of a woman admitting she'd helped the lie survive and didn't know how to apologize except by naming it.

Suna didn't answer. She didn't need to. The acknowledgment sat between them like a stone set down after a long carry.

Bett had not spoken during the entire debate. Leni watched her now — the compact stillness, the guarded face, the way she sat with her hands in her lap and her eyes tracking the room without offering anything in return. Hala had spoken for her, had named Ren through Ori's fear, and Bett had allowed it with that single nod. Whatever Bett would say in the naming tomorrow, she would say it there, in the ritual space, not here in the argument about whether the ritual space was legitimate.

Arash was watching Bett. He was trying not to, and failing. Leni saw the way his gaze kept drifting across the hall and then pulling back, the way his body was angled slightly away from the Kondo cluster and toward the Dao corner, the way his jaw held the same tension his father's held but different — younger, less certain, pointed in a direction that had nothing to do with a wedding.

Fasa stepped forward. She looked like someone who had been carrying a weight that was about to shift — not lighten, but shift from her back to the families who would bear it tomorrow.

"The naming will be held tomorrow at first light," she said. "Each family will declare. Force can make a body stand in the circle; it cannot make a mouth speak plain. What is spoken tomorrow will belong to the speaker, and to whoever must hear it." She looked around the room, and Leni thought she saw ritual composure slip for a second, exposing genuine feeling, deep and tired and close to the surface. "I have conducted the naming for twenty years. I have never asked it to cut this deep. I am asking now."

Nobody answered. The bird outside sang its single note into the quiet, and after a while the families began to drift apart.

---

Evening came slowly, the first evening without rain since they'd arrived, and it was strange. The light lasted longer and fell differently — warm gold slanting through the main hall's open side, turning the stone floor amber, throwing long shadows from the pillars. The compound felt larger without the rain compressing it, as though the walls had stepped back. Sounds carried differently too: laughter from the children's corner, the scrape of the cooking pot, Venn's low voice from the Kondo section issuing instructions nobody could quite hear.

Leni sat on the courtyard wall with her journal on her knees and her pen in her hand and didn't write. She chewed the pen cap instead and watched.

Suna was in the sleeping quarters, folding her children's clothes into the two bags the Meras had arrived with. The folding was precise, methodical — shirts pressed flat, edges aligned, everything sorted by child. Leni recognized the behavior. Her own mother did this before big days, turning the domestic into the manageable, using the orderly compression of laundry as a proxy for the emotional compression she couldn't otherwise achieve. But Suna's hands paused over one of Asha's small dresses, and she held it for a moment longer than folding required, and then set it down and picked it back up and folded it a second time, exactly the same way.

Deshi came in a few minutes later with Asha on her shoulder and the smaller Mera bag hanging from two fingers. She set the bag down beside Suna with more force than necessary. From the courtyard wall Leni could see straight through the doorway. Privacy in the shrine was mostly a rumor.

"Don't put Kael's sweater at the bottom," Deshi said. "He'll need it before dawn."

Suna kept folding. "Then move it."

Deshi lowered Asha onto the mat, one hand cupping the back of the child's head until she settled. When she straightened, she did not look at Suna. "You're going to say it tomorrow."

"I said I was."

"In front of the children."

That made Suna look up. "There isn't another front."

Deshi's mouth tightened. "Then say it cleanly. Don't soften it. Don't say he was delayed or taken or that some road swallowed him."

Suna went still in the way Leni had started to recognize as dangerous. "You don't get to tell me how to name my own life."

"No," Deshi said. "I get to stop lying for my son."

The room seemed to get smaller. Even from the courtyard Leni could feel it — that pressurized quiet when two people quit circling and finally put the blade down flat between them.

"You knew," Suna said.

"I knew enough."

"Enough to let me stand in that hall and tell strangers he was ahead of us."

Deshi looked at the sleeping Asha instead of answering. When she did speak, her voice had thinned. "I kept waiting for him to come back and make a fool of me for saying it aloud."

Suna laughed once, without humor. "He already made fools of us."

Deshi reached for the little yellow dress still in Suna's lap. Suna caught her wrist before she could touch it. For a second the two women stayed like that, linked by the same scrap of cotton, neither letting go.

"I'm not taking it from you," Deshi said. "Fold it smaller. The bag won't close."

Suna released her. She refolded the dress, tighter this time, and Deshi knelt opposite her and began rolling Kael's socks into pairs with quick, irritated hands.

"I should've said it before we left home," Deshi said. "I should've said my son walked out on his wife and children and that there was nothing ahead of us but this road."

"Why didn't you?"

Deshi gave a short, dry breath that was not quite a laugh. "Because then I'd have to say I raised a man who could do it."

Suna shoved the folded dress into the bag. "You did."

Deshi nodded once. She took the answer without flinching. "Seems I did."

Asha turned in her sleep. Both women looked at her. Neither moved until the child settled again.

"Say it tomorrow," Deshi said at last, still watching Asha. "Say he left. I won't dress it up in front of everybody again."

Suna did not thank her. She slid the last shirt into place and pulled the bag ties tight while Deshi held the mouth of the bag open with both hands. It was the first time Leni had seen them work at the same thing without fighting, and it did not look anything like peace. It looked like two people bracing a split seam so it would not rip wider before morning.

By the cistern, Jin had pulled Melie up beside him on the basin rim. He kept looking at her and then out at the road. Twice Leni saw his mouth move. Twice he stopped before sound came. Melie leaned against his arm, waiting without knowing she was waiting.

Arash was alone. This was unusual — the Kondo family moved as a unit, had always moved as a unit, Venn's gravitational authority holding all twelve members in formation. But Arash was standing at the edge of the terraced garden, looking down the hill toward the road, and there was a space around him that no Kondo had filled. Leni followed his sight line and saw the first switchback, mud-covered but solid, and below it the second, still half-submerged, and below that the brown water retreating slowly from the valley floor. Two days. Two days and the road would carry them away from here, carry Arash to a wedding, to a woman waiting somewhere in a city Leni didn't know the name of, and Bett Dao would walk a different road.

Unless.

Leni bit her nail and looked away.

The custodians' quarters were quiet. The covered walkway between their section and the main compound was empty. Whatever Olet and Fasa were doing tonight, they were doing it in private — preparing for the naming, or praying, or simply sitting with the knowledge that twenty days of their work was about to resolve into something they could not predict.

She picked up her pen.

For nineteen days they'd been stuck. Now they had two days and a road and a naming that would ask them to say out loud the things they'd been carrying in private — the things the compound had broken open without anyone's permission. Bram's argument was right, the way Bram's arguments were always right: the naming was coercive, the custodians held the leverage, compliance under duress wasn't consent. But Suna's point was right too, and it cut in a direction her father's logic couldn't follow: you could be coerced into an act that you also, separately, needed to perform. The naming was both things. The truth was both things.

Hala was right. The connections were real. Tomas stuttered on consonants he'd never stuttered on before. Asha rubbed her ear in an old woman's gesture. Ori screamed at puddles. Pik prayed with Gitta's hands. None of that went away because the road opened. None of it was resolved by leaving. They would carry it with them — the Kondos to their wedding, the Parras to wherever Jin was going, the Okoyes to wherever her father's principles and her mother's fear decided to take them.

She wrote:

*Tomorrow they do the naming. The real one. Not the one where everybody stood up and said what would get them through it, but the one where it actually costs something. Deshi just told Suna not to soften what her son did. She said, Say he left. She said it like she was ripping off a bandage that had stuck. Then she got on her knees and helped pack the same bag. I couldn't tell if that was loyalty or surrender.*

*Dad's going to do it too. I know he is. Even though he spent the whole morning arguing that we shouldn't have to, he's going to stand up and say whatever Mom's been wanting him to say, whatever she meant when she said we all left things out. I don't know what they left out. I'm fifteen. There are things my parents don't tell me and I'm supposed to be okay with that but right now, tonight, with the road opening and the naming tomorrow and Tomas talking like a man he barely knows, I'm not okay with it. I'm scared.*

*Jin still hasn't said whatever he has to say to Melie. Arash is still standing on the road side of the garden like being two steps closer to the switchback might turn him into a different man. Venn's going to declare because he can't leave without declaring and he can't stay. Cade's going to hold Pik and say he's his son and mean it. Bett's going to say Ren. I've heard the name once already, but tomorrow she'll say it where nobody can pretend they missed it.*

*And Arash. God. I don't know what Arash is going to do.*

She stopped writing. The lamp beside her flickered in a breeze that smelled of wet earth and wood smoke and something green underneath — the valley exhaling after three weeks of rain. She looked up and saw her mother in the doorway of the sleeping quarters, watching her. Nessa's face was soft in the lamplight, Ada asleep on her shoulder, and for a moment Leni felt the full stupid weight of how much she loved these people who had dragged her to a shrine on a hill where the world fell apart and reformed in shapes she didn't recognize.

She looked back at her journal and wrote the last line:

*They're going to do it. And this time it's going to cost something.*

She closed the journal, capped the pen, and sat on the courtyard wall as the first stars appeared over the valley — sharp and cold and entirely indifferent to the forty-three people below them who were about to tell the truth.
