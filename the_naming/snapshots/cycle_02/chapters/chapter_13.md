# Chapter 13

The rain came back before dawn, and by morning the courtyard stones were glazed with standing water. Venn stood at the kitchen overhang and watched his family eat. Bowl after bowl emptied under Tila's eye. At home and here, he measured mornings the same way: portions first, argument second, explanation never. Tila had divided the morning rice into servings so precise the custodians' assistant had stopped checking her work. She kept a tally on a scrap of cloth, tiny marks in charcoal, and Venn had seen her redoing them at night by lamplight, her lips moving silently. Twenty-two years of marriage and she still kept her own sense of what the family owed and what it could afford. He had never asked to see the cloth. He trusted her judgment more than his own.

The children ate in the main hall with the other families' children, a habit that had formed despite Venn's early objections. He had wanted the Kondos to eat together, apart. That had lasted three days. Now Ori sat between Melie Parra and Sem Dao, laughing at something Venn couldn't hear, and he let it go because the energy required to fight it exceeded the energy he had. Arash was not at breakfast. Arash had been absent from morning meals for the last several days, appearing later with wet hair and no explanation, and Venn had stopped asking because the asking produced a careful blankness on his son's face that was worse than the absence.

Mava sat on the stone bench near the courtyard edge, rubbing her left ear in her slow circle, watching the rain pool in the dips and channels of the uneven paving. Habel was beside her, mending a torn sandal strap with the heavy thread she carried in her apron pocket — she always had thread, needle, something to repair, as if the family's material integrity could be maintained stitch by stitch even as its other kind came apart. The thread was the dark red she used for everything, and Venn could trace a dozen repairs across the Kondos' clothing by its color.

It was an ordinary morning at the shrine. That was the thing Venn would remember afterward — how ordinary it had been, how much it had resembled every other morning of the seventeen they'd spent in this place, right up until the moment it stopped.

Ori had been in the rain earlier, before Habel could call her back. She was the water-child, the one who ran out barefoot when it started, who cupped her palms under the spout of the cistern overflow, who had to be dragged away from the courtyard channels where the runoff ran toward the terraced garden. Nine years old and she still drank the rain off her own arms, a habit Habel had given up correcting. Venn's sister had said once, with the resigned fondness of a mother who knows which battles are lost, that Ori would probably live on a riverboat someday and Habel would never see her. She had been smiling when she said it.

So when the scream came, Venn did not immediately connect it to Ori. The sound was wrong — too raw, too deep in the register for a child's voice, a terror that belonged to someone who understood what they were afraid of. He looked up from his rice and saw her.

She was at the edge of the courtyard where the stone dipped and the water pooled, ankle-deep in the lowest point. She was backed against the wall of the main hall, her shoulders pressed flat to the stone, her mouth open and the sound still pouring out — not screaming now but a high, sustained keening that Venn had never heard from any of his family's children. From any child at all. Her eyes were fixed on the water. Just the water. The flat, brown, still pool where the rain had gathered in the courtyard's lowest point, three inches deep at most, no current, no motion except the patter of fresh rain dimpling its surface.

Venn was moving before he decided to move. Seventeen days of confinement had not dulled the reflex that came with being the man responsible for twelve people. He crossed the courtyard in four strides, his feet splashing through the standing water Ori was staring at as though it were reaching for her, and he put his hands on her shoulders.

"Ori. Look at me."

She didn't look at him. She looked through him, past him, at the water behind his legs. Her body was rigid under his palms — not the rigidity of a child having a tantrum but the locked posture of genuine panic, every muscle braced against something her body believed was happening even though nothing was happening, nothing was wrong, it was three inches of rain on stone and she had been playing in it an hour ago.

Habel was there a moment later, her fingers already working at Ori's collar where the girl had twisted her own shirt tight against her throat. A length of red thread trailed from Habel's wrist where she hadn't had time to cut it from the sandal strap, and it swung against Ori's arm as Habel reached for her daughter.

"She's not hurt," Habel said, running her hands down Ori's arms, her ribs, checking for injury with the automatic efficiency of a mother who had done this a hundred times for scraped knees and fevers but never for this — never for a child who was shaking because of water. "She's not hurt, Venn. She's just — Ori, sweetheart. What happened?"

Ori pressed harder against the wall. The keening had stopped but she was breathing in short, shallow pulls, and tears ran down her face without the usual machinery of crying — no sobbing, no scrunched features, just water from her eyes as involuntary as sweat. She was watching the pool the way a trapped animal watches a predator it can't escape from.

"Move her inside," Venn said. He heard his own voice and recognized the tone: command, the voice that organized departures and settled arguments and said this is what we will do without inflecting it as a question. He lifted Ori — she was light, too light for nine, narrow-boned and long-limbed in a way that would make her tall later but for now made her easy to carry — and brought her into the main hall.

The hall was not empty. It was never empty. Forty-three people in a compound designed for traveling monks meant that any room large enough for a conversation also held witnesses. Suna Mera looked up from where she was braiding Asha's hair. Bram Okoye paused in the middle of something he'd been saying to Nessa. The Teshes were in their corner — Gitta in her usual place with her hands clasped, Fen beside her with a book open in his lap. Jin Parra sat against the far wall with Melie curled next to him, her head on his thigh. Leni Okoye was cross-legged on her mat, her journal in her lap, watching the courtyard with the sharp attentiveness that made Venn uneasy when he noticed it. The Leos were near the open side, where the valley view admitted gray light and the constant low rush of the flood below.

And Bett Dao was standing by the kitchen entrance, drying a cooking pot with a cloth.

Venn set Ori down on the nearest sleeping mat. Tila appeared at his side — she always did, she moved toward distress the way he moved toward it but without the noise, arriving with cloth and water and a hand on the child's forehead before Venn had finished setting her down. "No fever," Tila said, her palm against Ori's brow. "She's cold. She's soaked through."

"She was in the rain," Habel said, kneeling beside them, the red thread still swinging from her wrist. "She's always in the rain."

"Get her dry." Venn straightened and turned toward the courtyard, as if the threat were still out there, as if something in the standing water had teeth. Behind him Ori made a small, wrecked sound and said the first coherent words she'd managed since the scream:

"No. No, don't — don't let it come in."

The water. She was afraid of the water coming inside. Three inches of pooled rain on courtyard stone, and Ori was terrified that it would rise and follow her.

Venn turned back and knelt beside the mat. "It can't come in here, Ori. The hall floor is higher than the courtyard — look." He pointed at the threshold. "It would have to rise a foot to reach us. It won't. It's just rain."

She shook her head. She wasn't hearing him. She was watching the courtyard through the hall's wide-open side, watching the same pools she had been splashing in an hour before, and her body was vibrating with a fear that belonged somewhere Venn could not reach.

He knew what the manifestations looked like by now. Everyone did. The compound had been watching children borrow pieces of other families for seventeen days — a gesture, an allergy, a speech pattern, a lullaby. He had watched it happen to other people's children and told himself each time that the Kondos were intact, that his family traveled as a unit, that there was nothing wrong with his people that these strangers could fix or claim. Asha Mera rubbing her ear in Mava's gesture had been disturbing but manageable — Venn had managed it, had reframed it as imitation, as a child copying an old woman's habit. Yesterday Pik Leo's hands had moved in Gitta Tesh's prayer pattern, and Venn had watched Vera Leo's face tighten and felt, with a relief he was not proud of, that it was still someone else's problem.

It was not someone else's problem now. It was Ori, shaking on a mat in the main hall, afraid of rain.

He looked up.

Across the room, Bett Dao had stopped drying the pot. She was standing very still, the pot in one hand and the cloth in the other, looking at Ori with her mouth slightly open. The color had gone out of her face. Whatever she recognized in the child's terror made her set the pot down carefully on the stone floor and press both hands flat against her own thighs, as if the pressure might keep her upright.

Venn saw it. Recognition moved across her face too fast for thought. She knew what this was. She had seen it before.

The room was beginning to notice. Suna's hands had stilled in Asha's hair. Bram was watching Venn and Bett with the calculating attention of a man who could read the geometry of a room.

Venn stood up.

---

He did not decide to cross the hall. He was kneeling by Ori, and then he was standing, and then he was in front of Bett Dao, close enough to see the damp on her shirt where the pot had dripped, close enough to smell the kitchen smoke that clung to her clothes and her hair. The room behind him went quiet with a speed that had its own sound — the sound of forty people holding their breath at once.

"What do you know."

He did not frame it as a question. Bett looked up at him. She was shorter by half a foot, and she did not step back. Hala Dao was already moving, positioning herself at Bett's left shoulder with the automatic precision of a woman who had been putting her body between her sister and the world for forty years. Hala's mouth had already hardened. Her shoulders were set forward. Not angry yet, but close.

"Venn," Hala said. "Step back."

"I'm talking to her." He did not look at Hala. He was looking at Bett's face as he studied anyone who might endanger his family or keep something from it — suppliers, neighbors, the custodians when they announced another naming. She knew. He could see it in the fixed set of her jaw, in the care with which she kept her hands flat at her sides. She had been living beside this knowledge a long time and making room around it.

"My niece is nine years old," Venn said. His voice was rising and he let it rise. "She loves water. She has never been afraid of water in her life. She is in there shaking because she can't look at three inches of rain without thinking it's going to drown her. And you — you stood there and you recognized it. I watched you recognize it."

The room was fully still now. Children were being pulled close by parents who could feel the shift in temperature. Rimi Mera had taken Asha into her lap. Nessa Okoye was holding baby Ada against her chest with both arms. Even the younger Kondo children — Venn's nephew's two boys, who understood nothing of the manifestations except that adults were frightened by them — had gone quiet on their mats.

"Tell me what you know about what's happening to my niece."

"Venn." Hala again, louder. She had placed her body fully between him and Bett now, her sturdy frame blocking the space. "You don't get to stand over my sister and shout at her."

"Your sister knows something about Ori. She looked at Ori and she knew. I'm asking her what she knows."

"You're not asking. You're accusing."

"I'm standing in a room where my niece can't stop shaking and the one person who seems to understand why won't open her mouth. If that's an accusation, fine."

"I didn't do anything to your niece," Bett said.

Her voice was low. Not managed, not restrained. It had dropped into the register Venn had heard when she instructed Jin Parra through Melie's allergy, and once at night in the courtyard when she spoke to — Venn stopped himself. He was not going to think about that now.

"I didn't do anything to her," Bett said again. "But I know what I saw."

"Then tell me."

Hala put a hand on Bett's arm. Bett looked at her sister's hand and then back at Venn, and something shifted in her face — a decision arriving, or a defense coming down, or both at once.

"I had a son," she said.

The silence in the hall was total. Even baby Ada, who had been making the small restless sounds that preceded a full cry, was quiet.

"His name was Ren. He drowned."

She said it in the short pieces natural to her, each one complete before the next one arrived. But the weight of the pieces was different now. Each sentence landed on the stone floor of the hall and stayed there.

"He was three years old. He drowned in a creek near our house during the monsoon. Seven years ago." She paused. Not for effect — Bett Dao was not a person who paused for effect — but because what came next required her to open a door she had kept shut for the better part of a decade in front of forty-three people she hadn't known a month ago.

"The water came up fast. It wasn't deep — it reached his chest, maybe less. But the current was strong and he was small and his feet went out from under him and the water took him."

Venn stared at her. He had expected something else — a defense to dismantle, a lie to expose, something he could fight. Not this. Not a dead child named and placed on the ground between them like an object neither of them could pick up.

"In the weeks before that monsoon, he was afraid of water," Bett said. "He'd never been afraid of it before. He used to sit in the washing basin and splash until the floor was soaked. But that year — that last month — he wouldn't go near it. Wouldn't let me wash his face. Wouldn't drink from the cup if it was too full. I thought he'd grow out of it."

Her voice was very quiet now. Hala's hand tightened on her arm.

"He didn't grow out of it. He drowned in what he was afraid of."

She looked at Venn steadily. There was no hostility in it. Only exhaustion, and the blunt fact of what she had carried for seven years and had finally been forced to lay between them.

"Your niece is afraid of water exactly as my son was. I know because I watched him for weeks. I know what it looks like in a child's body. She's not sick and she's not pretending. She's afraid of something that happened to a boy she never met."

The room exhaled. Not a gasp — something lower, less shaped, the sound of forty people absorbing information that had no category in any system of understanding they carried.

Venn felt his mouth open and close. He had crossed the room to fight, and the fight had been removed from him by the most disarming weapon imaginable: a woman naming her dead son without asking for sympathy or offering defense. He was left standing with the momentum of his anger and nothing to throw it against.

And then, because he was Venn Kondo, because stopping would mean admitting something he was not prepared to admit, he found his footing.

"I don't care about your son."

He said it and heard it and understood instantly that it was the wrong thing — not morally wrong, he was past caring about that, but strategically catastrophic, the kind of sentence that turned a room against you and could not be taken back. He could feel the shift, the forty witnesses recalibrating, and he pushed through it because the alternative was silence, and Venn Kondo did not do silence in front of an audience.

"I'm sorry for your loss. But your loss is your family's tragedy. And now it's inside my niece. She's nine years old and she's sitting over there shaking because your dead son's fear is living in her body. How did that happen? How did it get into my family?"

"Venn." Arash's voice, from behind him. Quiet. Not defiant — not yet — just the sound of his name in his son's voice, a single word that carried the weight of a request.

Venn turned. Arash was standing near the corridor that led to the sleeping quarters. He was not with the Kondos. He was standing alone, his arms at his sides, and the split in him was the thing Venn had been bracing against for days — the same split Venn saw each morning when Arash came late to breakfast and each evening when he drifted to the edge of the Kondo group during the naming. He was not standing with his father. Everyone in the room could see that.

"Sit down, Arash."

Arash didn't sit down. He didn't move at all, which was worse. He stood in the corridor entrance, and the hesitation was so legible that every adult in the room could read it — Bram Okoye with his teacher's eye for the structure of a moment, Suna Mera with her quiet cataloguing attention, Leni Okoye watching from behind her hair, even Gitta Tesh in her corner, even Mava, who was gripping the edge of the stone bench with both hands.

"I said sit down."

"She didn't do anything wrong." Arash's voice was steady. "She told you what she lost. You asked her and she told you."

"This isn't about —"

"Baba." The old word, the child's word — Arash had not called him that in public for years. It landed in the hall like something breakable being set down too hard. "She lost her son. She told you. You can hear it or you can keep shouting."

Venn stared at his son across fifteen feet of stone floor and lamp-shadow, and for a moment he saw himself — the jaw, the brow, the set of the shoulders. Then Arash's face unsettled the resemblance. No defiance. No submission either. Only a man standing in the open with nowhere clean to place his loyalty, and the nakedness of that made Venn feel briefly, irrationally betrayed.

"Your niece," Bett said from behind him, and her voice was quiet enough that he had to turn back to hear the rest. "Your niece is carrying something that doesn't belong to her. She didn't choose it and neither did I. I don't owe you my grief and I don't owe you an explanation for something I can't explain. But I've told you what I know."

She picked up the cooking pot from the floor and walked toward the kitchen entrance. Hala moved with her, one hand on Bett's back, guiding her sister away from the space Venn occupied as if it were contaminated. At the threshold Bett paused and looked back — not at Venn but past him, toward the mat where Ori sat wrapped in a blanket with Tila and Habel on either side.

"You should get her away from the open side of the hall," Bett said. "Where she can't see the courtyard. It'll help."

Then she was gone, and Hala was gone, and the room was left with Venn standing alone in the middle of it, his family on one side with a gap where Arash should have been, and forty witnesses who had just watched him demand to see a woman's worst wound and then call it an intrusion.

He walked back to Ori. He sat down next to his niece and his sister and his wife, and Tila put a hand on his forearm and squeezed once — hard, brief, the kind of pressure that could mean I'm with you or could mean stop, there was no way to tell, and he did not ask.

Ori pressed against Habel's side. Her eyes were closed. The shaking had subsided to a fine tremor in her hands, and her breathing had evened out enough that she was no longer gasping, but she wore the blank, overstrained look of a child who had encountered something inside herself that she could not put a name to and could not make stop.

Venn sat beside her and did not look at Arash, and did not look at the kitchen doorway where Bett had disappeared, and the hall remained silent for a long time, broken only by the rain strengthening outside and the steady, indifferent sound of the flood.

---

Fasa rang the bell at dusk.

The sound was the same as every other evening — the clear, specific note that meant the naming, that meant come into the hall, that meant declare yourselves. Night after night had worn a groove in the compound's daily rhythm: the bell, the shuffling, the arrangement of families in their habitual places, Fasa's opening words. But the room that gathered tonight was not the room that had gathered yesterday. The confrontation between Venn and Bett had charged the air the way a lightning strike charges atmosphere, and every family carried the residue differently.

The Kondos sat where they always sat along the eastern wall, and the wrongness of them showed at once. Arash had put Mava and two other people between himself and his father. Ori sat wrapped in a blanket under Habel's arm, her face turned from the open side of the hall. Tila's tally cloth was tucked into her waistband, and her expression had the careful neutrality she used when she disagreed with Venn but would not do it where others could see.

The other pressure points were just as easy to read. Bram sat near the open side with his hands already moving over an argument he had not yet spoken. Nessa held Ada too tightly. Bett sat beside Hala with her spine straight and her face shut, never once looking toward the Kondos. In the Tesh corner Gitta was too still, her clasped hands fixed in the prayer pattern Pik Leo had begun borrowing, her body drawn inward as though the day had packed her smaller around something dense and private.

Fasa stood at the center. The oil lamps threw her shadow long against the stone wall behind her. Her face had a drawn quality that Venn had not seen before — a thinness around the eyes and mouth that suggested effort where there should have been habit. The naming was her domain, her life's practice, and she had conducted it every evening with a calm that Venn found maddening but could not deny was effective. Tonight the calm was there but it was working harder. He could hear it in the slight formality of her breathing before she spoke, the way a singer breathes before a note she is not certain she can reach.

"We are gathered for the evening naming," Fasa said. Her voice carried the cadence of the ritual, but beneath it — something Venn could hear because he was trained to hear weakness in other people's authority — there was strain. "Each family will declare its present members and their —"

"Why?"

The word came from Bram Okoye. It was not angry. It was exhausted in a way that was more dangerous than anger — the exhaustion of a man who had been making the same argument for seventeen days and had watched it fail each time and was now too tired to construct it properly.

"We've declared ourselves every evening for two and a half weeks, Fasa. The declarations haven't changed. But we have. My son speaks with another man's hesitation. A girl who loved water is terrified of it. Another child hums a song from a family she's never met. Pik Leo prays like a woman he's barely spoken to. So what exactly are we declaring? The names haven't changed. The people behind the names have."

Fasa's pause was longer than it should have been. Venn watched her reach for the liturgical syntax, the rhythmic cadence that usually anchored her when the naming met resistance.

"The naming asks for truth," she said. "If the truth has changed —"

"The truth hasn't changed. The truth is that half the families in this room are carrying someone else's damage in their children and nobody can explain it and nobody can stop it. The declarations are the same. The facts on the ground are not."

"Then the declarations must be updated to reflect the facts."

"Updated how? What's the category for 'my son has a stranger's speech pattern'? What do I write on the naming sheet for that? Where does it go?"

"That is what the naming is for," Olet said from the side of the hall where he stood, his arms folded, his face as constant as the stone behind him. His voice carried the unhurried weight that Venn had come to think of as institutional — not a person speaking but a building. "To find the category."

"There is no category." Bram's voice was rising now, and Nessa put a hand on his arm that he either didn't notice or chose to ignore. "Categories are what you assign when you understand something. We don't understand this. You don't understand this. You have a theory about ancestors and kinship maps and hidden connections, and we're supposed to stand up every evening and pour our families into a theory nobody can test or verify."

"The naming does not ask you to verify," Olet said. "It asks you to declare."

"Declare what? That my family is being —"

Habel stood up.

The room shifted. Habel Kondo did not speak in public settings. She was the sister who managed children and mended clothes and kept herself in the background of the family Venn organized and Tila administered. She did not stand up during namings. She did not raise her voice. But she was standing now, and the red-threaded repairs she'd made across the Kondo children's clothing were visible even in lamplight. The whole day showed in her — sleepless eyes, jaw set hard, shoulders locked against helplessness.

"My daughter plays in the rain," Habel said. Her voice was not loud but it was clear, and it cut through the murmur that Bram's argument had stirred. "She was in the courtyard an hour before she screamed. She was happy. She was my Ori, the one who drinks rainwater off her arms and has to be dragged inside. And then she wasn't. Then she was someone else's child, screaming at three inches of pooled water like it was going to rise up and kill her."

Nobody spoke.

"I don't care about your categories or your theory or your kinship map. I care about why my daughter has a dead boy's fear inside her body. Can any of you tell me that?"

She looked at Fasa. She looked at Olet. She looked at Bett Dao, and to her credit she did not look with accusation. She looked like a woman searching the room for one usable answer. Then she sat down and gathered Ori against her, and Ori buried her face in her mother's chest, and Habel held her with rigid tenderness, knowing that holding was not the same as helping.

For one beat the hall held.

Then Cade Leo stood up so fast Pik startled beside him. "Say it plain, then. What am I supposed to declare about my son? Yesterday I could say he's mine and stop there. Tonight he's sitting here making another woman's prayer with his hands."

Pik had already laced his fingers together in his lap. His thumbs were moving in the slow circling pattern Vera had been tracking for two days. Vera reached from behind him and caught his wrists. Pik jerked once, then went still.

"Then don't stop there," Fasa said. Her ritual cadence almost held. "Speak what has changed."

"With what words?" Cade asked. Wynn had a hand flat between his shoulders now, but Cade did not sit. "Give me one that doesn't sound like I'm losing him in front of everybody."

"That is exactly the problem," Bram said. He was on his feet before Fasa could answer. "There is no language for this because language follows understanding, and none of you understands what you're asking us to name. These children are carrying other families' losses, and you're trying to turn that fact into procedure."

"The fact will remain whether you speak it or not," Hala said. She did not stand, but her voice carried anyway. One hand stayed flat on Bett's knee. "My sister named her son this morning. Ori is still afraid of water. Refusing the words isn't helping that child."

"The naming is not procedure," Olet said. He had not moved from the wall. "It is how the room hears what is already true."

At Bram's side, Tomas said, "M-mama?" The catch on the first consonant was Jin Parra's, not the boy's. Nessa folded over it. The sound that came out of her was small and wrecked, but Ada woke against her chest and began to scream. Ori flinched so hard Habel had to lock both arms around her. Nori buried her face in Bett's shoulder. The hall lost the fragile quiet it had been balancing on all day.

"We can't keep saying the old lines," Suna said. She had Asha in her lap and one palm over the child's ear, but her eyes were on Fasa. "Not after this. Either the naming changes or it admits it has fallen behind the room."

"It changes when you tell the truth in full," Fasa said, louder now. "One family at a time. One voice at a time."

Miro Tesh half rose from the corner. "You keep asking for full truth and one day you'll get more than this place can hold."

Enn grabbed his sleeve. Gitta's hand came down over his wrist to force him back, and even from across the hall Venn could see that her fingers were shaking.

Bram started again. Hala cut across him. Cade had not sat down. Vera was still holding Pik's wrists. The voices piled over one another until Fasa's ritual cadence snapped and she shouted, "Silence."

The word struck the stone hard enough to make even Ada stop for a beat. The oil lamps jumped in their glass chimneys. Venn sat in the middle of his family and felt the hall slip its grip — voices crossing, children crying, nobody waiting to be called on anymore. Habit had carried the room this far. Habit was gone now.

He looked at Arash. His son was at the far end of the Kondo group, and at some point during the chaos he had turned his body slightly — not away from the family, not toward the Daos, but toward the middle of the room instead of back toward the Kondo wall. The shift was small. Anyone watching casually would not have seen it. Venn saw the gap it made anyway: his son no longer braced against the family line, no longer angled toward his father's voice.

"Arash."

His son looked at him.

"Come sit with us. With your family."

Arash held his gaze for a long moment. Then he looked away — not at Bett, not at anyone, but at the rain falling in the courtyard beyond the hall's open side — and he did not move back.

Fasa lifted both hands and spoke before Bram or Hala could begin again. "We are not finishing the naming tonight," she said. Her voice still carried the shape of ritual, but the strain was in it now for everyone to hear. "Go back to your families. Tomorrow evening you will return, and you will consider what this day requires you to say."

The families dispersed. Not in their usual patterns, not with the grudging orderliness that seventeen evenings of practice had established. The Daos went quickly — Hala steering Bett and the children toward their sleeping area with the purposeful stride of a woman removing her people from a situation. The Meras separated: Deshi and Peyam one direction, Suna and the children another, and Venn noticed that neither group looked back at the other. The Okoyes stayed where they were, Nessa's tears drying on her face, Bram holding Ada, his face gone flat with the knowledge that he had argued precisely and accomplished nothing. The Leos moved as a unit, Cade and Wynn bookending Pik, Vera behind them, all of them walking with the careful gait of people trying not to be noticed.

The Teshes moved last. Miro helped Gitta to her feet, and she rose slowly, and Venn saw — because he was watching everything tonight with the desperate attentiveness of a man whose world was cracking — that the shaking had not stopped.

Not the tremor of age. Gitta Tesh was seventy, but her hands had been steady throughout their confinement — steady when she declared Fen her grandson on the second day, steady through every naming and every communal meal, steady when she'd produced a recipe she couldn't explain knowing and served it without her voice wavering. Her hands were shaking now in a fast, dry vibration that ran from knuckle to wrist, and she clasped them tighter in her lap to stop it and they kept shaking.

Nobody else seemed to notice. The room was too full of its own grief. But Venn noticed, because he had spent his life reading families and he knew the signs of a person nearing the end of endurance. He had seen it in Suna Mera's eyes during the second naming. He had seen it in Jin Parra's face when Fasa's questions broke his story. He saw it now in Gitta Tesh's hands, and some part of him — the part that was not a patriarch defending a claim but simply a man who had watched too many things break in seventeen days — understood that whatever Gitta was carrying, it was heavier than a recipe, and close to breaking her open.

He stood. The hall was changing shape around him, families drawing back toward their mats and corners. Tila had Ori by the hand, leading her toward the Kondo sleeping mats along the inner wall, keeping her to the sheltered side of the hall where there was no view of the courtyard and its standing water. Habel followed. The other Kondo children trailed after, subdued, wide-eyed.

Arash was not among them.

Venn stood in the hall and looked at the empty space where his son had been sitting, and then at the doorway through which the Daos had disappeared, and he did not go looking. He did not need to. He knew what Arash was moving toward with the same certainty he knew his own name — not because he had proof but because the evidence had been accumulating for days, in Arash's morning absences and evening distances and the careful blankness he showed when Venn looked at him, and the confrontation with Bett had not caused it but had made it impossible to keep pretending he didn't see.

His family was coming apart. Not the family he administered — the twelve Kondos traveling to a wedding that now seemed as distant and as abstract as the capital — but the idea that the family was all there was, that the Kondos began and ended with the Kondos, that the border he had enforced for twenty-two years might be nothing more than a line drawn in dust and mistaken for permanence.

He stayed where he was in the main hall of the transit shrine until the oil lamps guttered low and the room took on the false stillness of people lying down without sleep. The rain fell. The flood, indifferent and immovable below them in the valley, made its low unending sound. From the Kondo mats along the inner wall, Ori whimpered, and the sound carried through the thin stone walls the way all sounds carried in this place — as if the shrine had been designed to make sure nothing stayed private, nothing stayed contained, nothing remained the property of any single family for long.
