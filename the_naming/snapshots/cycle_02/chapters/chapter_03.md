# Chapter 3

The bell hit the sleeping quarters like somebody throwing iron at stone. Leni dragged the pillow over her head and thought, with feeling, that four mornings at the shrine was already too many.

It wasn't a gentle bell or a good-morning-honored-guests bell. It was a flat iron clang that one of the custodians' assistants struck from the prayer courtyard at what had to be an hour no reasonable person would voluntarily inhabit. The assistant was a young woman with close-cropped hair and forearms like she split wood for a living, which, Leni had learned, she did. She rang the bell with the same expression every time — not unkindness, not warmth, just the face of a person performing a task that existed before she was born and would continue after she was dead.

Leni pulled her knees to her chest on the sleeping mat and pressed her face into the thin pillow, which smelled like stone dust and other people's hair, and listened to the compound wake up.

Forty-three people waking up sounds like this: baby Ada crying first, because Ada cried at everything, because Ada was one year old and that was her entire skill set. Then Nessa's quiet murmuring as she lifted Ada against her chest. Then the Kondo family — all twelve of them — rustling and murmuring in their corner like a single organism with too many limbs. One of the younger Kondo boys always coughed first thing, a dry bark that echoed off the stone walls and made someone else's child whine about being cold. Tomas turned over on his mat and bumped his knee against Leni's ankle, and she shoved his leg away without opening her eyes. The flood rose steadily beneath everything, that low permanent rush from the valley floor, like the hill itself was clearing its throat.

Bram was already sitting up. Leni could tell from the direction of his breathing — he breathed differently when he was upright, a teacher's posture reasserting itself even in the dark. He reached over Tomas and touched Leni's shoulder.

"Up," he said.

"Why."

"Because the bell rang."

"The bell's not for us. It's for people who want to pray."

"The bell's for everyone. Get up."

Leni opened her eyes. The sleeping quarters were dim — oil lamplight from the corridor, gray monsoon dawn barely reaching the high stone windows. Bodies lay in rows on mats, and the whole room had the intimate strangeness of sleeping near strangers. Across the partition, she could hear Deshi Mera telling Suna something in a voice that suggested Suna was doing it wrong, whatever it was. On the other side, one of the Kondo cousins was trying to fold his blanket while his mother corrected his corners.

She sat up, pulled her braids off her face, and bit the edge of her thumbnail before she caught herself doing it.

Morning prayers were in the courtyard, and by Day 4 Leni knew the cast well enough to count the absences before she was fully awake. Olet stood under the overhang. Fasa stood beside him with her hands folded and her face set in that workmanship expression, like she was checking the joints on a table nobody else could see wobbling. Peyam Mera was already there, small and precise, lips moving with words he somehow seemed to know. Jin Parra waited at the edge, not praying, just standing through the hour because the courtyard cost him less than lying awake beside Melie. Venn Kondo still didn't come. Tila did. Arash had shown up once, the morning before, and not returned. Bram had tried prayers on Day 3 and reported back to Nessa that they were "interesting from an anthropological perspective." He hadn't gone back. The Leos stayed away entirely.

After prayers the whole compound jammed itself onto the cistern steps. The stone was slick, the air smelled of wet moss and metal, and everybody needed water at once. Venn announced that the Kondos needed three jars before anybody else because twelve people drank more than four. Tila didn't argue with him. She just started moving bodies. She put the older children on the steepest stretch and the younger ones at the landing. Filled jars passed hand to hand instead of carried whole. When Suna Mera came up with two empty pots because breakfast was on her, Tila took the first full jar and put it straight into Suna's hands. Even Venn didn't object.

By the time Leni got back to the kitchen overhang with the onion basket, she'd identified the compound's real hierarchy, and it had almost nothing to do with who sounded important.

Day 4's cooking pair was the Meras and the Okoyes. Suna was already at the fire, rice soaking, flatbread dough under her palms, dal thickening in a pot she'd gotten started before the bell. Nessa arrived with Ada on her hip and a stack of bowls she nearly dropped when one of the Kondo boys cut behind her. Suna caught the top bowl without turning.

"Left side," she said. "Those stack easier."

Nessa shifted obediently. "Thanks."

Deshi took up position behind Suna like weather.

"Is that how much salt you're using?"

"Yes."

"For this many people."

"Yes, Deshi."

"Your mother, god rest her, always said salt is cheaper than complaint. But then she was cooking for four."

Suna added more salt. She did it without expression, which Leni respected. The trick with adults like Deshi wasn't winning. It was denying them the fight.

"Your mother's helping," Suna said to Leni as Nessa tried again with the bowls. It wasn't a request so much as a practical redeployment.

Leni sat on an upturned crate near the edge of the overhang, where the rain came down in a silver curtain two feet from her knees, and started peeling onions. The stone under her feet still held yesterday's heat. Smoke from the fire crawled into her hair and eyes. Jin came up from the cistern with another jar braced against his hip, shoulders rounded forward as if he'd agreed long ago to carry whatever anybody handed him. Melie trailed him, leaned against his leg when he set the water down, then wandered back toward the courtyard before he could look at her for too long.

Across the yard, Miro and Enn Tesh were already headed for the latrines with rolled sleeves and the expression of people who understood that life was mostly maintenance. Fen carried the sloshing bucket behind them, taller than Leni, dark hair in his eyes, moving with the specific resentment of a sixteen-year-old being told the disgusting job built character. He passed the kitchen, caught her looking, and she jerked her gaze down hard enough to nick her thumb with the peeling knife.

It bled fast. She shoved the thumb into her mouth and tasted iron under the onion. Nessa was beside her a second later, Ada still on her hip.

"Careful. Let me see."

"It's fine, Mom."

"Show me."

Leni held up her hand. Nessa peered at the cut as if the knife might've introduced a whole new plague to the shrine.

"Keep it clean," Nessa said. "We don't know what's on these surfaces."

"It's an onion, not a biohazard."

"Leni."

"I'm keeping it clean."

Nessa went back to the bowls. Her mother's fear was always looking for an object it could justify itself with. This morning it had chosen Leni's thumb.

Breakfast happened in a rush because forty-three people waiting for food turned even the quiet families into weather. Plates passed hand to hand. The Kondos took their usual corner and made a wall out of themselves. The Daos sat close enough to the kitchen that Hala could intercept anyone who approached Bett. The Leos made a small careful cluster around Pik. The shrine kept sorting itself into units, then leaking around the edges anyway.

When the last plate was scraped mostly clean, Olet stepped in and assigned labor with the same flat calm he brought to everything.

"The Parras and the Teshes will take the terraced garden this morning," he said. "The weeds have grown through the herb rows. The Daos will clean the main hall. The Okoyes and the Leos will gather firewood from the north slope — the fallen branches are dry enough."

"The Kondos?" Venn asked from his corner.

"The Kondos took the latrine yesterday, and the Meras cooked. They may rest this morning."

Venn accepted that with a single nod. Arash didn't. He was on his feet before Leni had swallowed her last bite.

"I'll help with the garden," Arash said.

Venn looked at him. Two bowls stopped halfway to mouths.

"I'd rather move than sit," Arash said, looking at the hall instead of at his father.

That hit harder than yelling would've.

Bram leaned toward Leni. "Ready for firewood?"

"Sure. Is there anything more thrilling than wet branches?"

"On a hilltop shrine in a monsoon? Probably not."

They gathered firewood on the north slope — Leni, Bram, Cade Leo, and Wynn Leo. The slope was steep and wet, and the fallen branches were heavy with rain. Cade moved carefully, testing each foothold before committing his weight, and Wynn walked ahead with the confidence of someone who didn't worry about falling. They worked in near-silence. Leni noticed that Cade and Wynn communicated in small touches — a hand on the shoulder to indicate a branch worth collecting, a tap on the forearm when the slope turned slippery. They had their own language, built from years of reading each other's bodies, and it was the opposite of how the naming wanted families to speak: no declarations, no formal categories, just a hand finding the right shoulder at the right time.

From the slope, Leni could see down to the terraced garden where Jin and Miro Tesh worked side by side, pulling weeds in the rain. Arash had joined them. Jin worked steadily, bending and pulling without pause, because the alternative was sitting with his own thoughts. Miro matched his pace. Arash was faster but less systematic, yanking weeds in clumps and leaving roots behind. Enn Tesh knelt at the far end of the terrace, replanting herb seedlings the rain had displaced, her fingers pressing the soil around each stem with the care of someone who knew food grown here meant food that didn't come from the custodians' stores.

By noon on Day 4, the shrine smelled of wet wood, cooking smoke, and forty-three bodies that hadn't had a proper bath since arriving. The rain had paused, which didn't mean it was over — the clouds sat low and fat over the valley like a held breath. From the main hall's open side, the flood was visible below: brown water covering the valley floor, the switchback road drowned past the third bend. The roads that had brought them here were still gone.

Leni looked at that water and thought: we're not going anywhere.

And it occurred to her — looking at who had cooked, who had hauled water, who had cleaned, who had pulled weeds — that the compound was already being run by the people who were useful, not the people who were loud. Suna's cooking gave her a say in how meals worked that Deshi's criticism couldn't touch. Jin's labor earned him a nod from Olet that Venn's resting did not. Tila organized the Kondos with more practical authority than Venn's pronouncements. And Enn Tesh, replanting herbs nobody asked her to replant, had just done more for the collective food supply than anyone who'd spent the morning asserting their rights.

Venn still spoke for the Kondos. Bram still framed arguments that nobody could answer. But the shrine didn't run on speeches. It ran on hands.

---

The children figured it out before the adults did.

Leni watched it happen from the courtyard steps, where she sat with her journal balanced on her knees, because the courtyard was the only place where she could be alone without being alone — people passed through, but nobody stopped to supervise her.

It started with Asha. Suna's youngest was four, and four-year-olds don't have politics. Asha had spent the morning following Suna around the kitchen, getting underfoot, being redirected, and finally being deposited in the courtyard with a firmness that suggested Suna's patience had a floor. Asha sat on the warm stone for exactly thirty seconds before she walked over to where Pik Leo was playing with a pile of smooth river stones he'd found near the cistern.

Pik looked up, shoved the flattest stones toward her, and Asha sat down next to him. That was apparently enough.

Then Melie Parra drifted over. She stopped beside them, eyes flicking from Pik to Asha to the tower. Three seconds, maybe. Then she sat and started picking through the pile for the smoothest stones.

Nori Dao came next — Bett's daughter, seven, with the same economy of movement Leni had noticed in Bett, everything purposeful, nothing wasted. She didn't ask to join. She brought a stone and placed it on top of the tower and that was that.

Kael Mera, eight, arrived running from the direction of the sleeping quarters, chased by Kiri Tesh, also eight, both of them making a noise that suggested they had decided a stone courtyard was a racetrack. They nearly knocked over the tower. Asha said something sharp — Leni couldn't hear the words, but the tone was pure Deshi, three generations distilled into a four-year-old's voice — and Kael stopped, looked, and sat down.

By the middle of the afternoon, the courtyard held a cluster of children that made no sense by the shrine's family map. Asha Mera, Melie Parra, Pik Leo, Nori Dao, Kael Mera, Kiri Tesh, Ori Kondo, two of the younger Kondo cousins, and Tomas — Leni's own brother, who had abandoned her to join the group with a ten-year-old's confidence that playing was more important than family loyalty.

The stone tower had evolved into a game Leni didn't recognize — partly building, partly tag, partly an elaborate system of rules that the children were inventing in real time and revising by shouting. Pik was the most inventive — he kept proposing modifications that the others either adopted or ignored, and he seemed equally happy either way, his face lit up — a child who had discovered other children after being, Leni guessed, often the only child in whatever room he was in. Ori Kondo was the enforcer — she had inherited her uncle Venn's sense of order, if not his rigidity, and she settled disputes with a firmness that made the younger children comply without resentment. Tomas, who at home would never have voluntarily spent time with anyone under the age of nine, was teaching Kiri a clapping game that involved increasingly complicated hand patterns and both of them failing and laughing.

Rimi Mera, twelve, sat at the edge of the group, old enough to feel the pull of both sides. She braided Ori's hair while Ori argued with Tomas about whose turn it was, and her fingers moved with the automatic skill of a girl who'd been braiding hair since she was old enough to reach. Raya Dao, thirteen, leaned against the courtyard wall with her arms crossed, watching the younger children with an expression that said she was absolutely not playing, she was just standing here, it had nothing to do with the game. Sem Dao, eleven, had no such dignity — he was in the middle of it, building and arguing and laughing, a lanky boy with a laugh that started in his stomach and didn't stop until someone pushed him over, which Kael did twice.

Leni watched. She was too old for this. Too old by exactly the right amount to make it painful, because part of her wanted to sit down and stack stones with Asha, and the rest of her knew that wanting that was itself the proof she was past it.

She bit her thumbnail and wrote in her journal: *The kids don't care whose kids they are.*

Fen Tesh was on the far side of the courtyard, sitting on the low wall that separated the prayer space from the sleeping quarters. He had a stick and was scraping patterns into the moss between the stones — not watching the children, not ignoring them, occupying the same in-between space Leni was in. The other teenager. She thought about going over and saying something, but what would she say? Nice shrine. Great flood. Love what they've done with the mandatory prayers. She stayed where she was.

The parents cared. Suna came to the courtyard door and stood there for a long moment, her eyes tracking Asha, Kael, Rimi — counting her children, placing them in the geography of the group. Her face stayed calm, but her fingers worried at each other as if she were sorting invisible tasks. Asha was sitting in Ori Kondo's lap while Ori braided a piece of string into something. Suna watched this and then turned back to the kitchen without saying anything, but Leni could see the effort of it — the deliberate decision not to call Asha back, not to draw a line, because drawing a line would require explaining where the line was and why.

Nessa appeared later with Ada fussing on her hip. She found Tomas in the scrum and relaxed visibly — Leni could see the tension leave her mother's shoulders when she confirmed all three of her older children were accounted for. Baby Ada reached toward the group with both fists and made a sound of longing that no one over the age of one could have produced without embarrassment. Nessa bounced her and retreated.

Jin Parra came to the courtyard and leaned against the wall, watching Melie play. He stood exactly far enough away that she wouldn't notice him watching, and his face held an expression Leni associated with people trying to memorize something. He rubbed the side of his jaw — that thing he did, the brief lock before his mouth opened, like speech was a door that stuck. After a few minutes, Melie saw him and waved, and he waved back, and she turned back to the game. He stayed.

Venn Kondo sent Tila to collect the Kondo children for afternoon rest, and Tila came to the courtyard with the authority of her voice alone. "Ori. The others. Come rest." The Kondo cousins peeled off from the group without argument, but Ori looked up from the string she was braiding and said, "Five more minutes."

Tila waited.

"Please."

Tila gave her five minutes. Leni timed it in her head. At four minutes and thirty seconds, Ori kissed the top of Asha's head — an odd gesture, casual and intimate, from one child to another child she'd known for barely four days — and followed Tila back inside.

The play group shrank but didn't dissolve. It kept reforming throughout the afternoon in different configurations, like water finding its level. The children moved between families without asking permission. They shared food, shared space, shared the game whose rules mutated hourly. By late afternoon, Melie was asleep with her head on Nori's stomach in the prayer courtyard, both of them curled on the warm stone like animals who'd chosen each other's warmth by instinct, and Kiri Tesh was trying to teach Tomas a song in a melody Leni didn't recognize — something pentatonic and haunting that sounded like it came from somewhere far away.

None of this was organized. None of it was sanctioned. The adults saw it happening and didn't stop it, because stopping it would require explaining why, and none of them had an explanation that didn't sound insane or cruel. *You can't play with her because she's a Kondo. You can't sit with him because he's a Parra.* Even Venn, who had the most rigid sense of family boundary, didn't say it outright. He sent Tila to enforce rest time and hoped proximity would create the distance he couldn't bring himself to name.

It didn't.

---

By evening on Day 4, Leni knew two things about the custodians' quarters: the radio was in there, and the families weren't supposed to linger outside the door. Nobody had actually said they couldn't listen. That was different. She'd figured that out on Day 2.

Now heads were turning toward the custodians' door every time it opened. Whatever was coming over the radio, it wasn't good.

The evening gathering was the custodians' final scheduled event. After the dinner meal, the families collected in the main hall for what Fasa called "reflection" and what Leni called sitting around while adults talked about nothing useful. Oil lamps threw their warm unsteady light across the stone walls. Moths the size of Leni's thumb circled the flames, casting shadows that pulsed and shrank. The evening air was heavy, saturated with the smell of rain and wet earth and the woodsmoke that permeated everything — hair, clothes, skin. By the fourth day, everyone at the shrine smelled the same. Like the shrine.

Leni was bracing for another round of adults talking in circles when Peyam Mera produced a deck of cards.

Leni didn't know where the cards came from. They were old, soft-cornered, played with for decades until the faces were blurred and the suits were guesswork. Peyam had been one of the quietest people at the shrine since they arrived — quieter even than Jin, and Jin had turned silence into a vocation. Peyam was small and deliberate in his movements, with his carefully trimmed white beard and his thread bracelet and his genuine absorption in Olet's prayers. His lips moved at the edge of everything else he did, so steady Leni sometimes stopped noticing them.

But he was also, it turned out, a card player.

"You play?" he said to Mava Kondo. He threw it across the room at full courtyard volume, and Mava looked up from the corner where she'd been sitting with her knees under a blanket and her left hand rubbing her ear in that slow circle Leni had already come to associate with her.

"I play," Mava said. Seventy-three, white-haired, frail enough that the wind through the open side of the hall seemed to push her around. But her eyes, when she said it, were sharp as glass.

They sat cross-legged on Peyam's mat, the cards between them, and Peyam dealt. The game was one Leni didn't recognize — a regional variation, something with trumps and tricks and a complicated bidding system that involved tapping the mat with different knuckles.

The first hand went quickly. Mava won. Peyam studied his remaining cards and said nothing.

The second hand went slower. Peyam bid high. Mava matched him. When the tricks were played out, Peyam turned over his final card with a small flourish and smiled — the first smile Leni had seen from him that wasn't directed at god.

Then Mava turned over hers.

"That's not a legal play," Peyam said.

"It is if you're playing hill rules."

"There are no hill rules."

"There are hill rules. My mother taught me, and her mother taught her, and I've been playing hill rules since before your beard came in."

"You can't trump a trump with a lesser suit. It's not done."

"It's done in the hills. You're playing lowland rules, and lowland rules are for people who don't know how to play."

Peyam straightened. His beard bristled. It was, Leni realized, the most animated he'd been since arriving at the shrine — more alive in this argument about card rules than he'd been about the flood, the naming, or the sleeping arrangements. He pulled the disputed card from Mava's hand, examined it, held it to the lamplight as if checking for forgery, and set it down with a firmness that suggested he was also setting down a point of principle.

"This card," he said, "is a seven."

"I know what it is."

"A seven doesn't beat a twelve."

"It does if the seven's suited and the twelve is blind."

"There's no such thing as a blind twelve."

"Then what do you call a twelve played without a bid?"

"I call it a twelve."

"And that's why you lose."

A laugh escaped from somewhere — Sem Dao, who was watching from behind Hala's shoulder. Then Rimi Mera giggled, and Bram's low chuckle followed, and suddenly the room was paying attention, and the argument between two people with a combined age of a hundred and forty-five had become the first thing at the shrine that everyone was watching because they wanted to, not because Olet told them to.

Peyam reshuffled. "We play again," he said. "Properly."

"Properly means hill rules."

"Properly means rules. One set. Agreed upon before the first card is dealt."

Mava pulled the blanket tighter around her shoulders. "Fine. But I deal."

"Why?"

"Because you shuffle like a man who learned from a book."

The laughter was real this time — wide and sudden, out before anyone could remember to be polite. Venn snorted from his corner. Deshi covered her mouth but her shoulders shook. Hala's face split into a grin that transformed her entirely — for a moment she looked like someone who wasn't keeping watch over anything, who wasn't positioned between her sister and the rest of the world. Even Bett Dao's guarded face shifted, a small upward pull at the corner of her mouth, there and gone. Jin leaned against the wall and something in his shoulders loosened — not a laugh, not quite, but the absence of the tension he carried like a second skin.

They played four more hands. Mava dealt. The rules were negotiated card by card, with arguments that kept circling back to fundamental disagreements about what constituted a legal play, and whether the hill rules were real rules or an elaborate justification for cheating, and whether Peyam's lowland rules were any better given that he was losing by three hands and his beard was doing the bristling thing again.

Gitta Tesh watched from her place near the Tesh family's mat, small and still, her hands folded in her lap. When Mava won the seventh hand with a play Peyam couldn't even identify, Gitta's mouth curved into something private and almost fond — the expression of a woman who had once played cards with people she no longer saw.

Leni found herself smiling. She was still smiling when she noticed Fen Tesh watching the game from across the hall, his dark hair in his eyes, his mouth doing the same thing hers was doing — something that wanted to be laughter but had been out of practice. Their eyes met for a half-second. They both looked away.

For ten minutes nobody thought about the flood.

Then Olet spoke.

"The news today," he said, and the leftover laughter snapped off. People straightened. Olet stood by the main hall's open side, the valley behind him invisible in the dark but audible — that steady rush of water that never stopped. The oil lamp nearest him lit the lower half of his face and left his eyes in shadow.

"The radio received a broadcast from the eastern station this afternoon. Checkpoints have been established on the main highway east of the pass. The lowland curfew has been extended from dusk to dawn. There are reports of fires and violence in the lowlands, including the capital district."

Bram leaned forward. "What kind of violence?"

"The broadcast did not specify."

"Are they sending road crews? Is anyone clearing the route?"

"There was no mention of road crews."

The silence that followed had weight. Leni could feel it pressing against the walls, against the high stone ceiling, against her own ribs. She looked at her father. Bram's jaw was tight, and his hands, which were usually in motion, had gone still on his knees. Nessa had baby Ada pressed against her shoulder, and her other hand was spread across Tomas's back, fingers splayed as if she were checking he was still there.

Venn spoke from his corner. "How long until the road is passable?"

"The water has not receded," Olet said.

"It's been four days."

"The water has not receded."

"Seasonal flooding on this road takes four to five days to drain. I've traveled this route before."

"Under normal conditions, yes. The conditions are not normal. The upstream infrastructure has been damaged. The drainage systems are not functioning."

Venn's voice hardened. "So what are you telling us?"

"I am telling you what the radio told us. The checkpoints, the curfew, the reports of violence. The water has not receded. That is what we know."

Leni looked around the room. The laughter from the card game had burned off like mist, as if it had never happened. Suna sat with Asha in her lap, one hand resting flat on the child's shin, her whole body held too still. Leni had started to recognize that stillness as the shape Suna took when she was managing a disaster she couldn't talk about. Hala had pulled Raya and Sem closer to her, a physical gathering that was also a reflex, the body's response to threat before the mind has named it. Cade Leo put his arm around Pik's shoulder. Wynn didn't move, but his hand found Cade's knee and stayed there.

Bram spoke again, and his voice was the careful voice he used when he was constructing an argument and needed every piece to land. "The political situation. The identity checks, the movement permits. That's what's keeping the road crews away. Not just the water."

"That is a reasonable interpretation," Olet said.

"So even if the water dropped tomorrow —"

"Even if the water dropped tomorrow, the road leads to a checkpoint. Whether you would be permitted to pass is not something we can know from here."

Bram sat back. He looked at Nessa. Their eyes met and held, and Leni saw something pass between her parents that she was not supposed to see. Nessa's mouth tightened. Bram gave the smallest shake of his head. It was a whole conversation anyway.

The gathering broke up slowly after that. Families retreated to their sleeping areas with the heavy quiet of people who have heard bad news and haven't figured out what to do about it. Peyam folded his cards and tucked them inside his vest with the tenderness of a man putting away something precious. Mava pulled her blanket around her shoulders and let Tila guide her back to the Kondo corner, her frail body listing slightly, the sharpness from the card game already draining from her face. The moth shadows danced on the walls as lamps were carried to sleeping quarters, each flame pulling its bubble of warm light along the stone corridors.

Leni lay on her mat in the dark. Tomas was already asleep beside her — he was ten, he could sleep through anything, his body still trusted the adults to handle whatever was happening. Ada was asleep on Nessa's chest, milk-drunk and limp. Bram and Nessa were lying down but not sleeping. Leni could hear them whispering.

She couldn't hear the words. She could hear the register — low, tight, whispering that wasn't a choice but a necessity, two people being afraid in the smallest possible voice. She caught fragments. "Checkpoints." "Papers." Her mother said something that ended on a rising sound — a question, or the word *how*, or a name Leni couldn't place. Bram whispered back. More fragments. "...morning..." and "...doesn't matter what we..." and then Nessa's hand found Bram's in the lamplight from the corridor — Leni saw it, her father's long fingers closing around her mother's smaller hand, and the grip was not comfort. It was ballast. Two people holding each other because the ground was moving and there was nothing else to hold.

Leni turned away from them. She reached into her bag and pulled out her journal and the pen she'd brought from home, from the school desk in her bedroom, a place that already felt stupidly far away. The pen was a cheap ballpoint, blue ink, almost out.

She wrote by the corridor's faint lamplight, her handwriting cramped and leaning hard to the right, the same ugly slant it got whenever she wrote in the dark or in a hurry or both. She wrote about the day. The cooking rotation and Deshi's salt commentary. The children's game that made no sense and didn't need to. Ori kissing Asha's head. Fen scraping moss off the wall with a stick. Her mother's freckles darkening in the humidity. Peyam and Mava arguing about hill rules while the flood erased the road below them and neither of them cared, because for ten minutes the game was the only real thing.

She wrote: *Nobody's coming to get us. Dad knows. Mom knows. They're just not saying it out loud because once you say it out loud you have to do something about it and there's nothing to do.*

She wrote: *Tomorrow the bell's going to ring again and we're going to get up and carry water and peel onions and the kids are going to play like nothing's wrong because nothing is wrong for them. They don't know the road goes to a checkpoint. They don't know what checkpoints mean.*

She didn't write: *I'm scared.* She didn't need to. The handwriting said it — the hard lean of the letters, the pressure of the pen against the page, the words driving hard and then cutting off.

She closed the journal and put it under her pillow and lay there listening. The flood. The rain starting up again, soft at first and then heavier, drumming on the stone overhead. Ada's small sleeping breaths. A man coughing in the Kondo section. The insects, that constant monsoon hum from the wet vegetation around the shrine, a sound so persistent you stopped hearing it until the night got quiet enough to bring it back.

There were forty-three of them on the hilltop, four days in, with no road, no phone, and nobody coming for them. Morning would come the way it did here now: people stirring before dawn, the bell sooner or later, everyone getting up to carry water, peel onions, clean, eat. By noon the courtyard would be full of kids crossing from one family to another with dirty knees and wet cuffs, like nobody had told them where the borders were supposed to be. The adults would watch and decide, depending on the minute, whether that felt like comfort or trouble.

Leni bit her thumbnail in the dark. She could taste stone dust and onion and the faint metallic residue of the pen she'd been chewing without noticing.

Day 5 would look like that. Then Day 6. Then however many days until the water went down or the checkpoints opened or someone, somewhere, figured out they were up here.

She didn't think anyone was figuring out they were up here.
