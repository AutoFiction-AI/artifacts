# Chapter 8

Suna had been watching Tomas Okoye for two days before she understood what she was watching.

It started the morning after the families agreed to keep sitting for the naming, when the work bell pulled them back into the circle. Not agreed — submitted. Bram Okoye had argued his case with a teacher's precision and it hadn't mattered, because the custodians held the food stores and the Kondos had twelve mouths and the calculation was simple and humiliating: you eat, therefore you comply. The compound's mood since then had been the mood of people performing cooperation that fooled no one, and against that backdrop Suna noticed Tomas because noticing things was how she managed, and managing was how she stayed upright.

He was ten, slim, with his mother's round face and his father's long fingers — hands too big for him still, palms and knuckles arriving years ahead of the rest of his body. He played with the younger children more than most ten-year-olds would tolerate, carrying baby Ada on his hip when Nessa needed both hands, showing Kael how to stack pebbles without using the flat ones as a base. His patience with smaller children had no shine to it. He'd already learned how often a baby woke, how many small hands tugged at you in a day, how many times you had to answer the same request without snapping.

The hesitation appeared at the morning meal. Or the morning before. She couldn't pin it, which bothered her, because Suna knew days by what they demanded of her: whether Asha refused lentils, whether Kael drained his cup and came back asking for more water, whether Rimi climbed off her mat rubbing the shoulder she'd slept on wrong. She knew the slap of the cistern bucket against stone, the look of a bread stack that would not make it around the circle twice, the adults who showed up sweaty from labor and the ones who arrived clean with reasons. She knew when a child had skinned both knees and when somebody else's baby had a cough starting. So the fact that she could not place the first catch in Tomas's mouth felt like dropping a bowl and never hearing it break.

So she couldn't say exactly when Tomas started catching on his consonants.

He was asking Kael to pass the water cup, and the word caught behind his teeth. Not a stutter — Suna had grown up with a cousin who stammered on vowels, and she knew that sound; a stammer pulled the whole face into the labor of speaking. This was different. Tomas's jaw locked briefly before the hard consonant, the muscle along his cheek tightening for less than a second, and then the word came out all at once, late and slightly stretched. "Cup," he said at last. A catch and release. A small mechanical failure in a mouth that had been working fine for ten years.

She knew the mechanism. She had been listening to it for ten days from the mouth of Jin Parra.

Jin's hesitation was subtle enough that most people seemed not to register it, or else they folded it into the general impression of a man who spoke as little as possible. Suna had noticed on the second day. She tracked speech with the same restless attention she gave a person's grip on a knife, their hands before communal bread, the small bodily facts that told her how close trouble was. Jin caught on certain consonants, the hard ones, the plosives, the sounds that required the jaw to close and open with force. C, k, p, t. His jaw would lock for a fraction of a second, the muscle along his cheek visible beneath the skin, and then the word would emerge slightly stretched. The first sound always seemed to come through a narrowed gap.

Tomas was catching on the same hard sounds, the lock flickering in the same place along his jaw.

Suna set down the flatbread she'd been pulling apart for Asha and watched. Tomas was talking to Melie now — Jin's daughter, six, who had attached herself to the older boy with the uncritical loyalty of a child who recognized someone kind. He was explaining a rule of the pebble game, and the word *put* sat shut behind his teeth the same way. The catch. The delay. The jaw unlocking like a hinge that had briefly rusted.

Across the morning circle, Nessa Okoye sat with Ada in her lap, bouncing the baby absently on one knee. She was watching her son. Nessa's face had no defense — never had, as far as Suna could tell. Fear, hope, confusion, tenderness — everything moved across her features without permission, which made her easy to read and hard to look at, because what Suna saw in her face right now was recognition, quick and consonant by consonant.

Their eyes met. Suna looked away first, but not before she'd registered the question in Nessa's expression: *You see it too?*

She saw it. Asha had already tied the Meras to the Kondos. Melie's throat had already tied the Parras to the Daos. Now Tomas's jaw had pulled the Okoyes onto the same map, and the count in Suna's head jumped to five families before she could stop it. The number didn't feel clarifying. It felt like the floor losing another board.

Suna finished pulling apart the bread and handed the pieces to Asha, who received them with both hands and ate with her mouth open, watching her mother with the unreadable calm of a four-year-old who did not know that her own body had already become evidence.

---

That afternoon the rain came in hard and the children compressed into the main hall. Twenty-one of them, ranging from Ada's one year to Fen Tesh's sixteen, occupying the stone floor in a shifting mass of noise and negotiation while the adults arranged themselves along the walls and tried not to contribute to the volume. Deshi had positioned herself near the cold cooking fire — an act of territorial habit that no longer drew comment from anyone except Rimi, who rolled her eyes with the practiced contempt of a twelve-year-old who had decided that adults were a failing species.

Suna stationed herself where she could see the children's clump without sitting in it. The older ones — Leni, Fen, Raya Dao — maintained their adolescent separateness near the walls, performing the studied indifference of teenagers trapped with younger children they considered beneath their dignity. The younger ones pooled at the center in a shifting economy of games and grievances. Tomas was in the middle, as usual, mediating a dispute between Kael and Sem Dao over a set of smooth stones that had become, through the alchemy of confinement, more valuable than anything the adults could offer.

She watched Tomas talk. Three hesitations in ten minutes, all on hard consonants. Each one stalled the same way: a visible seize at the jaw hinge, then the sentence came late, the first sound stretched thin. "Take it back. I already put it there. Come on, Sem." The children around him didn't seem to notice. A man limped, a woman hummed, a boy started catching on his k's. At eight or six, the world simply admitted such things.

Jin Parra was sitting against the far wall with his knees drawn up, Melie leaning against his side, her thumb in her mouth. He was watching the children with the specific vigilance of a man whose daughter's body had already done something impossible — whose daughter's throat had swelled shut from a food she'd eaten her whole life, claiming an allergy that belonged to another family. His jaw worked slightly as he watched, the unconscious motion of a habit he'd carried since adolescence.

Across the room, Tomas's jaw had started answering to it.

Suna looked at Jin's jaw, then at Tomas's jaw, then at Nessa. Nessa was nursing Ada in the corner with a cloth draped over her shoulder, her free hand resting on her thigh. Her fingers were opening and closing in a slow rhythm that had nothing to do with the baby and everything to do with the boy across the room whose mouth was becoming someone else's.

---

The next morning was the first without rain since the fourth day. The clouds didn't clear — they thinned, turning the light from gray to a diffuse white that made the courtyard stones steam as the moisture lifted. The children spilled outside with the urgency of animals released from a hold, and even Deshi stood at the edge of the covered walkway and lifted her face to the warm air with an expression that, on anyone else, would have been called relief.

Suna was wringing laundry at the cistern. The work was necessary and constant — forty-three people produced a volume of soiled fabric that had become the compound's most tangible communal problem, a problem she addressed with the same methodical attention she brought to everything else because the alternative was to address nothing, to let the vertigo underneath the method have its way. She wrung a shirt of Kael's until her knuckles whitened against the wet fabric, draped it over the stone lip, picked up the next piece.

Kiri Tesh was sitting on the courtyard steps about ten meters away, her legs stretched in front of her, sorting seed pods she'd collected from the edge of the terraced garden. She was a wiry child, all elbows and sharp collarbones, with a habit of biting the inside of her cheek when she concentrated that made her look older than eight. She had her father Miro's dark hair, but she carried herself with a physical self-possession that belonged to neither parent — Miro was cautious in movement, Enn nervous, and Kiri was neither. She arranged her seed pods on the stone step with the focused determination of a scientist organizing specimens, dividing them by size into precise rows.

She was humming.

Suna didn't register the melody at first. It entered as background, the hum of a child busy with a task, part of the compound's ambient soundtrack where someone was always making sound — coughing, arguing, praying, scraping pots. Then the melody turned at a specific interval. A minor third dropping to a major second in a pattern that was not common, not universal, not the kind of thing a child would pick up from exposure to ordinary songs.

Suna gave Kael's shirt one more twist before the turn in the melody reached her. The seam bit into her palm. She let go so abruptly the wet cloth slapped the stone lip and slid back against her wrist, cold and heavy. Water ran off her elbows and darkened the front of her skirt.

The melody was her mother's lullaby.

The one with the descending line that reversed itself in the third phrase and looped back to its own beginning instead of resolving. The one her mother had sung in the old dialect from a mountain valley four hundred kilometers south, where the roads were bad and the villages had been emptying for thirty years. It was built on intervals that leaned away from the scale most people learned. Suna's mother used to say the tune was older than the words.

Her mother had died when Suna was nineteen. The lullaby went with her. It belonged to that voice, those arms, the darkness of her mother's bedroom; anywhere else it curdled into imitation. Suna had never sung it for her own children, not here, not anywhere. She hadn't even thought of it in months, maybe years. Hearing it now from the mouth of an eight-year-old Tesh girl carried the flat impossibility of meeting a dead woman in the road.

Kiri hummed through the third phrase, took the turn cleanly, and kept going. Her pitch wavered only at the edges. The intervals held. So did the rhythm, including the suspended note before the loop, where Suna's mother used to let the breath sit for a beat and then begin again.

Suna braced both hands on the cistern edge and watched an eight-year-old girl hum her dead mother's song while sorting seed pods on the steps.

She should go over. She should ask Kiri where she'd heard the melody. She should go carefully, looking for the ordinary explanation before admitting the extraordinary. Someone in the compound might know the song. She might have hummed it in her sleep. The melody might be more common than she thought, a variant that had traveled farther than she knew.

She didn't move.

She stood at the cistern and listened, and the melody looped twice more before Kiri lost interest and started talking to one of the younger Kondo children who'd wandered over, and the song stopped as easily as it had started. Kiri's mouth moved on. The melody dropped dead in the air, stripped of history and weight for everyone but Suna.

The pattern had reached the Teshes too. A lullaby had crossed a dead woman and four hundred kilometers to land in Kiri's mouth.

Suna picked up the next piece of laundry. Her hands were trembling in a way that had nothing to do with cold water and everything to do with the specific frequency of the third phrase, which was still in her ears like a tone that continued after the source had stopped. She wrung the fabric until the tremor was absorbed into the work, and when Deshi called from across the courtyard to ask whether she planned to wash everything or just the things that mattered, she answered with a steadiness that cost her something she could feel but couldn't name.

"All of it," she said. "I'm doing all of it."

Deshi looked at her for a beat longer than necessary, and Suna understood that her mother-in-law's eye missed very little, and that the effort of seeming fine was itself a kind of signal to anyone watching closely enough.

She did not ask Kiri where she'd heard it. Later, when Enn crossed the sleeping quarters with a stack of blankets against her hip and Kiri trailed behind biting the inside of her cheek, Suna let them pass. Asking would give the song witnesses. Once it belonged to the compound, it would belong to the shrine.

---

That evening, after the children were settled and the compound had reached the uneasy quiet that passed for nighttime peace, Suna found Nessa at the edge of the prayer courtyard.

Nessa was standing with Ada asleep against her chest, the baby's weight pulling her slightly forward. She had wrapped Ada in a length of faded blue-gray cloth — her own shawl, repurposed, the fringe knotted where it had begun to unravel — and in the oil-lamp light her outline blurred into the stone behind her. She held Ada as armor, the baby positioned between herself and whatever was coming. The placement was irrational and absolute.

Suna had never spent time alone with Nessa. Their interactions had been practical — the choreography of women who cooked in the same kitchen and washed at the same cistern and negotiated sleeping arrangements for children who increasingly refused to sleep where they were told. They weren't unfriendly. They simply hadn't needed each other yet.

"Can I sit with you?" Suna asked.

Nessa shifted sideways on the low courtyard wall. "Please."

Suna sat. The stone was still warm from the afternoon's thin sun, and the warmth seeped through her skirt into her thighs. The courtyard smelled of the wet herbs from the terraced garden, a green mineral scent that the rain drew out of the soil and that clung to everything for hours after the water stopped.

They sat in silence for a while. Ada made small sounds in her sleep — the grunting, liquid noises of a baby whose breathing was a performance even unconscious. Moths circled the nearest oil lamp, their shadows huge and flickering against the courtyard stones.

"Tomas," Suna said.

Nessa's hand moved to Ada's back. "You've noticed."

"I've noticed."

"Since two days ago. Maybe three." Nessa looked down at the stones between her feet. "He's never spoken that way in his life."

"It's Jin's pattern."

"I know." Nessa touched two fingers to the hinge of her jaw. "I've been watching Jin, and so have you. Bram says imitation. Or coincidence. Or whatever else lets him finish a meal. Then he lies next to me at night and doesn't believe any of it."

"There's another one," Suna said. "Another manifestation. That's why I came to you."

Nessa's hand stilled on Ada's back. "Who?"

"Kiri Tesh. She was humming a song this morning on the courtyard steps. A lullaby."

"Lots of children hum."

"My mother's lullaby. A song from my mother's village, in a dialect nobody up here speaks. My mother's been dead nineteen years. I haven't sung it since."

The silence that followed pressed. Nessa pulled Ada fractionally closer, and the baby grunted in protest, and Nessa loosened her hold but didn't change the position of her arms.

"How can you be sure it's the same song?" she asked. "Songs travel."

"It doesn't sit in the normal scale," Suna said. "The melody never resolves. It loops back instead of ending. My mother used to say that was the point — a lullaby shouldn't stop, because sleep should feel like going around again, not arriving somewhere. Nobody writes songs like that. It was hers."

"Could you have hummed it without knowing? In your sleep, or while you were working?"

Suna sat with the question. Outside the courtyard, the flood's sound carried up from the valley, that constant rush that had become so woven into the compound's atmosphere that it registered only during strange silences, and then with the force of something that had been listening the whole time.

"I don't think so," she said. "But I can't be certain. Which is almost worse than knowing."

"That makes six," Nessa said quietly. "If you're counting."

Suna was. The count didn't steady her anymore. It only told her how little blank space was left.

"The Leos are quiet. Cade hardly talks to anyone who isn't Wynn or Vera."

"Have you noticed anything with Pik?"

Nessa shook her head. "Give it time." The words landed with the dead flatness of someone naming weather already on its way.

"Nessa."

"What?"

Suna let the sentence sit for a beat before she gave it weight. "When I watched you this morning — watching Tomas — you looked like you'd been waiting."

Nessa's face did what it always did, which was everything. The oil lamp caught it all — the tightening around her eyes, her mouth pressing shut and then opening again under the strain of holding the words back.

"I've been waiting since Melie's throat swelled shut," she said. "Since the allergy. Because when I watched Bett lean over her — knowing exactly what to do, knowing it was the Dao allergy before anyone said it — I thought: it's going to find us too. It's going to reach one of mine."

"And it has."

"And it has." She adjusted Ada's weight, resettling the baby higher against her chest. Ada's face was mashed against Nessa's collarbone, one fist curled against the faded cloth. "But this isn't — what's happening to Tomas isn't the first time something's been taken from me."

Suna waited. Something was already moving toward them. The pause had pressure in it, the next words already taking up space before Nessa said them.

"We had another child," Nessa said. "Before Tomas. A boy. His name was Efe."

The name entered the air between them and stayed.

"He died at three months. A fever. It came fast — by morning he was hot, by afternoon he wouldn't nurse, and by evening his breathing had changed. Bram drove for two hours to find a clinic that was open, and it didn't matter. By the time they got a line in, he was—" She broke off. The sentence hit something inside her and stopped. She breathed. Started again from a different place. "We buried him in the yard behind our house. Bram dug the hole himself because he said he wouldn't let someone else do it. The hole was very small. That's what I remember most. How small the hole was."

Suna didn't touch her. The impulse was there — hand on knee, hand on arm, the practical touch she used with her children when words weren't enough — but something in Nessa's stillness told her that touch right now would crack the structure that was holding the words in their sequence, and Nessa needed the sequence to finish.

"We don't talk about him," Nessa said. "We — Bram and I decided, after. That we wouldn't carry it forward. That Tomas and Leni would grow up without the weight of a dead brother they'd never met. We thought that was the right thing." Her voice was steady and her eyes were wet and the two facts weren't in contradiction — they were the specific product of a grief that had been managed for so long it had developed its own architecture, its own load-bearing walls. "I don't know anymore if it was the right thing."

"Is that why you've been waiting?" Suna asked. "Because if the children are manifesting other families' losses—"

"What's the loss?" Nessa finished. "What's the gap? Melie's allergy connects to the Daos, and the Daos lost a son. Asha connects to the Kondos, and Mava lost a sister. So when Tomas starts speaking like Jin — what's missing? Whose absence?" She looked at Suna with the directness of someone who had decided to be fully present rather than protected. "Ours. Maybe. Or maybe Jin's — his wife."

"His wife died," Suna said, and as she said it she heard the echo of her own constructed story — *my husband went ahead* — and recognized, not for the first time, how a lie took on the texture of fact when nobody pushed against it.

"His wife died," Nessa repeated, and her tone carried either doubt or the sharpened attention of a woman who'd learned to listen past declarations.

The compound sounds filtered through the stillness around them — someone coughing in the sleeping quarters, the creak of the kitchen overhang's timber frame, the low murmur of the custodians' radio bleeding through their closed door, an indistinct voice reporting news from somewhere that wasn't here.

"I can't tell what this is," Suna said. "I don't know if it's the shrine, or the custodians, or something the children are doing to each other without knowing it, or something that would have happened anywhere if you put this many families in a small enough space for long enough. I don't have an explanation."

"Bram cycles through his four explanations like a man shuffling cards. He deals himself a new hand every morning." For a second Nessa's mouth twitched with the old habit of making a joke because the alternative was worse. "I don't have four explanations. I've got zero. I've just got my son's jaw doing something it shouldn't be able to do."

Suna reached across the space between them and put her hand on Nessa's knee, the same steady pressure she used on her children when language stopped helping. Nessa's hand came down and covered hers, and they stayed like that for a while, two women on a warm stone wall with a sleeping baby between them and the flood's sound rising from the valley below like a fact that wouldn't be argued with.

"Efe would've been eleven this year," Nessa said. "Almost the same age as Tomas. They would've fought constantly — brothers do. Bram would've had to mediate, because I cave in about three seconds. Leni would've bossed them both." She was smiling now, a small smile that had nothing behind it but loss and the particular tenderness of imagining a life that hadn't happened. "Instead, Tomas is a patient older brother to a baby sister, and he's the kindest boy I know, and now he talks like a stranger he met ten days ago."

"Do you think we should tell the custodians?" Suna asked.

"About Tomas? They'll find out. They see everything."

"About Efe."

The name again. Suna felt its weight differently the second time. Surprise was gone. The word had mass now.

Nessa was quiet for several breaths. "About the gap, you mean. About what might be pulling Tomas toward Jin's pattern."

"Yes."

"I don't know. I don't know what they'd do with it." She turned Suna's question back. "Would you tell them about the lullaby?"

Suna thought about Fasa's steady gaze during the namings, the custodian's habit of holding silence until other people filled it. She thought about Olet's flat calm, which was either faith or indifference, and which unsettled her precisely because she couldn't tell which.

"Not yet," she said. "Not until I understand what they want from us."

"They want the map," Nessa said. "Fasa keeps saying it — the true kinship map. Like we're hiding it instead of trying to figure out what the hell is happening to our kids."

Nessa stood, lifting Ada higher against her chest, the baby's weight resettling into the familiar position. "I should go in. Bram's probably lying in the dark composing his fifth explanation."

Suna let the joke pass. She stayed on the wall after Nessa went inside, listening to the flood, feeling the stone cool beneath her as the last of the day's heat bled away. Six families. One left. The number no longer looked like order. It looked like approach.

Tomorrow, if Fasa asked again for the true kinship map, Suna would say nothing about the lullaby and nothing about Efe. Not yet. Let the custodians pull what they could from children's mouths and mothers' fear. She would watch the Leos first. She would see whether the pattern reached Pik before she handed the shrine another dead child to fit into its shape.

The melody came back to her, uninvited. Her mother's voice sometimes surfaced between waking and sleep with that same blunt force, not as memory but as presence. The song did not stay where the dead were supposed to stay. It returned whole, looking for a mouth to carry it forward. Kiri's mouth. An eight-year-old in a different family from a different region who sorted seed pods by size and bit the inside of her cheek when she concentrated and hummed songs she had no reason to know.

Suna pressed her lips together and didn't hum.

She sat with the silence and the flood and the cooling stone, feeling Nessa's dead son settle beside the older ache of Sev's absence and the daily fright of Asha's borrowed gestures. She kept her hands flat on the wall until grit marked her palms. If she let those three things hit at once, she would fold in half right there in the courtyard. Not yet. Not here. Not with three children sleeping in the next room and a mother-in-law whose sharp eye missed nothing and another day of shrine work and naming ahead of her tomorrow and a lullaby that had crossed nineteen years and four hundred kilometers to arrive in the throat of a stranger's child.

The oil lamps in the main hall threw soft light across the courtyard. Inside, someone coughed. Ada cried once — sharp, sudden, a single note — and then stopped. The flood went on. By morning the work bell would drag them back to damp coals and assigned places, and she'd carry the lullaby there without saying a word.
