# Chapter 18

The bell came at the usual hour, the same flat bronze note that had opened every naming since they arrived, but the light was what Suna noticed first: the yellow-white band coming through the main hall's open side and striking the stone floor in a way none of them had seen in three weeks. The sun was up. It had been up yesterday too, and the day before, since the rain stopped on the evening she and Jin sat in the courtyard and admitted they'd been telling versions of the same lie. But the light still startled her. It fell across the hall at a steep morning angle, and where it hit the stone it showed every scuff and water-stain of their occupation — the path worn between the entrance and the sleeping quarters, the grey smudge where the cooking fire's smoke had drifted in, the faint greasy mark near the wall where Peyam sat and ate his meals. Three weeks of living pressed into the floor.

She had Asha on her hip. The child was heavy enough now that she'd need to be put down before long, but Suna wanted the weight of her — the solid pull of a four-year-old's body against her side, the warmth of Asha's forehead against her neck. Asha's left hand was resting on Suna's collarbone, the fingers still, not rubbing her ear for once. Beside them Kael stood with his arms crossed in an imitation of Bram Okoye that he probably didn't know he was doing. Rimi was behind Deshi, close enough that her shoulder nearly touched the old woman's back. Peyam had taken his usual place near the wall, prayer beads still in his hand from the morning's devotion, his pipe tucked into his belt though there was nowhere to smoke it in the hall.

The families filed in. Suna counted them as automatically as she counted her own children, a reflex she had developed in the first days when she needed to know how many people stood between her and the entrance. The Okoyes came in together, Bram with his glasses pushed up on his forehead, Nessa carrying Ada against her chest, Tomas walking slightly apart from Leni with the sour distance of siblings who have recently argued. The Teshes: Miro carrying a folded blanket under his arm as though he expected to need it, Enn beside him, Kiri trailing behind, Gitta already seated on the stone bench against the far wall with Fen standing at her shoulder. Gitta wore the same grey shawl she had worn every day, pulled tight across her chest despite the warmth. Her hands were still.

The Daos entered from the kitchen side — Hala first, her posture protective as always, angled slightly so that her body screened Bett behind her. Kal walked with Sem, the old man's hand on the boy's shoulder in a way that was more about his knees than about affection, though maybe it was both. Raya had Nori by the hand. Bett came last, her face showing nothing.

The Leos were already seated. Cade and Wynn on the bench near the entrance, Pik between them, Vera on Cade's other side with her knitting in her lap — she'd been working on the same grey rectangle for days, something too small for a blanket and too large for a scarf, and Suna had never asked what it was. Pik was sitting on his hands, his usual tactic when the prayer gesture came on and he was trying to be still.

The Kondos were the last to enter, and they entered as they did everything — as a group, with Venn at the center and Tila half a step behind him. There were twelve of them. Mava walked with Habel, Ori close to her mother's side. Arash was at the edge of the group, not outside it exactly, but positioned like a person near a doorway he is thinking about walking through. He wore the same shirt he'd worn for three days, and the collar was frayed on one side where he'd been pulling at it. Suna had noticed this two mornings ago and hadn't thought about it since, and now she noticed it again.

Fasa stood at the hall's center, not behind the stone ledge where she usually conducted the naming but out from behind it, level with the families, on the same floor. Olet was near the open side, leaning against the stone railing, the valley behind him. From where Suna stood she could see past his shoulder to the view below: the switchback road emerging from the flood's brown margin, the lower bends still glazed with mud but visible, solid ground where for three weeks there had been only water. The waterline had dropped overnight. She could see the stone retaining wall at the third switchback, and clumps of grass and debris caught against it like a tideline.

"This is the last naming," Fasa said.

Her voice was different — not the ritual cadence, not the measured pauses of a woman who spoke liturgy daily, but plainer, as if she had set something down.

"Each family will declare. You know what the naming asks." She paused, and the pause was not ceremonial. "It asks for what is true."

The hall was quiet. Forty-three people and five custodians, and the only sound was Ada's soft breathing against Nessa's chest and the distant, diminished rush of the water below — quieter than it had been, retreating.

Deshi was behind her. Suna knew it without turning. The skin between her shoulder blades had tightened the same way it had tightened last night when, after the children slept and Peyam's snoring thickened in the dark, she told Deshi: I'm going to tell the truth tomorrow. About Sev. Deshi had smoothed the edge of her sleeping mat with her palm, once, twice, and asked nothing. She had given Suna no blessing, no objection, no rescue. The silence had stayed with Suna until morning.

Suna stepped forward.

She shifted Asha to her other hip. The child's hand came up to Suna's collar, fingers finding the seam again and worrying it as if the stitches themselves might hold.

"We are the Mera family," Suna said. Her voice carried. She had not planned it to carry — she had expected to have to push, as she did when she called the children in from the courtyard — but the stone walls did the work for her. "Deshi Mera, my mother-in-law. Peyam Mera, her brother. My children: Rimi, who is twelve. Kael, who is eight. Asha, who is four."

She paused. The weight of the next sentence was physical, sitting in her chest like a swallowed stone. It was not the words — the words were simple. She had rehearsed them in the dark, whispering to the ceiling while Asha slept against her side. The weight was in the room's reception of them, in Deshi's breathing behind her, in Rimi's face, which Suna could not see because Rimi was behind Deshi and Suna had placed her there deliberately so that she would not have to watch her daughter's expression while she said it.

"Sev Mera. My husband. Deshi's son." She said it to the hall, to the stone floor, to the view of the receding water. "He left us. Three months before we came here. He didn't go ahead. He didn't die. He left."

The silence after was not silence — it was the sound of forty people choosing not to speak, which had its own texture, its own particular quality of held breath and swallowed response. Suna felt the sweat on her palm where it pressed against Asha's back. She felt the stone floor through the soles of her shoes, warmed now by the sun that was still moving its band of light across the hall.

Behind her, Deshi closed her eyes.

Suna saw it only because she turned — she had to turn, she could not stand there facing the room and not know what was happening behind her. And Deshi's eyes were closed, and her mouth was set in the line that Suna had learned to read over thirteen years of marriage: not anger, not grief, not the sharp blade of judgment, just the closing of a door she had been holding open for three months, the acknowledgment that the thing on the other side of it was real.

Peyam's beads had stopped moving. He looked at Suna, and in his face — his old, lined, patient face with the pipe-smoke creases around his mouth — there was something she did not expect, not surprise and not sympathy but something closer to the expression of a man who has been watching a load carried poorly and is relieved to see it finally set down right.

Suna sat. She put Asha on the stone floor beside her, and Asha immediately crawled into Kael's lap, and Kael held her with the automatic competence of an eight-year-old brother who had been doing this since she was born. Rimi had not moved from behind Deshi. Suna did not turn to look at her again.

---

Jin stood before Fasa looked his way. Melie rose with him, her fist closing around his hand. He had told Melie last night. Suna knew because after her conversation with Deshi he had found her in the courtyard and said only: I told her. Lamplight had left his face scraped raw. Even now his free hand stayed half-curled, as if Melie's grip were still in it.

"Jin Parra," he said. The jaw caught on the first consonant, the slight lock and release that Suna had watched Tomas Okoye develop over the past two weeks, identical, borrowed, impossible. "My daughter. Melie. She's six."

He stopped. His throat worked once. Across the hall Tomas had gone still, watching Jin's mouth.

"Her mother's alive," Jin said. "She isn't with us. She left."

He sat down so quickly that Melie stumbled beside him. His hand found hers again before she'd settled, and she held on with both of hers, her face turned into his arm, not crying, not looking at anyone, just holding on.

Jin's free hand flattened on the bench beside his leg. The fingers spread hard against the stone, curled, then spread again. Tomas looked down at his own hand and then at his father.

---

Bram stood too quickly, as if he knew what would happen if the room stayed with Jin any longer. He pushed his glasses up with one finger. Nessa remained seated, Ada in her arms, but she had already shifted to the edge of the bench.

"The Okoye family," Bram said. His voice had the teacher's steadiness, the measured pace of a man accustomed to saying important things to people who needed to understand them. "I'm Bram. My wife, Nessa. Our daughter Leni, who is fifteen. Our son Tomas, who is ten. Our daughter Ada, who is one."

He paused. A longer sentence had clearly been waiting behind the first one. Suna saw it gather and die. The room would not take explanation now.

"Before Tomas," he said, "there was Efe." He did not look at Nessa. He looked at the room with the hard attention of a man who is asking to be heard, not pitied. "He was our son. He lived for three months. He died."

Nessa's voice came from behind him, quiet but distinct — the voice of a woman who had lived with a name inside her for more than ten years and was now saying it where other people could hear.

"Efe," she said — just the name, once — and then she pressed her mouth against Ada's hair and went quiet.

Tomas stared at his parents as if they had opened a door in the wall behind them. His jaw caught on the first letter of nothing at all. Leni's hand came down on his shoulder, hard enough to steady both of them.

---

Gitta rose. Fen was already on his feet by the time she had one hand on the bench. She straightened without taking help, shawl pulled tight, back rigid. Miro stood half a step off her shoulder and stopped there. Enn sat with her hands knotted in her lap, the skin around her mouth drawn pale.

Gitta's voice was slow. Each word arrived at a distance from the last, weighed and released, the cadence of a woman who had spent sixteen years editing every sentence before it left her mouth.

"The Tesh family," she said. "My son, Miro. His wife, Enn. Their daughter Kiri, who is eight." She paused, built the next line in her mouth, and laid it down whole. "My grandson, Fen. He is sixteen."

She drew a breath. Suna could hear it across the hall — the slight whistle of air through old teeth, the effort of a woman who was about to say a thing she had already said days ago but which required the same force each time, as if the truth had not gotten lighter with repetition.

"Fen is not my blood," Gitta said. "Sixteen years ago I came upon a dead family at the roadside. A man. A woman. A child old enough to walk. Under the woman's arm there was an infant still breathing. I took the infant. I named him Fen Tesh. I told my son he belonged to kin. I kept that lie for sixteen years."

She did not sit. She stood in the hall's yellow-white light, small and thin and seventy years old, her hands at her sides. She had not asked for forgiveness when she confessed in the hall days ago, and she did not ask now. The naming asked for the truth. She had given it.

Fen was already standing. He did not look at Miro or Enn. He looked straight ahead, tall beside Gitta, his face blank with effort.

"I am her grandson," Fen said.

It was four words. He sat back down, and his shoulder settled against Gitta's side, and the old woman did not move toward him and did not move away. They sat like that — touching at the shoulder, looking forward, two people who had agreed on what they were to each other and did not need the agreement to be louder than this.

Miro's face tightened until the skin around his eyes shone. He had known for days and still looked struck. He sat with his forearms on his thighs and his hands clasped between his knees. Enn put her hand between his shoulder blades and left it there.

---

Cade stood before the quiet around the Teshes could harden. Wynn rose with him, shoulder to shoulder. Cade drew breath as if he might offer the long version, saw Gitta and Fen still touching at the shoulder, and let the breath out empty. Wynn's hand rested on his forearm.

"Cade Leo," he said. "My partner, Wynn. My mother, Vera." He looked down at Pik, who was sitting between Vera and the bench's stone arm, his legs swinging, his right hand moving in the slow circling pattern — Gitta's prayer gesture, thumbs turning over each other, a motion he had never been taught by anyone in his family. "My son, Pik. He's seven."

He did not say adopted. He did not mention the institution, the paperwork, the records that might or might not have been accurate. All of that stayed outside the hall. He said my son and sat down.

Vera Leo had noticed Pik's hand. She had been watching it all morning, tracking the movement as you track a bird you can't quite identify, with attention that has not yet decided whether to be worried or amazed. Now Vera reached over and took Pik's hand in hers. She didn't press his fingers flat. She didn't still the circling thumbs. She held his hand with the gesture still moving inside her grip — contained but not stopped, like holding a small animal whose heartbeat you can feel against your palm. Pik looked up at her, and Vera looked back, and the grey knitting in her lap slid an inch toward the floor, and she let it go.

---

Hala stood. She was taller than Bett and broader, and she carried herself like a woman who had spent years stepping between her sister and whatever came at her. Kal was behind her, leaning on Sem's shoulder, his face set in its usual weathered squint.

"The Dao family," Hala said. Her voice was clear, matter-of-fact, a voice accustomed to organizing. "I'm Hala. My son, Sem, eleven. My daughter, Raya, thirteen. My father, Kal." She paused. "My sister, Bett. And Nori, who is seven. Nori is Bett's daughter."

Hala did not fully sit. One hand stayed braced on the bench as Bett stood.

Bett stood.

She was smaller than Hala, compact, her face showing the closed watchfulness Suna had come to recognize as Bett's permanent weather. She wore a cotton shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbows, and her forearms were brown and scratched from the terraced garden where she'd been working the day before, pulling weeds from between the herb rows with the focused energy of a woman who needed her hands to be busy.

"Ren," Bett said.

The name hit clean. Ada startled in Nessa's arms. Peyam's beads stopped against his thumb. Ori made a small wet sound and buried her face in Habel's skirt.

"My son," Bett said. "He drowned when he was three. Seven years ago." Her voice had dropped into the dry economy of a woman who had learned that the most important things required the fewest words. "Nori came after. She isn't his replacement. She's my daughter."

Bett sat. She did not look at Hala. She did not look at anyone. Arash was looking straight at her. By the time she settled on the bench, the gap between him and the rest of the Kondos had widened by half a step.

---

Venn stood last.

He rose with his shoulders squared, chin lifted, bringing back the formal posture that had carried him through the first naming. Tila was beside him in the pressed blouse she had saved for this morning, hair combed and pinned, the effort written all over her.

"We are the Kondos," Venn said. The formal voice was back. He used it like a brace. "We are twelve. Tila, my wife. Habel, my sister. Mava, our aunt. Ori, Habel's daughter." He named the others with the same even weight, rebuilding the family aloud one brick at a time.

"Arash," Venn said. "My son."

The name hung. Arash was no longer in line with the rest of them. He stood three feet off, one shoulder angled toward the Daos, close enough to be counted and far enough that no one could pretend not to see it.

Arash did not step forward. He kept his eyes on Bett for one beat, then lowered them to the floor and spoke.

"I'm not going to the wedding," Arash said. "I'm not doing it."

Venn stared. In the courtyard anger had sent him forward. Here it locked him in place. Suna had seen him loud. She had never seen him mute. The silence coming off him felt heavier than his voice ever had.

Mava, seated on the bench behind Venn, reached one hand toward Arash. Her arm extended and hung in the air between them — the same arm whose gesture Asha Mera had borrowed, the slow ear-rubbing motion that had started everything. Her hand did not reach him. She held it out for a moment and then brought it back to her lap, and the gesture was complete in its incompletion, an offer that did not need to be accepted to have been made.

Tila put her hand flat on her own knee. She looked at the stone floor between her feet. Her face did not change expression because the expression she had assembled that morning — the pressed blouse, the pinned hair, the composure — was built to hold, and it held. But her hand on her knee was pressing down, the fingers white.

Venn finished. He named the remaining Kondos — the ones who were staying, the ones who would walk with him down the road to the wedding that his son would not attend. His voice did not waver. He recited the names as though nothing had interrupted, as though the family were still twelve, letting the list do the work of shape and continuity after the people on it had chosen otherwise. He sat. His hands went flat on his thighs, and he did not look at Arash, and Arash did not look at him, and between them was a thick, unusable silence: two men who loved each other and could not, at this moment, find a form for it that either of them could use.

---

Fasa waited. She stood in the center of the hall, in the band of sunlight that had moved since the naming began and now fell across her hands, and she waited until the room's silence had settled from the active silence of people holding their breath to the quieter silence of people who have heard what they needed to hear.

"Is the naming complete?" she asked.

She asked it to the room, not to Olet, not to any family, but to the air and the stone and the forty-three people who had been strangers three weeks ago and were not strangers now.

No one answered. The question sat in the hall for a long moment, and in that moment Suna became aware of every sound the room contained — Asha's breathing against Kael's shirt, the distant and diminished rush of water from the valley below, a bird somewhere outside the hall that she had not heard before because it had been drowned by rain for three weeks, the creak of the stone bench as someone shifted weight.

Olet spoke from his place at the railing. He had not moved during the entire naming. He stood where he had stood from the beginning, leaning against the stone with the valley behind him, and his voice was the same voice it always was — measured, unhurried, carrying no more weight than it ever carried and no less.

"The naming is complete," Olet said. "The road is open."

The road is open. Three words. Suna looked past him to the valley and saw what she had been seeing since she entered the hall but had not yet allowed herself to understand as fact: the switchback path descending the hill, muddy and littered with debris, but passable. Brown silt on the stones. Tufts of uprooted grass caught against the retaining walls. The waterline a hundred meters below where it had been when they arrived, pulled back to the valley floor like a sheet drawn down from a bed, leaving the damp, stained evidence of where it had lain.

They could leave. The thought arrived with a simplicity that felt almost absurd after everything — the namings, the manifestations, the shouting and the silence and the confessions extracted and volunteered. They could walk down the hill and onto the road and continue to wherever they had been going when the flood stopped them. The thought should have been relief, and part of it was, but the relief was layered over something else, something Suna couldn't name and didn't try to. She had spent three weeks managing collapse one task at a time, and now the tasks were different, and she was already cataloguing them: pack the bags, fill the water skins, check Asha's shoes, make sure Kael had eaten. The list was shorter than usual. The list was almost done.

Fasa looked at Olet, and Olet looked back, and between them passed something Suna could not read — an acknowledgment, a completion, the end of a process they had been conducting together for twenty years and which had, this time, asked more of everyone than it ever had before. Fasa's face was tired, not with the tiredness of a performance completed but with the tiredness of someone who has been holding a thing open for three weeks and can now let it close, and the closing takes a different kind of effort than the holding.

The families began to stand. The naming was over. There was no ceremony for the ending — no prayer, no benediction, no formal release. Fasa stepped back from the center of the hall, and Olet remained at the railing, and the families rose from the benches and the floor and began to move with the restlessness of people who have somewhere to go and have been prevented from going there and are now allowed.

Asha pulled at Suna's hand. Suna lifted her.

---

The compound turned over fast. Sleeping mats rolled. Tin cups clinked into bags. Three weeks of settling gave way in under an hour. Bram took his jacket from the nail by the kitchen door. Mava's afternoon corner by the wall filled with footprints and then with sun.

Suna packed the Mera family's things. There was not much. They had arrived carrying what they could, and they were leaving with less — some of it traded for food during the first week, some of it simply worn past use. The bags smelled of cooking smoke and damp stone and the damp-sweet mildew that had colonized everything during the monsoon's worst days. She rolled the sleeping mats and tied them with the cord Peyam had braided from kitchen twine during the long afternoons, his fingers working the pattern while he watched the rain. Rimi packed the children's things without being asked. Kael carried Asha's mat, and Asha carried nothing, and this was correct — Asha was four, and four-year-olds carried nothing down mountains except themselves.

Deshi packed her own bag and cinched it shut with one pull. She still had not spoken. The knife-edge Suna had come to expect in her face was absent. She looked as if she had spent the last hour biting down on a seed she could not crack. Once she caught Suna watching, held her eyes for a beat, and went back to folding the spare shawl.

Peyam packed slowly. His knees. He tucked the prayer beads into his shirt pocket and took them out again. He wound them around his wrist and unwound them. On the third try he left them in the pocket. Suna said nothing. There were men who needed a last prayer before the road and men who needed the motions that came before one. Peyam was both.

Jin came to the edge of the Mera family's sleeping space with Melie by the hand. Their bag was already on his back, a single tight bundle for two people. Melie's face still held the puffiness left by private crying. She kept looking at Rimi and then down at her own shoes.

"We'll walk with you," Jin said. The words were steady. The question sat underneath them anyway.

"We're heading south-southwest," Suna said. This was practical, not emotional, and the practicality was the point — it was the language they shared, the language of directions and provisions and logistics, and by speaking it she was saying yes without the weight of saying yes. "We've got enough water for the descent."

"I've got extra," Jin said. He touched the water skin on his belt.

Deshi hauled her bag upright. "Then keep the extra where I can reach it if one of the children slips," she said without looking at him. "Kael runs."

"I will," Jin said.

Melie let go of his hand and went to stand beside Rimi. Rimi looked at Suna, then shifted so Melie was inside the line of bodies rather than out on the courtyard side.

Across the courtyard departures were no longer abstract. The three assistant custodians stood near the kitchen door and watched. Families that had fought and wept in the same hall moved around each other with the clipped courtesy of a market at closing time.

The Teshes left first. Gitta reached for her own bag. Fen took it before her hand closed and shouldered both without asking. She clicked her tongue at him and kept walking. He kept the bag. Miro went ahead with Kiri on his shoulders; Enn fell in beside him and hooked her arm through his. At the first switchback Enn looked back once. The others did not.

The Leos followed as a tight square: Vera and Pik in front, Cade and Wynn behind. Vera had tucked the grey knitting under her arm. Pik's free hand came up once, thumbs beginning their slow circling, and Vera folded her other hand over them without breaking stride.

On the far side of the courtyard Venn was lashing two packs together when Arash left the Kondo cluster. No one called after him. He crossed the stones slowly enough that anyone in his family could have stopped him. Hala saw him first; her shoulders rose. Bett was bent over Nori's bag, retightening the strap.

"If I come with you, I'm not turning back," Arash said.

Bett looked up. For a moment Suna thought she might send him away simply because he had made the choice in front of witnesses and witnesses made everything harder. Instead Bett reached up, caught the torn edge of his collar between thumb and forefinger, smoothed it flat, and let it go.

"Then don't," she said.

She handed him Nori's rolled blanket. Nori watched both of them, then put her hand into his free one as if the matter were settled. Hala let out a short breath through her nose and tossed Arash the second water skin. He caught it.

Venn did not look up. He finished the knot, called two younger cousins by name, and started for the road. The Kondos closed around the gap without filling it. Tila lifted one hand from her bag strap as she passed the place where Arash had stood, then gripped the strap again and kept moving. They were eleven by the time they reached the first switchback.

The Daos set off a minute later: Hala in front with Sem and Raya, Bett beside Arash, Nori between them whenever the mud thickened, Kal behind with his hand on Sem's shoulder and a low tuneless hum under his breath. Suna watched them take the turn below the hall, Arash's frayed collar bright in the sun.

Suna turned to her own family. Deshi was ready, her bag on her back, Peyam beside her with his prayer beads finally settled in his pocket. Rimi had Asha by the hand, and Melie was beside Rimi, and Kael was already three steps down the road, stopped, waiting, his face turned up toward his mother with the impatience of a child who did not understand why adults took so long to leave places.

Jin was beside her. His bag was on his back and Melie's small water skin was clipped to his belt and he smelled, as they all did, of smoke and stone and the stale-sweet closeness of three weeks' shared living. He did not say anything. He stood beside her as Bram had once sat beside him — present, not demanding, the kindness of company that asks for nothing back.

Suna looked up at the shrine. Fasa and Olet stood at the compound's upper entrance, near the covered walkway to the custodians' quarters. They did not wave. They did not call out. They watched the families leave with the same patient attention they had given the arrivals. Fasa's hands were at her sides. Olet leaned against the stone. The three assistants had gone back inside.

From the road the shrine was only stone, shadow, and the open mouth of the hall. Hard to believe three weeks had fit inside it. It was already receding in Suna's mind even while the mud still sucked at their shoes below the gate.

At the bottom, where the seven roads met, the mud was thick and dark. Flood debris lay in heaps against the stone markers: branches, cloth, a single sandal, things the water had taken and thrown back. The convergence was a wide stone-paved space, and the families stopped there long enough to shift weight on their shoulders and choose directions.

The goodbyes were brief and uneven. Kiri Tesh turned from the eastern road and waved at Asha with her whole arm; Asha answered in kind. Bram caught Cade's eye and nodded once. Hala clasped Nessa's hand hard enough to shift Ada on her hip.

The Kondos and the Daos crossed near enough that Suna could have stepped between them. Arash moved aside so Habel and Ori could pass. Ori stared at him, trying to place him in this new position. Tila slowed. For one beat she pressed her fingertips to Arash's wrist, right where Nori's hand had been a minute earlier. Then she followed Venn. Venn never turned his head.

The roads diverged. The families took them, one by one and two by two, until the convergence was empty and the hill behind them was just a hill with a building on top, and ahead of them the roads led to the places they had been going when the flood stopped them — weddings, funerals, new cities, old homes, the ordinary destinations that had been waiting, that were still waiting, that would receive them as they were now rather than as they had been. When it was her turn, Suna took the south-southwest road, and Jin and Melie fell in behind the Meras without discussion.

Suna walked. Deshi was beside her, and Peyam beside Deshi, and Jin behind them, and the children were ahead. The children were always ahead. Rimi and Melie walked together, Melie's hand slipping into Rimi's whenever the mud deepened. Kael was in front, his pace fast, face forward. Asha walked on her own feet, shoes sinking and pulling free with soft sucking sounds.

The children walked ahead because they had less to carry and because youth made forward motion feel simple. Their footprints striped the mud. None of them looked back at the shrine on the hill. The adults followed with bags, provisions, and the names they had finally said out loud. The road was long and slick. The sun was up. They walked.
