# Chapter 16

Deshi was still saying something sharp to Rimi about folding mats properly when Suna carried the last stack of bowls to the basin.

Nineteen days in the compound had worn the evening cleanup into her body — scrape, stack, carry, rinse, return. She tipped the final bowl, watched water sheet off the rim, and realized she could hear Ada Okoye crying from two rooms away. Peyam and Kal were still near the kitchen over the dregs of their shared pipe. Voices kept going. The rain was no longer underneath any of it.

She stood with her hands in the basin and listened into the gap it had left. The compound was full of its usual evening sounds, but behind all of them — where the monsoon had been for nineteen days — nothing. A silence so large it had shape.

She dried her hands on her skirt and walked into the prayer courtyard.

The stones were wet but the sky was not falling on them. She looked up and saw a break in the clouds to the west, a strip of dark sky that was actually dark — not the heavy grey-brown of monsoon cover but the real dark of open sky at night. She couldn't see stars yet. But the clouds were thinning, and the air against her face had changed. Lighter. Drier. Like someone had lifted a lid off the compound that she hadn't realized was there.

She sat on the low wall that edged the courtyard and let her hands rest on the wet stone. Cold through her palms. She'd forgotten what cold stone felt like without fresh water running over it.

From here she could hear the flood below, but it sounded different tonight — lower, steadier, more like a river and less like a catastrophe. Or maybe that was hope doing the hearing. She didn't trust hope. Hope was the voice that said he went ahead, he's waiting for you in Gelden, just get there and everything will make sense again. Hope was a three-month-old lie she'd told so many times it had almost calcified into fact.

She pressed her palms flat against the stone and let the cold climb into her wrists, her forearms. Nineteen days. She'd managed the Mera family through nineteen days in this place — Deshi's complaints, Kael's restlessness, Asha's unsettling new gestures, Peyam's quiet decline into exhaustion, Rimi's too-watchful silence. She'd managed the sleeping rotation and the meal rotation and the latrine schedule and the naming and every moment of every day when someone needed something decided. She'd managed all of it with a gap at the center of her family that she'd papered over with competence.

Her husband had not gone ahead. Her husband had left.

She knew this. She'd known it when she stood at the first naming and told Fasa that he was traveling separately, that he'd gone ahead to arrange housing at their destination. She'd known it when Deshi asked, on day three, when he might arrive. She'd known it when Nessa asked, gently, where he was.

She'd known it when she woke at two in the morning on a mat in a stone room with her children around her and felt the specific vertigo of a woman repeating the same answer to herself even after it has gone false, because if she stops the children will hear the silence where their father used to be.

Three months. He'd been gone for three months before the flood. He'd left on a Tuesday morning. She remembered that because Rimi had a school exam and he'd promised to walk her. He didn't walk her. He didn't leave a note. He took his second pair of shoes and his work bag and the framed photo of his mother, and that was how she knew it wasn't a work trip. Nobody takes their mother's photograph to work.

She sat in the courtyard and pressed her hands against the stone and let herself think about it properly for the first time in nineteen days. Not the organizational question of how to manage without him, which she'd been managing since before he left — he'd been absent in practice for months before he was absent in fact. The other question. The one under the management. The one that made her hands still and her breathing shallow whenever she came close to it.

Why wasn't she enough.

The thought arrived whole, like a piece of glass surfacing in wash water. Clean-edged and specific. Not why did he leave, which had explanations — his restlessness, his debts, his mother's death, his younger face in the window of another woman's house that one afternoon when Suna was carrying Asha home from the market and didn't let herself look twice. Not even what did I do wrong, which was a question that assumed she'd had the power to prevent it. But the barer thing: why wasn't what she was — capable, present, running their lives, raising his children — why wasn't that enough to make someone stay.

She heard the footsteps before she saw who was making them. Someone coming through the narrow passage from the main hall, walking carefully — the stones were slick — and then stopping at the edge of the courtyard as if uncertain whether to enter.

Jin Parra stood there with his arms folded against the evening chill, his thin frame almost lost in a borrowed jacket that was too wide across the shoulders. He looked at her and she saw the hesitation she'd learned to recognize — the jaw working before speech, the fractional pause that had become as familiar to everyone in the compound as the bell. He was holding a tin cup with both hands, the way Melie held things, as if each object deserved his full grip.

"I can — " He stopped. His teeth closed on the consonant. He looked down at the cup, then at her, then at the wet stones between them. "I can go."

"You don't need to," she said.

He didn't sit down. He stood at the edge of the courtyard, two body-lengths from the wall where Suna sat, and he looked up at the sky with an expression she couldn't read. Or maybe she could read it but didn't want to name it, because naming other people's feelings was a way of managing them and she was tired of managing.

"It stopped," he said.

"I know."

"Melie asked if that meant we could leave." His voice caught on the l. "I didn't know what to tell her."

His hands tightened on the tin cup. His thumbnails were bitten short, the skin around them raw — a habit she'd noticed in the first week but hadn't thought about again until now. He was shivering slightly. The evening wasn't cold enough for shivering. Something else was doing it.

"Sit down," she said. It came out the way she said things to her children: not a request, not a command, just the next practical step.

He sat on the wall, leaving the space of a person between them. He set the cup on the stone between his feet — empty, she saw now, or nearly. He'd brought it as something to hold.

For a while neither of them spoke. The compound behind them was settling. She could hear Deshi's voice from the sleeping section, the particular rhythm of her evening instructions — fold this, move that, where did Asha put her sandals. From the other direction, Bram's voice, low and indistinct, probably talking to Nessa. A moth circled the oil lamp in the passage they'd both come through, its shadow large and erratic against the stone wall.

"I watched Gitta," Jin said.

Suna waited.

"I watched her stand up and tell them about Fen. Sixteen years." He rubbed his thumb against the rim of the cup. "She didn't ask anyone to forgive her. She didn't explain why. She just said what happened."

"I know," Suna said. "I was there."

"And then Fen came back." His voice went quiet. Not quieter — she'd never heard Jin speak above a conversational volume — but the quality of the quiet changed, became something he was working to maintain. "He came back and sat next to her and didn't say anything and that was — I haven't been able to stop thinking about that."

"About Fen coming back?"

"About what she must have felt. All those years. Every time someone said he looks like his grandfather or he has your husband's hands. Sixteen years of people telling her a lie she invented and having to just — " He made a sound that wasn't a word. His jaw locked and released. "I told them Melie's mother was dead."

The sentence landed in the courtyard between them. Suna's body registered it before her mind did — her hands pressed flat against the stone, fingers spreading, and the cold of the wet surface shot up through her wrists and held her there. She didn't move. She didn't speak. She could feel the stone's grit under her fingertips, each grain of it distinct.

"She's not dead," Jin said. The words came faster now, as if he'd pulled a plug and the pressure behind it was doing the work. "She's alive. She's alive and she left. She left when Melie was four. Just — left. Packed a bag while I was at work and Melie was at the neighbor's and she was gone when I got home. She left a note on the table next to Melie's juice cup, just a note, not even a long one, just I can't do this anymore and I'm sorry and don't look for me."

Suna didn't speak. The wall took the force of his grip and passed it into the stone under her palms, a faint shudder running through the cold. His hands had abandoned the cup for the wall's edge; the skin around his thumbnails had gone white.

"I told Melie she died. I told her mama got sick and died. Because what was I supposed to — she was four. How do you tell a four-year-old that her mother looked at her and at me and at the house and the dishes in the sink and the juice cup on the table and decided — that she didn't — " His voice broke. Not dramatically, not with a sob. It broke the way a stick breaks, a clean snap, and then he was breathing hard through his nose and staring at the wet stone between his feet.

The moth's shadow moved on the wall behind them. The flood murmured below. Somewhere in the compound a child laughed — one of the Kondo girls, maybe Ori — and the sound was so ordinary and so distant that it felt like it belonged to a different place entirely.

"And then we came here," Jin said. His voice had steadied, but it was thin, scraped out. "And they started asking. And I said the same thing. She died. She got sick and she died. And every day they asked more questions and every day the lie got — I could feel it getting thinner, like cloth you've washed too many times, and then Fasa, in the naming, she asked when and where and how and I couldn't — " He swallowed. "The dates didn't work. What I'd been saying didn't hold because I'm not — I'm not good at this. I can't maintain it. Gitta did it for sixteen years and I can't do three months."

Suna sat very still beside him. Her hands were flat on the stone and the cold was in her wrists and the silence where the rain used to be was enormous around them. Beside her, his breath snagged and leveled and snagged again, and she felt something rise in her chest that she hadn't felt in nineteen days. Or three months. Or longer.

She recognized it. She'd been pushing it down every morning when she made the children's beds and organized the day and answered Deshi's questions and deflected Nessa's kindness and stood at the naming and spoke her family's name and composition with a voice so steady it convinced everyone including herself.

"He didn't go ahead," Suna said.

Jin turned to look at her. His face was wet and he wasn't wiping it.

"My husband." She heard her own voice, steady, factual, the voice she used for household logistics and naming declarations. "He didn't go ahead to arrange things. He left. Three months before the flood. He left."

She waited for the vertigo. It came — a dropping sensation behind her ribs, the feeling of saying aloud something she'd kept inside so long it had grown into her, become load-bearing, part of the structure she used to hold herself upright. But the vertigo was smaller than she'd expected. Or maybe she'd been falling for three months and just hadn't looked down.

"He took his mother's photograph and his second pair of shoes and he left on a Tuesday morning. Rimi had a school exam. He'd promised to walk her."

Jin was looking at her. He'd stopped gripping the wall. His hands were in his lap, open, empty.

"I told Deshi he went ahead. I told the children. I told Nessa. I told the naming." She heard the list forming, the cataloguing that was her own particular refuge — numbering the people she'd deceived as if they were tasks she could complete and check off and put behind her. "I don't know if Deshi believes it. I think she knows. I think she knew before I did. She doesn't ask about him. She asks about everything — where are Asha's sandals, why is the rice burned, did I comb Kael's hair this morning — but she doesn't ask about him. That's how I know she knows."

"Suna."

"Don't," she said, and the word was sharper than she intended. "Don't say you're sorry. I can't do anything with that right now."

He nodded. They sat in the silence together.

The clouds had thinned further. Above the western edge of the compound wall, three stars were visible — faint, tentative, as if they weren't sure the sky would hold. Suna couldn't remember the last time she'd seen stars. Nineteen days of cloud cover, and before that she hadn't been looking up. Before that she'd been packing what the children could carry and selling what they couldn't and telling Rimi they were going on an adventure and telling Kael his father would be there when they arrived and telling Asha nothing because Asha was four and absorbed whatever atmosphere surrounded her, which lately had been the atmosphere of a household quietly dissolving.

"How old is Melie?" she asked, and the question surprised her as it left her mouth. She knew how old Melie was. She'd watched her play with Asha and Kael every day for nearly three weeks.

"Six," Jin said. "She was four when — " He didn't finish.

"Asha was nearly four when he left." Suna looked at the three stars. "She'll remember pieces. His sandals by the door. The sound of him clearing his throat in the mornings. Not him, not the way Kael will. Rimi remembers everything."

"Does Rimi know?"

"I don't know." The admission cost her something — the first time she'd said it to anyone. "She hasn't asked. She's twelve. She's old enough to know that not asking is its own kind of answer."

Another star appeared. Then two more. The sky was clearing from the west, the monsoon cover pulling apart, and the exposed sky behind it was deep and scattered with light. Suna stared at it. The beauty felt almost aggressive after nineteen days of grey — intrusive, like a guest arriving too early and too cheerfully to a house still in mourning.

"I kept thinking about Gitta too," she said. "Sixteen years. Mine is three months, and it already sits in my mouth all day. I wake up and before I think about breakfast or Deshi's mood or the naming, I'm lining up the same answers. He went ahead to Gelden. He's arranging things. He'll be there when we arrive. By morning my jaw hurts from holding it in place. And I'm tired."

The last word left her throat scraped raw. Beside her, Jin kept looking at the stones between his feet.

Jin reached for his empty cup and held it. The metal whispered under his thumb as he turned it, slow and steady, the sound carrying in the quiet between them.

"Melie knows something's wrong," he said. "She doesn't ask about her mother anymore. She used to ask. Every day, for months. When's mama coming back, where did mama go, is mama in heaven. I said yes, heaven, and she stopped asking. But she didn't stop knowing." He pressed his thumb against the cup's rim. "Now when I hand her a bowl, she looks at my face before she looks at the food. At night she hooks her fist in my shirt and won't let go even asleep. The allergy thing — when Bett saved her — she wasn't watching Bett. She was watching me. My face scared her worse than her throat did."

"You're keeping her safe," Suna said.

"I'm lying to her every day."

Suna had nothing to offer that. She sat with it. The stone wall under her was losing its chill, warming toward the dry roughness she remembered from daylight hours, from afternoons when the rain paused and the stone absorbed the sun's heat and held it.

From the main hall, the custodians' bell rang once — not the insistent triple peal of a naming summons but the single tone that marked the end of communal hours. Settle in. Lamps out within the hour. The sound was so familiar now that Suna's body responded before her mind did: she straightened, began the mental list. Check that Asha is on her mat, that Kael's sandals are by the door, that Peyam has water for the night, that —

She stopped the list. Let it go. The children were with Deshi. They would be managed. She could sit here for a few more minutes without the Mera family collapsing.

"Gitta didn't ask anyone's permission," Jin said. "She didn't wait for Fasa to drag it out of her. She didn't let the naming break her open. She chose."

"She chose," Suna agreed.

More stars came through by the minute. The break in the clouds had spread across half the western sky, and the stars no longer looked tentative; they burned with the blunt, ordinary confidence of things that had been there all along, hidden only by weather. The sheen was draining off the flagstones.

"I'm going to tell the naming he left," she said.

She hadn't known she was going to say it until she said it. Or maybe she had. Maybe she'd known since Gitta stood up. Maybe she'd known since Nessa told her about Efe and Suna realized that everyone in this compound was carrying something dead they wouldn't name.

"This isn't for them," she said. "Fasa can make of it what she wants. Olet can keep saying what he likes about the road." She looked at the stars, not at Jin. "It's for the next time Kael asks when his father gets here. It's for Deshi's face when I say his name in that room. I can't keep putting Sev one town ahead of us when I still see the shelf where his mother's photograph used to be and the doorway with his second pair of shoes gone."

Jin didn't say anything for a long time. The hush in the courtyard kept widening; even the flood sounded farther away, and above them the gap in the clouds kept opening.

"What will you say?" he asked.

"That he left. That he's not waiting for us. That the Mera family is five people, not six." The wall's rough edge bit into her palm. Jin turned toward her, the cup forgotten between his shoes. "And Deshi will close her eyes and Rimi will look at me and Kael will ask where papa went and Asha won't understand and it'll be in the room where everybody can hear it, not just inside my own head."

"And then everyone will know."

"Everyone already suspects. I'm the worst liar in this compound. I just — I thought being competent was the same thing as being convincing." Air left her in a short hard burst. Her shoulders hitched once. "It's not."

Jin set the cup on the stone. He looked at her and his face in the starlight was the face of a man who had just spoken more in twenty minutes than he had in nineteen days. The exhaustion of it was visible — in his posture, in the way his hands lay open and spent on his knees, in the dark eyes that were no longer guarding anything.

"I can't," he said. "Not yet. I can't tell her. Not in the naming, not with everyone watching. She's six."

"I know."

"If I tell her that her mother chose to leave — if I take away died and give her left — she'll start hunting through the day for the reason. She'll ask if it was because I left her at the neighbor's. She'll ask if she cried too much, or asked too much, or held on too hard. She'll make it hers. She still sleeps with her hand twisted in my shirt. If I give her left, she'll think she let go."

"I know," Suna said again. "I have three of them."

He tipped his face to the stars. Beside her, his breath came out in one long unsteady stream, then caught hard on the next inhale.

"Tell her before the naming," Suna said.

He looked at her.

"Tell her yourself. Before they make you. Because they will — Fasa, or the naming, or the road opening, or Melie growing old enough to put it together on her own. Sit with her on her mat. Put water in her hand if she needs something to hold. Say it plain. Her mother left. Then stay there and answer whatever comes next. Don't drag her into that room with it still half-wrapped. Don't make the naming do your work for you."

His jaw worked. The catch, the lock, the release. He kept looking at the stone between his shoes.

"I don't know how," he said.

"You just told me. You found the words for me. You can find them for her."

"That's different. You're not six."

"She's not four anymore either. You said it yourself — she's stopped asking. That doesn't mean she believes you. It means the answer you've given her hurts. It'll hurt worse if the naming rips it open in front of everybody."

He put his face in his hands. His shoulders curved in, the jacket bunching around his neck, and he sat like that — bent, folded, his breathing loud in the quiet courtyard. Suna sat beside him and did not touch him and did not speak and did not manage. The naming room kept rising anyway: Deshi with her eyes closed, Rimi looking straight at her, Kael lifting his chin to ask where papa went.

The last dampness had gone out of the courtyard. Heat breathed up from the flagstones, and the sky above was thick with stars she hadn't seen in nineteen days. The flood below had dropped into the background, stripped for a moment of catastrophe and reduced to water moving in the dark.

"If the road opens," Jin said from behind his hands.

"When it opens."

"When it opens." He lowered his hands. His face was blotched, his eyes red, and he looked his age — twenty-nine, which was too young for this, though Suna wasn't sure what age would be old enough. "Where are you going?"

She hadn't thought about it. She'd been so focused on the daily management of being here that she'd stopped thinking about a destination, a city she'd been traveling toward, housing she'd been promised by a cousin who might or might not still be expecting them. Her husband was supposed to be there. He wasn't there. She didn't know where he was.

"Forward," she said. "The same direction. Just without pretending Sev will be in Gelden when we get there."

They sat together without touching, both of them facing the open square of sky. They were two parents on a stone wall in a shrine courtyard on a hillside above a flood, each with a child asleep one wall away and tomorrow's naming already pressing at the edge of the dark.

Suna listened to the place the rain had vacated. The silence carried no message. It was only the clean gap left when water stopped striking stone. Weather had simply stopped.

But the stars were real, and the air was drying, and somewhere in the sleeping quarters her children were on their mats with Deshi folding blankets over them, and tomorrow she would stand in the naming and say it aloud and the room would hear it and Deshi would close her eyes and Rimi would look straight at her and Kael would ask his question in that clear carrying voice of his.

She could force the words out. She couldn't stop what came after.

"Jin," she said.

He looked at her.

"It's not your fault either."

He opened his mouth and closed it. The jaw-lock held, then released into nothing. He looked at her. She didn't nod or look away.

Then he picked up his empty cup and stood. He was unsteady for a moment — the wall was low and his legs had stiffened — and she almost reached out to steady him but didn't. He found his balance.

"Thank you," he said. The hesitation on the th, the slight elongation, and then the rest coming out clean. "For listening. And for — " He stopped. Shook his head. "Thank you."

"Go check on Melie."

He nodded and walked back through the passage, his footsteps careful on the drying stone, the borrowed jacket too wide across his shoulders. The sound of him faded past the lamp and into the sleeping quarters.

Suna sat alone in the courtyard. She tilted her head back and looked at the sky, which was deep and dark and alive with more light than she could count. The compound was almost silent now. One oil lamp still burned in the passage. The moth was gone. She could hear, very faintly, the sound of someone humming — Mava, maybe, one of her old songs, the melody drifting from the Kondo sleeping section. Or maybe it was Kiri Tesh, humming the lullaby that had come from somewhere none of them could trace. She couldn't tell from here. It was just a thread of sound in the quiet.

She stood up from the wall, and it left its warmth along the backs of her thighs. In nineteen days the compound had printed itself into her body — rough stone, narrow passages, the give of a mat on the floor — a place she hadn't chosen and couldn't leave and had somehow come to know by feel.

Tomorrow she would stand in the naming room and say that Sev left on a Tuesday with his work bag, his second pair of shoes, and his mother's photograph. Deshi would close her eyes. Rimi would look straight at her. Kael would ask where papa went in the voice that carried farther than it should. Tonight she would check on her children and lie on her mat listening to Deshi breathe on the other side of the partition, listening for Kael to turn over, for Rimi to stay awake longer than she admitted, and wait for morning without rehearsing the old answer.

She walked back through the passage toward the sleeping quarters. The lamp flame bent as she passed. Behind her, the prayer courtyard lay open to the sky — stone and starlight and the low murmur of the flood below. Ahead, behind the thin partition, her family was sleeping through the last night she would answer Kael as if Sev were waiting in Gelden.
