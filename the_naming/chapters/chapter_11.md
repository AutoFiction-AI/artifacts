# Chapter 11

The thing that kept her awake wasn't the rain or the mosquitoes or the stone under the mat, though all three were doing their part. It was Tomas. He'd developed a new sound in his sleep — a small catch before each exhale, a micro-stutter that had nothing to do with dreaming. It sounded like Jin Parra's voice. It sounded like the hesitation Jin had been fighting all afternoon at the naming, that brief lock of the jaw at the front of certain words, except now it lived in her ten-year-old brother's breathing and nobody was talking about it.

Leni lay on her back and listened to it. She counted the catches. Fourteen in the last minute, or maybe twelve — she kept losing count because Ada would grunt and shift against her mother's chest and Leni's tally would reset. Her mother slept curled around Ada on the mat nearest the wall, her breathing slow and deliberate even in unconsciousness, like she was rationing air. Her father was on the far side, the bulk of his back to the room, his glasses folded on the stone by his head.

She sat up. The sleeping quarters were divided by cloth partitions that did nothing for sound and even less for smell — two weeks of bodies and cooking smoke had settled into the fabric like stain. Through the nearest partition she could hear one of the Kondo children coughing, a dry persistent sound that someone would tsk about in the morning. Beyond that, the steady backdrop of the flood — the valley water grinding against its own banks, a noise she'd stopped noticing for days at a time and then suddenly heard again, like a refrigerator cycling on.

She didn't decide to get up. She just did. Her feet met the stone — cool, almost cold, the kind of cold that traveled up through the soles and settled in the knees. She picked her way between mats, stepping over the edge of the Mera family's section where Kael had flung his arm across the gap between families. The main hall was a dark room of breathing. Oil lamps turned low at the exits cast the kind of light that made corners deep and faces unreadable. She counted shapes as she moved — Gitta Tesh was a small curve on her mat near the wall, unnervingly still for a seventy-year-old. Fen slept two mats over from his grandmother, long enough that his feet extended past the mat onto bare stone. He'd kicked his cover off.

She stepped into the covered walkway between the sleeping quarters and the prayer courtyard. It was open on one side, and the rain came in at an angle, misting her left arm and shoulder. She pulled her sleeve down over her hand. The air smelled green and brown at the same time — vegetation growing so fast it smelled alive, mud churning somewhere below. She could see the courtyard ahead, the stone paving slick and dark, and she nearly turned back because the courtyard was usually empty at this hour and empty things at night had a specific weight she didn't want to carry alone.

But it wasn't empty.

Two figures sat on the low wall at the courtyard's far edge, where the paving met the stone lip that overlooked the terraced garden and, beyond that, nothing — the valley, the flood, the dark. They sat with the deliberate closeness of people who had chosen each other and seen no reason to pretend otherwise.

Leni stopped walking. She stood in the shadow of the covered walkway, her wet shoulder against the stone pillar, and she looked.

She recognized Arash Kondo by the set of his shoulders, which were his father's shoulders made leaner, less squared, as if someone had taken Venn's frame and relaxed the hinges. He sat with his elbows on his knees, his head turned toward the other figure, and something about the angle of his neck — tilted, open, listening — was so unlike anything she'd seen in the Kondo family that it took her a moment to be sure it was him. The Kondos held their heads a certain way. Level. Declarative. Arash's head was tilted like a question.

The other figure was Bett Dao. Her hair was down. Leni had never seen it down — during the day Bett wore it pulled back and fastened with a strip of cloth, tight against her head, practical as everything about her. Now it fell past her shoulders, damp from the rain or maybe just the humidity that clung to everything, and it softened her. Her face seemed looser somehow. Less held. She sat with one knee drawn up, her arms wrapped around her shin, and she was talking.

Leni couldn't hear the first words. The rain was a curtain of small sounds between them, and Bett's voice rode low, almost below carrying range. But the rhythm reached her: steady, unhurried, none of Bett's usual chopped-off daytime cadence. Daytime Bett spoke in short sentences and shut conversations down. This voice had taken the lid off something and wasn't putting it back yet.

Arash said something. Bett laughed outright, sudden and surprised, as if the funny thing had caught her off guard. Arash's hand moved. Leni watched it travel from his own knee to Bett's back, then slip under the wet hem of Bett's shirt at her waist, flat against her skin. Bett went still. She did not recoil. She turned, caught his wrist, and pressed her mouth to the inside of it, right where the pulse sat close to the skin.

Leni bit the side of her thumb. She knew what she was looking at, or was starting to. Her body had already clocked it; her thoughts were only now catching up. Arash Kondo had a wedding to get to. He was traveling to his wedding. The whole Kondo family — all twelve of them — was heading to the same destination, and the destination was a woman Arash was supposed to marry, and here he was in the rain at midnight with Bett Dao's skin under his hand and her mouth on his wrist.

Rain thinned for a second, enough for Leni to hear Bett say, "Don't touch me like that if you're still going to marry her."

Arash left his hand where it was. "When the road opens, I can't do it. Not after this."

"Can't isn't won't."

"I know."

"Then know faster."

He kissed her then, and it wasn't quick or tentative. His free hand went into her wet hair. Bett grabbed the front of his shirt and pulled him harder against her, and Leni had to look down at the paving for a second because the scene felt too private and too impossible at once. When she looked back, Arash's forehead was resting against Bett's. Their hands were locked together between them on the wall, rain running over both of them as if the weather had no interest in being discreet.

Leni stepped back. She moved slowly, weight rolled onto the outside edges of her feet, the trick she used when she was trying not to wake Ada, and she retreated down the covered walkway until the courtyard was a rectangle of wet stone framed by the pillars and the two figures on the wall were just shapes.

Then she turned for the sleeping quarters instead of her family's mat.

If Arash and Bett were out there, they had left holes behind them. If those holes were obvious, the whole thing was already one bad turn of a head away from exploding. The thought got hold of her and would not let go. She slipped through the nearest cloth partition and cut across the dark hall.

The Kondo section carried the same family logic as everything else the Kondos did: close, orderly, each mat set square to the next. Venn lay on his back with one arm flung out from the blanket, the hand open near the edge of the mat beside him. Tila faced inward toward Ori and the younger children, her knees tucked up. Mava was a small bundled rise under two covers. The mat next to Venn held only a long dark hollow and a kicked-back blanket. Arash's sandals were gone from underneath it.

Leni stood there longer than she meant to. During the day the Kondos moved like a unit. Even their arguments had a family shape to them, one person talking and the rest bracing around it. One missing body cut the row open. If Venn rolled over and reached, his hand would find cold mat.

Venn made a low sound in his sleep and shifted his shoulders. Leni froze where she was. Tila drew Ori a little closer without opening her eyes. Nobody woke. The row resettled around the empty place as if it had practiced doing that too.

She crossed to the Dao section.

Hala slept on her side with one arm crooked under her cheek, her face turned toward the children. Raya and Sem were a tangle under one blanket. Kal snored softly through his nose. Nori had rolled half onto Bett's mat, one small hand spread over a rolled bundle of folded clothes shoved under the blanket. From the doorway it might have passed for a sleeping body. From here it looked like laundry with a child reaching for it.

Nori made a small sound in her sleep and dragged her cheek over the blanket, searching. Her hand patted the bundle once, twice, then stayed there. Leni felt a sharp useless urge to go out to the courtyard and hiss at Bett to get back in here right now, which was ridiculous for several reasons, including that Bett was forty and Leni was fifteen and also Bett was plainly doing exactly what she wanted to be doing. The urge stayed anyway. It was one thing to see Bett kissing somebody. It was another to see the blank place that kiss had opened up in her daughter's sleep.

Leni's throat went hot. Bett had planned it, then. Or else this had been going on long enough for Bett to know exactly how much shape a blanket needed to fake a person in lamplight. Leni did not know which version made her stomach turn harder.

By then she was looking for gaps whether she wanted to or not.

She checked the Mera section next. Deshi slept flat and severe, her jaw set even with her mouth closed. Kael was on his stomach with one foot hanging off the mat. Rimi had wrapped herself so tightly in her blanket she looked shrink-wrapped. Suna's hand was spread over Asha's back. At the end of the row, Peyam's mat was empty. His blanket was folded back once, neatly. The square of woven cloth Leni had seen hanging from the side of his bag earlier that evening was gone.

She heard prayer before she found him.

The murmur reached her from the outer corridor, rhythmic without being melodic, nothing like the shrine's morning prayers that Fasa led in a voice that curved and landed and rested before curving again. These words were different. They came in something older and more private than the common language or the shrine's ritual vocabulary — a language with the texture of childhood, kept alive for one purpose and one listener.

He was in the small alcove at the eastern edge of the compound, the nook between the terraced garden wall and the kitchen storage. Rain came down on him steadily, his thin shirt dark and clinging to the bones of his back. He knelt on the missing cloth, a woven square with a frayed edge that looked cut from something larger and better. His hands were pressed together in a shape the shrine's prayers never used. His thumbs tucked inward. His wrists stayed low. The whole posture had a different architecture.

Peyam had smiled and bowed his head through the shrine prayers every day. Leni had seen him do it. Here, in the wet dark with no audience but the stone and whatever listened past it, his mouth moved faster, surer. Rain ran down the side of his face, hung off his chin, dropped to the cloth. He didn't stop to wipe it. One drop kept landing on the back of his neck from the eave. He didn't flinch at that either.

Leni stayed in the corridor and watched until she felt the watching turn rude. Then she backed away before the soaked cloth could print the shape of his knees onto the stone.

When she came back through the sleeping quarters, she looked once more at the Dao mats without meaning to. Nori's hand had slid farther across the bundled clothes. Bett's fake sleeping shape held.

The Tesh section was quiet — Miro snoring softly, Enn on her side, Kiri's tangled hair on the pillow, Gitta's terrifying stillness, Fen on his back with his mouth slightly open. The Mera section had not changed. The Kondo section still had its cut-out gap where Arash should have been.

Then she came to the Parra section, which was barely a section at all: two mats, a travel bag, and a lamp left burning low.

Jin sat on his mat with his back against the stone wall. Melie was asleep beside him, her head near his hip, one hand flung out palm-up as if she'd been reaching for something in a dream. Jin wasn't reading or working or doing anything she could name. He was sitting. His face was visible in the lamplight, and Leni stopped in the shadow of the partition to look at it, because Jin's face during the day was a closed door — polite, minimal, the face of a man who had decided that expression was too expensive. But now, with no audience he knew about, the door was open.

He looked wrecked, with the bruised, emptied face of someone who had been carrying something too heavy for too long and had set it down for a moment just to see what it had done to him. The shadows under his eyes were deep enough to look permanent. His mouth was slightly open, his jaw slack in a way she'd never seen during the day, when the jaw was always working against the hesitation, fighting for each word. Smoke clung faintly to his shirt, sharper than cooking fire, the stale edge of a cigarette long finished. He was still wearing the same clothes he'd had on at the naming.

Melie shifted in her sleep. Her hand closed into a fist, then opened again. Jin's thumb, which had been moving back and forth on her arm, stopped. He bent at once, two fingers going lightly to the side of her throat. Not enough pressure to wake her. Just enough to feel the swallow, the movement of air, the life still going on under the skin. Then he slid the blanket higher over her shoulder and sat back against the wall.

That small check hit Leni harder than anything he'd done in the naming that afternoon. The naming had been all voices and eyes and adults pretending not to stare while Fasa kept asking where Jin's wife was buried and what illness had killed her and why the dates kept slipping in his mouth. This was quieter. Just two fingers to a sleeping kid's throat because once her throat had swollen shut at dinner and his body had apparently never gotten over it.

Leni pressed the heel of her hand against her left eye until the sting eased. She thought about touching his shoulder. Her mother would've done that, or said something. But the sight of Bett's fake body under the blanket was still in her head, and Peyam's wet shirt, and Arash's empty mat, and if she stepped into the lamplight she would become one more person Jin had to hide his face from. She moved on.

She had made it only as far as the walkway when she heard quick wet footsteps behind her.

Leni flattened herself to the stone beside the partition.

Bett came in alone. Her hair was darker with rain now, the ends stuck to the back of her shirt. She did not look toward the courtyard behind her. She went straight to the Dao mats and knelt beside the fake sleeping shape. One hand hovered over Nori's head without touching. With the other she pulled the rolled clothes out from under the blanket and tucked them into a bag in one smooth movement. It was the smoothness that got Leni: not guilt exactly, not panic, just competence.

Nori stirred at once and rolled toward the warmth Bett had brought back in with her. Bett lay down beside her fast enough to make it seem like she'd been there the whole time. Nori's hand slid off the blanket and onto Bett's ribs. Bett covered it with her own hand and stayed still.

Leni waited to see if Hala would wake. Hala didn't. Kal kept snoring. Somewhere in the hall somebody coughed and turned over. Bett's breathing slowed by fractions, not enough to fool anyone who was looking straight at her, enough for everybody else.

Arash's place, Leni thought, was still open on the other side of the hall.

She almost went back to look. The empty Kondo mat had a pull now. She wanted to know whether Arash would slip in dripping and cold and fold himself back into the family row without waking anyone, or whether dawn would find that space untouched and make the whole secret everybody's problem at once. She had taken one step in that direction and stopped, feeling stupid even as she did it. Fifteen-year-olds did not patrol other people's affairs at two in the morning. Then again, fifteen-year-olds also were not usually the only witness to a man sneaking back from the woman he wasn't supposed to want.

She was back in the covered walkway, heading for her family's section, when another sound found her under the rain and the flood's constant grind, or maybe beyond them. It came from the valley floor or from the hills on the far side, carried across the water by some trick of distance and night air that the daytime never allowed. A low concussion. Then another. Then a cluster of three, far enough apart that the flood swallowed the spaces between them.

Orange flared on the horizon. She counted four beats before the sound hit the stone under her feet.

She stood very still and watched the dark past the courtyard wall. Another orange pulse lifted and died. Another delayed thud came up through the wet night and into her ribs. Not thunder, then.

Her father would have a term for it. Ordnance. Vehicles. Something clean-sounding and adult. Leni stayed with the version her body had already picked: somebody was making the dark jump.

The sounds came again, farther off or maybe just aimed somewhere else. The rain intensified for half a minute, driving sideways through the open walkway and soaking her sleeve and the side of her face. She put a hand on the stone pillar because her knees had gone strange on her. The pillar was slick and cold. Water ran down over her wrist and into her cuff. By the time the squall eased, the horizon had gone flat and black again.

Inside, the sleeping quarters had gone thick with used air and body heat. She navigated by memory — past the Kondo section with its hidden absence, past the Mera section where Peyam's mat still waited, to the Okoye mats.

Tomas was still making the sound. The small catch before each exhale. Jin's hesitation, living in her brother's sleep. She looked at him — his face slack and young, his mouth slightly open — and she had the sick little urge to wake him up just to make sure his first word came out as Tomas and not as someone else.

Her mother was awake. Leni saw it in the set of her body — Nessa lay facing the wall, Ada tucked against her chest, but her breathing was too even, too measured. It was the breathing of someone pretending to sleep for the sake of the room. And then Leni saw the other thing: her father's hand, extended across the narrow gap between their mats, resting lightly on her mother's hip. Her mother's hand was over it, fingers laced between his. They were holding on in the dark under the fold of the blanket, low enough that from anywhere else in the room it would look like nothing.

No one who was actually asleep held still that carefully.

Leni looked at their joined hands and had the weirdest flash of the whole night: not that her parents were different from everyone else, but that they weren't. Bett leaving a shape in her blanket. Peyam taking his real prayers out into the rain. Jin keeping watch with the lamp on. Her parents hiding this small desperate grip under the blanket seam as if the room itself couldn't be trusted with it.

She lay down on her mat. The stone was warm where Tomas's body heated it beside her, cooler at the edges where the mat didn't quite cover the floor. She pulled her journal from under the folded cloth she used as a pillow and found the pen tucked in the spine. The light was almost nothing — the nearest oil lamp was four meters away, turned so low it barely threw a shadow — but she'd written in worse conditions. She wrote in the margins sometimes, and sideways, and in letters so small she'd have to squint to read them later. The point was never legibility. The point was getting the words out of her head and onto something that wasn't her.

She didn't think about what to write. That was the rule she'd made for herself early on. If she thought too hard, the sentences started sounding like school — neat, balanced, fake. The journal was where she was allowed to be stupid and messy and unfair and scared.

So she kept the pen moving. She wrote Arash's empty mat before she wrote his mouth on Bett's wrist. She wrote Venn's sleeping hand lying inches from the space where his son should have been. She wrote the bundle of clothes under Bett's blanket and Nori's little palm spread over it. She wrote Peyam's missing cloth and the rain sliding off his jaw while he prayed words the shrine never heard. She wrote Jin touching two fingers to Melie's throat to make sure the air was still there. She wrote the orange flashes on the horizon and the order the sound had arrived in: stone first, then her feet, then her chest. Then she wrote about her parents' hands hidden in the gap between the mats.

She stopped once, her pen resting on the page. The ink was bleeding slightly in the humidity — a soft halo around each letter, as if the words were already trying to blur themselves out. What she'd got down so far looked like notes for evidence. It still wasn't the ugly part.

She went back and wrote harder. She wrote the thought she hadn't had in words until the pen forced it there: that the adults spent all day standing up in the naming and saying who belonged to whom, then spent the night slipping out of those answers. She almost crossed that out for sounding dramatic. Instead she wrote the meaner version underneath it and left that one alone: that whatever this place was doing, it kept going for the kids first. Asha's ear. Kiri's song. Melie's throat. Tomas's breath.

She flipped back through the journal. Older pages crackled damply under her fingers. There was *Nushi* written three times across one page because she had not known how else to hold onto a word nobody could explain. There was the page from the cashew night, her handwriting blown wide and ugly because she'd been writing with her hands still shaking after Melie stopped choking. There was a page where she'd tried to pin down Kiri's humming by drawing stupid little note marks in the margin because the tune had gotten into her head and stayed there all afternoon. Tomas's name had started appearing more often after that, usually next to *weird* or *stop doing that*, which now looked babyish and useless.

The pages made a row of it whether she'd meant them to or not: ear, word, throat, song, breath. She didn't know what it added up to, only that it was adding up.

She turned back to the fresh page and stared at the line about kids. It sat there looking uglier and truer by the second. The adults could clamp their mouths shut for days. They could lie in circles and pray in borrowed words and sneak back to bed before dawn and hold hands under blankets and call all of that private, but the private part never stayed put. It turned up in the children anyway. In sleep. In skin. In the things a body did before anybody had agreed to tell the truth.

That, finally, felt like the night she'd just had.

She crowded the margins with the parts she did not want to lose: the cut-out space in the Kondo row, the fake body in Bett's bed, the wet square of Peyam's cloth dark against stone, Jin's fingers at Melie's throat, her parents' knuckles under the blanket seam. A few times she started a bigger thought and crossed it out so hard the paper nearly tore. Every time the writing tried to become a lesson, she hated it. Every time it went mean and specific, it felt closer.

After a while the page looked less like a diary and more like something she might one day shove at somebody and say there, read it, that was the shape of it. That was enough.

She capped the pen and slid the journal back under her pillow. She lay on her side facing Tomas. She watched his chest rise and catch and fall. After a while she reached over and put her hand on his arm, copying the quiet pressure Jin had used on Melie, and she held it there because the alternative was listening to him breathe and wondering what else the night might put into him before morning.

Outside, rain kept ticking against stone and running from the eaves in ropes. No more distant concussions reached her, at least not before her thoughts started slipping. Somewhere across the hall a lamp wick gave a tiny hiss. Tomas's breath kept catching under her hand, then easing, then catching again. If Arash and Bett were still in the courtyard, if Peyam was still on his cloth, if Jin still had his lamp lit, she couldn't know. The flood ground below the hill until the sound blurred and sleep finally got in.
