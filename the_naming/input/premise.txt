Forty-three people — members of seven unrelated families — are stranded at a hilltop transit shrine when the road below floods during a political crisis. The shrine's custodians enforce a daily protocol of shared prayers, meal preparation, sleeping assignments, and a confessional rite called the naming, in which each family must declare its members and relations. As the days pass, children begin manifesting traits — gestures, allergies, speech patterns — belonging to other families in the compound. The custodians insist this means the families share a forgotten common ancestor, and the naming must be repeated until the true kinship map emerges. The families must decide whether to accept impossible connections or resist a process that will determine who is allowed to leave together when the road reopens.
