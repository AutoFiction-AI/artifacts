# Chapter 9

They smelled Harrow before they saw it. Ozone and char, layered thick enough to close Maren's throat while the town was still just a smudge between hills. Underneath was something sweeter, hot stone baking in late summer, and the particular warmth that quest-light left on surfaces after it had pooled long enough. The road had been getting worse for two hours. Scorch marks overlapped on the packed dirt until the whole path looked like the floor of a forge, burn patterns crossing at angles that made no sense until you understood that heroes didn't clean up after themselves. Some of the marks were old, pale gray, scuffed by cart wheels. Others were darker. One was still warm when Maren's boot caught its edge, and he moved his foot off it the way you'd step off a hot coal.

*Thirty-seven,* Aldric said. *Thirty-eight. Thirty-nine. That one is fresh, less than a day old. Someone deployed a class-three fire projection in the middle of a public road.*

"How do you know it's class three?" Maren muttered, barely moving his lips. He'd gotten better at that, the ventriloquism of talking to his own skull. A week of practice, and he could almost pass for a man muttering to himself, which was at least socially acceptable if not dignified.

*The scorch radius. The char depth. The residual heat signature is still readable on the interface. Do you want the full damage assessment or can I trust you to walk around it?*

"I can see a burn mark on the ground. I've been seeing burn marks."

*You have been seeing scorch marks the way a child sees a hearth fire. I am reading you System-verified damage signatures with class attribution, decay rates, and—*

*Four,* Vess said.

Aldric stopped. Maren waited.

*She does that on purpose,* Aldric said.

*Four heroes ahead. Two at the gate, one on the wall, one buying something from a stall just inside. The gate pair are low-tier, two maybe three. Wall is tier four. The one at the stall is interesting.*

"Interesting how? Interesting meaning they won't bother us, or interesting meaning we turn around right now?"

*Interesting meaning I can't get a clean read at this distance and I don't like not knowing.*

Derry was already sweating. He'd been sweating since they'd spotted the first road marker for Harrow, a charred wooden post with HARROW burned into it in common and a wash of quest-light crawling over the top like wet blue moss. Maren could read the wood. Derry read whatever the light said and went pale, the color draining out of his face and leaving only a thin damp sheen behind.

"Big place," Derry said, which was unnecessary. He wiped his forehead with his sleeve and the sleeve came away dark. "Lot of people, though. That's good, right? More people means it's harder to spot one, harder to pick one person out of—"

"Derry."

"Yeah."

"You're fine."

"I know I'm fine. I'm saying it's good. The crowd thing. That's, yeah."

Derry stopped talking for about fifteen steps, which under these conditions was something like a personal best.

Harrow sat in a shallow bowl between two hills, and the hum hit Maren before he could see the town proper. He'd always heard it, the low vibration that System-heavy areas made, a wasps' nest buried in a wall. In Fenhold it had been background, the kind of sound you stopped noticing the way you stopped noticing your own breathing. Here it was a pressure. It sat behind his sternum and stayed. The air felt denser, like walking into a room where too many people were talking. His ears popped twice on the slope down.

*The interface density is remarkable.* Aldric's tone shifted, less irritation now, more the careful attention of a man cataloguing something he'd only read about in reports. *Quest notifications are layered three deep in some areas. I can barely isolate individual prompts. There, that board ahead, the tall frame by the market square. Do you see it?*

A wooden frame taller than he was stood nailed to a post at the edge of the market square. Things were pinned to it. Papers, maybe. Leather strips. Some kind of thin fabric that moved in the breeze. To him it was a bulletin board outside a village hall, just bigger and busier.

*That is a quest board. There are forty-one active quest postings. Sixteen standard dungeon clearances. Eight contamination reports, and the residue levels listed on some of these are frankly appalling. Nine bounties. Four escort requests. And four that I would classify as recreational.*

"Recreational."

*Someone is offering three gold pieces to recover a cat from a low-tier dungeon mouth. The cat is named Brindlefur. There is an illustration. It appears to be hand-drawn. The cat appears to be wearing a small hat, and I cannot determine whether that is artistic license or an actual hat.*

Vess said nothing, which was its own kind of commentary.

"Three gold for a cat," Maren said.

*The bounty on your head is four thousand gold crowns,* Aldric said. *At three gold per cat, that is one thousand three hundred and thirty-three cats, with one crown left over. In case you needed perspective.*

They passed through the gate without challenge. The two heroes there barely looked at them. One was eating something that dripped grease onto her gauntlet, and the other was arguing with a cart driver about road-damage fees. The argument was loud and specific, involving scorched wheel rims and a dispute over which hero's fire ability had done the actual damage. Maren kept his eyes forward, his pace unremarkable, his mouth shut.

Aldric did not keep his mouth shut.

*Gear shop. Gear shop. Another gear shop. That one specializes in elemental resistances, judging by the signage. Tavern. The sign says "The Cinder" and there are scorch marks on the sign itself, which I suspect is intentional. Tavern. That building has no sign and I have no idea what it is, but there is a significant amount of quest-light pooled against its foundation and someone has drawn a rude picture on the wall in what appears to be—*

The quest-light was the only part Maren could verify independently. It gathered in the gutters like pale, lukewarm water, faintly luminous even in full daylight. In Fenhold it collected in ditches and hollow stumps, barely enough to notice. Here it ran in actual rivulets between cobblestones, and where it pooled against walls the stone had taken on a glow like bone held against a candle flame. Maren stepped over a stream of it that ran across the road from a cracked drain and felt the warmth through his boot.

A man walked past them and raised one hand, casually, the way someone might wave to a friend. Something happened, a sharp localized heat on the left side of Maren's face, a crack like a branch splitting, and then a scorch mark appeared on the wall beside him, black and smoking. The man hadn't broken stride. He was adjusting the strap on his shoulder bag with the hand that had just put a burn mark on a building.

"What," Maren said, very quietly.

*Class-two fire projection. Casual discharge. He appears to be, ah. He moved up one rank on the damage board. He was testing a new calibration.* Aldric's tone carried the specific disbelief of a man who had once served an order that regarded System abilities as solemn responsibilities. *He scorched a wall. For a leaderboard position.*

*Damage rankings,* Vess said. *Overhead. Every building has one. It is a local hero ranking system based on verified damage output. Updated in real time.*

Maren looked up and saw nothing but rafters and awning poles and the pale sky between them. The rankings were invisible to him, like everything else. He was standing in a town built entirely around information he couldn't access, navigating by scorch marks and smell and the commentary of dead people.

*The man who just scorched the wall is ranked sixty-three,* Aldric said. *He is now sixty-two. He appears to be checking. He has stopped walking to verify his new position. He looks pleased.* A pause. *A woman across the street appears displeased. I believe she was sixty-two. She is gesturing at him. He is gesturing back. This may escalate.*

The woman fired something at a wall further down the street. Another scorch mark. The heat reached him from twenty feet away. A child walking between them didn't flinch. Nobody flinched. This was weather to them.

Maren walked under the invisible leaderboards and the visible scorch marks and tried not to think about the bounty floating over his own head.

---

The market square was worse. Heroes were everywhere, not fighting, not questing, just existing in the specific way that people with System-enhanced abilities existed in their natural habitat. Two women were comparing weapon stats loudly enough that Maren could hear numbers he couldn't verify. A man in scaled armor was doing something to the air around his hands that made it shimmer and pop, drawing a small crowd of onlookers who apparently found this entertaining. A knot of younger heroes, teenagers maybe, with the shiny-faced confidence of people who'd just gotten their first class assignments, clustered around the quest board and argued about which dungeon had the best loot-to-risk ratio.

*Knife woman by the wall,* Vess said. *If she decides you're a problem, you bleed before you get your hands up.*

Maren flinched before he realized she wasn't talking about anyone nearby. She was looking through his eyes at a woman leaning against a wall across the square, cleaning under her fingernails with a short knife.

*Short blade. Clean grip. No wasted motion. The man behind her is slower, but he keeps checking the crowd reflections in that shop window, which means he's not here to shop. The boy at the fruit stall in green has training. If he commits, he reaches you before Derry finishes saying your name. The woman with the ale is useless unless she falls on you. The tall one at the corner watches exits first and people second. I don't like him.*

"Thanks."

*You're welcome.*

"Can you stop sorting the whole town into ways I die?"

*No.*

"Is there anyone in this town who couldn't kill me?"

*The cat on the quest board, presumably. Though I lack information about the hat.*

Derry had fallen behind again. Maren turned and found him three stalls back, nodding vigorously at a vendor who was showing him something wrapped in leather. Derry's smile was so wide it had to hurt. His shirt was soaked through at the collar and the dark stain was working its way down between his shoulder blades.

"What are you doing?"

"Just looking!" Derry's voice cracked upward, too loud, too cheerful. "This is, what did you say this was? A grip thing?"

The vendor, a broad woman with forearms like rolled rope, looked at Derry with the flat patience of someone who'd dealt with confused farmers before. "Grip wrap. For polearm users. Absorbs impact on the shaft."

"Right, right, grip wrap! My cousin uses a, he's got a thing, a pole, a spear thing, and he always says the grip is the most important—"

"You don't have a cousin with a spear," Maren said.

Derry's smile didn't falter so much as solidify, like plaster setting wrong. "I might. You don't know all my cousins."

"I know all your cousins. There are two. One raises goats and the other one can't lift his right arm above his shoulder."

The vendor looked between them with the expression of someone deciding whether to be amused or annoyed. Maren put a hand on Derry's arm and steered him away from the stall. Derry resisted for a moment, turned back, waved at the vendor, said "Sorry, we might come back, it's really nice stuff, the grip thing," and then let himself be moved.

"You have to stop doing that," Maren said, low.

"Doing what?"

"Buying things. Talking to people. Smiling like that."

"Like what?"

"Like a man who's about to be arrested and wants everyone to think he's having a nice day."

Derry wiped his face with both hands. The sweat came right back, beading along his hairline within seconds. "I'm trying. I am. I just, every time someone looks at me, my mouth opens, and it starts. I told that woman back there about Pell's counting. With the beads. The little counting frame with the beads. She didn't ask. She was selling rope. I told a rope vendor about my daughter's arithmetic."

*His cortisol levels must be extraordinary,* Aldric observed. *The man is producing enough sweat to—*

"Not helpful," Maren said, to Aldric and to no one visible, which earned him a glance from a passing hero that he pretended not to notice.

They needed food that would keep, and they needed to know which road past Harrow went toward the Crook without cutting through active dungeon territory. They needed to accomplish both without anyone looking at Maren long enough to see what wasn't there.

*The dried-goods vendor three stalls east,* Aldric said. *But the man sitting beside him, armored, large, on a crate, has a passive scan ability. I can see the active aura. He is reading the classification of everyone who passes within ten yards.*

"So we go around."

*The next nearest food vendor is across the square. Past the quest board. Past the two taverns. Past the table.*

Maren looked. The table was, in fact, on fire. A woman in scalemail sat next to it, laughing, while a man in a leather coat poured ale over the flames. The ale did nothing useful. A third person, a boy maybe sixteen, was trying to smother the fire with his jacket, and the jacket was also now on fire.

"Do they know," Maren said, "that the table is on fire?"

*They appear to consider it recreational.*

"And the jacket?"

*Also recreational, I must assume, given that no one is alarmed. The boy is laughing now. The jacket is ruined. I want you to know that in fourteen years of service I never once set furniture on fire without tactical justification.*

Derry had stopped beside a stall selling polishing compound and was asking the vendor about the proper way to maintain leather boots, which was a topic he did actually know something about, and the vendor was answering with the weary tolerance of a man who'd had this exact conversation six hundred times. Derry's hands were in his pockets, but Maren could see the tremor in his forearms.

Maren reached the food vendor on the far side of the square, a thin man with sun-bleached hair and a stall that smelled like salt and old smoke. His provisions looked road-worthy: dried meat, hard bread, something in waxed paper that might have been cheese or might have been soap. Maren was reaching for his money when the vendor's eyes shifted, not to Maren's face but to a point slightly above and to the left of his head, the place where, Aldric had explained weeks ago, System-sighted people saw the classification data that accompanied every person like a name pinned to a shirt.

The vendor looked at the spot, then looked again. His expression did something subtle and wrong, not suspicion, but the face a person makes when they reach for a step that isn't there.

"Class?" the vendor asked in the same conversational tone someone in any other town might use for *where are you from.*

"Sorry?"

"Your class. Just, you passing through? Most of my regulars are ranged-damage types, they like the jerky. Melee goes for the bread. What's your build?"

The hum in his chest intensified, though that might have been his heart. "Support class," he said, with the easy tired confidence of a man stating something too boring to lie about. "We're just passing through. Need road food."

"Support should still display, though." The vendor squinted at the empty space. "Everyone displays. Even noncombat roles. Healers, buffers, scouts. They all show a classification."

"Gear interference. It's been, look, it's a whole thing with my interface. It's a known issue."

The lie came smooth, but the vendor's hand was slow reaching for the jerky, and his eyes kept drifting back to the void where Maren's classification should have been, the way a person's eyes will keep returning to a gap in a fence where a board ought to be. Not alarmed yet. Bothered.

*He is not satisfied,* Aldric said. The sarcasm was gone. *He is going to—*

"Hey, Korr?" The vendor turned his head toward the armored man on the crate three stalls over. "Can you run a—"

Maren put coins on the counter, too many, enough to buy the vendor's entire stock of jerky twice over. "Just the bread and the jerky. Keep the change. We're in a rush."

The vendor looked at the coins, then at the void, then at the coins again. Greed and curiosity fought a brief visible war across his face, and greed won, but not by enough to stop his mouth.

"I've never seen a blank read," he said, handing over the food. "Not once. Not even broken gear gives you a blank. You'd get an error code, at least. Some kind of—"

Maren took the food and walked away. Behind him the vendor was still talking, not to Maren anymore but to the woman at the next stall, and Maren heard the word *blank* and then *weird* and then something he couldn't catch because Derry was beside him now, breathing hard, clutching a small jar of boot polish he'd apparently purchased.

"You bought boot polish," Maren said.

"She was very convincing."

"You're wearing sandals."

Derry looked down at his feet. He was, in fact, back in sandals. His boots were tied to the outside of his pack, which made the boot polish worse. He looked from the jar to the boots and then back again, as if the objects had conspired against him.

"We're leaving," Maren said. "Now. Before—"

*The woman at the stall,* Vess said. *The one the vendor spoke to. She has turned her head. She is looking at you.*

"Before that."

---

Maren didn't turn around. The road north forked past a tavern with a green-painted sign; he could see that much. The left fork should take them toward dungeon territory and eventually the Crook. He angled toward it.

*She has stood up,* Vess said. *The woman. Blue cloak. She was seated at the stall where the man with the passive scan sits. She looked at you when you passed and now she is walking.*

"Could be nothing."

*She is walking at pursuit pace. Not running. Matching your speed at fifteen yards. She signaled with her off hand, two fingers and a lateral sweep. That is a flanking directive.*

The market noise continued around them, the burning table, the invisible leaderboard overhead, the boy with the smoldering jacket, who had given up on his jacket and was now watching it burn on the cobblestones with his arms crossed. None of it had changed, but the air around Maren had turned thin, and the hum in his chest had become a specific directional pressure, like a finger pressing on his sternum.

*Two more are moving from the tavern entrance on your right,* Vess said. *They were seated. They are not seated now. The one in front is tier four, minimum. He moves like someone who has done this before.*

Derry hadn't heard any of it, the fragments were inside Maren's skull, but he'd seen something change in Maren's face, or his pace, or the way his shoulders had come up, because Derry's own face drained to the color of old cheese.

"What?" Derry whispered. "What is it? What's—"

"Don't look behind us."

Derry immediately looked behind them.

"I said—"

"Sorry, sorry, I know, I just, there's a woman, she's sort of, she's walking fast—"

*She is not the primary concern,* Vess said. *The man from the tavern is. He has a scan active. A real one, not the passive ambient type. He is actively querying you. And he is getting—*

*Nothing,* Aldric said. His composure had cracked and the words came fast. *He is getting nothing. The physical description matches half the men in this region, that was never enough. But a blank read is unique. She spotted the void, not your face.*

A voice behind them, not shouting but pitched to carry across a market without sounding like a command: "Hey. Support class. With the blank. Stop walking, please."

*Please,* Vess noted. *That is someone trained to de-escalate before escalating.*

Maren did not stop. He walked faster. The distinction between walking fast and running was closing rapidly, and Derry was beside him, breathing in those awful wet hitches, the boot polish still in his hand.

"Oh god," Derry said. "Oh god, oh shit, Maren—"

"Walk."

"I'm walking. I'm walking, but they're, she's—"

*Run,* Vess said.

Vess only spent single-word instructions when the decision was already made. If she said run, walking had expired a second ago.

They ran. Maren's pack bounced against his spine. Derry's sandals slapped the cobblestones, too loud, too flat, the wrong footwear for flight and the wrong man for it too. Behind them the voice called out again, louder now, and other voices picked it up. Not a mob, not yet, but the specific rising attention of a crowd that had noticed something interesting happening and was turning to watch.

*Left at the fork,* Aldric said, his formality abandoned entirely, directions barked like a man reading a map while the house burned. *Left, then straight, the road opens past the—*

*Right,* Vess said.

*I said left.*

*You said left because the road goes north. I said right because there are three heroes between us and your left fork and two of them have already turned.*

Maren went right. The turn took him between a shuttered gear shop and a squat building that stank of animal fat and tallow. Quest-light pooled in the alley's gutter, warm against his ankles when he splashed through it, and the scorch marks on the alley walls were so dense they'd merged into a continuous dark stain, years of hero traffic burned into the stone like a record of carelessness.

Derry was behind him, still running and still making that sound, not panting but something closer to the noise a man makes when he's trying very hard not to scream and the effort is coming out through his breathing instead.

*Alley opens onto a side street in twelve yards,* Vess said. *Wall breach beyond it. Half-blocked by collapse, but still passable.*

"How do you know that from in here?"

*Interface damage report. No repair ticket. Nobody bothered.*

They burst out of the alley onto the side street and Maren understood at once why the breach had stayed open. One side of the wall had been melted smooth as candle wax. The other had slumped into a heap of blackened timbers and broken stone. Someone had shoved a pair of planks across the rubble to turn it into a rough shortcut, and right now a woman with a handcart was trying to drag cabbages through while a mule balked beside her.

Behind them a whistle cut the air. Another answered farther up the wall.

A boy in a soot-streaked apron tore past the mouth of the alley, sprinting uphill toward the proper gate. "Blank read!" he shouted. "North side breach! Blank read moving north!"

The woman with the cart froze. The mule rolled an eye white.

"Move," Maren said.

She did not move fast enough. Maren hit the handcart with his shoulder, hard enough to jolt his teeth. The cart tipped. Cabbages burst across the stones. The woman screamed at him. The mule jerked sideways and slammed its flank against the broken wall, blocking the gap for one stupid terrible second.

*Three seconds,* Vess said. *Maybe four.*

Maren grabbed the mule's harness and yanked. Derry squeezed past him, hit the planks wrong, and went to one knee in the rubble with both hands out, skinning one palm open on the stone.

"Up," Maren said.

Derry made a frightened choking sound. Maren caught the back of his shirt and hauled him bodily through the gap. His own pack snagged on a splintered beam hard enough to wrench him backward. Cloth tore. One loaf of bread and half the jerky spilled into the dust and the warm luminous slime crusted along the breach stones. He let them go.

A horn sounded from somewhere deeper in Harrow. Then another, farther off, nearer the north road.

They stumbled down the outer slope of rubble and hit dirt. Maren looked back once, just long enough to see the proper north gate fifty yards over and a rider already breaking from it at a gallop, leaning low over the horse with a satchel bouncing against his hip.

*Courier,* Aldric said. His voice had gone cold again. *The relay is already moving.*

They ran until the wall fell behind them and the road bent between scrub hills. Only then did Maren force himself to slow. His lungs burned. Derry was cursing under his breath now, clutching his scraped palm against his shirt.

"Walk," Maren said. "Normal. Like we belong here."

Derry was doubled over beside him, hands on his knees, making bellows sounds. The boot polish was gone. So was half their food.

"I can't. I don't remember what normal—"

"Walk like you're going to check on the cow. The cow got out again. You're annoyed but not surprised."

Derry made a noise that was either a laugh or the first stage of something worse, but he straightened up and walked. The walk was terrible, stiff-armed, too rigid, a man wearing the idea of casual movement like an ill-fitting coat, but it carried him forward along the road beyond Harrow's walls.

The hum lessened. Quest-light thinned to occasional dull patches in the roadside ditches. The scorch marks became older, paler, spaced apart enough that the packed dirt between them looked like actual road again.

*Confirmed,* Aldric said after a silence just long enough to hurt. *Multiple witnesses. Every town between here and the Crook will have the read before we get there.*

Maren didn't answer. He walked. The bread and jerky in his pack were lighter now. His money was lighter too.

Derry was walking beside him with the rigid mechanical gait of a man whose legs hadn't caught up with the fact that they were no longer sprinting. His hands shook, not with the subtle tremor from the market but with a proper shake, the kind that made his fingers useless, and he pressed them flat against his thighs to make them stop and they didn't stop.

"Maren," Derry said.

"Yeah."

"I talked to a woman about cloaks."

"I know."

"I don't wear cloaks. I've never, I told her about Pell. About the counting beads. She was selling cloaks and I told her my daughter was learning to count. And then I bought boot polish, and my boots were on the pack the whole time." He looked down at his hand. "I lost the polish anyway."

"Yeah."

"My hands won't stop."

Maren looked at Derry's hands. The shake was bad, fingers spread, tendons standing out, the kind of tremor you got when the body's terror outran the mind's ability to manage it. Maren's own hands were steady, and he didn't know what that meant. Maybe the killing two weeks ago had burned something out, some connection between fear and its physical expression. Or maybe the fear had just moved deeper, past the hands and the sweat and the shaking, down into the flat dry recognition that things were worse now and would keep getting worse, and the road ahead led into dungeon territory because the road behind had a description of him running along it faster than his feet could.

*The Crook is four days north,* Vess said. *Five if the road has deteriorated past dungeon-approach standards.*

*If we reach the Crook,* Aldric said.

Vess said nothing.

Behind them, faint but carrying, a horn answered another horn. Not market noise anymore. Not rumor either.

Maren walked north. The scorch marks on the road grew older and fewer, until there were none at all and the road was just dirt, and the dirt was just the world, and the world didn't care what the System thought he was or wasn't.

But the people in it did. That was the problem. That had always been the problem.
