# Chapter 13

The first cool morning came with mist. Not the stinging wet of a storm front but something thinner, like breath caught between the hills and held there. Maren woke with his back against a stone wall — the remains of a waymarker shelter, three walls and no roof — and found Derry already up, sitting with his pack on his knees, facing the road.

Not the fire. The road.

Maren catalogued that the way he'd catalogued a hundred tells across eleven years of fraud: the set of Derry's shoulders, the packed bag, the direction of his attention. The grammar of a man preparing to move. He filed it and said nothing.

"Morning," Derry said, without turning. "There's water in the skin. I didn't — I boiled some earlier, it should still be warm. Warmish."

"Thanks."

Maren drank. The water tasted like the iron pot Derry carried, which tasted like every meal they'd shared since Fenhold. He sat down across the dead fire and pulled his boots on, working the left one over the blister on his heel that had gone from raw to calloused over nineteen days.

*He packed before he built the fire,* Aldric observed. *The fire was afterthought. The pack was first.*

Maren didn't answer. The paladin had been quieter since his own revelation — the System as containment, the wall, the crack, the architecture visible only from inside a blind man's skull — and when he did speak it was with a kind of exhausted precision, as if each sentence cost him something he couldn't replenish. The constant sarcasm had thinned. What replaced it was worse: sincerity.

*I merely note the sequence,* Aldric said. *You've already drawn your own conclusion.*

"So," Maren said aloud, rubbing his hands together against the chill. The air smelled different this morning — less of the baked-road dust that had been their constant companion, more of something turning. Green things starting to let go. "How far do you figure we are from the Crook?"

Derry's shoulders shifted. "Three days, maybe? Four, if the road keeps going to shit. I asked at that farm stand yesterday — the woman with the, the bad teeth — she said the valley's about two days past the split, but I don't know which split she meant."

"The one by the creek crossing, probably."

"Right. Yeah. Probably."

Silence. Usually Derry would've stuffed something into it by now, whether it was weather, bread, or Pell. This time he kept facing the road.

The silence held.

*Vess,* Maren thought, directing it inward. *You're quiet too.*

*Listening,* she said. Just the one word, and nothing after it.

They packed and walked. The road between Harrow and the Crook was a generous word for what they followed — a track beaten into the ground by sin-eater boots and contamination-survey teams, pocked with old scorch marks from hero patrols that came this way and left their residue burned into the dirt. The terrain rose and fell in long, rolling swells, treeline thickening as they moved deeper into dungeon territory. Quest-light pooled in the ditches like rainwater that glowed faintly from underneath. Maren could see it — had always been able to see the raw shimmer, just not the data overlaid on it that everyone else took for granted.

The hum was louder here. A low-frequency drone that sat behind his teeth and in the hinge of his jaw. System activity, Aldric had explained. The infrastructure maintaining itself. The sound of the wall holding.

Derry walked a half-step ahead. Not alongside. Ahead.

Maren noticed. Of course he noticed. He'd spent his adult life reading the geometry of human bodies — the distance between people, the angle of a turned shoulder, the way someone's stride changed when they wanted to be somewhere else. It was how he'd faked rituals for eleven years: not through knowledge of the System but through knowledge of people. How their faces moved when they were afraid, when they were grieving, when they believed something was happening even if it wasn't. Derry's body was saying something his mouth hadn't gotten to yet.

Around midday they stopped at a stream crossing where the water ran clear over flat stones. Derry pulled out bread and the end of a hard cheese he'd traded for at the farm stand. He handed Maren his portion without meeting his eyes, then sat on a rock facing downstream. Away from Maren.

"Cheese isn't bad," Maren said.

"Yeah, she said it was — I don't know, sheep? Goat? One of those." Derry bit into his bread. Chewed. Looked at the water. "Pell would like this stream. She's at that age where she wants to put her hands in everything. Lenna found her elbow-deep in the flour bin last — well, before we left. Covered in it. Looked like a little ghost."

The image landed in Maren's chest like a stone dropped into water. He could see it: the small hands white with flour, the round startled face, Lenna's exasperation that was really delight. Derry had told him about Pell learning to count back at the stream after the killing — the numbers that didn't go in order, the cow that was always "two cow" regardless of how many there were. That conversation had been warm. This one had a blade in it.

"She's probably counting past ten by now," Maren said.

"Yeah." Derry's voice caught, just barely. He covered it with another bite of bread. "Yeah, she might be. Lenna said — in her last letter, before — she said Pell counts the chickens every morning. Keeps getting a different number because they won't hold still."

"Sounds right."

"She only miscounts in one direction, though. Always more than there are. Lenna says it's optimism." Derry's mouth did something that was trying to be a smile. "I think she just can't tell the brown ones apart."

Maren laughed. It came out smaller than he meant it to, and the sound died in the space between them like something that had misjudged the distance.

*He hasn't looked at you once,* Vess said.

*I know,* Maren thought.

*You're not going to say anything.*

*No.*

*That's either kind or cowardly. I can't tell which.*

Maren didn't answer her either.

They walked through the afternoon. The road deteriorated — ruts deepening, scorch marks older and more layered, the ground itself taking on the faint discoloration that came from years of quest-light exposure. A darker hue, like a bruise that had settled into the landscape's skin. The trees were starting to turn, just barely. A single branch of yellow in a canopy of green, like a warning flag someone had hung and forgotten.

Derry tried to make a joke about the smell — the ozone-sweet contamination stink that hung heavier in these lower valleys — and laughed before he got to the end of it. Maren gave it what he could. The sound died fast.

*The road forks in roughly a mile,* Aldric said. *The left branch descends toward the valley she described. I can see increased System density in that direction — the interface thickens.*

"There's a fork coming up," Maren relayed. "Left takes us down toward the Crook."

"Right," Derry said. "Good."

He said it the way you'd say good to a doctor telling you the results were back.

They made camp before the fork, in a clearing where someone had built a fire ring out of flat stones. The ground was tramped down — other travelers, other sin-eaters maybe, had used this spot before. Derry gathered wood with more energy than the task required, moving through the treeline with the focused busyness of a man who needed to keep his hands occupied. Maren watched him from where he sat building the fire and tried to remember the last time Derry had started a conversation that wasn't about food, weather, or the road.

Three days ago. At least three days ago. Maybe four. Before Aldric's revelation — before Maren had sat in the dark with the paladin's description of the System's architecture ringing through him like a struck bell, containment structure, wall, crack, you are the gap — Derry had asked him something real. He'd asked whether Maren thought they'd ever go home. And Maren, still vibrating with everything Aldric had shown him, had said something honest. He couldn't remember the exact words. Something about not knowing if the person who left Fenhold could go back to it.

Derry hadn't asked a real question since.

The fire caught and they ate. Dried meat, the last of the cheese, water from the stream. Derry faced the road again. Maren faced the fire and didn't comment on the arrangement.

"I was thinking," Derry said.

Maren's hands went still around the water skin. Here it was.

"About — just, logistics. How far you said the Crook is, and what happens when you get there, with the sin-eaters and the — the examination, or whatever Thessa's going to — and I was thinking about what makes sense. For, you know. Going forward."

His voice kept starting over. He pushed the fire with the stick, added clauses, backed away from them, started again.

"Derry."

"Because the thing is, and I've been — I know this isn't, I don't want you to think —"

"Derry."

Derry stopped. He was holding a stick he'd been using to push the fire around. His knuckles were white on it. In the firelight, Maren could see the sweat on his neck despite the cool air. The open, friendly face that had made him such a good partner in the fraud — that trustworthy farmer's face, the face that made marks relax — was trying to hold an expression it hadn't been built for.

"Just say it," Maren said. He kept his voice even. Warm, even. The con man's gift: giving people permission to do the thing they were going to do anyway, and making them feel like it was okay.

"I can't come to the Crook."

There it was. Maren's chest did something — not tightening, not the performative physical anguish he'd faked during rituals, but a slow, heavy settling. Like a door closing in a room he'd known was being emptied.

"Yeah," Maren said. "I know."

Derry's face crumpled. Not into tears — Derry wasn't a crier, at least not in front of people — but into a relief so naked it was worse than crying. His whole body sagged, the stick dropping from his hand, and he said, "Shit, Maren, I've been trying to — for days, I've been —"

"I know."

"Pell's going to be — Lenna's going to need — and the longer I'm out here the more —" He stopped. Started again. "She's turning three while we're on the road. I won't be there. I don't even know if Lenna did anything for it, because who's going to help her? Her mother's dead and my brother is — well, you know what Hest is like, he'd forget his own name if it wasn't on his mailbox, and Pell needs —"

"She needs her father," Maren said.

Derry looked at him. In the firelight his eyes were wet, but the tears stayed where they were. "I talked to Caul."

The settling in Maren's chest went colder. The farm stand, the packed bag, Derry facing the road all morning: loose facts, suddenly not loose anymore.

"When."

"Two days ago. At the farm stand. When you were — the woman was showing you the road on her — she had that map. I went around back." Derry's voice was steady now, the way it got when the hard part was done and all that was left was the reporting. "He was there. Or, he found me, more like. He must've been tracking us. Following."

*Obviously,* Vess said, inside Maren's skull. *Caul is a ranger. Of course he's been tracking.*

*Be quiet,* Maren thought.

*I'm noting operational fact, not offering commentary.*

*I said be quiet.*

She went quiet.

"What did you tell him," Maren said. It wasn't a question. The flatness was involuntary — his voice losing its texture the way it did when the performance stopped and the real thing underneath couldn't figure out what shape to take.

"I didn't — I didn't sell you out. I didn't tell him where we were camped or how fast we're moving or anything like — I told him the direction. That we were heading for the Crook. That's all." Derry's hands were gripping his own knees now. "He already knew, Maren. He already knew where you were going. He just wanted confirmation."

"And he gave you safe passage."

"He said I could go home. That the bounty's on you, not on me, and if I walked south he'd make sure no hero patrol gave me trouble." Derry swallowed. "He said Pell deserved a father who was alive."

The specific cruelty of that — Caul using Derry's daughter's name, a name Derry must have given him because you don't use a child's name as leverage unless someone has handed it to you first — sat in Maren's mouth like a coin. Not anger. Something flatter and more permanent.

"I'm sorry," Derry said. "Maren, I'm — you're my best friend, you're my — we've been doing this since we were kids, and I wouldn't —"

"Don't."

"I just —"

"Did you tell him about the fork?"

Derry blinked. "What?"

"The split by the creek. Left branch. Did you give him that too?"

Derry swallowed. "Yeah. I said left. I said three days if the road held, four if it kept getting worse. I told him that was what you'd heard. That was all, Maren. I swear that was all."

The stone turned once against Maren's palm. Left fork. Three or four days. Enough to stop Caul ranging wide and start him walking like a man who knew where the road narrowed.

*He no longer has to search every approach,* Aldric said. *He can commit to the valley.*

*Or wait where the road pinches and let you come to him,* Vess said.

Derry scrubbed both hands over his face. "I wasn't trying to make it easier for him."

"No." Maren looked at the fire. "You were trying to get home."

Derry made a small, miserable sound and nodded.

"Leave before light," Maren said. "Go south from the fork and keep going. If you see a patrol before the main road, use Caul's name before they decide to make a story out of it."

Derry stared at him. "You're giving me instructions."

"I'm telling you how not to die between here and Fenhold."

They sat with the fire between them. The mist was coming back, settling into the valleys below the treeline, and the quest-light in the ditches along the road glowed through it — pale, diffuse, like lanterns in a distant house. Maren could hear the hum louder than usual, a vibration that lived in the base of his skull and the roots of his teeth. The System maintaining itself. The wall holding. For now.

The cut cheese sat between them on a flat stone and sweated in the firelight.

"When are you going," Maren said.

"In the morning. Before light, probably. If I head south from the fork I can make the main road in a day and a half, and from there it's — well, it's a long walk. But it's toward home." Derry pulled his knees up. He looked smaller than Maren remembered. Stocky, solid Derry with his farmer's hands and his open face — he looked like a man who'd been carrying something for weeks and was about to set it down, and the relief hadn't arrived yet because the guilt was still in the way.

"Take the cheese knife," Maren said. "And the extra water skin. And the — that fire-starting kit you made in Harrow."

"I'm not taking your —"

"I'll be at the Crook in three days. There'll be other sin-eaters. I'll manage."

"I came because you needed me."

"I know." The stone kept turning in Maren's hand. "I wish you'd told me before you told him."

Derry opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "Remember that well-cleaning we did when we first started charging? The widow with the red scarf. You dropped the ash packet straight into the water and told her the blessing had to go all the way down."

Maren huffed once through his nose. "You were the one hanging off the bucket rope with the rake."

"Because you said I had longer arms."

"You did."

"You let go."

"I slipped."

"You slipped on purpose."

"I remember, Derry."

Something in Maren's tone made Derry stop talking. Not the words — the fracture underneath them, the place where the performance cracked and the real thing showed through. Maren's most honest moments were his least controlled, and right now his voice had a grain in it like wet wood splitting.

Derry stood up. He crossed the fire ring and sat down next to Maren — beside him, not across from him, the first time in days — and neither of them said anything for a while. The mist thickened. The quest-light pulsed once, slowly, like a heartbeat.

"Lenna's going to ask," Derry said at last. "What do I tell her when I get back?"

Maren felt something hot behind his eyes and blinked it back. He looked down at the stone in his hand. Old char blackened his thumb where he'd been rubbing at it.

"Tell her I'm still north," he said. "Don't tell her more than that."

"And Pell?"

"Tell her it isn't her fault she can't count the brown chickens. They don't hold still."

Derry's face changed again. Smaller this time. More dangerous. "You remember that?"

"Yeah. And tell Lenna I'm sorry about the herb box by the door."

"The — what?"

"The one the slugs kept getting into. You said the chives never had a chance."

Derry stared at him.

"You kept talking about home," Maren said. "Pell, the chickens, that box by the door. I heard you."

Derry's hand landed on Maren's shoulder. Heavy, rough, the hand of a man who knew soil and livestock and the specific weight of holding a child while she slept. It stayed there for exactly long enough, and then Derry stood up and went to his side of the fire and lay down with his back to Maren and pulled his blanket up and said nothing more.

Maren sat with the stone until the fire died to coals. The mist settled. The hum continued.

*You should sleep,* Aldric said.

*In a minute.*

*There is nothing productive about —*

*In a minute, Aldric.*

The paladin went quiet. Vess was already silent — had been since Maren told her to stop. Her silence wasn't absence; it was the specific withdrawal of a woman who'd been trained to vacate a space without leaving it. She was still there, listening, but she'd given him the room.

Maren set the stone back in the fire ring and lay down. The ground was cold through his blanket. The blister on his heel throbbed in time with his heartbeat, and the hum of the System sat under both rhythms like a third pulse.

In the morning, Derry was gone.

His side of the fire ring was neat — blanket folded, ground swept clean of crumbs. He'd left the iron pot by the cold ashes with the extra water skin propped against it. He'd taken the cheese knife, and the fire-starting kit, and nothing else.

He'd brushed out the heel marks around his bedroll too. Not enough to fool a ranger. Enough that Maren knew he'd tried.

Maren stood in the grey pre-dawn and looked at the road forking below the clearing. The left branch descended into the valley toward the Crook. The right branch disappeared into the mist, heading south toward the main road and Fenhold.

He could feel the place where Derry had been like a gap in the camp. The clearing sounded wrong without Derry talking to the fire before it was even lit.

*The iron pot,* Aldric said.

"I can see that."

Maren picked up the pot. It was cold and heavier than it looked. He slid it into his pack on top of the ceramic bowl. Iron tapped clay, then settled.

*The left fork,* Vess said. Speaking for the first time since he'd silenced her. *The Crook. Three days.*

"Yeah." Maren shouldered the pack. It rode differently now, one bedroll gone and the pot knocking low against the bowl. "Three days."

He took the left fork. The road dropped into thicker mist and stronger contamination stink, sweet and rotten at once. Quest-light burned pale in the ditches, and the right fork vanished behind him before the sun got over the ridge.

Caul had the direction now. That meant less guesswork for the ranger and less room for Maren to disappear.

Maren hitched the pack higher, felt iron knock once against ceramic, and kept walking.
