# Chapter 3

Derry had brought eggs — two of them, brown-shelled, still warm from Marta's henhouse — and he'd been talking about leeks for what might have been five minutes. The whole west bed had gone to leeks this year. He didn't know why. Soil, maybe. Or Pell had been watering everything the same amount because she wasn't three yet and believed all plants were equally thirsty, and Lenna was too tired in the evenings to correct her, and anyway who needed that many leeks? You could only make so much soup.

Maren cracked one of the eggs into a cup and drank it raw. The yolk was warm and thick and sat heavy in his empty stomach.

*He has been talking for four minutes,* Aldric said. *I have been timing it. He has not paused for breath. The man is terrified.*

Maren knew. He'd known Derry for most of his adult life, and the leek monologue was a tell — the worse things got, the more Derry talked about his garden, his daughter, what the cow did yesterday. The content didn't matter. What mattered was the noise, the filling of space, the refusal to let silence in where fear could pool.

"So the farmer's family," Derry said, his tone shifting abruptly, the way you set down something light and pick up something heavy. "They're telling people you had a seizure during the ritual."

"I heard."

"They're not angry, if that helps. More concerned. Breda's been saying you might've been touched — she means it the religious way, not the —" He waggled his hand. "You know."

*Touched,* Aldric said. There was something in his tone that sat between contempt and recognition, like a word he knew being used in a way he hadn't expected.

Maren drank the other egg. It was cooler than the first, the yolk thinner. He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand.

"I'm handling it," he said.

"You're handling it."

"Yeah."

"Maren, you were looking at the table just now."

"I look at the table all the time. It's where the table is."

"You were looking at it the way people look at someone who's talking. You tilted your head. Like you were listening to something near the table, except nothing was near the table, so."

The dwelling was quiet except for a chicken outside performing its daily complaint about the existence of morning. Light came through the single window in a bar that landed on the bowl — the ceramic one, the ritual prop, sitting on the table where it always sat, ordinary and specifically worthless.

"Derry —"

"And yesterday. In the square." Derry's voice had lowered, not for secrecy but for the weight the volume implied. "The threat notification that came through around midmorning — standard dungeon-mouth alert, nothing urgent. You flinched. You looked up and to the right, which is where the notification renders on the interface, and then you looked away like you'd caught yourself."

Maren said nothing.

"I've been standing next to you for eleven years while those come and go. I'm the one who tells you what they say. I've described hundreds of those notifications to you and you've never once — not once — reacted to one on your own. You've never looked where they actually are, because you can't see where they are. That's the whole point. That's why we work."

*He is more observant than you give him credit for,* Aldric said.

The silence had weight to it. Morning heat and dust and the faintly sweet smell of contamination that drifted from the north when the wind came off the fields wrong.

"Something answered," Maren said.

Derry went still. His hands, which had been pulling at a loose thread on his sleeve, stopped.

"At the ritual. Over the farmer. I wasn't — I mean, it was fake, same as always, but something — someone —" Maren stopped and started again, a worse sentence but a more honest one. "I've got a dead man in my head. A paladin. He died and ended up in the contamination and when I did the ritual he came through. He's been in my skull for two days and he reads me the interface."

He waited for the reaction. Derry's face went through a sequence — disbelief, then calculation, then a kind of stalled horror that didn't resolve into any single expression but sat there like a sum that wouldn't balance.

"A paladin," Derry said.

"Yeah."

"Talking to you."

"All the time. He's got opinions about what I eat, how I walk, whether I'm —" Maren cut himself off because the sentence was heading toward the fact that Aldric had critiqued his posture during urination yesterday and that was not a conversation for this morning. "The point is he can see the System. He reads it to me. And I'm reacting to what he says without meaning to — he tells me something and I flinch, or I look, and I can't stop doing it."

*You could, in fact, stop it,* Aldric said. *With discipline. Which you do not have.*

"He says it's my fault," Maren added.

Derry rubbed the back of his neck. His hand was trembling, a fine vibration visible in the sweat that caught the morning light on his knuckles.

"So the fraud's done."

"The fraud was done the second I threw up in that ditch."

"And he's not — this paladin isn't going away."

*I would prefer to go away. I am in this room against my preference and my will, same as every room I have been in for the past two days.*

"He's not going away."

Derry looked at the eggs, the bowl, the stained table. His gaze snagged on each thing in turn and stayed there a beat too long, as if he were already trying to remember the room from someplace else.

"All right," he said. "All right. So what do we do?"

***

They walked to the well because water still had to be fetched, even now, even with a dead paladin commentating on the structural integrity of the well rope and the ambient contamination levels of the drainage ditch and the specific threat classifications of three birds sitting on the chapel roof.

*Residual contamination at the northwest field boundary reads point-three,* Aldric said. *Climbing. Yesterday it was point-two-six. Consistent with uncleared accumulation from six to eight months of inadequate sin-eating maintenance.*

Maren pulled the bucket up. The rope was rough and familiar in his hands and the bucket came heavy with cold water that sloshed against its sides.

"Six to eight months of inadequate maintenance," he muttered. "That'd be me."

*I was not going to say that.*

"You were absolutely going to say that."

Breda was on her doorstep with her morning broth, chin tipped toward the square, following everything in it without seeming to do more than move her eyes.

"Morning, Maren. You're looking better."

"Thanks, Breda. Bit of a rough patch." The performance rose automatically — easy warmth, a slightly sheepish smile, his shoulders loose and open. He'd been performing since seventeen and the muscles of it were deeper than thought.

"My sister's boy had something similar," Breda said. "Fits. He grew out of it."

"I'm twenty-eight, Breda."

"So was he, at the time. A man can grow out of things at any age." She smiled and sipped her broth.

Derry had stopped a few paces back, hands in his pockets, eyes moving between Breda, the well, and the lane out of the square.

"She doesn't know anything," Maren said quietly once they were past.

"I know she doesn't." Derry's voice was tight. "That's not — I wasn't thinking she —"

"Then what?"

"I'm thinking about who does know. You flinched in the square yesterday, Maren. I'm the one who noticed because I know what to look for, but I'm not the only person in this village with eyes."

Maren splashed water on his face. The cold sat on his skin, a sensation with no data attached — no System overlay, no numbers. Just cold water on warm skin on a summer morning.

*Maren.*

Aldric's tone had changed. The running commentary — the contamination indices and threat assessments and moral objections to Maren's hygiene — had been constant for two days, a stream that Maren had started learning to push to the back of his attention the way a person stops hearing a clock. But this was different. This was the voice Aldric used when the formal architecture of his speech went rigid, when the sentences got shorter and the words got more precise, and Maren had known him for only two days but he already understood that Aldric getting more formal was the same as a normal person screaming.

*The System has been auditing a contradictory record since last night,* Aldric said. *Yours. The queries were broad and regional, but not public. I did not tell you because until it resolved there was no classification, no bounty, only the fact that the System had noticed something it could not sort.*

Maren dried his face on his sleeve. Derry was watching him, waiting, reading the pause the way he'd learned to read Maren's pauses over eleven years of partnership.

"What?" Derry said.

"Hold on." Maren put the bucket down. His hands wanted to grip something and the bucket handle was the available option.

*It is resolving now. The classification has just gone live. Designation: unclassified entity. Threat level: active. I have never —* A pause. *This designation is not standard. It is reserved for systemic anomalies. Infrastructure failures. The classification itself is a contradiction — a designation for the unclassifiable. And the System has attached a hero bounty.*

"A bounty," Maren said, and the word came out quieter than he'd meant.

*Four thousand gold crowns. Broadcast initiated now. Hero-class interfaces receive the contract first. Civilians receive only the hazard banner, and it writes in layers. People in villages this close to dungeon territory learn to ignore the first flash unless the second one tells them why.*

The bucket handle bit into his fingers. He was gripping it hard enough to feel the grain of the wood, the places where years of hands had worn the surface smooth and the places where splinters still caught, and these details mattered because the number didn't make sense and the handle at least was real.

"That can't be right. Say it again."

*Four thousand gold crowns. Capture or elimination. Unclassified entity, Fenhold region. The bounty is live and broadcasting at maximum range, which at this threat designation I estimate to be — considerable. Far beyond this village.*

"Maren." Derry's voice had changed. Not the chatter, not the nervous fill, not even the low-and-serious register from this morning. Something else. Something flat. "Maren, I've got a regional warning on my interface."

Maren looked at him. Derry's eyes had the unfocused quality of a person reading System text — the slight divergence, the small involuntary tracking movements that Maren had spent eleven years learning to mimic and never actually experienced. He'd spent those same eleven years reading every prompt that crossed his vision fast enough to keep Maren's fraud upright. If anyone in Fenhold was going to catch the first ugly blink instead of waiting for the chores to finish, it was him.

*That is the civilian banner,* Aldric said. *It does not include the bounty.*

"Active threat in Fenhold region," Derry said. "Stay clear. Report hero contact if sighted. Maren, what did he say?"

"I'm the threat," Maren said. "Four thousand gold crowns."

"That's —" Derry's voice went high and cracked. "That's not real. That can't — bounties like that don't — people put together whole companies for numbers like that. Real companies, with gear and party compositions and — four thousand crowns buys you a hero-class squad and enough left over to retire on, and if the hero feeds have that already then they've got our region marked."

"I know."

"Every hero between here and wherever the hero feeds reach can see it, and if that warning banner finishes writing before we're gone every farmer in the square is going to know Fenhold's got something bad sitting in it."

"I know, Derry."

They were standing at the well in the village square on a late-summer morning and the blacksmith was hammering and a dog was barking at the chapel door and three children were chasing something down the lane with the directionless joy of children running, and all of it was the same as yesterday and the day before and every day for eleven years. Breda was still drinking broth. The blacksmith still had his hammer up. Whatever red had started writing itself across the village's vision had not reached the part that made people drop what they were holding and start shouting. But the hero feeds had the number already, and the direction was here.

*At this bounty value, organized parties will mobilize within hours,* Aldric said. *I am uncertain of the nearest hero staging area, but this is not a sum that inspires patience.*

Derry's face had gone the color of wet clay. His hands hung at his sides and his wedding ring — a plain copper band gone green at the edges from years of soil and sweat — caught the sun the way metal catches the sun, without caring what kind of morning it is.

"We have to go," he said. "Right now. We have to — Maren, this village, these people — if heroes come here tracking the bounty and start asking about the sin-eater—"

"They'll find out I've been faking."

"They'll find out we've both been faking. And they'll find out there's an unclassified whatever-it-is living in the village where their neighbors live, where the children are, and that's not — people don't handle that well, Maren, they don't — and if anyone in Fenhold figures out what that bounty means before we're gone—"

*He is correct. Your departure should be immediate.*

"How fast can you be ready?" Maren asked.

Derry looked at him. The answer had been sitting in his face since he walked in with the eggs, in the late pauses and the way his eyes kept slipping toward the lane home. Maren had read it hours ago and kept skidding past it anyway.

"Lenna," Derry said. "And Pell."

The bucket handle dug into Maren's palm. He'd done the arithmetic already, but hearing their names made it uglier. Heroes tracked fast, and a woman carrying a child not yet three would leave a trail all the way to whatever caught up.

"They can't come." Maren didn't phrase it as a question.

"I know they can't come." Derry's voice was still flat, but there was strain under it now, breath forced through clenched teeth. "I know. I'm just — give me a minute to—"

He didn't finish. His mouth pressed thin and his hands found each other and the fingers laced and squeezed, the green-edged ring pressing between his knuckles.

"Go talk to her," Maren said. "I'll pack."

"Yeah." Derry took a step toward the lane that led to his house and then stopped. "Maren."

"What?"

"This is because of you. All of this — the bounty, the ritual, whatever happened at the farmer's house, this paladin — and my wife and my daughter are in that village while that number's hanging over it with our location on it, and I'm not — I'm not saying it to be cruel, I just need you to know that I know."

The blacksmith hit iron. A dog kept barking at the chapel door. Derry's jaw worked once. The hand with the ring opened and closed at his side, and his eyes went past Maren toward the lane to his house before coming back.

"I know," Maren said.

Derry went.

***

Maren packed. The bag took two minutes.

He rolled the spare shirt around the ceramic bowl so it wouldn't crack against anything. The bowl was light and plain and the cheapest he'd been able to find at market eleven years ago, when he'd first decided to fake sin-eating rituals and needed a prop. It had no System properties. It was not special. He'd held it over sixteen bodies and murmured the words he'd memorized from watching a real sin-eater work once, at a distance, in a village that wasn't Fenhold, when he was sixteen and hungry and already learning that the world gave you nothing you didn't steal or fake.

He tucked the candle stubs into the shirt's sleeves and counted his money: forty-three coppers in a leather fold. He counted them twice because the first count felt wrong — not the number, which was accurate, but the scale of what the number needed to cover.

*Forty-three coppers,* Aldric said, having apparently done the same arithmetic. *The bounty on your head is four thousand gold crowns and your total wealth is forty-three coppers.*

"I didn't ask for commentary."

*That is not commentary. It is arithmetic.*

Outside, the village kept making its usual noises: the blacksmith's hammer, the dog finally giving up on the chapel door, children farther down the lane still busy with whatever mattered to children on a hot morning. Maren shut the door before he could start turning Fenhold into inventory.

Derry came back.

He'd been gone twenty minutes. His bag was larger than Maren's — stuffed with bread, a blanket roll, a second pair of boots strapped to the outside with twine he'd tied badly because his hands were shaking. His eyes were red. One knot had already slipped half loose. His wedding ring was gone, and the skin beneath it made a pale band through the dirt on his finger.

He smelled like his house: woodsmoke, the herb soap that Lenna made from whatever grew in the garden that wasn't leeks, and underneath both, something smaller and sweeter — the particular warm scent of a child's hair pressed close, breathed in deep, held as long as the breathing would allow.

He set a cloth-wrapped heel of bread on the table. "Lenna said take this part first. It keeps longer."

"South?" he asked.

"South. North puts us closer to the dungeon mouth and the hero patrols."

*The southern route adds approximately two days before intersecting the main road, but avoids the highest concentrations of quest-light and active hero presence. Given the bounty's range, this is the correct decision.*

"South works," Maren said.

Derry nodded, picked up his bag, and went out first. He kept two paces between them before they'd passed the forge.

They walked past the blacksmith, who nodded at them without pausing his hammer, past Breda's doorstep, where her broth was finished and the cup sat empty on the stone beside her chair and she raised a hand without even sitting forward, already looking back into the square, past the chapel with its contamination haze in the ditch, and past the well where half an hour ago the number had fallen on them and the morning had emptied out.

At the village edge, where the lane turned to packed dirt and the first fields opened out toward the southern road, Derry stopped.

He turned and looked back at Fenhold, at the roofs and the forge smoke and the lane kinked left. Somewhere behind the first row of stone sat the house he'd built over six years of weekends and borrowed tools, with Lenna in it and Pell likely asking why the door kept opening and shutting.

His hand went to the pale band where the ring had been and stayed there.

Maren kept his eyes on the ruts ahead. The bowl in his bag tapped once against his spine when he breathed.

"Come on," Maren said, not unkindly.

Derry turned from Fenhold. He hitched his bag higher and fell back into that two-pace distance. Maren matched him without looking back. Dust climbed his boots at every step, and the bowl tapped his spine hard enough to count the pace for him.

*The southern road,* Aldric said. *Approximately six days of travel to the market town of Silt, assuming no encounters. I will provide updates as conditions change.*

"Thanks," Maren said.

Under Aldric's voice, the hum stayed low and steady and close.
