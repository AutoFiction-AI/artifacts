# Chapter 10

The road past Harrow turned to packed earth and stayed that way. No scorch marks out here. The heroes went the other direction, toward the dungeon mouths, and left their damage there. Maren's feet had been swollen since yesterday, and the cooler morning air was the first mercy in a week, settling into the backs of his hands like water. Late summer letting go. He could feel the season turning in the longer shadows, in the grass along the road going brittle and pale at the tips.

Derry hadn't said much since they'd run from Harrow. Not nothing. He'd offered Maren some dried meat at dawn, asked if the road felt right, made a half-observation about the clouds and let it die halfway out of his mouth. But the chatter was gone. The Derry who filled every silence with stories about Pell's counting lessons and Lenna's tomato plants was walking beside Maren now like a man saving his words for something he hadn't decided whether to say.

*Two figures on the ridge. North side. Half a mile back.*

Vess, calibrating threats. She'd been doing it since Harrow, quiet updates delivered with the flat precision of someone checking instruments.

*Hero-class?* Maren thought.

*Farmers. Hay rakes. Not a concern.*

Aldric hadn't spoken all morning. When the paladin went quiet, it meant he was either seeing something in the System's architecture that he didn't know how to name or composing a lecture so thorough it required structural planning. Both demanded silence, and both usually ended with Maren feeling worse about himself.

They came around a bend where the road widened into a clearing. Someone had cut back the brush on both sides, maybe for a seasonal market nobody used anymore. The stumps were sawed low and gone grey. The man was sitting at the far edge. Sitting, not standing. His hands on his knees, empty.

Maren stopped.

Derry almost walked into him, then looked where Maren was looking, and his hand went to Maren's elbow. Not to guide him. To hold on.

The man wore ranger gear that had seen real weather, leather darkened at the stress points, stitching redone along one shoulder where something had torn through it and been repaired without vanity. His pack sat against a stump, buckled shut. A short sword rested on the ground beside his right boot, positioned where he could reach it but clearly not where he was reaching for it. His hair was cropped close, and his face had the color of someone who slept outside more often than in. He favored his right knee. Maren could see it in how he sat, the leg extended slightly, not quite willing to fold all the way.

What Maren noticed first wasn't the gear or the weapon or the deliberate stillness. It was the old smile lines at the corners of the man's mouth, still there even though nothing in his face was using them.

*Hero-ranger. Active class. High level. The bounty is open on his interface.* Vess, each word placed like a stone. *He can see what you are. He's been here at least an hour.*

*And he has not drawn his weapon,* Aldric said. The paladin's voice was careful, which was different from his usual formality. Careful like a man stepping around something fragile on the floor. *That is not a good sign.*

Maren understood why immediately. A ranger who attacked was a problem you could solve, by running, by fighting, by being lucky. A ranger who sat down and waited for you to walk to him wanted something that couldn't be solved with any of those.

"There's two ways this goes," the man said. His voice carried the clearing without effort, trained to project over distance, the habit so deep it worked even here. "One of them involves me trailing you for another three days, and honestly my knee isn't up for it this week. So I'd rather the other way."

Derry's hand tightened on Maren's elbow.

"What's the other way?" Maren said. His own voice came out steadier than the inside of him warranted, the performance booting up on its own, charm and deflection and the right tone, mechanical as breathing.

"We talk." The man shifted on the stump, resettling his weight off the bad knee. "I'm Caul. I've been behind you since Harrow. Ahead of you now, which I realize looks staged, but the road only goes one way from here and I walk faster than you do."

*He is telling the truth about his name. Caul Henning. Registered with the Wayward Reach ranger company. Twelve years active service.* Aldric stopped. A silence that felt different from his usual pauses, not deliberate, not tactical. Arrested.

*What?*

*His party roster shows two members deceased. Recent. Three months ago.*

"You can sit," Caul said. "I've got water. Your friend looks like he's about to fall over."

Derry did look bad. The color had left his face, and his shirt was damp at the collar despite the cool morning. His grip on Maren's elbow kept tightening and loosening, his body already in the fear before the rest of him caught up. But he was looking at Maren, not at Caul, and Maren could read that look with all his professional practice: *Do we run?*

*Running would be inadvisable,* Vess said. *He has sight lines on both exits from this clearing. Brush cover to the south, but the terrain drops into a gully thirty metres in. He chose this spot.*

"Yeah," Maren said to Derry, though it wasn't really an answer to the question Derry was asking. He let go of the tension in his shoulders, a conscious decision to rearrange his body into something that looked like ease, and walked to the middle of the clearing and sat on the ground. Not because he wanted to sit. Because standing in front of a seated man with a sword on the ground made the power too obvious, and Maren's whole professional life was about rearranging the power in a room until he could find a way to work it.

Derry sat too, but farther away. Not beside Maren, the way he would have a week ago. About four feet to Maren's left, knees drawn up, arms wrapped around them. The distance was small, and Maren measured it automatically, with the part of himself that catalogued what people were telling him with their bodies before they'd decided to tell him anything with their mouths.

"So," Maren said.

"So." Caul looked at him. His eyes were the light grey of old iron, and he didn't push, didn't fill the quiet, just kept Maren in that steady look until the clearing felt arranged around the answer he wanted. "You're the unclassified. The bounty."

"I've been told."

"Your profile had a sin-eater registration before the classification scrambled. Did you know that? It's still there underneath. Eleven years of service logged."

Something in Maren's chest went very still. Not a tightening. A cessation, the way a room goes quiet when everyone hears the same sound at once.

"I know who I am," Maren said. "I don't know what you want."

"I want to tell you something." Caul recapped a water skin he'd been holding and set it beside his boot. The gesture was unhurried, almost domestic, a man settling in for a conversation over a table, not confronting a fugitive in a clearing. "After that, you can go. For a while." He looked at Derry, then back at Maren. "Your friend should hear it too."

Derry said nothing. His arms were locked around his knees, and he stared at the dirt between his feet like there might be a seam in it wide enough to crawl through.

"Three months ago," Caul said, "my party cleared a dungeon near the Ashfold border. Standard contract. Minor threat creatures, containment perimeter, System-flagged quest. We'd done a hundred of these. Probably two hundred. You stop counting after the first year."

He spoke the way someone reads aloud from a document they've memorized, each sentence placed and cadenced with the even rhythm of a field report. But the rhythm was too even. Too practiced. Maren recognized it because he recognized performance, and this man had rehearsed what he was about to say. Not to be convincing. To survive saying it.

"The site was supposed to be clean. That's what the log said. Proper sin-eater on file, all the seals in place. System records showed the contamination cleared eighteen months before we got there. Haley checked it herself. She always checked. She trusted the record."

*Haley Keth,* Aldric said, very quietly, reading from whatever the System kept on file for the dead. *Healer-class. Twenty-six at time of death. Renn Ostara. Shield-bearer. Twenty-nine.*

Caul's hands stayed on his knees. "The dungeon was routine. Threat level minimal. But the contamination wasn't gone. It had settled into the substrate, below what a standard scan reads, in the deep residue a real sin-eater is supposed to pull. Old contamination. Layers of it. Years built up because someone kept marking the area clean and walking away."

The clearing was quiet. Somewhere behind them a bird kept repeating a three-note call, and Maren wanted it to stop because the sound was too ordinary for what was happening in his chest.

"Haley went down first. The contamination spiked when the quest activated. The old residue caught the fresh System energy and everything came up at once, like pulling a tarp off a floor that's been rotting underneath." Caul paused. The practiced rhythm slipped, and what was under it showed for a second, a man trying very hard to hold a shape that grief kept pulling out of true. "Renn tried to get her out. The spike caught him too."

Maren's hands were in his lap. He was looking at them. They were still, which surprised him, because the rest of him felt like something had come loose inside his ribs and was swinging free, knocking against the walls of him with each breath.

*He is describing contamination persistence from incomplete sin-eating,* Aldric said. The paladin didn't sound like the paladin, not lecturing, not moral, not correcting. Something Maren hadn't heard from him before. *A genuine sin-eater clears the contamination from the substrate. A fraudulent one only removes the surface. What he is describing is exact.*

*I know,* Maren thought, hard enough that the thought had edges. *I know what he's describing.*

Caul was watching him. The ranger's face hadn't changed, still the settled field-report composure, but his eyes were doing something that wasn't professional. They were looking at Maren the way you look at a word you're trying to read in bad light.

"The sin-eater on that job looked real on paper," Caul said. "Class mark. Ritual sequence. Every line stamped and filed. Everything that was supposed to mean safe. The contamination was still there because the work under it was empty." He paused and let the silence sit. "Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

"Yeah," Maren said. "I understand."

"There are more of you than people think. Fake sin-eaters. Some of you are System-blind. Some just figured out that doing it wrong pays the same as doing it right." Caul's fingers pressed into his knees, the knuckles whitening briefly. "The System doesn't audit the work. It checks the paperwork. It checks the registration. It doesn't check the ground. It assumes the job got done."

Derry made a small sound. Not a word, a tight exhaled noise, the kind of sound he made when he was trying to process something too large for speech. Maren didn't look at him. He couldn't look at Derry right then, because if he looked he would see Derry's face doing what it was doing, understanding exactly what Caul was describing and knowing exactly who it applied to, and that understanding would be written in every line of Derry's open, honest, terrible face.

"Haley liked birds," Caul said. The shift was sudden, a crack in the practiced frame he didn't try to cover. "She kept a journal. Drew them. She wasn't any good at it. Heads too big. Wings too short. Renn used to tell her they looked like angry potatoes with beaks." Something moved in his face that wasn't a smile. "He could draw better than she could, but he'd never have told her that. He just let her show him, and he said they were good."

*Don't say anything,* Vess said. The assassin's voice carried its usual operational flatness, but there was a precision in it that Maren had learned to recognize as something more than professional. *He isn't asking for a response. He's showing you what he lost. Let him.*

Caul reached into the front of his ranger's jacket and took out a folded piece of paper, creased and re-creased so many times the folds were nearly worn through, the edges gone soft. He didn't unfold it. He held it in his hand for a moment, then put it back inside the jacket.

"I've been tracking since they died. Not your bounty specifically. I picked that up in Harrow. Your profile matched reports I'd been collecting. I've been tracking the problem. Certification gaps. Places where the contamination records don't match the service history." He smoothed the front of his jacket where the paper sat. "There are a lot of them."

Maren started to say he wasn't what Caul thought, that Caul had the wrong man, that he didn't know what he was talking about. The lie came up whole and familiar. Years of practice had made it as natural as swallowing.

He didn't say it. Not because honesty won. Honesty had never won a fight inside Maren. But the lie would have required him to look at Caul's face while he said it, and Caul's face belonged to a man carrying a dead woman's bad bird drawings in his jacket. Some performances couldn't survive the audience.

"You're not what?" Caul said. Patient. Waiting the way a stone waits.

"I don't know what to tell you," Maren said. "That's true. I don't know what to tell you."

It was the truest thing he'd said in thirteen days. The sentence sat in his chest like a river stone.

Aldric said nothing.

"I'm not asking you to tell me anything," Caul said. "I'm telling you so you know what's out there. What's been left behind in the villages where sin-eaters took the fee and didn't do the work." He looked at the sky, checking the light or maybe checking himself. "You're headed for the Crook."

Maren didn't confirm or deny. His mouth was dry. His hands were very still in his lap.

"I know you are, because there's nowhere else from here unless you're planning to walk into dungeon territory and die. Which I don't think you're doing." Caul stood up from the stump. The right knee gave him trouble. Maren could see the hitch, the way he braced a hand against the stump and pushed through the stiffness, the joint complaining in a way he'd clearly learned to manage but not hide. "I'm going to give you a day. Not because you've earned it. Because the Crook is where sin-eaters answer for this kind of thing, and I'd rather they handle it than me doing something on a road that I'd have to write up afterward." He picked up his pack strap, then stopped. "I want this done right. Haley would've wanted it done right."

"A day," Maren said.

"Starting now. I'll be a day behind you. If you're at the Crook when I arrive, we do this properly. If you're not there, we do it differently."

He picked up his short sword and sheathed it in a motion so practiced it looked involuntary, the hand finding the scabbard the way a tongue finds a missing tooth. Then he shouldered his pack. The buckles clinked against each other, a small ordinary sound. He looked at Derry for the first time with something that wasn't tactical assessment. Just one ordinary man looking at another and seeing the specific misery of being here because of someone else's decisions.

Then he walked past them, back the direction they'd come from. His footsteps on the packed earth were even and unhurried. The bad knee didn't slow him. It only changed the rhythm, a slight hitch every other step, an asymmetry in the sound that Maren listened to until it faded into the distance and the clearing was empty except for the two of them and the three voices in his skull, all of them quiet.

---

Maren sat where he was. The ground was cool through his trousers, and a root was pressing into his left thigh, a specific insistent discomfort that gave him something to organize his attention around.

*Haley Keth,* Aldric said eventually. *She completed her healer certification at twenty-two. Three years of study at the Ashfold academy. She specialized in contamination triage. The medical side. How to stabilize someone exposed to quest residue.* A pause. *She should not have needed to use that training on herself. The area was supposed to be clean.*

*I know.*

*You do know. You have known for years what your work leaves behind.*

*Yeah. I know that too.*

Aldric went silent. Not the pointed quiet of disapproval this time. Something heavier.

Maren closed his eyes. The red behind his eyelids was warm from the morning sun, and he kept them shut because opening them meant the clearing was still there and Derry was still in it, four feet to his left, quiet.

"Maren." Derry had kept his mouth shut so long the name came out dry and misplaced, like he had to test it before trusting the rest of the sentence. "He was talking about us."

"Yeah."

Derry swallowed. His hands moved against the fabric of his trousers with a dry scraping sound.

"His friends went into that place and it wasn't clean, because — because somebody did what we did," he said.

Maren opened his eyes. The clearing looked the same, grey stumps, brushed-back scrub, the road curving away in both directions, the bird still going at its three-note song as though the morning hadn't changed. The root was still pressing into his thigh.

"Not us specifically," he said. "He said the sin-eater was certified. Properly registered. We never had that. I had a bowl and a routine." He heard himself doing it, laying out the thin careful distinction that left room for his own shape inside the guilt. He hated it and kept going anyway.

"But someone like us," Derry said.

"Someone like me."

"Like us, Maren. I was — I fed you the cues, I told you what the System was showing so you could fake it, and I was right there. Every ritual, every village, I was right there."

*He is correct,* Vess said. No judgment. Only the fact.

Maren looked at Derry. The four feet between them felt built now, not empty. Derry's arms were locked around his knees. Dirty red-brown hair lay flat on one side from sleep. The skin at the corners of his mouth had gone white. He looked at Maren for a second too long, then dropped his eyes back to the dirt.

"But how many, though," Derry said. "How many villages — in all that time, how many places where it's still sitting in the ground because we said it was gone."

"I don't know." True. Maren had never counted. Counting would have required him to think about the villages together, and the whole fraud only worked if each one stayed small enough to leave behind. Twelve coppers. A bowl. A nod from the family. Move on. "I never counted."

"A lot," Derry said.

"Yeah."

Derry rubbed a thumb across his knuckles. "Lenna'd had honey set aside before we left, and she'd been talking about this cake for weeks, and I just kept — I kept thinking I'd get back in time for it. That's the thing I was holding on to. Out of everything." He swallowed again. "People were living in it, Maren. In Fenhold. In my house."

The sentence landed and stayed there.

*He is right,* Aldric said. Not to punish. Just to confirm.

"Did you know, though?" Derry said. "All that time — did you know the contamination was staying?"

Maren opened his mouth. The truth came up whole and hot enough to choke him. By the time he got breath around it, he'd already started cutting it smaller.

"I didn't think about it," Maren said. "Not the way you're asking. I thought about the ritual and the fee and the next village. I didn't think past that."

Derry's face tightened in a small specific way around the eyes.

"If somebody at the Crook asks me what we were doing out there," he said, "I'm not — I can't make it sound clean. I can't do that. I can't. And if they ask about Fenhold, about my place, I'm telling them that too."

He stood up. He turned toward the road without offering a hand, without the half-second pause where he'd usually check Maren's direction and match his pace. He just started walking.

Maren got up. His left leg was stiff from the root, and he shook it out. The ceramic bowl in his pack shifted when he shouldered it, the smooth familiar weight of it against his spine. The same bowl. The same one he'd carried into houses where people were grieving and used to perform a ceremony that did nothing while they stood in the doorway with the particular relief of people who believed the contamination was being lifted from the room where their husband or mother or child had died.

He followed Derry.

They walked. The road was empty, and the packed earth was dry. The shadows had that longer, thinner quality late summer took on when autumn started winning. The hum was louder out here, farther from Harrow's System-dense interference, the background frequency of ambient System activity settled into Maren's hearing like tinnitus.

Derry walked ahead. Not far. Ten feet maybe. But he didn't fall back. For all the years they'd walked between villages, splitting the fee and the fraud and the ordinary shape of a friendship, Derry had always matched Maren's pace. Now he didn't.

The distance held through the rest of the day, steady and precise. Derry never let Maren draw even; if Maren gained a step, Derry found one too, keeping that strip of road between them as if he needed the space to think inside. They climbed into the cooling hills where the road narrowed and the trees grew closer together. The hum got louder. The shadows stretched.

At dusk Derry cut off the road without asking and took the first campsite that would do, a ring of old stones beside a dry wash where the bank dropped away under scrub roots. He put his pack down on the far side of the fire circle before Maren had shrugged his off. Ten feet on the road became fifteen with the blackened stones between them.

No one said they should stop. No one said they were tired. Derry crouched and gathered deadfall in quick efficient motions, all business now, no farmer's chatter about the quality of the wood or whether the weather would hold. Maren went for water because it was the task left. By the time he came back from a thin trickle caught in the wash, Derry had the fire lit and two hunks of bread set out on separate stones.

He didn't hand Maren his portion. He set it down and looked away. That was worse.

Maren unslung his pack. The bowl knocked once against the side of it, a small ceramic click. Derry's head came up at the sound. His eyes went to the pack, then away so fast the movement had shame in it, like he'd seen something indecent by accident.

They ate without appetite. The bread was stale enough to scrape the roof of Maren's mouth. Smoke drifted low because the air had gone cooler after sunset, carrying that dry-leaf smell autumn sent ahead of itself. Under it sat another smell, faint but ugly now that Thessa had given it a name: sweet-metallic, damp stone, the edge of old rot under clean water.

*There is contamination in the wash,* Aldric said after a while. *Old. Thin. The water is passing over it rather than holding it.*

*Not enough to hurt you from drinking once,* Vess said. *Enough to taste, if you know what you're tasting.*

Maren looked at the cup in his hand and wished he hadn't.

Derry was turning his bread over and over without eating it. Finally he said, "How many houses do you actually remember?"

Maren had to think about it. That was answer enough before he opened his mouth.

"Bits," he said.

"What bits?"

"A doorway with smoke worked into the lintel. A woman with flour on her wrists. A man who kept coughing into a rag and apologizing for it. Candle grease down the side of the bowl. Mud floors. Wet wool. Kids being told to wait outside." He looked at the fire because looking at Derry made the list worse. "Not names. Mostly not names."

Derry shut his eyes for a second. "Fuck."

Nobody spoke after that. The fire settled lower. Once, somewhere down the wash, a night animal crashed through brush and went still.

When Maren spread his blanket, Derry shifted his own farther back without comment, just an extra drag through the dirt, another two feet bought and set in place. He lay down with his back to the fire and his back to Maren both, curled around himself with one hand under his head and the other closed hard around the strap of his pack.

Maren stayed sitting.

The bowl was out before he registered taking it out. He held it in both hands, thumbs along the chipped rim, and the glaze had that greasy-smooth feel it always had after years of being washed in cold water and wiped on whatever cloth came closest. He could have traced every part of the fraud on it with his fingers. The nick near the base from when it clipped a doorstep and survived. The hairline scratch inside from when Derry packed it with the cookpot and swore for ten minutes because he thought he'd broken the whole act. Eleven years of handling it like a tool, a prop, a good reliable lie.

*You never once asked what was left behind,* Aldric said.

"No," Maren said under his breath. Not an argument. Just the shape of the thing.

Below them the wash kept giving off that thin wrong smell. He'd walked past it for years without knowing why certain wells tasted off, why some rooms had a sweetness under the mold, why the back of his throat sometimes prickled after a ritual and he blamed candle smoke. The knowledge did not arrive like revelation. It arrived like a man coming home and finding his own furniture in someone else's house.

Across the fire Derry did not move, but Maren could tell he wasn't asleep. His breathing stayed wrong. Too deliberate. Too even.

Maren set the bowl in his lap and listened to the hum in the dark until it separated into layers: the ordinary ambient thrum of the System in the road, the thinner dirty thread down in the wash, the fire settling inward on itself, Derry awake and not speaking. He kept his hands on the bowl until they ached.
