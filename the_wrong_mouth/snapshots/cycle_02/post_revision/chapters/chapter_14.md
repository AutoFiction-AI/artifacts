# Chapter 14

The valley between the dungeon mouths tasted like scorched copper.

Maren had been walking through worsening contamination for two days, the smell thickening from background haze to something that lodged behind his sinuses and wouldn't clear, but the last quarter-mile dropped into a different register entirely. The ground under his boots was grey-dark and slick with decades of accumulated quest-light residue. The hum from three dungeon mouths — northeast, southeast, and the wide one dead ahead — layered into something that wasn't quite a sound anymore. It pressed against his chest, a low vibration that worked up through the soles of his boots and settled into the gap where the unclassified thing lived, and the thing hummed back.

*Five sin-eaters,* Aldric said. His voice had the edge it got when he was cataloguing rather than commenting — precision as habit, the ghost of field reports filed to an order that no longer existed. *All registered. Properly classified — maintenance tier, if the System still bothers to differentiate. The profiles are thin. I don't think anyone's updated them in years.*

"Five," Maren said. He'd been expecting more.

*The historical registry shows fourteen designations for this site. Most are marked inactive.*

"Dead?"

*Or quit. The classification doesn't distinguish.*

The valley opened below him in the last of the evening light, and the Crook was smaller than anything he'd prepared himself for.

He'd had a picture in his head — not grand, not that naive, but substantial. A camp with structure. Tents in patterns, a central fire, the organized bustle of people who did the same essential work gathering to share the load. Instead: five canvas shelters strung between boulders on the valley floor, smoke from two cook fires, a handful of figures moving with the careful efficiency of people who had stopped wasting motion years ago. One of the shelters had collapsed on its windward side and no one had fixed it. A line of washing hung between two stunted trees, the clothes stiff and faded from contamination exposure. The whole thing looked less like a professional gathering and more like a disaster camp that had forgotten to leave.

*Three approach angles from here,* Vess said flatly. *Two with cover. One visible from every point in the camp. You're on the visible one.*

"Good."

*That wasn't a recommendation.*

"I know." He kept walking down the exposed slope, hands open at his sides, no pack on his back because he'd lost it two days ago and hadn't replaced it. Nothing on him but his clothes, his bowl wrapped in cloth inside his shirt, and three coppers he'd found in the mud outside a collapsed waystation. Coming to the sin-eater gathering looking like exactly what he was: a man with nothing left to try.

The absence to his left was louder in the silence. No nervous chatter about the road or the weather or whether the bread had gone off. No second pair of feet on packed dirt, no Derry filling every quiet moment with stories about his daughter's counting or his wife's tomatoes or the cow that wouldn't stay fenced. Four days of walking alone, which meant four days of walking with only the voices in his head, and the voices in his head had the decency to be quiet about the person who should have been walking beside him.

The woman by the nearer cook fire saw him first. Short, square-shouldered, a grey bandage wrapped around her left hand that had gone stiff and dark at the edges. She stood fast enough to knock the ladle against the pot. A tall man by the collapsed shelter straightened from the rope he'd been retying, one shoulder lifting a little higher than the other before he rolled it back into place. Beside the second fire, an older man kept working a piece of dried root between his teeth and watched Maren over the flames. At the far shelter a woman with three stoppered clay jars at her feet shifted into the doorway. Near the water cask, a young man barely past twenty caught his own wrist in his other hand and went still.

Then Thessa came around the far shelter, a ceramic bowl in her stained hands, and stopped.

The distance between them was maybe forty feet. There was a fresh split across the base of her thumb, blackened at the edges from old residue. She looked at Maren the way she'd look at a contamination pool that had appeared where one shouldn't be.

"You came," she said.

"Wasn't anywhere else."

"No." Her eyes tracked across him, or past him — reading the interface, the profile, whatever it showed about the thing he was carrying. He'd never seen what they saw when they looked at him, but he'd gotten better at reading the reactions. The bandaged woman's reaction was fear. The older man's was professional assessment, the kind that measured before it responded. Thessa's was harder to parse. Something between fury and a recognition she didn't want.

"Your friend's not with you," she said.

"No."

She waited. He didn't elaborate. The silence held the shape of everything he wasn't going to say about Derry, about a man who'd chosen his daughter over a fight that wasn't his, about waking up on the road alone for the first time in weeks with the understanding that he had earned exactly this.

Thessa turned to the bandaged woman. "Petra. Get him water."

"I saw his profile," Petra said. "Thessa, that bounty—"

"I saw the bounty. I know what happened in Silt. I'm not reading him against three live mouths while the valley's still turning over." She pointed with her bowl toward a flat stone beyond the outer fire, where the ground ran darker. "Denna, mark him outside the shelter ring. Ruald, first watch. Kael relieves him at moonset. Dawn's when the hum banks low enough for a clean read."

The woman with the jars bent immediately and chose one without comment. The older man by the second fire — Ruald, then — grunted around his root and stood. The tall man at the collapsed shelter gave one short nod.

Petra went. Denna crossed to the flat stone, shook a ring of grey powder around it, and pressed three fired-clay markers into the ground at the widest points. The powder gave off a faint sour hiss where it touched the darkest residue patches.

Maren reached the camp's edge and stopped because he'd learned not to walk into spaces where people were afraid of him. He stood at the border — contamination-darkened stone on both sides — and waited to be invited or turned away.

"You sleep inside that," Thessa said. "If your residue jumps in the night, the line tells us before you reach a shelter."

The tall man by the collapsed shelter came over, wiping his fingers once on his trouser seam. Taller than Maren had expected from above, with the kind of angular thinness that came from decades of physical work on insufficient food. The left side of his coat hung lower where that shoulder never sat quite right.

"Kael," he said. "I work the western reaches."

"Maren."

"I know who you are. Your bounty's been visible for weeks — every hero in three provinces can see it." Kael's voice stayed dry and even, as if he were naming weather that had already arrived. "Thessa told us about Silt."

*He doesn't seem hostile,* Aldric said carefully. *His System profile shows contamination tolerance readings well below baseline. He's been absorbing more than he should be, for longer than is sustainable. All of them are.*

"Overworked," Maren murmured.

*Severely. Yes.*

Thessa was watching him from across the camp. Ruald dragged an upturned bucket to the far side of Denna's marker line and sat on it without taking the root from his mouth. Denna went back to the shelter opening with the jars. The young man still had his hand locked around his wrist.

*The young one isn't on the registry,* Aldric said. *No maintenance designation at all.*

"Sit," Thessa told him. She pointed inside Denna's ring. "Eat if they'll feed you. At dawn we look at what happens when you open."

"And then?"

"That depends on what we find."

He sat. Petra brought him water in a clay cup with a crack running down one side, mended with brown resin. She set it on the stone beside him rather than placing it in his hand — the distance of a woman who'd seen his readings and didn't want to be within arm's reach. Then she sat across the fire and unwound the bandage from her hand, working carefully, and the skin underneath was raw and mottled with a contamination pattern Maren recognized from his own failed attempts — the discoloration of flesh that had opened to something toxic and hadn't fully recovered.

"Bad consumption?" he asked.

She looked at him sharply. "You'd know what that looks like?"

"Had a few."

"Yeah. Source pool in the northeast mouth. Standard approach barely dented it and my hand was in the bowl when the backwash hit." She shook her head and started rewrapping. "Doesn't matter."

"She nearly lost the use of it," Kael said, easing himself down onto his stone and bracing a hand against that bad shoulder until it settled. "The contamination is denser this season. The standard approaches work less than they did."

The fire was warm. The cool of early autumn was settling into the valley with the fading light, the first real chill Maren had felt since leaving Fenhold, and his body registered it gratefully after weeks of late-summer heat on dusty roads. His legs ached from four days of solo walking. The gap in his chest — the space he'd started thinking of as the crack, because Aldric had called it that and the word had stuck — pulsed heavier in the presence of concentrated contamination, as if it were responding to the density around it. A slow, dull throb in time with the triple hum.

*The young one is still watching you,* Vess said. *He's not deciding whether to trust you. He's deciding whether you're going to kill them in their sleep.*

Maren looked at the young sin-eater, who stood ten feet away with his hand still on his wrist, his face tight with an anxiety that didn't seem to belong exclusively to Maren's arrival — more like the residue of something that had been building for a while.

"I'm Maren," he said.

The young man said nothing. After a moment, he went back inside the shelter.

"Hale," Petra said. "He doesn't talk much lately. The work's been..." She trailed off, poked the fire with a stick.

The valley settled into its evening sounds — the low compound hum of three dungeon mouths, the crack and pop from the fires, the distant drip-echo of quest-light pooling somewhere below the surface. Maren ate what Kael put in front of him: grain porridge with a heel of dense bread that tasted faintly of the contamination in the water they'd used to cook it. Everything here was contaminated. The food, the water, the air, the ground. These people lived in it, worked in it, ate it, breathed it, and went back to work the next morning.

After he ate, Denna freshened the grey line around the stone and set the clay markers deeper. Ruald took first watch on the bucket just beyond it, root in his teeth and an iron poker across his knees. Kael relieved him sometime past midnight. Maren slept outside the shelter ring on the thin bedroll Kael lent him, wrapped in a blanket that smelled of ritual residue and woodsmoke, while the line around him stayed dull and unbroken until dawn. The hum from the dungeon mouths didn't stop. Neither did the thing in his chest, which pulsed faintly in time with the contamination outside, as if it were breathing too.

---

The examination started at dawn, when the light was grey, the dungeon hum had banked low into the earth, and the contamination separated into layers instead of one long night-churned blur.

They didn't call it a trial. They didn't call it anything. Thessa told him to sit on the flat stone near the collapsed shelter where the contamination was densest, and the five working sin-eaters arranged themselves around him at varying distances — Thessa closest, Kael three paces back, Petra and Denna at the midpoint, Ruald beyond them with the root gone still in one cheek. Hale stayed outside the circle altogether, his hand on his wrist, his face white.

Thessa knelt in front of him. She held her bowl — darkened from long use, edges worn smooth by her thumbs — and closed her eyes. Around her, the others did the same.

*They're scanning you,* Aldric said. *Through the contamination channels — using their sin-eating sensitivity to read your residue patterns. I don't have a clean term for it. The closest I can get is that they're listening to your contamination the way a physician listens to a heartbeat, but through the System's maintenance layer.*

Maren sat still. Something shifted in the contamination around him — not their attention exactly, but a change in the ambient density. The contamination pulled toward the sin-eaters' open channels the way water flows toward a drain. And inside him, the fragments stirred.

*They can feel us,* Vess said. Her voice was flat and controlled, which meant she was working to keep it that way. *Both of us. The scan is reaching the fragment layer.*

*I'm aware.*

*They shouldn't be able to reach that deep. That's not standard sin-eating.*

*No. But this close to three active dungeon mouths, with contamination this dense, their sensitivity range is significantly extended. They can see things that wouldn't be legible in a normal setting.*

Twenty minutes. Maren sat through it the way he'd sat through every uncomfortable examination of his life — a performance of stillness that had once been a fraud's best tool and was now the closest thing to honesty he could manage. He couldn't see what they were reading. He couldn't contribute to his own assessment. He sat on the stone and waited for the verdict while people with decades of real skill read the contamination in his chest and decided whether it was something they could work with or something they needed to destroy.

Thessa opened her eyes first. Her jaw had gone tight enough to show the muscle jumping near the hinge. She took one breath through her nose before she spoke.

"How long have you been carrying the second fragment?" she asked.

"Since the road past Fenhold. Sixteen, seventeen days."

"The collision between them has gotten worse. The opposed classifications are generating friction at the interface boundary — two currents pushing against each other in a narrow channel. The turbulence is—" She stopped. Looked at Kael. "You felt it?"

Kael nodded, his bad shoulder ticking once before he stilled it. "The fragments aren't seated properly. Neither of them. It's as if they're floating in the host rather than anchored, and the space between them—"

"The unclassified thing," Thessa said.

"Yes."

"It's grown since Silt."

"Considerably."

Maren's hands were flat on his knees. The gap pulsed once, dully, as if it knew it was being discussed.

*She's not wrong,* Aldric said quietly. *Whatever we are — whatever I am, inside you — it's less stable than it was two weeks ago. Whether that's the entity or the proximity to this level of contamination, I can't say. But something has shifted.*

"There's more," Thessa said. She stood and faced the circle. "His contamination pattern is inverted. Every time he's tried to consume, he's opened too—"

"Wide," Kael said. He was already nodding. "Too fast. The contamination never seats long enough to break down."

"You felt it," Thessa said. Not a question.

"During the scan." He shifted his weight forward. "Like pouring through a cracked bowl. It hits the gap and goes back out."

Denna spoke for the first time. Her voice came out rough, as if it had spent years breathing dungeon air and had finally given up on ever smoothing back down. "Goes out where?"

Thessa's thumb pressed hard against the rim of her bowl. "Outward. Wider dispersal, lower density. The source point looks cleaner—"

"Because he's shaved the top off it," Kael said.

"—and smeared the rest thin across the surrounding field." Thessa stopped. The next words cost her something. "The local reading drops. The total doesn't."

The silence this time landed differently. Maren could read it in the way Petra's bandaged hand clenched around nothing, in the way Ruald forgot to chew, in the way Hale took a step backward — the horror of contamination workers discovering that a contamination source had walked into their camp.

"The sites behind you didn't clear," Thessa said, turning back to him. "You blew the tops off the pools and shoved the rest out past standard catch lines. The center reads cleaner, so people think the work is done. It isn't. The spread just stops showing where anyone knows to look."

"I didn't know," Maren said.

"No. You didn't. Because you were faking."

He had nothing for that.

*She's right,* Aldric said. *At the creek the contamination crossed you like water through a broken sluice. It never seated. It touched the gap and went back out thinner. The width made it look like clearance.*

"Consume him," Thessa said.

It took Maren a moment to understand she wasn't talking to him. She was talking to the circle.

"Break down the fragments. Dissolve the unclassified structure. We eat it. One of us takes him, and we end this."

"Thessa—" Kael began.

"He's carrying two hostile fragments in an unstable configuration." She turned to the circle as if Kael hadn't spoken. "An entity the System can't classify. A contamination pattern that—"

"Poisons every area he passes through," Petra said. Her bandaged hand was clenched at her side. "We can read that much."

"Then you can read what happens if we leave it." Thessa's voice went flat. "He's not a sin-eater. He's a contamination event with legs. If a pool showed these readings, we'd consume it without discussion."

"A pool doesn't speak," Kael said mildly.

"Neither does the contamination in the villages he's been spreading for eleven years. The children growing up in it don't get to speak either."

Maren took it. He sat on his stone and looked at the split at the base of Thessa's thumb and understood, with a clarity that bypassed every defense he'd ever built, that she was right. From every angle that mattered to these people — the only people who did the work that kept the world from drowning in its own waste — he was the problem, not the solution.

"Who takes him?" Petra asked. The question was practical, not moral, and the practicality was worse. "I can't. My hand's barely functional. Kael?"

Kael shook his head slowly. "His readings are too high. The unclassified structure alone—" He looked at his own hands, at the staining that wouldn't wash out. "If any of us tried a full consumption and failed, the backwash would redistribute him instead of dissolving him."

"So we — what?" Petra looked at Thessa. "Call someone?"

"There's no one to call." The first words came flat. Then the rest came faster. "There are five of us. There should be fourteen. The contamination's denser every season and there are fewer of us every year and the standard approaches don't — they don't work the way they did, and I—"

She bit the line off. Her fingers cinched around the rim of her bowl until the knuckles went pale.

"I don't have a clean answer," she said. "But leaving him as he is means leaving the problem to grow."

"Test me," Maren said.

Thessa's eyes snapped to him.

"You can't consume me safely. You said it yourself — the readings are too high, the risk is too great. So test me instead. Make me do a consumption under your supervision. If I can't manage it, if I'm nothing but a contamination event with legs—" He used her words deliberately, watched them register. "Then you work out something else. But you've got nothing to lose from watching me try."

"Except more redistributed contamination across our workspace," Denna said from her position in the circle.

"If it goes wrong, the contamination's already here. This valley's baseline is worse than anything I could add."

*That's not entirely true,* Aldric said. *But it's close enough to be tactically useful.*

Kael scrubbed a hand over the bad shoulder and said, "Let him try."

Thessa looked at Kael. Looked at Maren. Looked at the valley around them, the three dungeon mouths breathing their contamination into the low ground, the grey-dark stone, the faint shimmer of quest-light pooling in the morning air — the raw glow without data, without labels, without the overlay the others navigated by.

"Fine," she said. "Supervised. Every step. And if what I see looks like redistribution instead of clearance, I stop you. Hard."

"Fine."

"Get up. We do it now."

---

They moved to the nearest dungeon mouth within the hour, the morning mist still thinning around them. Vapour rose from the contaminated ground in thin tendrils, catching the pale light, and the hum from the opening was a physical pressure that Maren could feel in his teeth. The stone around the mouth was nearly black from years of quest-light saturation, and the contamination smell was dense enough to coat the inside of his throat.

Maren knelt ten paces from the mouth with his ceramic bowl unwrapped and in his hands. His bowl sat on the ground in front of him, and in this place, surrounded by real sin-eaters, it looked exactly as fraudulent as it was. No staining. No ritual wear. Clean, cheap, useless.

Thessa stood behind him and to his right. Kael, Petra, Denna, and Ruald were arrayed in a loose half-circle at twenty paces — close enough to observe, far enough to absorb the backwash if the consumption went wrong. Hale stood another ten paces back with both hands at his sides, fingers curled, watching with the rigid attention of a young man who didn't want to be the first to flinch.

"Kneeling posture," Thessa said. "Bowl in front of you, open. Elbows off your knees — you need your chest open for the intake."

He adjusted. The stone was cold and damp through his trousers.

"Breathe first. Not the performance breathing you've been doing — the actual pattern. In through the nose until you feel the contamination enter your sinus cavity. Hold it. Let it settle behind your sternum before you open. If you tear the channel wide on the first pull, you'll spray it back out the way you've been doing."

*She's describing the primary intake sequence,* Aldric said. *It matches what the Order's texts describe, but more embodied. Less theoretical. She's teaching from practice, not doctrine.*

Maren breathed in. The contamination entered through his nose — not a metaphor, a physical sensation, the dense copper-sweet mixture that was the valley's baseline air — and he held it. The taste crept along the roof of his mouth, down the back of his throat. The gap in his chest stirred.

"Open," Thessa said.

He opened. Not his mouth — this wasn't about the mouth, despite the name. Opening was the thing he'd learned on his own near the creek bed, the thing that had nearly broken him: a deliberate surrender of the boundary between himself and the contamination around him. Like unclenching a fist he hadn't known was clenched, somewhere behind his sternum. The inside of his chest peeled back, and the first rush of contamination hit hard enough to make his vision spark.

This time it didn't flash straight through. It caught under his sternum instead — heavy, grinding, hot — as if the contamination had finally found purchase instead of a hole.

"There," Thessa said sharply. "Hold it there. Don't reach."

*It's seating,* Aldric said, startled. *For the first time it's actually seating.*

Then the pull ran wider anyway. Contamination answered from beyond the nearest pool, dragging through him in a pressure wave that locked his shoulders and set his teeth buzzing.

"Wider," Thessa said, and her voice had changed. The instructional flatness was gone. "He's pulling wider. Kael — do you see the radius?"

"I see it."

*Outflow's lower than intake,* Aldric said. *You're not venting all of it. Some of it's breaking down in the channel.*

The contamination poured through the gap and the gap was not a hole but a channel, a crack, and the crack widened as the contamination flowed, and the flow was not something Maren was controlling. His hands were locked on the cold stone beside his knees. His bowl sat empty because the bowl was for collection and there was nothing to collect. The load under his sternum stayed heavy while the rest moved through him in a thinner stream, some of its weight burning off in the gap. His vision blurred. The hum from the dungeon mouth rose in pitch and the mist around the opening churned and his body wanted to double over and vomit but Thessa's hand was on his shoulder, holding him upright, her grip hard and professional.

"Don't collapse. Keep the channel open. If you close it with contamination still in transit—"

He kept it open. His muscles were shaking. Aldric had gone quiet — the quiet that meant he was seeing something in the System's architecture that outpaced language — and Vess was counting.

*Fifteen seconds. Your body starts losing structural coherence around thirty. Close soon.*

"I can't — I don't know how to close it from—"

"Close the same way you opened," Thessa said. Her voice was tight but held. "Clench. The fist behind your sternum. Find it and close it."

He found it. He closed it. The contamination flow cut off like a valve shutting and the shock — the abrupt cessation of something that had been flowing through the core of him — knocked him sideways off his knees. He caught himself on his palms. His bowl tipped and rang against the stone, a clear ceramic note that sounded absurd in the contaminated dawn. His arms shook. His stomach heaved once, dry, and he spat on the ground and the spit was dark with residue.

"Stay down," Thessa said. She was standing over him, reading the interface. Around her, the other sin-eaters were reading too.

He was on his hands and knees on contaminated stone, shaking, spitting dark, and the gap in his chest felt wider than it had before the consumption, as if something had been stretched that wouldn't go back. His bowl lay on its side next to his hand, empty. A prop. A fake thing in a real place.

*Maren.* Aldric's voice came back, rough, stripped of its usual register. *The mouth dropped. The northeast seep dropped with it. So did the residue shelf above camp. I'm not seeing the outward bloom we got at the creek. Nothing on the perimeter is rising.*

The silence in the circle was different now.

Maren got himself back to kneeling. His hands were smeared with the dark residue of contaminated stone, his forearms trembling. He picked up his bowl and set it flat in front of him and looked at the clean white interior, then at Thessa.

She was staring at the interface readouts without blinking. Her thumb had gone still against the bowl's rim.

"No scatter bloom," Kael said. He had one hand hooked under the high shoulder as if holding it in place. "The mouth is down. The side pools are down."

"Outer markers held," Denna said from her position. She sounded startled.

"I can see that," Thessa said.

Petra was flexing her bandaged hand, staring at whatever the interface was showing her. "How? That's not — we consume and dissolve. We don't reduce ambient levels across an entire—"

"The gap," Thessa said. She was still reading the interface, her thumb pressed white against the bowl rim. "Same thing that made him dangerous. Before, the width threw the contamination back out thin. This time the intake seated first, and once it started breaking down—"

"The width pulled on the connected field instead of pushing," Kael said slowly. "Carried the drop instead of the spill."

Thessa nodded once. The other sin-eaters were looking between her and Maren.

"Sixty years ago," Kael said. "The one you told us about."

"They were consumed by the System. Corrected." Thessa's voice went flat. "Nothing left."

"But before that—"

"Before that, the records say contamination levels in their region were the lowest in a century."

Petra had stopped flexing her bad hand. Ruald had forgotten the root in his cheek. Hale was no longer at the far edge of the observation line. Denna was looking at Maren instead of the ground.

Thessa looked at Maren for a long time. He knelt on the cold stone with his dirty hands and his clean bowl and his trembling arms and the gap in his chest that was wider than it had been an hour ago, and he waited.

"You're sloppy," she said. "Your intake form is wrong. Your closing—"

"He shut the channel on live contamination," Kael said to the others, not unkindly. "You could feel the tear from twenty paces."

"Keep doing that and the rebound starts eating you from the inside within a month." Thessa hadn't taken her eyes off Maren. "You've got no control over the width of your aperture. You're a hazard to yourself and anyone near you."

"Yeah."

"And you did more in twenty seconds than I've done in a day."

Maren said nothing.

*Don't say anything,* Vess said. *She's working through something. Let her get there.*

Thessa looked at the valley, then at Maren kneeling on the contaminated stone with his clean bowl.

"Not entirely useless," she said.

Maren breathed out.

"We start with the intake form. Your posture's wrong, your breathing's wrong, your closing technique will kill you." She turned to the circle. "He doesn't eat, sleep, or breathe without my say-so until I'm satisfied he won't blow us all apart." Back to Maren. "And the first time you fake anything — one gesture, one posture, one breath that's performance instead of practice — I stop teaching and we go back to the consumption discussion. Clear?"

"Clear."

"Get off the ground. You look like you're praying."

He got off the ground. His legs barely held. Aldric said nothing. Vess didn't either. The morning light reached the bottom of the valley and the quest-light pooling near the dungeon mouths caught it — a faint shimmer, like water reflecting something Maren couldn't quite see, beautiful in the way things were beautiful when you didn't have the data to know what you were looking at.

Thessa walked toward the cook fire. Petra followed, flexing her bandaged hand. Ruald spat his root into the dirt and went after them. Kael hooked a spare flat stone a little closer to the fire with his heel before he turned away. Denna stooped to gather her clay jars from beside the shelter entrance and carried them in under one arm. Hale stood where he was a moment longer, then nodded once at Maren before turning away.

Maren picked up his bowl. The ceramic was cool in his hands, the same cheap clay it had always been, no heavier and no lighter for what had just passed through it. He went to the fire and sat on the stone Kael had nudged into place while the others moved around him and nobody told him to move.
