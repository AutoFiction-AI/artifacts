# Chapter 6

The bread sat in Maren's hand like a stone. Two days in the bottom of the pack had turned the crust the color of dry mud, cracked along one side, and the inside was dense and cold, the kind of cold that comes from being carried against a body all night and still not warming. Derry had broken the loaf in half and put the bigger piece in Maren's hand without looking at his face.

They were sitting by a stream maybe half a mile off the road, in a spot where the bank had been cut back by spring flooding and then left alone. Birch roots clawed out of the exposed dirt, pale as tendons. The water was shallow and moved without much purpose, collecting in a flat pool before finding its way through a gap in the rocks and continuing. Late summer light came through the canopy in uneven pieces. It was a good place to stop. It would have been a good place to eat.

Maren brought the bread to his mouth and his throat closed.

Not dramatically — no gagging, no heave. Just a quiet, total refusal. The muscles in his neck went tight and his jaw locked and the bread touched his lips and his body said no. The smell of it — yeast and flour and the faint sourness of age — hit his stomach and his stomach moved in a slow, oily roll and he put the bread back down on his knee.

His right hand shook once against the crust, small enough that only the crumbs gave it away.

*You should eat,* Aldric said.

The voice was flat. Not the usual delivery — no lecture underneath, no implicit comparison to the dietary habits of men who had served the Order with discipline and purpose. Just the statement.

Maren didn't answer.

*Your body requires fuel. Weakness will worsen without caloric —*

*He knows,* Vess said. *Leave it.*

A silence inside his skull. The two of them pulling back to their separate corners, or whatever the equivalent was when three people occupied the same head. Maren could feel them like pressure changes — Aldric's disapproval a dull weight behind his right eye, Vess's watchfulness a cooler thing, lower, settling somewhere near the base of his skull where his neck met his shoulders.

Derry was eating. Methodically, without apparent enjoyment, tearing pieces off his half and putting them in his mouth and chewing and swallowing. His jaw worked with the steady, mechanical patience of a man who had spent his life doing necessary things without passion. He sat with his legs stretched toward the water, his boots unlaced, the sweat stains on his shirt dried to pale maps of salt. His face was red from the walking and from the heat and from something else that Maren would not look at directly.

"Pell counts everything now," Derry said.

Maren looked at him.

"She's — I don't know when it started, but it's everything. The steps to the door. The chickens. She counted my buttons the other morning, stood there in her nightshirt and pointed at each one and said the number." He tore another piece of bread. "She gets stuck on seven. She can do one through six and then she says seven and then she says seven again and then she says eight. But the second seven — she knows it's wrong, you can see it on her face, she just can't —" He chewed. Swallowed. "Lenna thinks it's because I taught her the song wrong. The counting song. I probably did. I can't remember which one has seven in a different — anyway."

"That's good," Maren said. His voice came out strange. Thin, like he was talking through cloth.

"Yeah." Derry pulled at the bread. "And Lenna — she got it in her head to grow herbs. Not the cooking kind, the kind you sell. The apothecary in Silt buys dried fennel, and she read somewhere that fennel grows in anything, which — I could've told her, fennel's a weed where I'm from, my mother spent years trying to keep it out of the onion rows. But she planted it in that little bed by the south wall, the one that gets no sun because of the shed, and she was so — three coppers a bundle, she said. She had the whole thing worked out."

Maren watched Derry's hands on the bread. He held it with the care he gave everything else — firm, careful, the grip of a man who'd spent his life picking things up and putting them down without damage. Turnips. Fence posts. His daughter.

"It didn't grow," Derry said. "Or it grew about an inch and then just sort of — stopped. She'd go out every morning and look at it. Just stand there. And I didn't say anything because I'm not — I mean, you know Lenna, she doesn't want to hear it from me." He ate. "But she's already planning what to try next. She told me before I — before we left. She said, 'When you get back, I'll have something growing.' Like it was a promise she was making, but to the garden, not to me."

*His wife is growing produce in shade,* Aldric said. *That is not going to —*

*Shut up,* Vess said.

The stream made a sound like someone running their thumb along the edge of a bowl. Maren listened to it and to Derry's chewing and to the nothing that was happening inside his own body where hunger should have been. He could feel the absence of appetite like a shape — a hollow where the mechanism of eating normally sat, gummed up and stuck.

"And the cow," Derry said. The words came out with the exhausted affection of a man who had been bested by livestock. "She got out again. The day before we — the day before. I spent an hour and a half walking her back from Garron's field. An hour and a half. She doesn't run, that's the thing. She just walks. Slowly. In the wrong direction. With this look on her face like she's — like she's doing it on purpose but she wants you to know she doesn't care if you know."

Maren almost laughed. It came out as a breath through his nose, a small convulsion that died somewhere in his chest, but Derry heard it and glanced at him and something in Derry's face opened a crack. Not a smile. An easing.

"I reinforced the fence three times," Derry said. "Three times, Maren. And she just — she finds the one spot. Every time. Like she's testing the whole fence with her face and she just knows where the —"

He stopped talking. He was looking at Maren's untouched bread.

"You should eat," he said.

"I know."

"You really should."

"I know. I'm going to."

Derry didn't push it. He went back to his own bread and for a while they sat there and the stream moved and the light shifted in the canopy and the day kept going around them, loose and unresolved, nothing required of it and nothing settled. A bird did something in the birches — Maren couldn't see it, just heard the branch bounce and resettle. The air smelled like warm dirt and water and, underneath that, the faint sweetish ozone that meant quest-light was somewhere nearby. He'd smelled it his whole life and never known what it was until Aldric told him, four days ago, in a voice that made the information sound like an indictment.

*We need to choose a route,* Aldric said.

Maren closed his eyes.

*The main road is untenable. Selwick Pass removes us from it and limits approach angles. There is stone cover for most of the —*

*And a bridge,* Vess said. *One approach, no cover. Any tracker with half a class sits there.*

*I am aware of the bridge. The pass itself —*

*Doesn't matter when he can't fight.*

The word sat in Maren's skull. Fight. What had happened yesterday — his body moving without him, the camp knife going in while he watched from behind his own eyes — that wasn't fighting. He didn't know what it was.

*East gives us brush, bad sightlines, and room to disappear,* Vess said. *Off the roads. Move at night when the interface reads shorter.*

*East also gives us residual pools,* Aldric said. *If necessity drives him to consume again before we understand what the previous ingestion produced, the result may be catastrophic.*

Silence.

*I know,* Vess said.

Maren opened his eyes. The stream was still there. The birch roots were still there. Derry was lacing his boots back up, his thick fingers working the leather through the eyelets with the patience of a man who laced boots every morning of his life and would lace them every morning until he was dead.

"Hey," Maren said.

Derry looked up.

"Tell me about Pell again."

Something happened in Derry's face — a complicated thing, several expressions arriving at once and then settling into one that Maren couldn't have named if pressed. Gratitude, maybe. Or just the relief of being asked to talk about something that was real and good and had nothing to do with any of this.

"She's doing this thing now," Derry said, "where she puts all her shoes in a line by the door. Not just her shoes. Everyone's shoes. She'll take mine and Lenna's and line them up smallest to biggest, and if you move one she'll put it back. She's very — she has opinions about the order. Strong opinions." He pulled the lace tight. "She's going to be three next month, and she already has opinions about shoes."

Inside Maren's skull, the silence kept its shape. The Crook meant real sin-eaters, a whole camp of them looking at him long enough to understand what he was and then deciding whether the cleanest way to deal with the thing in him was to clear it or clear him with it. East meant low ground, hidden places, the sort of wet ditches where quest-light liked to gather, and the possibility that sooner or later desperation would put him on his knees in front of it again. Vess had said *I know* in the same tone she used for distances and arteries. That frightened him more than if she'd kept arguing.

He pinched the bread until the crust cracked and crumbs fell onto his thigh. Derry was still talking about Pell putting shoes in order by the door, furious at any boot that broke the line. Maren kept his eyes on Derry and let him go on. He could listen to shoes for another minute. He could do that instead of choosing.

---

The quest-light was in the water.

To Maren it was what it had always been: a faint shimmer, like heat haze, except the air wasn't hot enough and the shimmer moved against the current rather than with it. It pooled in the flat stretch of the stream where the water slowed, collecting in the still places behind rocks and in the eddies where fallen leaves turned in slow circles. To Maren it looked like moonlight would look if moonlight had weight and opinions about where to gather. He'd seen it a hundred times. A thousand. In ditches outside dungeon mouths, in the corners of rooms where someone had died, in the air above the ceramic bowl when he'd performed his fake rituals and nothing had answered.

He was standing at the edge of the stream because he'd gone to wash his hands and then forgotten to wash them. The water moved over his boots — he'd stepped in without thinking, and the cold was a clean, specific thing against his ankles, and he'd just stopped. The light was doing something in the pool. Twisting. Catching on itself.

"There's quest-light in the water," he said. Quietly, because Derry had gone to refill the water skins upstream and Maren wasn't really talking to Derry.

*I see it,* Aldric said.

A pause. Usually the reading came fast — the paladin's narration of what the interface laid over the world, the data that everyone else saw and Maren never had, delivered with the clipped rhythm of a man cataloguing someone else's mess. Threat assessment, residual quest class, contamination grade, probability of hostile emergence. The information had been useful and humiliating in roughly equal measure.

But Aldric didn't start reading.

*It is a residual pool,* he said. *Low-grade. Passive. No active quest structure, no threat emergence within — I would estimate none.* Another pause, longer. *The light is refracting through the water in a way that — I have not seen this particular configuration before. The interface shows standard residual markers, but the visual phenomenon is — separate from the data. The data says it is ordinary. The light suggests otherwise.*

Maren watched the shimmer. It bent and stretched as if the whole pool were warped glass, pulling itself into shapes that had no name and didn't need one. Where it caught on the surface tension of the water it made something he could almost call beautiful, except that word had never had much use in his vocabulary and he didn't trust it now.

*How it moves,* Aldric said, and his voice was different. Quieter. Stripped of the cadence he used for reports and corrections and the careful articulation of his ongoing disappointment. *It looks as though the light is trying to become something. Not a quest, not a threat. Something that does not have a classification. It simply moves through the water and it is — it is quite —*

He stopped.

Maren stood in the stream and the cold water ran over his boots and the quest-light turned in the pool and for a moment neither of them said anything. Not the loaded silence of disagreement or the pointed silence of withholding. Just quiet. The two of them — the con man and the dead paladin — looking at the same thing from different sides of the same skull, and the thing being, for once, not a problem to solve or a danger to assess or a failure to name.

The light twisted. It caught something in the rocks — a vein of quartz, maybe, or just a wet edge — and flared briefly, a pulse of warmth brushing his face, brief as candle heat when someone opens a door.

*It is beautiful,* Aldric said.

He offered no qualification after it and didn't repeat it.

Vess said nothing. Whether she saw through Maren's eyes at all, Maren didn't know. She hadn't offered, and he hadn't asked. Her silence now was neither agreement nor dismissal. It was the silence of someone watching someone else have a private moment and choosing not to be in it.

Maren stood in the water until his feet went numb. The light continued its slow, purposeless work in the pool. Upstream, he could hear Derry filling the water skins, the leather gulping and then the sound of him flicking the last drops off and capping them. Ordinary sounds. The sounds of a man doing a small task well because it was the task in front of him.

He looked down at his hands. They were in the water now — he'd let them hang at his sides and the stream had taken them, the cold wrapping around his fingers and wrists, pulling the heat out of him until it stopped feeling like discomfort and started feeling like an offer. The current worried at every little jump in the muscles until it all looked like water.

He bent down and scooped water to his mouth and drank. It tasted like stone and cold and the faint sweet chemical edge of whatever quest-light left behind when it sat in water long enough. He drank again. His throat opened for the water. It had shut against the bread. It wasn't food, but it was something going in instead of everything coming up, and his body accepted it with the desperate, uncritical gratitude of a system that had been locked for a full day.

When he walked back to where they'd been sitting, Derry was repacking the bag. The bread was still on the ground where Maren had left it. Derry looked at it, looked at Maren, and put it in the bag without comment.

"East, I think," Maren said. "Off the road. Vess says there's better cover."

Derry nodded. He didn't ask who Vess was. He'd stopped asking about the voices after the second day. It was the same principle as a neighbor's dog that barked all night — not acceptance, just the dull knowledge that asking didn't change anything.

*Acceptable,* Vess said.

*I did not agree to east,* Aldric said.

*You don't have legs,* Vess said. *You agreed when he did.*

They walked east. The stream fell behind them. Derry kept talking beside him, mostly about Pell learning the word no and using it on spoons, boots, sleep, anything that came near enough to deserve it.

Maren listened to Derry's voice and the leather creak of the water skins and the dry hiss of weeds against their legs. He tucked his right hand against his thigh and kept walking.
