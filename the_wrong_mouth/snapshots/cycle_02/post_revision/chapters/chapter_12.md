# Chapter 12

The hum had changed.

It wasn't louder. Maren had been hearing the System's hum since he was old enough to lie awake wondering what it was, and it had always sat at the same low pitch, ambient and ignorable, like wind or someone else's heartbeat through a shared wall. But since the consumption at the creek bed, the real one, the one that left him on his hands and knees with his mouth tasting like pennies and ozone while Vess talked him through the shaking, the pitch had dropped. There was something underneath the sound he'd always known. A vibration that sat in his back teeth and the flat bones of his skull.

He'd tried to ignore it for two days. Two days of walking through scrub oak and exposed shale, deeper into dungeon territory than he'd ever been, with the road no longer pretending to be a road. Two days of Aldric's silence. These were not his usual silences, the pointed ones, the annoyed ones, the ones that came loaded with moral judgment. This was the silence from the consumption. The one that started when Maren's eyes were rolling back and his hands were cramping into claws around the ceramic bowl. Aldric had seen something during the ritual and he'd stopped talking and he hadn't started again, not really.

Derry was asleep across the dead fire, curled on his side with his pack wedged under his head. He'd turned in an hour ago, said he was tired, which was accurate in the way most of Derry's stated reasons were accurate without being the reason. Derry slept facing the road now. He'd been doing that since Caul. His back to Maren, his breath even, his body aimed in the direction of home.

The first cool night of the season. It wasn't cold yet, but the air carried an edge the summer air hadn't, a sharpness that cut through the contamination smell, which was itself different this deep. It smelled less sweet and more mineral. The ozone undercurrent that usually signaled quest residue had thickened into something almost geological, like the air over limestone when it rained.

Maren sat against a birch trunk, the bark a pattern of rough and smooth against his shoulder blades, and watched the embers die. His body had mostly recovered from the consumption. His hands were steady. His stomach had settled around midday. But the hum sat in his molars and something about the night, the thin quality of the starlight, the way quest-light bled above the southern horizon like a stain on wet paper, made the ordinary act of sitting still feel precarious. As if the ground under him was not quite solid.

*You should rest.* Aldric. The words had the flat, procedural quality of a man checking a box, concern filed and duty noted.

"You've been quiet for two days," Maren said. Low, because of Derry. "Not your normal quiet. Not pissed-off quiet or disgusted-with-my-choices quiet. This is the quiet from the consumption, and I'm done waiting for you to come out of it on your own."

The hum sat in his teeth while the embers went grey.

"Aldric."

*I know.*

"Then talk to me. What did you see?"

*What I have to say will change the shape of things. I am telling you that before I begin because once I say it, it does not unsay. I have been trying to put it in order for two days. It resists order.*

"That's your problem. Start anyway."

Another silence. Then Aldric's voice, and something in it Maren had never heard. Not the formality, the sarcasm, the controlled grief, or the careful way he laid every sentence out like a report. There was something raw under all that. The rough catch in a man's breath when he bites down on pain and talks anyway.

*When I first entered your consciousness, I thought your System-blindness was absence. Missing faculty. A dark room with no lamp. That was wrong. The System did not behave around you the way it behaves around absence. It kept... sliding.*

"Sliding how?"

*Off you. Around you. Failing to find purchase. I noted it. I filed it as an irregularity. I should not have filed it.*

Maren's hands went flat on his thighs. His fingers pressed the seam of his trousers, each thread under them clear and separate.

"You're doing that thing where you make it sound precise and useless at the same time."

*Very well. You keep using the wrong word for yourself. Blind suggests the thing is there and you cannot perceive it. That is not what is happening. The System reaches for you and cannot get hold. It cannot write on you.*

Maren looked over at Derry's sleeping back, then back at the fire that wasn't fire anymore, just a soft bed of ash.

"So what, I'm immune now?"

Aldric took a beat before answering.

*If you require the blunt word, yes.*

Immunity was the word.

The word did not reach back and fix anything. It did not fix the years Derry had stood half a step behind him at rituals, coughing once for green prompt and twice for red because the village sin-eater could not see the damn residue over a corpse. It did not fix the market habit of tapping prices into Maren's palm because numbers in the air were for other people. It did not fix his mother's face the first time he told her there was nothing there, no glow, no prompt, no class-mark, and watched her decide that careful gentleness was going to be the rest of her life with him.

"That is a clean word for a filthy life," he said.

*I did not say it spared you anything. I said the diagnosis was wrong.*

"Fine. Then use the right one."

The hum pulsed again. It came up through the shale and the tree and into his jaw.

*The interface every other person sees from birth is not the thing itself. It is a surface. An inner surface. A wall.*

"A wall to what?"

*Containment.*

It was one word, plain enough that Maren almost missed it.

"No. Don't do that. You don't get to drop one priest-word into the fire and leave me to sort out the ashes."

For once Aldric did not bristle.

*I have been watching from the only vantage I think this world allows. Inside a consciousness the System cannot touch, I can see seams. Borders. Places where the visible interface meets structure. During the consumption at the creek bed, when you actually opened yourself to it, I saw the structure flex. I saw where contamination comes from. I saw pressure on the far side of the wall.*

*The hum changed then,* Vess said. *He's not imagining that.*

Aldric went on as if each word had to be worked past a mouthful of grit.

*Everyone else sees classifications, levels, prompts. They see wallpaper. I am seeing what is under the paper. The thing those surfaces were built to hide. The System is not arranging the world, Maren. It is holding something in place.*

"You're sure that's what you're seeing," Maren said. "Not just the kind of story a dead paladin tells himself when the rules stop making sense."

The answer did not come quickly.

*I am sure about the seams. I am sure about the pressure. I am sure the System fails at your edges in ways it should not. What lies beyond the wall, I cannot name with certainty. Containment is observation. Motive is hypothesis.*

"What's on the other side?"

His answer came slower than anything Maren had heard from him. It wasn't reluctance. It was more like a man groping elbow-deep through muddy water for something small and important.

*I do not know. Not in any way I can classify, which is part of the problem. It does not fit the categories the System uses because it is older than the categories. Older, wilder, and pressing. I can see where the containment has thinned. I can see where something behind it pushes back.*

The vibration hit his teeth hard enough that he had to unclench his jaw.

"And me? Where do I fit in that?"

*You do not fit. That is the point. The System writes itself onto human consciousness at birth. Interface, classifications, all of it. Like dye taking cloth. It has been trying to do that to you since the day you were born and it keeps failing.*

"So I'm what. Bad cloth."

*No. A place the dye does not take. A place the wall does not close cleanly near active mouths and heavy contamination. That is where System-blindness appears. Not at random. At strain points.*

Maren's laugh came out thin and mean.

"Careful. You're about half a sentence from calling me a crack in the world."

*If the phrase is offensive, take that up with the architecture. I am describing where you stand in it, not complimenting you.*

He should have had something ready for that. Some easy answer. Some deflection that would let the whole thing slide past without sticking. Instead he sat there with his hands on his knees and the hum in his bones and thought of every person who had ever spoken to the air above his shoulder because Derry was the one who could tell them whether a prompt had changed color.

Vess spoke into the quiet.

*I can feel it too. Since the creek bed. Not all the pretty wall language. Just pressure. Like something leaning when it ought to stay put.*

Aldric answered her, but he was really answering Maren.

*What formed when Vess entered you, what the System named unclassified, is not just a classification error. From where I am looking, it reads like widening stress. The collision between us inside a host the System cannot write on has made the breach worse.*

"The bounty."

The thought arrived whole. The same sick speed old fraud-logic used to arrive with, a pattern seen all at once from outside.

"It's not — " He stopped. "The bounty isn't punishment. It's maintenance. I'm the leak and it's trying to close me up."

*Most likely.* A pause. *The corrections. The heroes. Repair attempts. I cannot prove intent, but the behavior fits repair better than punishment.*

"With me in it."

*Yes.*

Maren swallowed. His throat worked dry.

"At the creek bed I felt something push back. I thought that was the contamination or me about to throw up."

*You did throw up,* Vess said. *Those are not mutually exclusive.*

For one breath, absurdly, he nearly smiled.

Then Aldric said, *You touched the structure itself.*

The smile died.

*Contamination is erosion made material. Sin-eating is maintenance work. That much I would stake my name on.* The report-cadence stumbled. *The rest is theory. But when you opened yourself properly, you touched the seam from the wrong side. The bowl narrowed it. Gave the opening a —* He stopped. *A boundary. I watched it happen and I do not have the framework to explain what I saw.*

Maren looked toward his pack without meaning to. The ceramic bowl was wrapped in cloth inside it. Same chipped rim. Same market glaze. Nothing about it looked equal to the sound in his teeth.

"Thessa's been doing cleanup blind."

*From inside it,* Aldric said. *She knows the work. She does not know the architecture. I do not think anyone alive does.*

The night bird had not called again. The scrub oak stood black against the stained horizon, each branch a fracture in the faint quest-light. Derry's breathing was even and unknowing, the breath of a man who still got to think the world broke in ordinary ways. Bad weather. Bad roads. A sick cow. A hero with a grudge.

Maren sat with it.

---

He sat with it long enough for the ash in the fire pit to lose every last vein of orange.

Vess did not speak. Her silence had a different quality from Aldric's. It was less pressurized, more watchful. Maren was grateful for it. The revelation sat in his chest like something swallowed too fast, all corners.

He picked at the birch bark behind him. Pulled a strip free and turned it between his fingers until it tore.

"The Order of the Veil," he said.

Aldric's silence shifted. Became the kind of quiet that falls over a card table when someone finally names the cheat.

"You reached for me on purpose."

*Yes.*

"Why."

*Because I was already looking for people like you.*

"People like me," Maren repeated. "Meaning what."

*The Order investigated anomalies. Misclassifications. Bounty errors. Places where the interface showed seams it should not show. I was assigned to that work. Later I asked for more of it.*

"And somewhere in all that righteous paperwork you found the blind ones."

*The System-blind,* Aldric said automatically, then, a moment later, *yes.*

He kept going before Maren could bite at him again.

*I found a pattern first. Not names. Birth clusters near active mouths. High contamination regions. Interface distortion around specific individuals. Then I found older records. Very old. Copies of copies. Bad copies. They treated System-blindness not as failed integration, but as structural irregularity. Stress marks in the containment.*

"Fenhold."

*Yes.*

"You came to inspect the stress mark."

This time Aldric did bristle.

*I came to investigate a pattern that might have explained what the System was hiding.*

"Near enough."

The birch bark gave under his thumb. Split into white curls that clung to his skin.

*I requested survey assignment to the dungeon mouth nearest Fenhold. I told the Order it was a routine structural assessment. It was not routine. I wanted proximity to a confirmed System-blind host and an active site.*

"And then the site killed you."

The answer took time.

*The site was stable when we entered. Three independent assessments confirmed it. Four hours into the survey, the dungeon's classifications changed in real time. Walls marked load-bearing became pass-through. Supports redesignated. Stone that should have held dissolved under new rules. The dungeon did not fail geologically. It was redefined into collapse.*

"By the System."

*That is what it looked like.*

"Aldric."

The name stopped him.

"Did the System kill you for looking too hard."

When Aldric answered, the old report-cadence was still there, but wearing through.

*I cannot prove intent.* A silence. *I can say the changes were targeted. I can say no other site in the region shifted that day. I can say I was —*

He stopped. Started again, quieter.

*I was buried alive. In the deepest part of the architecture I had ever seen. If you want the plain version. Yes. It may have killed me for looking too hard.*

May have was not certainty. Somehow that was worse.

"And after that you climbed into my skull because it was the only place your investigation could keep breathing."

*Yes.*

There was no padding around it this time, no tidy framing, just yes.

"Without asking."

*I could not ask.*

"You could've left me alone."

*I could have ceased. I did not.*

He waited for Aldric to dress it up. He did not.

Then, after a silence long enough to feel chosen, Aldric said, *I told myself embedding in you would let me observe without changing the fault itself. That was convenient. It was also wrong.*

That was the one that hit.

The back of Maren's throat flooded sour. He got up too fast, turned away from the fire pit, and bent over with his palms braced on his knees. Spit hit the shale. It was not enough to vomit. That was worse than vomiting. He stayed there, breathing through his mouth, until the taste of acid thinned. Then he shoved both hands into his hair and held on. If he could have taken the top of his skull off like a lid and shaken both of them into the dirt, he would have.

When he straightened, he went to his pack, dug through cloth and stale bread and spare shirt, and dragged out the ceramic bowl.

Derry shifted once in his sleep, then settled.

Maren came back to the dead fire with the bowl in both hands.

"Before we talk about getting anybody out," he said, "prove one damned thing."

No one answered for a beat.

Then Aldric said, *Proof may be too strong a word.*

"Then we're done here."

*There is one part we can test,* Aldric said, fast enough that Maren heard the strain under the formality. *Not the wall. Not what is beyond it. The bowl. Set it in the ash.*

*Small test,* Vess said. *If your fingers start locking, stop. If your teeth feel like they're humming from the roots, stop sooner.*

Maren stared down at the bowl. It was cheap ceramic. There was a small chip at the rim where he'd knocked it against a cart wheel in Fenhold three summers ago. His thumb found that chip automatically.

"You chose me," he said. "You got in. Since then it's been — bounty hunters, and Caul, and people dead because I left villages dirty, and — " He couldn't get past Derry. "That thing at the creek bed nearly killed me and you're still hiding behind the investigation."

*The investigation matters,* Aldric said, and there was the old armor again, thin and familiar and infuriating.

*No,* Vess said. Quiet. Flat. *Not that part first. He knows it matters to you. Say the other part. You picked him anyway.*

Aldric did not answer.

Vess added, *He's allowed to hate that.*

Maren gave one short laugh that was not laughter.

"Good. Somebody in here can still count."

He set the bowl in the center of the dead fire pit instead. Ash shifted under the base with a dry whisper. The stone ring was still warm in two places if he held his palm low enough over it, heat hiding under the grey the way embers hid under a kicked-over fire.

The bowl looked stupid there. Market ceramic in a fire pit three days from anywhere sensible. Thin glaze, cheap curve, one hairline scratch running from the chip toward the inside. The kind of thing you'd use for oats or onions or washing blood off your hands if you had no better basin. Not a tool for anything built into the bones of the world.

"What do I do."

*What you did at the creek bed,* Aldric said. *Less of it. Do not take anything in. Just open enough for the pressure to notice the shape.*

"That sentence means nothing."

*It means the thing you did by instinct and nearly died from,* Vess said. *Do the first inch of it. Not the rest.*

His mouth went wet at once. Not with hunger. With memory. Copper under the tongue, the awful full-body warning that came before the real heave. His hands knew before the rest of him did. The old fake motions had always been theater: thumb on rim, two fingers under base, head bowed at an angle meant to read devout from a grieving family's doorway. The real opening had nothing to do with that. It was lower. Meaner. A slackening somewhere behind the breastbone, like unlatching a gate you had spent years leaning your whole weight against.

He hated that his body remembered.

Derry breathed in, breathed out. Soft. Unaware. A sleeping man ten feet away while Maren crouched over a dead fire trying to let just enough of the wrong thing touch him to prove a dead paladin wasn't just wrong in an expensive way.

"If this wakes him," Maren muttered, "I'm blaming both of you."

Neither answered.

He crouched. One hand on the rim. One hand braced on his own knee because the shale had started its slow tilt again, the not-quite-level feeling that came before his balance went treacherous. He let his shoulders loosen by half an inch.

The hum changed immediately.

Not louder. Narrower.

The sound that had been spread through the ground and his jaw and the back of his eyes drew inward as if someone had taken a wide, blunt knife and turned it edgewise. His molars stopped ringing all at once. The silence there was so abrupt it hurt. The ache moved into his palm instead. Ceramic against skin, and under the glaze a vibration so clean and steady it made the tendons in his wrist tighten.

Nothing happened in the pit for one breath.

Then the ash nearest the bowl twitched.

Not from wind. The night was still enough that the birch leaves held their faces flat. Not from Maren's shaking either. The tremor in him was fine and fast and private. This was different. A grey line of ash no wider than a bootlace lifted, slid across the blackened stone, and laid itself against the outer curve of the bowl.

He froze.

Another line followed. Then three more, each one dragging a faint trail through the soot, until the dead fire looked combed. Ash from opposite sides of the pit moved inward in slow dry threads and began collecting against the ceramic. When the first little heap hit the chip in the rim, it climbed. It should not have climbed. Dry ash did not crawl uphill against glazed pottery. This did. Grain by grain, patient as frost, until a dark crescent built itself along the inside of the bowl.

Maren's throat closed.

*There,* Aldric said, and for once the word held no lecture, only naked relief. *It narrows when given a boundary.*

*No breeze,* Vess said. *No hand in the ash. That's real.*

The bowl grew heavier.

Not truly heavy. Still cheap ceramic, still something he could crack by dropping on stone. But his hand received it as if weight were accumulating from someplace that did not respect distance. The vibration in the glaze deepened until it matched the pulse that had lived in his teeth for days. A smell came off it too, sudden and intimate: wet limestone, pennies, the sweet-rotten edge of contamination stripped of all its village disguises. Not grave flowers. Not blessed oil. Not the things he'd taught himself to expect while lying to widows. This smell was cave water over old metal. Ground opened with something dead in it.

The southward stain of quest-light shivered.

It did not vanish. It narrowed, same as the hum had, pulling from a broad smear into one brighter thread for the space of a heartbeat before spreading again. Small change. Brief. But Maren had been staring at that stain half the night, and he knew what it had looked like a blink earlier.

His fingers started to cramp.

Not the full seizure from the creek bed. Nothing that violent. Just the first treacherous drawing-in, tendons shortening, thumb wanting to lock against the rim. He snatched his hand back.

The ash fell at once.

The little crescent inside the bowl collapsed with a whisper. The threads on the stone loosened into ordinary mess. The hum rushed back into his teeth hard enough to make him gasp through them. His stomach lurched. He swallowed it down and tasted acid anyway.

*Enough,* Vess said.

Maren sat back on his heels. The shale bit through his boots. He kept both hands spread wide on his thighs so nobody could mistake the shaking for agreement.

The bowl sat in the ash with a charcoal smear inside it. Nothing miraculous now. Just cheap ceramic dirtied by a campfire. But the smear was wrong. A crescent line, higher on one side than the other, like something had leaned there from within.

"What," he said carefully, because if he didn't say it carefully he'd start shouting and Derry would wake, "does that prove."

Aldric answered in the same stripped voice.

*It proves the vessel changes the behavior of the pressure when you open. It proves the bowl was not incidental at the creek bed. It proves there is a difference between leakage diffused through a place and leakage given shape.*

"And the rest."

*The wall is still the best model I have,* Aldric said. *What presses against it, what the System intends, whether this bowl can separate anything or repair anything, those remain inferences.*

Maren looked from the bowl to Derry's sleeping back and then south again where the quest-light had already gone broad and ugly as before.

That was the part that settled it. Not the lecture. Not the old Order records. Not even the ash climbing the bowl, though that would be living in his head a while. It was the way both of them, finally, had run out of certainty in the same place. They had one true thing and a pile of guesses around it, and the one true thing was dangerous enough to drag ash uphill and start locking his hand after a few breaths.

He reached in and picked the bowl up by the base. It had gone cool again. Almost innocent.

"All right," he said.

He sat down again, not against the birch this time. On bare shale, the stone biting cold through his trousers. The bowl stayed in his lap. Ash marked the inside in a crooked grey crescent. His hands had started shaking, fine and fast, a tremor running from thumb to wrist.

"Here's what happens now," he said. "You see something in there, you tell me when it happens. Not two days later when you've turned it into a report. Not after you've decided what I can handle. When it happens."

Aldric was quiet long enough that Maren thought he might refuse.

Then: *Agreed.*

"And I'm not putting this to my mouth again blind." Maren tightened his grip on the bowl until the glaze bit his palm. "Not for your Order. Not for your wall. Not for whatever — " He stopped. Drew a breath through his teeth. "It just pulled ash uphill because I breathed wrong for three seconds, Aldric. I'm not improvising with that. We get to the Crook, we find Thessa, somebody who's actually been doing the work for thirty years looks at this and tells me what I'm holding. Then maybe the Woundmouth."

*It is the thinnest site,* Aldric said, automatically, because of course that was the first place his mind went.

"Good. Then it'll still be there after the Crook."

*It nearly turned your hands inside out last time,* Vess said. *He's right.*

That shut Aldric up.

Somewhere in the scrub oak, a branch cracked under its own weight and fell. Derry shifted again, one hand coming free of the blanket, palm-up on the shale, fingers loosely curled. The hand of a man who planted things and pulled weeds and held his daughter while she counted to ten and missed seven every time. A hand that had spent eleven years feeding lies to Maren at exactly the right moment so the village could keep believing. A hand that did not belong anywhere near a camp where ash could climb a bowl because Maren breathed wrong for three seconds.

Maren looked south. The Woundmouth was out there somewhere beyond the dark ridges, quest-light bleeding into the sky instead of pooling. The Crook was between here and there. Thessa was between here and there. If the bowl could answer him at a dead campsite, if one sloppy half-opening could make the ground answer back, then the road had narrowed hard. Thessa first. The Woundmouth after. There was no room left for pretending and even less for guessing.

The ceramic was cool at first. Then, slowly, under his palms, it picked up the same low vibration that lived in his teeth.

The ash mark inside it held.

He sat with the bowl in his lap and the terms he'd just set hanging in the dark between all three of them. No one argued. The hum kept on, deep and patient under the ground, while the night thinned one grain at a time around Derry's sleeping back and the southward stain of quest-light, and the wrong little crescent of ash stayed where the ceramic had taught it to stay.
