# Chapter 5

Aldric hadn't spoken since the night before.

Maren had walked for hours with that silence inside him. It had weight, a mouth-shut pressure that told him somebody was choosing it. He knew that shape. Priests had gone quiet like that when they caught him mid-fraud. Derry's wife had worn it the night they left Fenhold. Villagers had carried it while deciding whether to pay for a ritual they suspected was fake. It was the loudest kind of quiet he knew, the kind that meant someone had seen something and still hadn't decided how to carry it.

The road ran between two fields that had gone fallow long enough for the weeds to turn woody, knee-high tangles of dry yellow scrub that rattled when the wind shifted. Late-summer sun pressed on the back of his neck. The pack kept rubbing the same raw spot on his right shoulder it had been working on since Fenhold. Half a step behind him, Derry kept the same cramped gait he'd had ever since they left the hollow where the botched consumption had scrambled everything sideways. Neither of them had spoken since the creek crossing.

Vess was not quiet.

She'd been inside his skull for less than a day and was already doing something Maren could only call *watching*, a constant low alertness sitting behind his eyes like the front edge of a headache. She tracked the road ahead, the treeline to the east, the ditch running alongside the packed dirt, and the angle of shadows on the ground. Aldric would have narrated the interface. Vess simply worked, and Maren got the result: a prickling presence behind his eyes, uninvited, constant, thorough.

*Two hundred yards. South. Two, armed, approaching from the sheep fold.*

She delivered it with the household flatness of a warning that soup was getting cold.

"What?" Maren said aloud, and his voice came out hoarse, scraped thin from disuse.

Derry turned his head. "Hm?"

*They've been paralleling the road from the fold. They're closing now.* After a pause that sounded almost clinical, she added, *They look like they've been waiting.*

"Someone's coming," Maren said.

Derry didn't carry weapons. His hand went to the camp knife riding in the pack on Maren's shoulder, the short utility blade he used for rope and kindling and stubborn jar lids. His fingers brushed the handle and stayed there. That was as far as his courage went.

"How do you know that?" Derry started, but Vess cut across him.

*Aldric.*

Her voice cut sharp and impatient, like she'd knocked on a door she expected to find open.

The paladin came back like a latch lifting. His voice was flat and controlled, every word set down with the precision of a man filing a report for an audience that no longer existed.

*Two hero-class combatants. System profiles active. They have been tracking the bounty marker. The younger one is reading your classification field and seeing nothing. He is confused. The woman is not confused.*

"How many?" Maren asked.

The question was aimed at both of them, the dead paladin and the dead assassin. The absurdity of asking two ghosts for a tactical count on a road in the afternoon sun might have been funny thirty seconds later, if thirty seconds later were not about to become a different kind of experience entirely.

*Two,* Vess said.

*Two,* Aldric confirmed. Then, with something that might have been reluctance, he added, *The bounty is flagged for hostile engagement. They are authorized to kill.*

They stepped out from behind the sheep pen's collapsed wall at maybe sixty yards. The late-afternoon sun was behind Maren, so they were walking into it and squinting, and for one stupid second he thought he could wave and keep going and they'd take him for some farmer heading home.

The woman was the closer threat. The knowledge reached his muscles before his mind managed to name it. She was around forty, maybe older, with weather-darkened skin and bracers that had been repaired more than once. The leather had been patched over with mismatched hide that said years of field work and no budget for new gear. A short sword rode on her hip, its pommel wrapped in faded blue cloth, wind-frayed and tied with the tight automatic knot of something retied every morning. It was someone's color, something she had carried long enough to do it by habit.

Her partner was younger, mid-twenties. A mace hung in one hand while he kept shifting his grip with the jerky uncertainty of a man who'd practiced in a yard but not against a body that bled. A port-wine birthmark ran along the left side of his jaw from ear to chin, and the pack on his shoulders sat too stiff and too new, every buckle still bright.

"That's the mark," the woman said. Her voice carried easily. It was calm and professional, the tone of someone who had done a version of this before.

The young one squinted at Maren. "His profile's empty. There's nothing, I mean literally nothing. I've never seen that."

"I know."

*The younger one's threat level is moderate. The woman is high. She has a class-enhanced blade, System augmentation on the edge. Her stance indicates...*

Aldric's assessment dissolved into irrelevance because Maren's body was already moving.

The camp knife rode in Derry's pack on Maren's right shoulder, short-bladed and ash-handled and meant for rope and kindling. His fingers snapped around it as reflexively as a hand finding a railing when the stair gives out. The grab beat thought. His body knew where the weapon was before his mind finished registering the word weapon.

The blade came free. The pack shifted on his shoulder. Behind him, Derry made a sound, a small choked syllable that might have been "Maren" if it had gotten far enough to form.

The woman drew her sword and the hum thickened, packing itself into a drone close as insects at the inner ear. Something in the System's local field woke around the blade before Maren could place it. The air around the edge rippled with a faint shimmer, heatless and thin. Aldric could read it. Maren could only hear it.

She came forward at a walk. Each step covered exactly the distance she wanted. The blade sat in a guard position her System probably overlaid with optimal angles and threat trajectories and all the other combat mathematics Maren would never see. All he got was the woman herself: weight on her toes, shoulders set, eyes fixed on his center mass.

His legs ran toward her before any part of him approved it.

That choice came from Aldric. The paladin's doctrine was to meet an engagement rather than absorb one. Maren's body was six inches shorter and sixty pounds lighter than the body the training had been built for, so the stride came out wrong. His feet hit the packed dirt at intervals his muscles did not want, and the mismatch made him stagger on the second step.

*Lower,* Vess said. *You're too tall for this stance.*

The instruction made no sense. He was short. He had always been short. Even so, his knees bent and his center of gravity dropped, and suddenly the running worked. He went lower, faster, ugly and wrong-looking. Aldric's long stride fought Vess's crouch in his legs, and the result was a lurching sprint that ate the distance because neither set of instincts would give.

The woman swung.

She was good. The blade came in flat, a lateral cut at his ribs, and the System enhancement on the edge made the air whine like a finger drawn across wet glass. Up came Maren's left arm, forearm turned out, palm flat, the guard position for a shield he didn't have and had never held. Aldric's body memory put it there. Fourteen years of training with a steel disc landed on bare flesh and thin cotton.

Steel bit him below the elbow. The cut ran long and shallow; the enhancement wanted a thrust, and the flat angle turned it into a scraping drag across the outer forearm. Pain went up his arm in one wave, through shoulder and jaw. Everything below the cut turned cold. Blood ran into his palm and between his fingers, hot against skin gone numb, and the warmth pooling in the creases of his hand was the most physically real thing he had felt in six days.

The knife hand came up.

Vess supplied the intent. His hand found the gap between the woman's sword arm and her neck, the geometry of a body opening in the follow-through of a swing. She was committed to the arc. Her sword had gone wide and pulled her shoulder with it. The space between jawline and jerkin collar stood open. The camp knife was in his fist. The fist was already moving. He was inside her guard, closer than a sword could reach.

The blade went up into the soft triangle below her jaw.

The whole thing traveled through the handle: skin catching, then soft give, then cartilage yielding with a small wet pop. The handle twisted in his grip as the knife seated itself. Vess put the quarter-turn into it to widen the channel, a blank professional motion learned somewhere blood had to stay quiet.

The woman grabbed his right wrist.

Each ridge of her grip stood out against his forearm. Each finger was a separate pressure point digging into the tendons above his hand. She was strong. She was dying and stronger than he was, and her grip had nothing to do with combat now. It was only a body's refusal to accept what was happening to it.

*Pull it. Side-draw. Don't lift.*

The knife jerked sideways.

Dark blood ran down her collar and into the neck of her jerkin, dripping from the leather to the road. In the dust beside her lay the blue cloth from the sword pommel. She had dropped the weapon when her hand went to his wrist.

Then her grip went slack all at once, like a rope cut through under tension. She sat down in the road instead of falling, legs folding under her with a strange neatness. Her mouth moved. No sound came out. Her eyes stayed on his face, fully present and still processing, and then they were looking at nothing. The change was so thin he could not have pointed to it if his life had depended on naming the instant.

She died sitting up in the dust with one hand still raised toward the wrist of the man who'd killed her.

The younger hunter was already running. His mace clanged somewhere behind him on the packed dirt. The too-new pack bounced against his spine, bright buckles flashing in the sun. He ran like a man whose practice had all happened in yards and drills, all elbows and knees, no economy, breath ragged by twenty yards.

Maren's legs tensed with Vess's reflex, the professional urge not to leave a witness.

*No.* Aldric gave the word with more force than he'd used since the fragment collision.

That was enough. The pursuit stopped before it started.

Dust and copper sat on his tongue. The cut arm hung at his side. Blood ran from fingertips in slow drops that hit the road with small sounds he could not stop hearing or counting. The camp knife remained locked in his right fist, the ash handle glued to his palm by a cramp his conscious mind could not reach. He tried to open his hand. Nothing moved. A second try only tightened the forearm muscles until they stood out against the skin.

Behind the sheep pen wall, Derry breathed fast and shallow, pressed so flat against the stone he might have believed less space meant less danger.

In front of him, the woman sat with dust hazing around her in the low sun. A fly had already found the blood on her collar. Beside the dropped sword, the blue cloth lay in the dirt with the knot still tight, simple and habitual, the knot of something retied every morning because it mattered.

Then he looked at his own right hand, at the knife he still could not drop, at the dark slick on the blade and between his fingers and in the lines of his palm. The thought came with the cold clarity of something borrowed from a more competent mind: Those are not my hands.

---

Behind the sheep pen wall, twenty feet from the body, he threw up.

The vomiting was mechanical, his stomach clenching around nothing, thin bile rising in two hitches from a body that had not eaten enough to give up much else. After that came only the clench and dry heaves, the diaphragm spasming against emptiness. By then the knife had dropped. He braced a palm against the stone and fixed on its warmth, the grit, the grain biting into his fingertips.

Derry sat with his back against the far end of the wall, hands shoved between his knees. Through the whole fight he'd been there behind the stones. He had not run and he had not helped, and neither of those facts looked likely to come up between them.

"We should move, I think. The other one ran, right? He'll go back wherever they came from, and there'll be more, won't there? So we should probably, I mean not right now. You need a minute. But soon. We should move soon. I've got the cloth strips. Lenna cut them from that old shirt of mine, the one with the..."

The sentence dried up when it reached the domestic detail. That shirt belonged to a life that was far away now, and both of them knew it.

Maren straightened and spat. Blood had started seeping through the left sleeve again, a slow dark spread that looked worse than it was because the edges were already drying. The cut ran about six inches along the outer forearm, shallow enough that the skin was trying to close. Cleaning it properly meant cutting away the sleeve and picking up the knife.

But the knife stayed where it was.

"Let me see it," Derry said, already on his feet and already digging into the pack for cloth strips. His hands shook, not as badly as Maren's but enough to show. He talked while he rummaged, about a creek that might be nearby, about whether this was the right roll of cloth, about how Lenna always said water should be boiled before it touched a wound and maybe they would just have to ignore that part.

Work steadied him. He wrapped the cloth around Maren's forearm with the same care he used on fence posts, tight and even and methodical. Once the knot was tied, he patted it lightly.

"There. It's not deep. I mean, the arm isn't. Are you okay there? That's a stupid question."

*A clean kill,* Aldric said.

The voice was quiet, almost gentle. Maren knew the register from the memories he carried: the tone a paladin used on young soldiers after first blood, calm and affirming, a trained kindness built over years of watching people die and teaching survivors how to name it.

*She felt no extended pain. The strike was precise and the angle correct. Your body performed with discipline under pressure.*

"Shut up."

Derry's hands stopped on the bandage.

"Not you," Maren said. His voice came out rough and scraped raw. "Him."

Derry nodded and went back to smoothing the edges of the cloth with careful fingers.

*I understand that the shock of it can...*

"You don't understand shit." The words came out harder than he intended, stripped of the charm and deflection he usually wrapped around anything that mattered. What was left was anger and fear and the raw misery of a man whose body had done something unspeakable while he watched from inside it. "Don't put your words on it. Don't call it a clean kill. That's the framing you're going with? A clean kill?"

Aldric went silent.

"Because it wasn't clean. Nothing about it was. I had a camp knife, Aldric. The thing Derry cuts rope with. I stuck it in a woman's throat and she held onto my arm while she died. She grabbed me. She held on. And you want to tell me that was clean?"

*I was attempting to provide context.*

"I know what you were attempting." Warm stone pressed against his back while he half sat against the wall with his left arm cradled to his chest. Blood was already seeping through the bandage. "You were attempting to make it okay. To put it in whatever box you've got. 'Clean kill, no suffering, proper engagement.' You've done this before. You've got a whole system for it, a whole way of talking about killing people that makes it fit inside your doctrine, or your framework, or whatever word lets you sleep after you've..."

The next word never came. His throat had closed around it and left it trapped somewhere behind the sternum.

From here he could still see the blue cloth lying in the dust beside the dropped sword, the knot tight and habitual. It was someone's color, something she had carried into a fight on a road she'd probably walked a dozen times before.

*I have no framework for this.* Aldric's voice had changed. The trained gentleness was gone. What remained was rawer, stripped of protocol. *I had a framework. I am attempting to use it because it is the only thing I have. The alternative is to describe what it felt like to watch your body kill that woman with a precision that was partly mine and partly Vess's, and I do not have...*

The sentence stopped there. For Aldric, whose sentences usually closed like doors, that break carried its own alarm.

Vess spoke into the silence he left.

*They were a scouting pair. Standard bounty protocol for unknown targets. Send two to confirm, then dispatch a full party. Their approach puts the staging town less than a day east. When the younger one reports, someone will come looking.*

She could have been reading tomorrow's weather.

"How long?" Maren asked.

*Tomorrow. Maybe tonight, if he ran the whole way.*

Derry stood with the remaining cloth strips in his hands and watched Maren's face. Eleven years of reading faces let Maren read this one fast enough. Derry looked like a man who had just learned that someone he had known for years could do things he could not, and that ordinary conversation would never close the distance between who he thought his friend was and what his friend had turned out to be capable of.

"We should move," Derry said. "Before... yeah. We should move."

So they moved.

The knife stayed on the ground by the sheep pen wall. Maren never looked back at it. Derry bent, picked it up, wiped it on something, and slid it back into the pack. Neither of them said that Derry had done it. Neither of them said what had needed wiping off the blade.

Road and sun and dust stretched on. Shadows lengthened east. Dry weeds rattled in the ditch. Once, blood seeped through the bandage again, and Derry retied it without speaking. The hum from the nearest dungeon mouth came and went with the terrain. Quest-light pooled to the right of the road, faint in late sun, just heat-haze shimmer to Maren and data to everyone else alive.

His hands shook against his thighs. Pressing his palms flat into the fabric helped for a few seconds, and then it stopped helping. Fear had burned off somewhere in the first mile. What remained was the body's record of what had happened, written into the small muscles of his hands and the tendons in his forearms, testimony his mind could outrun for a moment but not erase.

Aldric said nothing. The silence had changed shape. Last night's had held its breath over something he could not describe. This one belonged to someone who had tried to help and been told, accurately, that his help was the wrong shape for the wound.

Vess said nothing because she had already said everything she considered relevant.

Derry talked instead. He talked about the road, about the deeper ruts and what they meant for carts, about the chance of a market town ahead and the fact that his waterskin was nearly empty. Then he found the well in Fenhold, the one by the south wall with the rope that always jammed. Pell used to hang on the crank handle and try to pull it up herself. She was too small to move the rope, but she'd hang there with both hands and her feet off the ground and laugh.

He stopped when he reached his daughter. The talking had carried him straight to the place it was supposed to carry him away from.

"Sorry," Derry said.

"Don't be sorry."

"I just, she keeps, I keep thinking about her. Is that stupid? We're out here and you just... and I'm thinking about whether she can count to twenty yet. She was stuck on fourteen. She'd go thirteen, fourteen, fourteen, fourteen."

"Not stupid," Maren said.

Dark took its time. Off the road, Derry found a fallen oak that made a windbreak, its trunk mossy and soft enough on top that leaning against it did not hurt. They sat with Derry across from him and the pack between his knees. No one built a fire. The evening stayed warm, late-summer warmth that would not break until deep night, and the sky drained itself by stages until the quest-light in the ditches shone brighter than the last blue overhead. It looked pale and cold against the dark, System residue pooled in every low place as patient as groundwater.

Back against the trunk, hands flat on his thighs, Maren listened to the hum. It was louder here than on the road. Maybe they were closer to a dungeon mouth. Maybe the residue in the ditches ran denser than usual. The sound sat below ordinary hearing, bone-deep and steady. Aldric and Vess and everyone else System-sighted in the world filtered it out because the interface told them what it meant, and meaning was enough to make it disappear. Maren had heard it for twenty-eight years without knowing what it was. Aldric had arrived and told him the hum was the world running.

A woman was dead by his hand, and he did not know her name. She had blue cloth on her sword, someone's color, something she had carried into the field the way a soldier carried a letter. Her bracers were patched with mismatched leather. She had been around forty. She had been good at what she did. She had died sitting in the road with one hand reaching for the wrist of the man who killed her.

The camp knife had gone into her throat, and the resistance of the blade still lived in his palm: skin, give, the quarter-turn Vess's muscle memory had worked through his tendons. The feeling stayed with him like the ache after a splinter was pulled, the body's memory of what had been there.

For eleven years he'd built a life out of fake rituals, practiced gestures, borrowed grief, and the careful architecture of a lie kept standing by charm and observation and Derry's whispered System readings from the back of a crowd. For eleven years nothing had been real. Then something answered. Then the System noticed. Then his body, operated by dead people whose skills lived in his muscles like tenants he had never invited, put a blade into a woman's throat on a road outside a sheep pen while he watched from inside and could do nothing to stop it.

It was the first real thing he had ever done.

He pressed his shaking hands flat against his legs and held them there, watching the quest-light brighten in the ditches, pale and cold and permanent, and did not sleep.
