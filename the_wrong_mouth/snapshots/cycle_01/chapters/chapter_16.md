# Chapter 16

They found a sheep pen half a mile from where the valley started to taste wrong.

Not a farmstead — just a low stone wall in a rough circle, open to the sky, with a gate that had rotted off its hinges years ago. The kind of place shepherds used for overnight holding during drives, back when anyone still drove sheep through dungeon-adjacent territory. Nobody had used it in a long time. Grass had come up through the gaps between stones and died there, thin brown stalks that crumbled when Maren brushed against them.

He sat with his back against the wall and tried to catalogue what still worked. His hands were shaking, but that was ordinary — they'd been shaking since the consumption at the Crook, since the quest had been too large and the unclassified entity had surged and the System had turned the valley into a threat zone. Two days of shaking wasn't ideal, but it wasn't new. The headache behind his left eye was new. The way his stomach kept clenching around nothing, as if his body were still trying to reject something that had already been absorbed — that was new too.

The air out here smelled like autumn coming in under contamination: decaying leaves and the acrid sweetness of old quest residue, layered over each other like stains on a floor nobody mopped. The hum was louder than it should have been this far from the dungeon mouths, as if the System's reclassification had pushed the noise boundary outward. Everything within a day's walk of the Crook now sounded like standing next to an active quest.

*The instability is increasing.*

Vess. She hadn't spoken in hours — not since they'd cleared the valley and the running had stopped and there was nothing left to be tactical about. Her voice was the same as it always was: flat, economical, stating things.

Maren didn't answer. He was concentrating on not throwing up again, which required most of his attention.

*Maren. The instability is increasing and it's not going to stop.*

"I know." He said it out loud because talking to the voices inside his skull silently still felt like a thing insane people did, which was funny, because talking to them out loud in a sheep pen probably wasn't the picture of mental health either. "I can feel it."

He could. The unclassified entity — the thing born from the collision of Aldric's paladin classification and Vess's shadow-guild classification, the thing the System couldn't name and therefore wanted to destroy — was doing something in his chest. Not pain, exactly. More like a vibration at a frequency his body couldn't quite resolve, an internal hum that didn't match the external one. It had been getting worse since the overloaded consumption. Before the Crook, it had been background noise. Now it was the loudest thing in any room he walked into, including rooms without walls.

*It's me.*

"What?"

*I'm the variable. Two opposing classifications in a System-blind host produce an entity the System can't categorize. Remove one classification and the entity simplifies. Stops surging. The System can still track it, but it doesn't—* A brief pause, recalibrating. *The interaction between us is what creates the instability. Between what he was and what I was. Take me out and the math changes.*

"The math," Maren said.

*The math.*

He pressed the heel of his hand against his left eye where the headache lived. The stone wall was cold through his shirt. Early autumn had crept in while they'd been running — the nights were longer now and the warmth went out of things faster. He missed the late-summer heat of Fenhold, which was an absurd thing to miss when he could never go back there.

"Aldric," he said.

Nothing.

"Aldric, she's—"

*I heard her.* The paladin's voice was clipped. Formal. The register he retreated to when the framework was failing. *I am considering her assessment.*

*Don't consider it. It's accurate.* Vess didn't wait for silence the way she usually did. She spoke into Aldric's pause as if she'd been listening for exactly that gap. *The entity surged because I'm here. It'll surge again. Worse each time. The next time the System responds, it won't reclassify the area. It'll reclassify him.*

Maren's stomach clenched again. He swallowed and tasted contamination — that ozone-sweet undertone that clung to the back of his throat after consumption, fainter than it had been yesterday but still present, like something spoiled he couldn't spit out.

"So what are you saying?" He knew what she was saying. He wanted her to say it anyway, because some part of him was still the con man who needed to hear the version of events someone else committed to before he committed to his own.

*I need to leave.*

The wind came through the gap where the gate had been. It brought the contamination smell with it — stronger from the east, where the valley lay reclassified and the quest-light had flooded in so thick that even Maren could see the glow from the ridge. The dead grass crumbled against the stones.

"Leave," he said. "As in—"

*Separation. You've learned enough from the old woman to perform a consumption. The reverse should be possible. Expulsion instead of absorption. Push me out instead of pulling me in.*

*That is not—* Aldric stopped. Started again. *The ritual framework Thessa taught him was not designed for expulsion. There is no precedent for—*

*He ate a quest mid-combat two days ago. We're past the precedents.*

*You would become an unhosted fragment.* Aldric's voice had gone quieter. The formal register was holding but something underneath it wasn't. *In the open. Without a consciousness to anchor to. The dissipation rate for an unhosted fragment in contaminated territory is—*

*I know the rate.*

Aldric went silent.

Maren sat with his back against the stone wall in a sheep pen that smelled like autumn and old quest residue and listened to the wind. The hum was steady from the east. His hands shook. The wrong-frequency vibration in his chest ground against his ribs, and he thought about Vess's voice the first time he'd heard it — furious, professional, livid about the paladin and the incompetent host and the fact of being dead — and he thought about the way she'd talked him through the physical recovery after his first real consumption at the creek bed, the unexpected steadiness of it, how she'd narrated his breathing the way she must have narrated approach routes in the life she'd had before. In, hold, out. Professional. Something close to kind. Alive, even dead.

"How long?" he asked. "After. How long before you—"

*I don't know.* The first time she'd admitted not knowing something since he'd carried her, and it wasn't really a correction — it was a gap in her information, a hole where the answer should have been, and the specific distress of it was quiet and precise. *Theory says hours. Maybe longer in contaminated air — fragments persist where the residue is dense. But the theory also says a System-blind host can't carry fragments at all, so.*

So the theory was shit. Maren almost laughed but the headache pressed down harder and the laugh died somewhere in his sinuses.

"There's got to be—"

*There isn't.* She said it once. No repetition. No emphasis. The words occupied their space and that was all. *Two fragments from opposed classifications are destroying your body. One fragment from a single classification is stable. The choice is straightforward.*

"For you, maybe."

*For everyone. That's the point.*

Maren closed his eyes. The darkness behind his eyelids was the same darkness he'd seen his whole life when the System's interface should have been there — a comfortable nothing, warm and unwritten. Twenty-eight years of that nothing. Twenty-three days of it being crowded with voices, and the thought of one of those voices going quiet made the nothing feel different. Larger. Colder.

"What about Blackhall?" he said.

The silence that followed was different from Aldric's silences. Aldric's silences were retreats — he went somewhere inside himself and closed the door. Vess's silence was presence without speech. She was there, fully, and the words had stopped.

*What about him.*

"Your contract. The massacre. The thing you were — the reason you were in that quest in the first place. You were hired to—"

*I know what I was hired to do.*

"And you never finished it. Torren Blackhall is still alive. Still—"

*I know.*

Maren opened his eyes. The sheep pen. The dead grass. The glow from the east where the reclassified valley bled quest-light into the sky. His hands, shaking.

"You've been carrying that since before you died. You told me his schedule. Tuesdays the lower market. Thursdays the holding pens. Fridays he meets the reeve and drinks too much and walks home through the garden alone, and you know the garden, and you know where the hedgerow blocks the sight lines, and now you're just going to—"

*Yes.*

The word sat in the air between them. Inside him, technically — all of this was inside him, the voices and the argument and the silence — but it occupied space the way a physical thing did.

"Vess."

*I thought it would matter more.* Her voice was the same — flat, professional. But the sentence was longer than anything she'd said about herself before, and that alone was a fracture in the surface. *The contract. Blackhall. Justice, if that's what it was. I thought when the time came it would be the thing I couldn't let go of. The hook that kept me here.*

*And it isn't?*

Aldric. Quiet. Unadorned. None of the formality, none of the classification language. Just a question from someone who needed the answer.

*No.* Vess. Simply. *It matters. He killed those people and used the System's own infrastructure to hide it and he's still alive and it matters. But it doesn't matter more than this.*

She didn't specify what "this" was. She didn't need to. Maren could feel the unclassified entity shuddering against his ribs, the wrong-frequency hum that was getting worse with every hour, and he understood that "this" was him staying alive long enough to do whatever came next. "This" was the work. "This" was what remained when you let the last piece of your old self go.

"When?" he asked.

*Now. Before I find a reason to stay.*

---

Maren took the bowl out of his pack.

It was ceramic, glazed once and badly — the color somewhere between mud and dried blood, an indeterminate brown that had never been attractive. One side was chipped from eleven years of careless packing and road travel; the chip exposed the pale clay underneath, a half-moon of lighter color near the rim. He'd bought it from a woman at the Fenhold market when he was seventeen and needed a prop for his first fake ritual. Three coppers. The woman had tried to sell him a nicer one for five, and he'd said no, because he was seventeen and two coppers was dinner and the dead didn't care about pottery.

He turned it in his hands. The glaze was worn smooth in the places where his fingers always sat — two spots on either side where he'd held it during every performance for eleven years. He could feel the depressions his thumbs had made, so shallow they were invisible, worn there by nothing more than repetition and time and the hands of a man who had been lying about everything else but who had always held the bowl the same way.

It had never done anything. Not once. Every ritual where he'd placed it before a body and murmured words he'd invented and watched the family's faces for cues about when to nod, when to be solemn, when to close his eyes as if something were happening — the bowl had sat there being ceramic. Three coppers' worth of clay doing the work of nothing.

"I don't know if this'll work," he said.

*It'll work.* Vess.

*You cannot know that.* Aldric.

*He consumed a quest the size of a village two days ago with nothing but bad training and stubbornness. He can manage a single fragment.*

*That is not the same as certainty.*

*Nothing is. Start.*

Maren set the bowl on the ground between his knees. The grass was cold and damp through his trousers. The sheep pen's stone wall circled them — waist-high, roofless, open to the sky where the early autumn stars were coming out in patches between thin clouds. The hum from the east was steady. Quest-light reflected off the cloud cover in a pale wash that Maren could see only as ambient brightening, a sky that refused to go fully dark.

He put his hands on either side of the bowl, thumbs settling into the depressions, and tried to remember what Thessa had taught him.

The proper consumption sequence was: preparation, opening, acceptance, dissolution. He'd managed it twice now — badly at the creek bed, barely at the Crook. Both times the process had felt like pulling his own skin back, an exposure of something underneath himself that had no business being exposed. Like finding a door in a wall you'd thought was solid and being told to stand in the doorway while something came through.

For this he needed the opposite. Not a door opening inward. A door opening out.

He closed his eyes. The bowl was cold under his hands. The grass was cold under his knees. The contamination smell was sharp in the air — that ozone-sweet decay, stronger toward the valley. He could hear his own breathing, and underneath it, the voices: Aldric's formal silence, Vess's professional waiting.

"Vess."

*I'm here.*

"I know." His thumbs pressed deeper into the worn depressions on the bowl, and for a moment the clay felt thin enough to crack. "I just — yeah. I know."

He began.

The opening was the same whether you were consuming or expelling — Thessa had been clear about that much. The sin-eater's work started with making yourself available. Permeable. Maren had spent twenty-eight years being impermeable to the System and eleven years performing a pantomime of permeability, and the real thing was still like learning a language his body didn't want to speak. He breathed and tried to mean it. Not perform it — mean it. The difference between those two things had been the entire distance of his life, and it was narrowing, and that should have been a comfort but mostly it just hurt.

Something shifted. The same something that had shifted at the creek bed, at the Crook. A loosening in his chest, as if a fist he'd been making since birth had opened one finger at a time. The unclassified entity responded — surging toward the opening, confused and eager, the way it always did when he made himself available to the work.

*Careful.* Aldric. Not a command. Something closer to worry, though he'd never use that word.

Maren pushed.

The consumption was about pulling — drawing the contamination in, dissolving it, accepting the weight. The expulsion was about the opposite, and the opposite turned out to be harder, because you had to push something out of yourself that had made itself part of you. Vess's presence in his consciousness had been specific and constant for weeks — her tactical awareness at the edges of his perception, the way she noticed exits and approach angles and the exact distance between himself and any potential threat. She'd been the cold voice that assessed while Aldric moralized, the one who counted moves instead of sins. She was in his reflexes now, in the way he'd started checking sight lines when he entered a room, in the automatic inventory of objects within reach that could be weapons.

He pushed, and she began to move, and it hurt.

Not the dramatic pain of the consumption — no convulsions, no retching. A subtler thing. Like removing a splinter that had been in his hand so long the skin had grown around it. His body had accommodated her the way a tree accommodates a fence post, growing against and around until the foreign object was structural, and now he was pulling it out and the gaps it left were shaped exactly like what was missing.

The bowl warmed under his hands.

That was new. In eleven years of fake rituals, the bowl had never been anything but room temperature or cold — whatever the air was, the bowl was. Now it heated from the inside, not hot, not painful, but warm the way a thing being used was warm. The glaze under his thumbs changed from dead ceramic to something that conducted, that accepted, that did what it was made to do even though it had never been made for anything. Three coppers. A potter who couldn't center a handle. And now, for the first time in its dull, chipped, unremarkable existence, the bowl was working.

*There's—* Vess started, and for the first time since Maren had known her, she didn't finish. A gap. A fracture in the controlled voice, as if she'd hit one of her own memory gaps mid-sentence and the sentence had fallen into it.

*I can feel it,* she said, and this sentence was complete but it cost her something. *The bowl. It's doing the real thing.*

The fake tool. The market bowl. The prop. Something was passing through it — through Maren, out of him, into the ceramic — and the bowl was receiving it with the patience of an object that had waited eleven years to be useful, though Maren knew that was his own sentimentality talking and the bowl was just clay and heat and function and didn't care.

He kept his hands on it and pushed, and Vess moved, and the gaps she left filled with quiet.

*Maren.*

"Yeah."

*The contract is released. If you ever get close enough—* She stopped. *No. It's released. That's all.*

He held the bowl and said nothing, because there was nothing to say to a woman letting go of the last thing that had kept her shape, and anything he tried would be wrong.

*The old woman's teaching will hold. Aldric's theory is sound, even when it's pompous.* Quieter now. Not softer — Vess didn't do soft. But the volume had dropped, as if the space between them was widening even before the separation was complete. *You're bad at this. But you're real at it. That's the part that counts.*

He pushed. The bowl heated. The thing in his chest — the unclassified entity, the collision, the impossible classification — shuddered and simplified. He could feel it happening: not a loss of power but a loss of complexity. Like a chord resolving to a single note. Still a sound. Just less.

*My sister's name,* Vess said. *There should be a name here. There always should have been a name here.*

And then she left.

It was exactly like a sound leaving a room. Not a door closing, not a dramatic exit — just the moment when you realized the noise that had been there a second ago wasn't there anymore. Had it stopped just now? Or a moment ago, and you were only noticing? The boundary between presence and absence was blurred, and Maren sat with his hands on the warm bowl and his knees in the cold grass and waited for the difference to become legible.

The difference was enormous.

Her tactical awareness went first — the constant peripheral assessment of angles and distances and threats that had become so automatic he'd stopped noticing it was hers. Then the coldness, the professional temperature of competence that she'd maintained even inside someone else's skull. Then the small things: the way she'd noticed what people ate, how rooms smelled, the domestic inventory she kept alongside the lethal one. Gone. All of it. Gone the way a sound left a room, which was to say: completely, suddenly, and with nothing remaining except the quality of the silence it left behind.

The bowl cooled under his hands. Whatever had passed through it — whatever Vess had been, whatever the ritual had carried out of him and into the ceramic and then into the contaminated air — was already dissipating. The bowl was ceramic again. Three coppers' worth. But the wear on the glaze looked different to his fingers, or maybe it didn't — maybe it was the same bowl it had always been, and the difference was in the hands that held it.

Maren sat in the sheep pen with the empty bowl between his knees and listened to the quiet inside his own skull.

It was so quiet.

For weeks his head had been a room with three people in it — himself, the paladin, and the assassin. There had always been someone talking, someone objecting, someone assessing a threat or cataloguing a moral failure or describing the System interface in tones of sarcasm or professionalism or barely contained fear. The noise had been maddening and constant and alive, and now a third of it was gone, and the space it left was the emptiest thing he'd ever experienced — emptier than his twenty-eight years of System-blindness, because at least the blindness had been a familiar emptiness. This one had a shape. The shape was hers.

"Aldric."

Nothing.

"Aldric."

Nothing. Not the hostile nothing of his early silences, when he'd withheld speech as punishment for Maren's moral failures. Not the frightened nothing of his System-observations, when he'd seen something in the architecture he couldn't describe. A different nothing. A nothing that sat where an argument should have been.

Maren picked up the bowl. The chip near the rim. The indeterminate brown. The thumb-depressions worn smooth by eleven years of lies and three weeks of something else. He put it back in his pack and the weight of it against his spine through the canvas was so familiar it hurt, and he pulled his knees up to his chest and wrapped his arms around them.

The stars came out over the contaminated valley. The quest-light painted the clouds from below in the pale wash that was the closest thing Maren ever saw to what everyone else navigated by. The hum from the east was steady. The grass rustled against the stones. Somewhere — close, closer than it should have been — a night bird called once and didn't call again.

Inside his skull, the paladin who had argued with the assassin every day for three weeks, who had talked over her without apology, who had corrected her moral positioning and questioned her tactical assessments and interrupted her field reports and been interrupted in turn with a patience more unsettling than any interruption — that paladin sat in the silence where her voice had been.

The silence lasted a long time.

When Aldric finally spoke, his voice was different. Not warmer. Not kinder. Different the way a room was different after you removed a piece of furniture that had always annoyed you — the same walls, the same dimensions, but the space was rearranged and you kept looking at the place where the thing used to be and discovering that annoyance was a kind of company.

*The entity has stabilized.* Formal. Clipped. A report delivered to an order that no longer existed. *The opposition is resolved. Single classification within a System-blind host. Stable, as far as I can determine.*

"Good," Maren said. His voice sounded wrong in the quiet. Too loud. Too present.

*Consumption capability should remain largely intact. Thessa's training addressed the foundational techniques. What you have lost is...* He stopped. *Tactical assessment. Threat analysis. Her specific observations about—*

He stopped again. Maren waited. The night bird didn't call.

*She was wrong frequently.* Aldric said it with the cadence of a man reaching for a familiar position and finding that it no longer fit his hand. *About matters of moral consequence. Her classification training prioritized expedience over—*

He stopped a third time.

*She was not wrong about this.*

Maren put his chin on his knees. The stone wall pressed cold against his back. His hands had stopped shaking at some point during the ritual — the first time in two days — and the stillness of his own fingers against his shins was strange, as if the tremor had been keeping time and now the music had stopped. The headache behind his left eye was gone too. The wrong-frequency vibration in his chest had resolved into something simpler, steadier — still the unclassified entity, still the thing the System couldn't name, but quieter now. One note instead of a chord.

He missed the chord.

"She didn't say goodbye," he said. "Not exactly. She said the contract was released and she said something about her sister and then she was just — not here."

*She said things once.* Aldric. Quiet. *You knew this about her.*

"Yeah."

*Then you know that was the goodbye.*

Maren sat in the sheep pen. The bowl in his pack pressed against his spine through the canvas. The air smelled like autumn and contamination and the fading trace of something that might have been a person, or might have been nothing more than quest residue thinning in the night wind. He couldn't tell the difference. That was the worst part, or maybe the most fitting part — that Vess, who had always been precise about what she could and couldn't perceive, would leave behind an absence that smelled like everything else that was lost and dissipating in this world, and that he was too blind to sort her from the rest of it.

The skull was quieter.

Maren stayed in the sheep pen until the stars moved. Aldric didn't speak again. The hum from the east went on, the System doing its work, the wall maintained, the containment holding against whatever lived behind it. Somewhere in the dark, in the contaminated air between the dungeon mouths, what was left of Vess was thinning into nothing — becoming part of the residue, the background, the particular smell of a world that consumed its own.

In the morning he would get up and keep walking toward the Woundmouth. He would carry the bowl and the single voice and the simplified thing in his chest, and he would be less than he had been, and the world would be exactly as indifferent to that as it was to everything else.

But that was the morning. For now there was the night, and the quiet, and the place where a sound had been.
