# Chapter 18

The hum was quieter on this side of the valley.

Not gone — the hum was never gone, not since Fenhold, not since the first morning he could remember hearing it — but stretched thin, like water across too much stone. Three days past the Woundmouth and Maren's body still hadn't forgiven him. His knees ground when he bent them. The muscles along his ribs ached in a deep, wrung-out way, as if someone had reached inside his trunk and twisted everything a quarter turn to the left. But his hands were steady. That was new.

The road — what passed for a road this deep, where dungeon territory gave way to farmland and the path widened from packed dirt to actual ruts — had scorch marks in the earth. Old ones, layered dark over darker, from heroes who'd passed through seasons ago. Nobody cleaned them. Nobody ever cleaned them. Early autumn had crept in while he wasn't paying attention, and the light hit those burn marks differently now, cooler, less shimmer and more of a pale, flat quality that made the road look tired.

*The contamination index has dropped significantly since your... intervention,* Aldric said. He paused on "intervention" the way he used to pause on "incompetent" — with a precision that suggested he'd considered and rejected several worse options. *The residual levels in this stretch are within normal seasonal parameters.*

"So it worked."

*I believe I just said that.*

"You said 'contamination index' and 'seasonal parameters.' I said 'it worked.'"

A pause. Then, quieter: *Yes. It worked.*

That was the new thing. The pauses. Aldric had always been careful with language — fourteen years of paladin training had given him the syntax of reports and prayers, every sentence a finished architecture — but the pauses were different now. Before the Woundmouth, his silences had been dramatic. The silence of a man who had seen something terrible and was deciding whether to describe it. Now they were just pauses. The kind where someone was sorting through what they actually wanted to say, rather than what they should say.

Maren missed the dramatic silences, honestly. They'd been easier to ignore.

The early-autumn air sat in the back of his throat with a crispness that wasn't unpleasant — just present, like a taste he couldn't quite place. The fields on either side of the road had been harvested recently. Cut stubble scratched at his ankles where the road narrowed, and the smell was turned earth and drying grain and, underneath it, the faint sweet-ozone note of contamination residue that had been sitting too long in the soil.

*There's quest-light pooling in the drainage channel to your right,* Aldric said. *Several weeks of accumulation. No active quest attached — just residue that hasn't been cleared.*

Maren hadn't asked. A month ago, Aldric wouldn't have offered. The interface readings had been transactional — Maren requesting, Aldric delivering with whatever degree of moral disdain the question warranted. Now the descriptions came unprompted. Not constantly. Not like the frantic narration of those first days in Fenhold, when everything had been new and the paladin had been processing his own horror by cataloguing the world around them. This was different. Someone pointing out a bird because they'd both noticed the tree.

It was harder to be annoyed by it when it wasn't transactional. Maren was working on that.

The road crested a low hill and the valley opened up — farmland, a scatter of buildings in the middle distance, the dark mouth of a dungeon visible on the far ridgeline like a bruise against the tree line. Quest-light pooled around it, brighter than the residual traces in the ditches, and even from this distance the hum thickened. Someone would need to clear it eventually.

Maybe him.

The thought still had a quality of absurdity to it. Like wearing shoes that fit but belonged to someone else.

*Your stride has improved,* Aldric noted. *Less compensatory tilting. The muscular damage from the consumption appears to be resolving.*

"You could just say I'm walking better."

*I could. I won't, but I could.*

That was a joke. A gentle one. Before the Woundmouth, Aldric's humor had been a blade — precise cuts aimed at Maren's competence, his hygiene, his moral architecture. The sarcasm of a man who'd been stripped of everything except his ability to judge. Now the edge had been filed down to something that didn't draw blood every time.

Maren sat on a flat stone at the roadside to eat. The bread was stale — two days old, bought from a farming woman who'd sold it to him without looking at his face, which was either generosity or indifference, and Maren had been grateful for either. His jaw worked at the crust. Crumbs fell on his shirt and he brushed them into the dirt.

Derry would have had better bread. Derry always had better bread — baked by Lenna, or bought fresh because Derry couldn't walk past a baker's stall without stopping, the same way he couldn't pass a cow without commenting on its quality. A small, specific failure of impulse control that Maren had found endlessly funny for eleven years.

The thought arrived and sat there, like the ache in his ribs. Not sharp anymore. Not even unpleasant, exactly. Just present.

He'd worked out the shape of it during the three days since the Woundmouth, the way you work out a bruise — pressing on it, testing the edges, seeing how much weight it could take before the pain changed quality. Derry had told Caul their direction. Not their location, not their camp, not anything that would have gotten Maren killed immediately — just the direction. Toward the Crook. Enough for Caul to narrow the search. Enough for people to die.

But also: Derry had a daughter who was learning to count, and a wife who'd planted tomatoes for the first time, and a cow that kept pushing through the fence. He'd talked about all of it, across campfires and long roads, in the specific voice of a man trying to hold the world steady by describing its ordinary parts. And then he'd done the math that Maren couldn't argue with, the one where his daughter needed a father more than Maren needed a friend.

The math was right. Maren could see it clearly — he'd always been good at seeing what other people were feeling, it was the foundation skill of his entire fraudulent career — and the math was right.

He still couldn't forgive it. Maybe later. Maybe not.

He finished the bread, brushed his hands on his trousers, and stood up. His knees complained. The road went on.

* * *

Thessa was sitting on a low wall at the edge of a settlement Maren hadn't been able to see from the road. The village was small — a handful of stone buildings clustered around a well, a livestock pen, the low walls that marked property in this part of the country. She was eating an apple. Her hands, stained dark from thirty years of ritual contamination exposure, made the fruit look very white.

"You're alive," she said. Not a question. An assessment.

"Apparently."

She looked at him the way she always looked at him — with the specific evaluative attention of someone checking a structure for cracks. Her eyes tracked from his face to his hands to his stance.

"Your feet are wrong."

"My feet."

"You're favoring your left side. The Woundmouth consumption pulled everything right — the asymmetry's contamination-patterned. Correct it or you'll be limping inside a week."

"Thanks."

"It wasn't a compliment." She took another bite of her apple. The crunch was very loud in the morning quiet. "There's a farmstead twelve miles east. Settlement called Barren Rill. Contamination pool behind the main house — it's been building three seasons because their sin-eater died and nobody replaced her. They've got children."

She said it the way she said everything — like a report, like information with an obvious response, like the next item on a list that had always been too long for one person.

"Twelve miles," Maren said.

"Twelve miles." She finished the apple and tossed the core over the wall into someone's garden. "Contamination's mid-grade. Standard clearing. Within your capability, assuming you don't botch the approach sequence."

"I might botch the approach sequence."

"Then you'll do it again." She stood up, wiping her hands on her trousers. The apple juice left a streak on the dark fabric. "There are three other sites on the line between here and Silt. I'll take the two nearest. You take Barren Rill and the one past it." She paused, calculating. "Meet me in Silt in ten days. If you're not dead."

*She's not the most encouraging teacher I've encountered,* Aldric said. *The Order of the Veil had a man who once told me my prayer posture made him question whether I had a spine. He was gentler.*

Maren didn't answer him. He was looking toward Thessa — or looking in her direction, which was the best he could manage, orienting by the sound of her breathing and the particular scrape of her worn leather bag against her hip.

"So that's it," he said.

"What were you expecting? A ceremony?" She slung the bag over her shoulder. "You're a sin-eater. There's contamination. Go eat it."

She left the way she did everything — without looking back, without checking whether the instruction had landed, with the absolute confidence of a woman who had been doing this work alone for longer than Maren had been alive and had stopped waiting for anyone to catch up.

*That was an acknowledgment,* Aldric said.

"That was a job assignment."

*From her, those are the same thing.*

He was right. Maren stood on the road with the low autumn sun hitting the side of his face and the hum threading through the air and his knees aching and stale bread sitting heavy in his gut, and he understood that Thessa had just treated him as a colleague. Not a student. Not a threat. Not a fraud who'd collected fees for eleven years while contamination built up in every village he'd visited. A colleague — someone she could split the work with, someone she trusted to take a site and clear it and show up in Silt in ten days.

It wasn't warm. It wasn't generous. It was better than both of those things. It was practical.

"Twelve miles east," he said to nobody in particular.

*Twelve miles east,* Aldric confirmed. *I'll have the route for you.*

He started walking.

* * *

Barren Rill was exactly what it sounded like — a thin settlement strung along a creek bed that had mostly dried to mud, fed by runoff from the hills where a dungeon mouth sat, old and leaking quest-light into the groundwater. The contamination pool behind the farmstead's main house was visible even to Maren: a faint shimmer in the packed earth, like heat haze that had gotten lost and settled down to stay.

The farmer's name was Reth. Stocky man, weather-beaten, with the cautious courtesy of someone who'd been told a sin-eater was coming and wasn't sure whether to be grateful or afraid. He had calloused hands and a habit of rubbing the back of his neck when he talked, as if the words had to be physically pushed out. Two children — a boy of maybe seven who stood behind his father's leg, and a girl younger than that, hiding behind her brother's shoulder and peering at Maren with the frank evaluation of someone who hadn't yet learned to pretend she wasn't staring.

"It's been three seasons," Reth said. He stood with his arms folded, the posture of a man protecting something without knowing how. "Our Dalla — she was our sin-eater, my wife's aunt — she passed in the spring and nobody came after. We sent word. Twice."

"I'm here now," Maren said.

*The contamination profile is mid-grade, consistent with approximately nine months of accumulation,* Aldric reported. *Concentrated behind the house, with secondary spread into the well-water table and the eastern field. No fragment signatures — the contamination is inert, not hosted. A standard clearing.*

"Standard clearing," Maren repeated aloud, for Reth's benefit.

"What's that mean?"

"Means it'll take a few hours. I'll need the area behind the house. Somewhere to sit."

Reth studied him. The same evaluative look people had given him for eleven years — is this person real? Can he do what he says he can? Is he worth the fee? Maren had always passed that test through performance. The confident posture, the right words, the practiced gravity of a man doing spiritual work. Now he didn't bother. He just stood there with his worn pack and his stained ceramic bowl visible through the open flap, his grinding knees and his unwashed hair, looking roughly in Reth's direction because that was the best his eyes could manage.

"You look young for this," Reth said.

"I am."

Something in that — the simplicity of it, maybe, the lack of the expected reassurance — made Reth uncross his arms. "Right. Well. There's a bench out back. I'll show you."

The bench was a plank across two stumps, set against the rear wall of the farmhouse where the stone held the day's warmth. The contamination pool started six feet in front of it and spread across the hardpack toward the creek bed. This close, it had its own smell — the sweet-ozone note of quest residue, older and more sour than fresh contamination, with a metallic undertone that sat on the tongue. Nine months of accumulation. Three seasons of slow poison leaching into the water, into the fields, into the soil where the children played.

Maren set the bowl on the bench beside him and sat down. The ceramic was cool against his thigh through the fabric. An ordinary bowl — bought in the Fenhold market when he was seventeen, chosen for its weight and how it sat in his hands, how the glaze caught light in a way that looked ceremonial to people who didn't know better. No System properties. No resonance. Just fired clay with a chip on the rim that he could feel with his thumb. He'd performed the fake ritual with this bowl hundreds of times. He'd performed the real one with it — the separation, with Vess. The supervised attempts at the Crook with Thessa barking corrections. The Woundmouth, which had been something else entirely, something that defied the word "ritual" the way a flood defied the word "rain."

This would be the first time it was just work.

*Shall I walk you through the contamination boundaries?* Aldric said.

"Go ahead."

*The primary pool extends approximately two meters from your position to seven meters at its widest, tapering east toward the creek. Moderate depth — the contamination has penetrated roughly half a meter into the soil. The composition is agricultural: grain residue, livestock-adjacent contamination byproducts.* A pause. *Nothing exotic.*

"Nothing exotic," Maren repeated. The comedy of a dead paladin delivering contamination flavor profiles as though reading a wine list was not lost on him. He picked up the bowl.

*Maren.*

"What?"

A pause. One of the new pauses. *The family is watching from the kitchen window.*

He'd known. He could hear them — Reth's weight shifting on the floorboards, the children's breathing, the younger girl whispering something her brother shushed. They were watching the way every family had watched him for eleven years: with the specific, desperate attention of people who needed something fixed, something cleared, something taken away so their children could play in the yard without breathing in whatever the last three seasons had left in the ground.

For eleven years, he'd taken their money and left. The contamination had stayed. The residue had seeped. Children had grown up breathing it. Thessa had told him that, back in Silt, with the specific measured fury of a woman who'd spent her entire adult life doing real work while he collected fees for performance.

He held the bowl in both hands and closed his eyes.

The gesture was meaningless — he was blind, had always been blind, his eyes open and closed gave him the same flat nothing — but it marked a transition. Between the man who walked roads and ate stale bread and couldn't forgive his oldest friend, and the man who opened himself to what was in the ground.

Thessa had taught him the real approach sequence in three sentences. *Open the channel. Name the contamination. Let it enter.* She'd said it like she was explaining how to mop a floor — with the impatience of someone who couldn't fathom why this required instruction. The rest of it — the specific way his chest had to soften, the grip on the bowl, the breathing — he'd learned through the particular education of doing it badly, throwing up, and doing it again while Thessa stood over him and said, *Slower this time.*

He opened the channel.

The contamination answered.

It came up through the ground and into him the way cold enters a body — not all at once but in waves, starting at the edges, working inward. Through his feet on the packed dirt, through his hands on the bowl, through the skin of his forearms where his sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Nine months of accumulated quest residue, the slow poison of spiritual waste left to fester, entering the one kind of person who could take it in and break it down.

It hurt. Not the scouring agony of the Woundmouth — not the sensation of having the architecture of the world forced through his chest. A working hurt. The ache of sustained effort, the burn of muscles doing what they were built for after years of disuse, the particular discomfort of holding himself open when every instinct said to close.

*Contamination entering the consumption field,* Aldric reported. His voice was steady. Quieter than it used to be during consumptions — he'd dropped the habit of narrating at a volume that suggested he was filing an urgent report with a superior officer. Now he spoke the way someone speaks in a room where important work is happening and they don't want to disrupt it. *Dissolution rate is good. Better than your last practice attempt. The residue is breaking down cleanly.*

"How much?" Maren's voice came out thick. The contamination sat in his mouth like a taste — grain and old iron and something animal underneath, the ghost of dead quests that had seeped into this farmer's soil and sat there waiting.

*Approximately a third of the pool has cleared. Steady progression. The secondary spread into the water table is receding as the primary pool diminishes.* A pause. *You're doing this correctly.*

"Don't sound so surprised."

*I'm not surprised. I'm documenting.*

The consumption settled into a rhythm. Maren sat on the plank bench with the bowl in his hands and the autumn sun on the back of his neck and the contamination moving through him like a slow tide, and he broke it down. Layer by layer, the accumulated months of neglect dissolving through his body and out the other side into something the ground could absorb. The process had no drama to it. No spectacle, no interface fireworks, no light show that the family at the window could watch and be reassured by. Just a blind man sitting very still with a ceramic bowl, breathing slowly, while the bad thing in the ground got smaller.

At the halfway mark, the girl — the younger one — came out the back door and sat on the steps. Her brother hissed at her from inside but she didn't move. She had dirt on her knees and was holding a piece of bread with a bite taken out of it.

"Is it working?" she asked.

*She's approximately four,* Aldric said. *Brown dress, let down at the hem twice. She has what appears to be jam on her chin.*

"Yeah," Maren said. "It's working."

"Can you see it going away?"

"No." And then, because the question deserved honesty and honesty was the only thing he had left that was worth anything: "I can't see anything. But someone I work with tells me it's going."

*Colleague,* Aldric offered.

"Someone I work with," Maren repeated.

The girl considered this for a while, chewing her bread. "That's funny," she said, not laughing, and went back inside.

*She's right,* Aldric said. *It is.*

The last of the contamination broke apart just as the sun moved behind the ridgeline and the air turned properly cold — the kind of cold that meant the season had genuinely shifted, that summer's long hold had loosened and wouldn't tighten again until spring. Maren sat with the empty bowl in his hands and the clean ground in front of him and the water moving back toward the well with nothing riding in it, and he was tired. Not the system-shocking, bone-deep collapse of the Woundmouth. Just tired. The way a body gets tired after a day of physical work — muscles used, energy spent, the simple animal accounting of effort and cost.

Reth came out when it was done. He stood at the edge of where the shimmer had been — where the packed dirt was just packed dirt now, holding nothing worse than the memory of a dry summer — and his face did something complicated that Maren couldn't see but could hear in the way his breathing changed.

"My aunt used to do this," Reth said. "Dalla. I watched her when I was small. She'd sit for hours, just like that."

"Yeah."

"She never said much about it after. Just went and washed her hands and ate something."

"That sounds about right." Maren stood up. His knees registered their objection. He let them.

"What do I owe you?"

The question every family had asked for eleven years. He'd always had a number ready — rehearsed, itemized, calculated his first week on the job for maximum plausibility. A practiced fee for a practiced fraud. The numbers came to him automatically and he let them pass.

"Whatever you can manage," Maren said. "I'm not particular."

Reth gave him six coppers and a meal. The meal was better than the money — stew with actual meat in it and bread that hadn't gone stale, eaten at the farmer's table while the children watched and the girl asked him three more questions about whether he could see her hand, her face, the bowl. No, no, no. She seemed satisfied by the consistency of his answers if nothing else. The boy didn't speak but put an extra piece of bread on Maren's plate when his father wasn't looking, which was its own kind of sentence.

*The contamination field has fully dissolved,* Aldric said as Maren cleaned the bowl at the well outside. The water was cold against his hands, clean-smelling for the first time in nine months, tasting of nothing but iron and stone when it splashed his wrist. *No residual trace. The groundwater readings are returning to pre-accumulation levels. The eastern field should be safe for planting by spring.*

"Good."

*The next contamination site on Thessa's list is eight miles north. A crossroads settlement. Minor pooling, no fragment signatures.* A pause. *I'll walk you through the route when you're ready.*

"I'm ready."

He dried his hands on his trousers and put the bowl in his pack. The ceramic clinked against the water skin, the stale bread he'd saved from morning, the six coppers — the small collection of objects that constituted his entire professional inventory. The girl was waving from the doorway. She'd been told twice that he couldn't see it. She was waving anyway. He waved back, roughly in the right direction, because it would have been rude not to and because she was four years old and standing in a yard that was clean for the first time since spring.

The road north was quiet. The early-autumn dark came on faster than summer dark, and the light was already going gold at the edges, the low slant that made everything look specific and temporary — each stone in the wall throwing its own shadow, each rut in the road distinct. The hum threaded through the air like it always did. Not gone, not ever gone, but present the way the ground was present, the way the road was present. Part of the architecture of a world that had been built on top of something older than itself and needed people — ordinary, unglamorous, underpaid people — to keep the seams from splitting open.

*Eight miles,* Aldric said. *The road forks at a cairn. Left.*

"Left."

*Your left.*

Maren's mouth pulled sideways. Not quite a smile. Close enough.

He walked north in the cooling air, with the hum around him and the voice beside him and the bowl in his pack knocking gently against his spine with each step — ceramic on canvas, the quiet percussion of a tool being carried to the next job. Behind him, the farmstead sat in clean ground, the children safe, the water running clear. Ahead, another site waited, another pool of contamination left too long in the earth, another question that had been asked and gone unanswered because nobody had come.

He was coming now. He'd keep coming. Not because he was brave or chosen or special, but because he'd spent eleven years not doing this, and people had been hurt, and the work was there, and he could do it.

Something had answered him once, in a dead farmer's house, when he hadn't expected it and hadn't deserved it. Now he was the one who answered.

Maren walked toward the work.
