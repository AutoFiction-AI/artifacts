# Chapter 14

The valley between the dungeon mouths tasted like scorched copper.

Maren had been walking through worsening contamination for two days, the smell thickening from background haze to something that lodged behind his sinuses and wouldn't clear, but the last quarter-mile dropped into a different register entirely. The ground under his boots was grey-dark and slick with decades of accumulated quest-light residue. The hum from three dungeon mouths — northeast, southeast, and the wide one dead ahead — layered into something that wasn't quite a sound anymore. It pressed against his chest, a low vibration that worked up through the soles of his boots and settled into the gap where the unclassified thing lived, and the thing hummed back.

*Six sin-eaters,* Aldric said. His voice had the edge it got when he was cataloguing rather than commenting — precision as habit, the ghost of field reports filed to an order that no longer existed. *All registered. Properly classified — maintenance tier, if the System still bothers to differentiate. The profiles are thin. I don't think anyone's updated them in years.*

"Six," Maren said. He'd been expecting more.

*The historical registry shows fourteen designations for this site. Most are marked inactive.*

"Dead?"

*Or quit. The classification doesn't distinguish.*

The valley opened below him in the last of the evening light, and the Crook was smaller than anything he'd prepared himself for.

He'd had a picture in his head — not grand, not that naive, but substantial. A camp with structure. Tents in patterns, a central fire, the organized bustle of people who did the same essential work gathering to share the load. Instead: five canvas shelters strung between boulders on the valley floor, smoke from two cook fires, a handful of figures moving with the careful efficiency of people who had stopped wasting motion years ago. One of the shelters had collapsed on its windward side and no one had fixed it. A line of washing hung between two stunted trees, the clothes stiff and faded from contamination exposure. The whole thing looked less like a professional gathering and more like a disaster camp that had forgotten to leave.

*Three approach angles from here,* Vess said flatly. *Two with cover. One visible from every point in the camp. You're on the visible one.*

"Good."

*That wasn't a recommendation.*

"I know." He kept walking down the exposed slope, hands open at his sides, no pack on his back because he'd lost it two days ago and hadn't replaced it. Nothing on him but his clothes, his bowl wrapped in cloth inside his shirt, and three coppers he'd found in the mud outside a collapsed waystation. Coming to the sin-eater gathering looking like exactly what he was: a man with nothing left to try.

The absence to his left was louder in the silence. No nervous chatter about the road or the weather or whether the bread had gone off. No second pair of feet on packed dirt, no Derry filling every quiet moment with stories about his daughter's counting or his wife's tomatoes or the cow that wouldn't stay fenced. Four days of walking alone, which meant four days of walking with only the voices in his head, and the voices in his head had the decency to be quiet about the person who should have been walking beside him.

The woman by the nearer cook fire saw him first. Short, square-shouldered, a grey bandage wrapped around her left hand that had gone stiff and dark at the edges. She stood and said something to the man sitting behind her — older, weathered, working a piece of dried root between his teeth with the concentrated patience of a long habit — and both of them turned to face the slope with expressions that had nothing to do with recognizing Maren's face and everything to do with what the System was telling them about the thing walking toward their camp.

Then Thessa came around the far shelter, a ceramic bowl in her stained hands, and stopped.

The distance between them was maybe forty feet. She looked the same as she had in Silt — tall, angular, grey-streaked hair cut short for practicality, hands dark to the wrist from thirty years of contamination exposure. Tired in a way that had gone structural, settled into the bones of her face. She looked at Maren the way she'd look at a contamination pool that had appeared where one shouldn't be.

"You came," she said.

"Wasn't anywhere else."

"No." Her eyes tracked across him, or past him — reading the interface, the profile, whatever it showed about the thing he was carrying. He'd never seen what they saw when they looked at him, but he'd gotten better at reading the reactions. The bandaged woman's reaction was fear. The older man's was professional assessment, the kind that measured before it responded. Thessa's was harder to parse. Something between fury and a recognition she didn't want.

"Your friend's not with you," she said.

"No."

She waited. He didn't elaborate. The silence held the shape of everything he wasn't going to say about Derry, about a man who'd chosen his daughter over a fight that wasn't his, about waking up on the road alone for the first time in weeks with the understanding that he had earned exactly this.

Thessa turned to the bandaged woman. "Petra. Get him water."

"I saw his profile," Petra said. "Thessa, that bounty—"

"I saw it too. Get him water."

Petra went. Maren reached the camp's edge and stopped because he'd learned not to walk into spaces where people were afraid of him. He stood at the border — contamination-darkened stone on both sides — and waited to be invited or turned away.

The older man finished chewing his root, spat the end into the fire, and stood. Taller than Maren had expected from above, with the kind of angular thinness that came from decades of physical work on insufficient food. His hands were stained like Thessa's — the dark residue that wouldn't wash out, the visible record of years of contamination handling. He looked at Maren with an expression of measured calm.

"Ruald," he said. "I work the western reaches."

"Maren."

"I know who you are. Your bounty's been visible for weeks — every hero in three provinces can see it." Ruald's voice was dry, unhurried, the voice of a man who'd spent enough years in contaminated silence that conversation was something he maintained rather than needed. "Thessa told us about Silt."

*He doesn't seem hostile,* Aldric said carefully. *His System profile shows contamination tolerance readings well below baseline. He's been absorbing more than he should be, for longer than is sustainable. All of them are.*

"Overworked," Maren murmured.

*Severely. Yes.*

Thessa was watching him from across the camp. Behind her, two more sin-eaters had emerged from the shelters — a young man barely past twenty who kept touching his own wrist as if checking for something he couldn't find, and a woman who stayed half-inside the canvas opening, arms crossed, watching with the fixed attention of someone deciding whether to run.

"Sit," Thessa told him. She pointed at a flat stone near the cook fire. "Eat if they'll feed you. Tomorrow we look at what you are."

"And then?"

"That depends on what we find."

He sat. Petra brought him water in a clay cup with a crack running down one side, mended with brown resin. She set it on the stone beside him rather than placing it in his hand — the distance of a woman who'd seen his readings and didn't want to be within arm's reach. Then she sat across the fire and unwound the bandage from her hand, working carefully, and the skin underneath was raw and mottled with a contamination pattern Maren recognized from his own failed attempts — the discoloration of flesh that had opened to something toxic and hadn't fully recovered.

"Bad consumption?" he asked.

She looked at him sharply. "You'd know what that looks like?"

"Had a few."

"Yeah. Source pool in the northeast mouth. Standard approach barely dented it and my hand was in the bowl when the backwash hit." She shook her head and started rewrapping. "Doesn't matter."

"She nearly lost the use of it," Ruald said, settling back onto his stone with a new piece of root. "The contamination is denser this season. The standard approaches work less than they did."

The fire was warm. The cool of early autumn was settling into the valley with the fading light, the first real chill Maren had felt since leaving Fenhold, and his body registered it gratefully after weeks of late-summer heat on dusty roads. His legs ached from four days of solo walking. The gap in his chest — the space he'd started thinking of as the crack, because Aldric had called it that and the word had stuck — pulsed heavier in the presence of concentrated contamination, as if it were responding to the density around it. A slow, dull throb in time with the triple hum.

*The young one is still watching you,* Vess said. *He's not deciding whether to trust you. He's deciding whether you're going to kill them in their sleep.*

Maren looked at the young sin-eater, who stood ten feet away with his hand still on his wrist, his face tight with an anxiety that didn't seem to belong exclusively to Maren's arrival — more like the residue of something that had been building for a while.

"I'm Maren," he said.

The young man said nothing. After a moment, he went back inside the shelter.

"Hale," Petra said. "He doesn't talk much lately. The work's been..." She trailed off, poked the fire with a stick.

The valley settled into its evening sounds — the low compound hum of three dungeon mouths, the crack and pop from the fires, the distant drip-echo of quest-light pooling somewhere below the surface. Maren ate what Ruald put in front of him: grain porridge with a heel of dense bread that tasted faintly of the contamination in the water they'd used to cook it. Everything here was contaminated. The food, the water, the air, the ground. These people lived in it, worked in it, ate it, breathed it, and went back to work the next morning.

He slept that night near the fire, wrapped in a blanket Ruald gave him that smelled of ritual residue and woodsmoke. The hum from the dungeon mouths didn't stop. Neither did the thing in his chest, which pulsed faintly in time with the contamination outside, as if it were breathing too.

---

The examination started at dawn, when the light was grey and the contamination smelled sharpest.

They didn't call it a trial. They didn't call it anything. Thessa told him to sit on the flat stone near the collapsed shelter where the contamination was densest, and the five other sin-eaters arranged themselves around him at varying distances — Thessa closest, Ruald three paces back, Petra and the woman from the shelter doorway (Denna, her name turned out to be, and she still hadn't spoken to Maren directly) at the midpoint, and Hale at the furthest remove, his hand on his wrist, his face white.

Thessa knelt in front of him. She held her bowl — stained dark, edges worn smooth, a tool that had seen thirty years of genuine use — and closed her eyes. Around her, the others did the same.

*They're scanning you,* Aldric said. *Through the contamination channels — using their sin-eating sensitivity to read your residue patterns. I don't have a clean term for it. The closest I can get is that they're listening to your contamination the way a physician listens to a heartbeat, but through the System's maintenance layer.*

Maren sat still. Something shifted in the contamination around him — not their attention exactly, but a change in the ambient density. The contamination pulled toward the sin-eaters' open channels the way water flows toward a drain. And inside him, the fragments stirred.

*They can feel us,* Vess said. Her voice was flat and controlled, which meant she was working to keep it that way. *Both of us. The scan is reaching the fragment layer.*

*I'm aware.*

*They shouldn't be able to reach that deep. That's not standard sin-eating.*

*No. But this close to three active dungeon mouths, with contamination this dense, their sensitivity range is significantly extended. They can see things that wouldn't be legible in a normal setting.*

Twenty minutes. Maren sat through it the way he'd sat through every uncomfortable examination of his life — a performance of stillness that had once been a fraud's best tool and was now the closest thing to honesty he could manage. He couldn't see what they were reading. He couldn't contribute to his own assessment. He sat on the stone and waited for the verdict while people with decades of real skill read the contamination in his chest and decided whether it was something they could work with or something they needed to destroy.

Thessa opened her eyes first. Her face was tight — the expression of someone who'd found something worse than expected and was still working out whether to be angry or afraid.

"How long have you been carrying the second fragment?" she asked.

"Since the road past Fenhold. Sixteen, seventeen days."

"The collision between them has gotten worse. The opposed classifications are generating friction at the interface boundary — two currents pushing against each other in a narrow channel. The turbulence is—" She stopped. Looked at Ruald. "You felt it?"

Ruald nodded. His jaw worked steadily on a fresh piece of root. "The fragments aren't seated properly. Neither of them. It's as if they're floating in the host rather than anchored, and the space between them—"

"The unclassified thing," Thessa said.

"Yes."

"It's grown since Silt."

"Considerably."

Maren's hands were flat on his knees. The gap pulsed once, dully, as if it knew it was being discussed.

*She's not wrong,* Aldric said quietly. *Whatever we are — whatever I am, inside you — it's less stable than it was two weeks ago. Whether that's the entity or the proximity to this level of contamination, I can't say. But something has shifted.*

"There's more," Thessa said. She stood and faced the circle. Her voice hardened into something instructional — the voice of the most experienced person in the room who was done being polite about it. "His contamination pattern is inverted. When he consumes — even the partial consumptions he's managed — the contamination doesn't clear. It redistributes. He's moving it, not dissolving it."

Silence.

Denna spoke for the first time. Her voice was rougher than Maren expected, scratched by years of contamination exposure or maybe just a voice that had never been smooth. "Where does it go?"

"Outward. Wider dispersal, lower density. It reads as reduced locally, but the total volume—" Thessa stopped. The next words cost her something. "The total volume isn't decreasing. He's spreading contamination across a wider area and making it look like clearance."

The silence this time landed differently. Maren could read it in the way Petra's bandaged hand clenched around nothing, in the way Ruald's jaw stopped working, in the way Hale took a step backward — the horror of contamination workers discovering that a contamination source had walked into their camp.

"Every village you touched," Thessa said, turning back to him. "I told you this in Silt. The contamination is still there. But it's worse than I thought. You haven't just been leaving it in place — you've been redistributing it. Thinning it, spreading it. Making it harder for real sin-eaters to find and clear because it's no longer concentrated enough to locate through standard channels."

"I didn't know," Maren said.

"No. You didn't. Because you were faking."

He had nothing for that.

*She's right,* Aldric said. *I've been watching the pattern since your consumption attempt at the creek. The contamination enters you, and when you can't fully dissolve it — which you can't, not yet — it exits through the gap. The entity acts as a diffusion engine. It scatters what you take in across a wider field.*

"Consume him," Thessa said.

It took Maren a moment to understand she wasn't talking to him. She was talking to the circle.

"Break down the fragments. Dissolve the unclassified structure. It's the same process we use on contamination that can't be cleared through standard consumption — we eat it. One of us takes him, and we end this."

"Thessa—" Ruald began.

"He's carrying two hostile fragments in an unstable configuration, an entity the System itself can't classify, and a contamination pattern that poisons every area he passes through. He's not a sin-eater. He's a contamination event with legs. If a pool showed these readings, we'd consume it without discussion."

"A pool doesn't speak," Ruald said mildly.

"Neither does the contamination in the villages he's been spreading for eleven years. The children growing up in it don't get to speak either."

Maren took it. He sat on his stone and looked at Thessa's stained hands and understood, with a clarity that bypassed every defense he'd ever built, that she was right. From every angle that mattered to these people — the only people who did the work that kept the world from drowning in its own waste — he was the problem, not the solution.

"Who takes him?" Petra asked. The question was practical, not moral, and the practicality was worse. "I can't. My hand's barely functional. Ruald?"

Ruald shook his head slowly. "His readings are too high. The unclassified structure alone exceeds my consumption capacity. If any of us attempted a full consumption and failed, the backwash would be catastrophic. We'd be redistributing him instead of dissolving him."

"Then we — what? Call someone who can?"

"There's no one to call," Thessa said. The anger in her voice cracked, and underneath it Maren heard something he recognized from the inside — the exhaustion of someone who'd been carrying a weight alone for so long that the weight had become invisible to everyone but her. "There are six of us. There should be fourteen. The contamination is denser every season and there are fewer of us every year and the standard approaches don't work the way they did a decade ago, and I—"

She caught herself. The control came back down over her face like a door closing. The professional. The one who did the work.

"I don't have a clean answer," she said, and the words sounded like they'd been pulled out of her against her will. "But leaving him as he is means leaving the problem to grow."

*She almost said it,* Vess said. *That was as close as she's ever gotten to admitting the work isn't holding.*

Maren looked at Thessa's face and saw it: the private doubt she'd been keeping sealed. Not just about him. About all of it. The contamination getting worse. The methods losing ground. The numbers dwindling. The work that the System classified as maintenance-tier, that no one funded or praised or even noticed until it wasn't done, that was somehow supposed to be performed by six exhausted people in a poisoned valley while heroes left scorch marks on clean roads and collected bounties worth more than a sin-eater would see in a lifetime.

"Test me," Maren said.

Thessa's eyes snapped to him.

"You can't consume me safely. You said it yourself — the readings are too high, the risk is too great. So test me instead. Make me do a consumption under your supervision. If I can't manage it, if I'm nothing but a contamination event with legs—" He used her words deliberately, watched them register. "Then you work out something else. But you've got nothing to lose from watching me try."

"Except more redistributed contamination across our workspace," Denna said from her position in the circle.

"If it goes wrong, the contamination's already here. This valley's baseline is worse than anything I could add."

*That's not entirely true,* Aldric said. *But it's close enough to be tactically useful.*

Ruald spat the end of his root into the dirt. "Let him try."

Thessa looked at Ruald. Looked at Maren. Looked at the valley around them, the three dungeon mouths breathing their contamination into the low ground, the grey-dark stone, the faint shimmer of quest-light pooling in the morning air — the raw glow without data, without labels, without the overlay the others navigated by.

"Fine," she said. "Supervised. Every step. And if what I see looks like redistribution instead of clearance, I stop you. Hard."

"Fine."

"Get up. We do it now."

---

They moved to the nearest dungeon mouth within the hour, the morning mist still thinning around them. Vapour rose from the contaminated ground in thin tendrils, catching the pale light, and the hum from the opening was a physical pressure that Maren could feel in his teeth. The stone around the mouth was nearly black from years of quest-light saturation, and the contamination smell was dense enough to coat the inside of his throat.

Maren knelt ten paces from the mouth with his ceramic bowl unwrapped and in his hands. The bowl — the ordinary ceramic he'd bought in a Fenhold market at seventeen, the prop of eleven years of fake rituals — sat on the ground in front of him, and in this place, surrounded by real sin-eaters, it looked exactly as fraudulent as it was. No staining. No ritual wear. Clean, cheap, useless.

Thessa stood behind him and to his right. Ruald, Petra, Denna, and Hale were arrayed in a loose half-circle at twenty paces — close enough to observe, far enough to absorb the backwash if the consumption went wrong. Hale had both hands at his sides now, fingers curled, watching with the rigid attention of a young man who didn't want to be the first to flinch.

"Kneeling posture," Thessa said. "Bowl in front of you, open. Elbows off your knees — you need your chest open for the intake."

He adjusted. The stone was cold and damp through his trousers.

"Breathe first. Not the performance breathing you've been doing — the actual pattern. In through the nose until you feel the contamination enter your sinus cavity. Hold it. When the ozone taste reaches the back of your throat, open."

*She's describing the primary intake sequence,* Aldric said. *It matches what the Order's texts describe, but more embodied. Less theoretical. She's teaching from practice, not doctrine.*

Maren breathed in. The contamination entered through his nose — not a metaphor, a physical sensation, the dense copper-sweet mixture that was the valley's baseline air — and he held it. The taste crept along the roof of his mouth, down the back of his throat. The gap in his chest stirred.

"Open," Thessa said.

He opened. Not his mouth — this wasn't about the mouth, despite the name. Opening was the thing he'd learned on his own near the creek bed, the thing that had nearly broken him: a deliberate surrender of the boundary between himself and the contamination around him. Like unclenching a fist he hadn't known was clenched, somewhere behind his sternum. The inside of his chest peeled back, and the contamination rushed in.

*Intake volume is—* Aldric's voice sharpened. *That's significantly more than your solo attempt. The density here is — Maren, slow down. You're pulling from more than the immediate pool. You're drawing from—*

"Wider," Thessa said, and her voice had changed. The instructional flatness was gone. "He's pulling wider. Ruald — do you see the radius?"

"I see it."

The contamination poured through the gap and the gap was not a hole but a channel, a crack, and the crack widened as the contamination flowed, and the flow was not something Maren was controlling. His hands were locked on the cold stone beside his knees. His bowl sat empty because the bowl was for collection and there was nothing to collect — the contamination was going through him, not into him, passing through the space where the unclassified entity lived and coming out the other side as something that read, to everyone watching, like clearance. His vision blurred. The hum from the dungeon mouth rose in pitch and the mist around the opening churned and his body wanted to double over and vomit but Thessa's hand was on his shoulder, holding him upright, her grip hard and professional.

"Don't collapse. Keep the channel open. If you close it with contamination still in transit—"

He kept it open. His muscles were shaking. Aldric had gone quiet — the quiet that meant he was seeing something in the System's architecture that outpaced language — and Vess was counting.

*Fifteen seconds. Your body starts losing structural coherence around thirty. Close soon.*

"I can't — I don't know how to close it from—"

"Close the same way you opened," Thessa said. Her voice was tight but held. "Clench. The fist behind your sternum. Find it and close it."

He found it. He closed it. The contamination flow cut off like a valve shutting and the shock — the abrupt cessation of something that had been flowing through the core of him — knocked him sideways off his knees. He caught himself on his palms. His bowl tipped and rang against the stone, a clear ceramic note that sounded absurd in the contaminated dawn. His arms shook. His stomach heaved once, dry, and he spat on the ground and the spit was dark with residue.

"Stay down," Thessa said. She was standing over him, reading the interface. Around her, the other sin-eaters were reading too.

He was on his hands and knees on contaminated stone, shaking, spitting dark, and the gap in his chest felt wider than it had before the consumption, as if something had been stretched that wouldn't go back. His bowl lay on its side next to his hand, empty. A prop. A fake thing in a real place.

*Maren.* Aldric's voice came back, rough, stripped of its usual register. *The ambient contamination readings across the valley have decreased. Not just around the dungeon mouth. The entire valley. Every reading point I can access has dropped, by a margin that would normally require days of individual consumption by multiple sin-eaters.*

The silence in the circle was different now.

Maren got himself back to kneeling. His hands were smeared with the dark residue of contaminated stone, his forearms trembling. He picked up his bowl and set it flat in front of him and looked at the clean white interior — his eleven-year prop, his fake tool — and then he looked at Thessa.

She was staring at the interface readouts with an expression he had never seen on her face before. Not anger. Not disgust. Something more complicated than either — the look of a woman who'd been doing essential work alone for decades watching someone accomplish in twenty seconds what she couldn't do in a full day, and trying to reconcile that fact with the knowledge that the someone was a fraud, a contamination hazard, and an unclassified threat to everything she'd built her professional life around.

"The redistribution pattern," Ruald said. He was chewing a fresh root, but his voice had changed — something careful in it, something wondering. "It's different from what you described. The contamination didn't scatter. It decreased. Genuinely. The total volume in the valley dropped."

"I can see that," Thessa said.

"How?" Petra asked. She was flexing her bandaged hand, staring at whatever the interface was showing her. "That's not — sin-eating doesn't work like that. We consume and dissolve. We don't just reduce ambient levels across an entire valley in one intake."

*The unclassified entity,* Aldric said. *It's acting as an amplifier. Or a wider aperture. The standard sin-eating process works through a narrow channel — one pool, one consumption, one dissolution. But the gap in Maren's chest isn't narrow. It's connected to the broader contamination structure in a way that individual sin-eaters aren't. When he opens, he opens wider than any of them can.*

Thessa was looking at Maren. The other sin-eaters were looking at Thessa.

"The last person who could do this," Ruald said slowly. "Sixty years ago. You told us about them."

"They were consumed by the System," Thessa said. "Corrected. Nothing left."

"But before that—"

"Before that, the records say contamination levels in their region were the lowest in a century."

The mist was thinning in the growing light. The hum from the three dungeon mouths continued, as it always did, as it would continue regardless of what six exhausted sin-eaters and one fraudulent newcomer decided to do about it. The contamination was still there — thick, structural, getting worse every season. The valley still needed more people than it had. Petra's hand was still raw under the bandage. Hale was still standing at the far edge of the half-circle, though he'd moved three steps closer at some point and his face had shifted from fear to something closer to wary attention.

Thessa looked at Maren for a long time. He knelt on the cold stone with his dirty hands and his clean bowl and his trembling arms and the gap in his chest that was wider than it had been an hour ago, and he waited.

"You're sloppy," she said. "Your intake form is wrong. Your closing is dangerous — you're shutting the valve too hard and you'll give yourself internal contamination rebound within a month. You have no control over the width of your aperture, which means any consumption could pull more than your body can structurally handle. You're a hazard to yourself and anyone standing near you."

"Yeah."

"And you did more in twenty seconds than I've done in a day."

He didn't know what to say to that. He'd never known what to say when someone told him the truth about himself — he'd spent eleven years making sure no one had the chance.

*Don't say anything,* Vess said. *She's working through something. Let her get there.*

Thessa looked at her own hands. The stained, weathered hands of a woman who'd done the real work for thirty years, who'd cleared contamination from farmland and villages and the spaces where children played, who'd done it alone and underfunded and unrecognized while the System classified her labor as maintenance-tier and heroes collected bounties worth more than she'd earn in a decade. She looked at her hands and she looked at the valley and she looked at the man kneeling on the contaminated stone with his fake bowl and his real problem.

"Not entirely useless," she said.

Maren breathed out.

"We start with the intake form. Your posture's wrong, your breathing's wrong, your closing technique will kill you within a month if you keep doing it that way. You don't eat, sleep, or breathe without my say-so until I'm satisfied you won't blow us all apart." She paused. "And the first time you fake anything — one gesture, one posture, one breath that's performance instead of practice — I stop teaching and we go back to the consumption discussion. Clear?"

"Clear."

"Get off the ground. You look like you're praying."

He got off the ground. His legs barely held. Aldric was quiet in the way that meant he was still processing, and Vess was quiet in the way that meant she'd already processed and was running scenarios for what happened next. The morning light reached the bottom of the valley and the quest-light pooling near the dungeon mouths caught it — a faint shimmer, like water reflecting something Maren couldn't quite see, beautiful in the way things were beautiful when you didn't have the data to know what you were looking at.

Thessa walked toward the cook fire. After a moment, Petra followed. Then Ruald, chewing his root. Denna went back to her shelter without comment. Hale stood where he was, still watching — but he nodded at Maren once before turning away, and the nod carried the weight of something being permitted that hadn't been before.

Maren picked up his bowl. The ceramic was cool in his hands, the same cheap clay it had always been, no heavier and no lighter for what had just passed through it. He wrapped it in its cloth and held it against his chest where the gap pulsed faintly in time with the contamination in the air, and walked toward the fire where six sin-eaters were already beginning their day in the valley between three dungeon mouths, doing the thankless essential work that kept the world from choking on itself.

He had not been invited. He had not been welcomed. He had been judged dangerous, nearly consumed, and grudgingly spared because his particular kind of danger happened to do something useful. It was the worst version of belonging he could imagine, and it was the first real one he'd ever had.

*Well,* Aldric said, and underneath the paladin's usual register there was something that might have been warmth, or at least the ghost of where warmth would live if he'd permit it. *Not entirely useless.*
