# Chapter 15

The valley smelled different in the early morning. Cooler air pushed the contamination stink below the threshold of constant notice, thinned it enough that Maren could almost forget the ozone-sweet undertow rising from the discolored ground. Almost. The hum from the three dungeon mouths was lower at dawn too, the way a furnace is quieter when the fire banks down but the metal stays hot. He'd slept badly on the thin bedroll Kael had lent him, the entity pressing against the walls of his consciousness in slow, rhythmic surges like breathing, and when he'd given up on sleep and crawled out of the lean-to in the grey pre-dawn, the valley had looked peaceful in the way that only contaminated places can look peaceful — the quest-light pooling in the low spots with a faint shimmer he could barely see, pale as moonlight on standing water.

He was on his knees now, east of the main camp, working through the second containment pass Thessa had drilled into him the afternoon before. The ceramic bowl sat in his lap. His hands moved through the postures — or tried to. His left wrist kept dropping at the wrong angle, and each time it did, the thin thread of contamination he was trying to draw from the nearby residue pool snapped back like a fishing line cut.

Behind him, the camp was waking up. Errin had the fire going — Maren could hear the crackle and the particular scrape of her clay jars as she sorted contamination samples on the wooden table. She did this every morning, Kael had told him. Thirty years of collecting and cataloguing residue, and Errin could identify the age and source of contamination by smell alone. She hummed while she worked, something tuneless and repetitive, the way someone hums who has forgotten they're doing it. She walked with a bad left hip that made her gait lopsided, and her fingers were stained the same dark brown as Thessa's — the permanent record of decades spent handling what other people wouldn't touch.

Paran was sitting on his upturned bucket near the eastern lean-to, rewrapping the cloth strips he used for his own version of the containment pass. His method was different from Thessa's — regional, she'd said with the particular displeasure she reserved for techniques that weren't hers. The strips were grey-brown from repeated contamination exposure and smelled faintly of burnt sugar, a smell that followed Paran around the camp like a second body. He had a wife somewhere south of Silt, and he'd mentioned her once in Maren's hearing — not her name, but the way she folded his strips for him when he came home, matching the edges precisely, a kindness so small and specific that Maren had looked away.

Goss — thin, quiet, ate with his left hand and wrote with his right — was somewhere behind the second lean-to. Maren had barely spoken to him. He knew Goss had arrived at the Crook three days before Maren, and that Goss kept a tally of the contamination pools he'd cleared on a strip of leather tied to his belt, notching it with a knife after each job. Kael had said he was up to two hundred and nine.

*Four hostiles approaching from the northwest ridge.*

Aldric's voice came fast and flat, the subordinate clauses stripped away, the formal syntax compressed under something that wasn't quite urgency — it was worse. It was recognition.

*Full combat loadout. Hero-ranger lead, two melee fighters, one support caster. Moving in formation. The ranger is Caul.*

Maren set the bowl down in the dirt. The containment thread dissolved. "How far?"

*Quarter mile. Descending the ridge. The caster is already generating a targeting overlay — they have visual on the camp.*

The bowl's rim bit into his palm where he'd been gripping it too hard. He'd known Caul would come. Derry had given him the direction, and Caul was a tracker by training, patient the way men are patient when patience is the only thing between them and the grief underneath. But Maren had thought he'd have days. He'd thought the Crook's contamination would slow them, make the approach harder, buy time that could become teaching that could become competence that could become — something. Something other than this.

He stood and his knees cracked from the cold ground. His balance shifted wrong, the entity's weight redistributing inside him like water in a barrel, and he caught himself on the trunk of a dead birch. The bark came apart under his palm, dry and soft as old bread.

He crossed the camp at a run.

"Heroes," he said. He said it to all of them and none of them. "Four. Northwest ridge, quarter mile out. They've got a targeting overlay on the camp."

Errin's humming stopped. The clay jar in her hand went still.

Paran looked up from his cloth strips. His face did something complicated — not panic, exactly, but the rapid calculation of a man who understood immediately what this meant and was already measuring the distance to every exit he could see.

Thessa came out of the largest lean-to. Her grey-streaked hair was flat on one side from sleep and her stained hands were empty, which meant she'd heard him coming and had already set down whatever she'd been holding. She took one look at his face.

"How many?"

"Four. Ranger, two melee, one caster. The ranger's Caul."

Something moved behind Thessa's eyes — not fear, but the sharp recalculation of a woman who'd been managing contamination crises for thirty years and had just had the crisis revised upward. She turned to the camp. "Leave the lean-tos. Take what you can carry. We're moving south toward the third mouth."

"We can't outrun a hero party," Paran said. He'd stood up. The cloth strips hung from one hand, grey-brown and limp. "Thessa, we're not — none of us are combat-classified, we don't have—"

"I'm not suggesting we fight. The contamination pools around the third mouth are deep enough to degrade their targeting. Hero interface loses precision in heavy-contamination zones." She looked at Maren. The look was not warm. "You're the reason they're here."

"Yeah."

"Then you stay and give us time."

It wasn't a question. Thessa turned and began herding the other sin-eaters south, issuing instructions in the clipped imperatives that meant she was working the problem rather than discussing it. Errin gathered her clay jars against her chest, clutching them the way someone clutches something precious, and her bad hip made her gait worse than usual as she moved. Paran stuffed his cloth strips into a pack and fumbled the buckle — once, twice, his hands doing the thing hands do when the body knows something the mind hasn't caught up to. Kael was already moving, her pack on her back. Goss emerged from behind the second lean-to with his notched leather strip swinging from his belt.

*They won't make it.* Vess's voice, colorless and level. *The targeting overlay covers the full valley. The caster will track them.*

*Confirmed. The overlay shows every heat signature in the camp. The sin-eaters are flagged as non-combatant civilians, but—* Aldric paused. The pause was worse than what followed. *Collateral authorization has been granted. The bounty's threat level exceeds the containment threshold.*

"What does that mean in actual words?"

*It means the heroes are allowed to kill anyone between them and you.*

The first arrow hit the support pole of the eastern lean-to with a flat crack, and the canvas slumped sideways like a tent with its spine broken. Maren hadn't heard the bow. He heard the impact, the crack of the wood, and then a second arrow buried itself in the dirt three feet from where Paran was still wrestling with his pack buckle.

"Move!" Maren shouted. "Paran, move, don't—"

The two melee heroes came over the northern lip of the camp at a run. Maren saw them as shapes — one tall, carrying something long, one shorter and fast. His body recognized what his eyes couldn't parse. Aldric's combat training flooding into his muscles, Vess's killing instinct prickling along his nerves, and his feet were moving before he'd decided anything, shifting his weight, dropping his center, putting himself between the descending fighters and the retreating sin-eaters.

The tall one reached him first. Polearm — he felt the displaced air before he saw the sweep, a low strike aimed at his legs, and his body wasn't there. It had sidestepped, half-turned, and his hand was on the shaft, redirecting the arc past his hip. The weapon was heavier than his wrists expected and the shock of it traveled up his arms to his shoulders. He could smell the fighter — leather oil, road sweat, something herbal in the armor's padding.

*She's third-tier melee. Polearm specialization. Weakness: inside range. Get past the—*

He was already past it. His body closed the distance while his mind was still processing Vess's tactical feed, and his elbow connected with the side of the woman's head, just above the ear guard. She staggered. Blood ran from somewhere he couldn't see.

The second fighter came from the right. Dual blades. Young — Maren caught that much in the fragment of attention his body spared him. A face that still had the smooth finish of someone who hadn't been doing this long, mouth open with effort. The blades came in fast and crossing, and Maren's body did things he couldn't track — a block that used his forearm against the flat of one blade, a pivot that took him outside the arc of the other, something with his knee that buckled the young fighter's stance.

He was fighting and he was not fighting. The fragments operated his body while Maren occupied it, present but not in command, experiencing the combat through sensation without data. No damage numbers. No threat calculations. No targeting overlay telling him where to hit and how hard. Just the jar of impact traveling through his bones, the smell of disturbed contamination rising from the ground under their feet, the specific sound of metal on leather and leather on dirt.

Errin screamed.

Not a combat sound. A high, ragged sound — the sound a sixty-year-old woman makes when something hits her body that her body cannot survive. Maren's head turned. His body was still engaged with the dual-blade fighter, feet working patterns that belonged to a dead paladin, and he saw Errin on the ground near the third lean-to. Her clay jars were smashed around her, dark contamination samples soaking into the discolored earth. The arrow was in her back, below the left shoulder blade, and she was trying to crawl. Her bad hip dragged behind her. The humming had stopped.

*Caul's position. Northwest ridge. Sixty-yard range. He's cycling—*

The second arrow took Paran in the throat.

He'd turned back. That was the thing — he'd turned back when Errin screamed, the way you turn back for someone you've worked beside for years in a job nobody thanks you for, and the arrow found the gap between his chin and his chest. The broadhead went in clean and Paran sat down hard, as if someone had removed a stool from under him, and his hands went to his neck. The cloth strips — grey-brown, smelling of burnt sugar, folded by a wife whose name Maren would never know — spilled from his open pack onto the contaminated ground.

*Maren.* Vess, still level, still precise, the tone of someone recording data during a disaster. *Third melee engagement on your left. The polearm fighter is back up.*

His body spun. The polearm came in overhead this time, a chopping strike, and Maren ducked under it and drove his fist into the woman's diaphragm. She doubled and his body did something terrible to her knee that made a sound like a branch snapping and she went down.

The dual-blade fighter was backing off. Maren could hear the young hero's breathing — ragged, panicked, the breathing of someone whose combat-class training hadn't prepared him for an enemy his interface couldn't classify. The targeting overlay showed nothing where Maren stood. A void. A gap in the System's classification where a person should be.

"Goss is down." Kael's voice, from somewhere south. Flat with shock. "He's — there was a System pulse, something from the caster, and he just—"

Three sin-eaters. Three people who'd come to the Crook to do the work that kept the world's contamination from poisoning farmland and children. Dead because Maren had brought his bounty to their door.

*The caster is repositioning to the east. She's trying to establish a new targeting lock on you.*

Maren looked south. Thessa was fifty yards out, moving fast, and Kael was with her. The other sin-eater — Maren hadn't caught a name — was running for the southern ridge. Behind Maren, the polearm fighter was on the ground holding her ruined knee, and the dual-blade boy was retreating upslope toward Caul's position, and the caster was somewhere Maren couldn't see because he couldn't see anything the System generated.

*Maren.* Aldric's voice had changed. Not the compressed urgency of tactical feed — something rawer, closer to the grief that lived underneath his formality. *The caster's next pulse will establish a targeting lock that allows precision strikes at range. If she completes the scan—*

"How long?"

*Thirty seconds. Perhaps less.*

Maren looked at the residue pool. Not the small practice pool where he'd been doing containment passes. The big one — the wide, shallow depression between the first and second dungeon mouths where contamination had collected for years, deep enough and old enough that even Maren could see it clearly: a steady, cold glow spread across the low ground like a frozen lake. The smell coming off it was dense and layered — fresh ozone on top, the sweetness of accumulated quest residue underneath, and below that the sour-metal stink of contamination so old it had begun to change the chemistry of the soil.

*No.* Aldric understood before Maren moved. *That is a nexus-grade contamination pool. You consumed a fraction of a minor pool under supervision and it nearly broke you. If you open yourself to that—*

"If I don't, the caster gets a lock and everyone south of here dies."

*You cannot control—*

"I'm not trying to control it." Maren was already running toward the pool, his boots slipping on the discolored ground, the entity pressing outward inside him as if it could sense what he was about to do and was already reaching for it. "I'm trying to break their targeting."

Vess said nothing. Her silence, as always, was its own assessment.

---

He dropped to his knees at the edge of the pool. The contamination hummed against his shins through his trousers — not the diffuse background hum of the valley but a focused vibration, as if the pool had its own frequency, its own voice. The glow was cold and steady and he could feel it on the skin of his hands, a faint pressure like being watched.

He placed the ceramic bowl at the pool's edge. The bowl that had been a prop for eleven years of fraud, then a beginner's tool for three days of real practice. His hands were steadier than they should have been. The steadiness of someone who had gone past the point where shaking helped.

He used the second opening — the one Thessa had called reckless, the one she'd said was only for emergencies, the one that required the least preparation and the most willingness to let the contamination in. The willingness was the part that mattered. Not technique. Not knowledge. The simple, skin-crawling act of opening himself to the world's accumulated wrongness and letting it pour into the space a person leaves when they stop defending against what's outside.

The contamination came.

Not the thin trickle of his practice sessions. This was a torrent — cold, vast, heavier than anything he'd felt, pouring into him through whatever channel sin-eating opened between the world and the willing. His chest filled with something that was not air and not liquid but had the mass and pressure of deep water. The taste hit the back of his throat — ozone and copper and the ghost of sweetness, like fruit rotting in a cellar. His teeth ached. The roots of them. His eye sockets ached, the bones behind his eyes pressing inward under a weight that had nothing to do with physics.

*The targeting overlay is degrading.* Aldric's voice came from somewhere distant, as if he were speaking from the bottom of the pool Maren was swallowing. *The heroes' interface — it is flickering. The local System architecture is destabilizing around the consumption point.*

More. He pulled more. The contamination wanted to be consumed — that was the thing nobody told you, the thing Thessa's instructions hadn't covered, the thing that made real sin-eating different from every other form of work Maren had ever done. The contamination rushed toward the opening he'd made in himself the way water rushes toward a drain, eager, almost grateful, and the pool's glow dimmed as the residue flowed into him.

Inside his skull, the unclassified entity — the thing born from the collision of a dead paladin and a dead assassin, the nameless presence the System couldn't categorize — responded to the flood of contamination like a fire responds to fuel.

It didn't stir. It surged.

The sensation was unlike anything in Maren's body's vocabulary. His bones vibrated at a frequency that didn't belong to his skeleton, as if his marrow had begun resonating with something that lived in the contamination, something that recognized the entity and answered it. The muscles of his back seized — not a cramp but a recruitment, every fiber contracting at once as if his body were trying to become a fist. The bowl cracked under his knee. He heard it — a sharp, ceramic pop — and felt a shard bite into his kneecap through his trousers, but the pain was a small thing, a detail, a single note inside a sound that was becoming deafening.

*Maren, stop.* Vess, sharp and immediate, the professional flatness fracturing. *The entity is expanding beyond your containment threshold. If it—*

He couldn't stop. The consumption had its own momentum. The pool was feeding into him faster than he could regulate, and the entity was drinking from the same channel, unfolding, growing, pressing against the inside of his consciousness like something trying to stand up inside a room with a ceiling too low to permit it.

*The System sees it.* Aldric's voice had changed entirely. Not formal. Not even frightened. Awed, in the specific way a man is awed by the thing he has always suspected and had spent his career hoping he was wrong about. *The entity is registering on the System interface. It is — Maren, it is not reading as a threat classification. It is reading as a void. A gap in the architecture itself. The System is—*

The pool went dark. Not empty — dark. As if something had consumed the light from inside the contamination rather than from the surface, eating the glow inward the way a body absorbs a bruise. The last of the residue rushed into Maren and the channel snapped shut and he was on his hands and knees in dead soil where a nexus-grade contamination pool had been, retching, and nothing came up because the contamination was not in his stomach. It was everywhere else.

The hum from the dungeon mouths spiked.

Not a gradual increase. A step-change — from the low furnace-bass that Maren had been hearing for two days to something that scraped the inside of his sinuses and set his back teeth ringing. The frequency was wrong. Too high for the dungeon mouths, too structured, as if the hum had been replaced by something deliberately generated.

The air temperature dropped. All at once, five or six degrees in a single breath, the warmth of the early-autumn morning yanked away and replaced by the damp chill of a root cellar. Maren's exhale fogged.

*Local reclassification in progress.* Aldric was reading from the System interface now, his voice stripped to the formal, clipped cadence that meant he was too frightened for anything else — retreating into the language of reports and briefings because the alternative was silence. *The Crook Valley is being redesignated. Threat classification: Critical. Contamination index: Extreme. Authorized response: full containment protocol.*

"What does that—"

*It means the System is treating this valley as a dungeon breach. Every entity within the designated perimeter is reclassified as a potential threat target.* A pause that lasted too long. *Every entity. Including Caul's party.*

---

The quest-light came first.

Not the slow pooling of normal residue — the patient accumulation that Maren had watched settle into low ground over the past two days like luminous groundwater. This erupted. It came up through the soil in threads and runners, bright and cold and hard-edged, crawling across the discolored earth with the purposeful speed of roots finding water. In seconds the valley floor was laced with it — a web of pale light so dense that even Maren, who had never seen the System's interface overlay, could see individual filaments branching and connecting, building a visible architecture across the ground.

The contamination followed. It spiked from the dungeon mouths in a wave — Maren smelled it before he felt it, the ozone-sweet-rot smell going acrid and caustic, the kind of contamination density that Thessa had told him could blister exposed skin in minutes. The air turned thick with it. Breathing went from automatic to effortful, each inhale carrying a metallic weight that sat in his chest like a coin placed on a sleeping man's sternum.

And then the dungeon mouths opened.

Not literally — they were already open, had always been open, the dark holes in the valley's hillsides that breathed contamination and spawned quests. What happened was worse. The nearest mouth, forty yards north, began producing shapes. They emerged from the darkness the way condensation appears on glass — gradual, then suddenly present, then impossible to ignore. Maren couldn't see their System data. He saw their bodies, and their bodies were wrong. Too many joints. Limbs that bent at angles no living skeleton supported, moving with the jerky, over-precise gait of something that had been designed by a system that understood anatomy as a structural diagram rather than a living process. They were the size of large dogs and they glowed faintly from within, the same cold light as the quest-filaments crawling across the ground.

Threat creatures. System-generated. The antibodies of an immune response, spawned to neutralize everything in the reclassified zone.

The first one moved toward Maren. Then stopped. Its malformed head — if it was a head — turned toward the northwest ridge, where heat signatures that the System could actually read were broadcasting their positions to anything with targeting access.

*They are not prioritizing you.* Aldric, quiet. Almost wondering. *The entity — you are not registering as a classified target. The creatures are targeting everything they can read. You are a blank.*

"And Caul's people?"

*They are the loudest signatures in the valley.*

From the ridge, Maren heard Caul's voice. Not the measured field-commander tone of earlier. Louder, rawer, the voice of a man whose professional composure had just been punctured by something he could see on his interface and could not reconcile with everything he had believed. "Fall back! Fall back to the ridge — all units, disengage the target, we have—" A sound that might have been a weapon striking something that wasn't flesh and wasn't stone. "What is this? This isn't standard. These aren't — the System is—"

The polearm woman screamed. Not a combat shout — the raw, involuntary sound of someone being hit by something she hadn't seen coming, something her System-enhanced reflexes should have warned her about but hadn't because the reclassification had rewritten the threat parameters and her own interface was telling her the thing killing her was authorized.

*She's down.* Vess, steady and distant. *The creatures reached her before she cleared the combat zone. The polearm — they are dissolving the polearm. The material is—* A pause. *System-forged equipment is not resistant to System-generated threat creatures. There is a logic to that.*

A logic. Maren got to his feet. His body was wrong — too heavy, the consumed contamination distributed through him in a way that shifted his center of gravity and made each step feel like walking through knee-deep water. The cracked ceramic bowl lay in the dead soil at his feet. The crack ran from rim to base, a clean split through the glaze. He picked it up. His fingers were numb, thick, contamination-clumsy, and the shard that had bitten into his kneecap had left a smear of blood on his trouser leg that he noticed without caring about.

More creatures were emerging. From the nearest mouth, from the second mouth to the south, and from the faint glow of the third mouth farther along the valley where Thessa and Kael had fled. The spawning was accelerating. Ten. Twenty. The valley floor crawled with their wrong-jointed movement, each one trailing faint quest-light like a snail trails slime.

From the ridge, the young dual-blade fighter was making a sound that Maren recognized. He'd made the same sound weeks ago, the first time his body killed a man without his permission — the raw, unprocessed noise a person makes when the framework they trusted to protect them reveals that it never had. The boy was crying. Actual crying, heaving sobs between the clang of his blades against things that shouldn't exist, fighting creatures sent by the very System his training had taught him to serve.

Caul's voice cut through. Not orders this time. A single word, repeated twice, directed at something he could see on his interface that Maren never would.

"No."

And then, louder: "*No.*"

*He's reading the reclassification order.* Aldric said it quietly. The formality was gone, stripped away by what he was watching through Maren's ears and the System data flooding his inherited interface. His voice sounded the way it had when he'd first described the System's architecture — the containment wall, the edges — the voice of a man confronting a truth he'd spent his career suspecting and had organized his entire identity around not confirming. *He can see that the threat creatures were generated in response to the consumption event. He can see that the targeting parameters include his own party. He can see the System weighed the cost of four loyal heroes against the cost of allowing the unclassified entity to persist, and—*

*He can see that the System chose.* Vess finished it. Flat. Final.

Caul stopped fighting Maren's direction. On the ridge, Maren could hear him — the sound of his sword against the creatures' strange bodies, the grunt of effort, the boy's sobbing — fighting the things the System had sent to maintain the integrity of a classification that could not accommodate what Maren was.

Thessa appeared at his side. Maren hadn't heard her approach. She moved through contaminated terrain with the silence of someone who had spent three decades learning where every bad patch and unstable pocket lay, and even in a valley that had just been remade by the System's immune response, her feet found the solid ground. Her face was grey in the harsh quest-light, the deep-set lines around her mouth carved deeper by the shadows. Her stained hands held a clay jar — one of Errin's jars, Maren realized, retrieved from the dead woman's scattered collection.

She uncapped it and poured the contents across the ground between them and the nearest threat creature. The dark, thick residue of years of careful collection spread across the contaminated soil and the creature's jointed legs found no traction on it. The thing skidded, stumbled forward onto too many elbows, and Thessa kicked it sideways with the flat of her boot — a practiced motion, not a combat move, the motion of a woman who'd spent decades moving contamination from where it was dangerous to where it was useful — and the creature slid into a puddle of concentrated residue where its body began dissolving, the quest-light inside it guttering like a candle in water.

"I have three jars left." Thessa's voice was steady. The steadiness of someone who had been dealing with impossible work for thirty years and who understood that panic was a luxury the profession didn't offer. "That'll buy us four minutes, maybe five. The third mouth is spawning too, but the contamination there is older — older residue dissolves the creatures faster."

"How many got out?"

"Kael. Maybe Renna — I didn't see." She said the next part without changing her tone, the way she'd say the next contamination site was twelve miles east. "Errin's dead. Paran's dead. Goss is dead. The caster got Goss with a System pulse before the reclassification. The other two were Caul's arrows."

Three sin-eaters. Two hundred and nine notches on Goss's leather strip, lying in the dirt somewhere behind them. Errin's thirty years of sample collection, shattered in clay shards. Paran's wife who folded his cloth strips with matching edges, who did not know yet and would not know for days.

Maren did not say *I'm sorry*. The words existed in his mouth and he swallowed them because they were insufficient and Thessa would know they were insufficient and saying them would cost time they didn't have.

"Can we get past the third mouth?"

"Through the heavy contamination on the south slope. The older residue will cover our heat signatures — the creatures track by System-readable data, and the old contamination generates enough noise to mask us." She was already moving, pouring residue from a second jar as she walked, laying a path of concentrated contamination that the creatures had to route around. "Stay behind me. Step where I step. Don't touch the quest-light filaments — they're mapping the valley's architecture and they'll tag anything biological that contacts them."

Maren followed. His legs felt like they belonged to someone else — heavy, unresponsive, the consumed contamination making his movements thick and approximate. The entity pressed outward inside him with each step, pulsing against the boundaries of his consciousness, and he held it through something that wasn't skill or training but the brute stubbornness of a man who had spent eleven years maintaining a lie and had at least that much practice in keeping the inside from showing on the outside.

Behind them, the valley was unrecognizable. Quest-light covered the ground like luminous frost. Threat creatures moved through it with their wrong-jointed gait, converging on the ridge where Caul and his surviving party member fought. The contamination smell was so thick Maren's eyes streamed. The hum was a physical thing now, a pressure against his eardrums that made his balance worse and turned every step into a negotiation between his body and the ground.

*Caul has disengaged from pursuit.* Vess reported it the way she reported everything — without inflection, without judgment, as data. *He is defending the surviving hero. The caster is incapacitated. He is — he is not retreating. He is holding position on the ridge.*

Maren stopped. He was at the base of the south slope, where the contaminated valley floor began to rise toward the third dungeon mouth. Thessa was five yards ahead, pouring the third jar. Below and behind him, the valley seethed with cold light and the chittering movement of things that shouldn't exist.

He could hear Caul's sword. The steady rhythm of it — a man trained in long-endurance combat, efficient with his strikes, conserving energy. Fighting something his System told him was authorized. Fighting it anyway, because the alternative was letting it kill the crying boy behind him.

*He could leave.* Aldric said it softly. The tone was not admiration or contempt. It was the tone of a man watching someone else reach the conclusion he had reached months ago and finding no satisfaction in the confirmation. *His interface is showing him the evacuation route. The reclassification zone has a perimeter. He could reach it in three minutes if he ran.*

"He won't leave the boy."

*No.* A pause that was almost, but not quite, a formal silence. *He will not.*

Thessa had turned back. She stood on the slope with the empty jar in one stained hand, looking down at Maren with an expression he couldn't read in the grey light. The quest-light filaments cast strange shadows on her angular face, making her look older, or maybe she just looked her age for the first time since he'd met her — fifty-five years of thankless work in a valley full of the System's waste.

"Move," she said. "There's nothing you can do for him."

She was right. Maren knew she was right. He carried no combat class, no hero designation, no System tools that could help a man fighting on a ridge. He carried a cracked ceramic bowl and an entity that the System treated as an infection site and the borrowed muscle memory of two dead people who could not help Caul any more than Maren could.

He climbed. The south slope was steep and the contamination underfoot was old — Thessa had been right about that too. The residue here was decades-deep, structural, woven into the soil itself, and it hummed at a lower, older frequency than the fresh contamination the System was pumping into the valley floor. Maren's boots slipped on the contaminated ground and the smell changed as they climbed — from the sharp ozone-metal of the reclassification to something earthier, deeper, the smell of contamination that had been part of the landscape for so long it had begun to smell like dirt.

Thessa led without looking back. Her long stride ate the slope and her footing never faltered. She knew this terrain the way Maren knew the social architecture of a village — every pocket and pressure point mapped through decades of repetition. Even now, even with the valley remade beneath them, her body navigated the contamination by memory and instinct.

They crested the ridge. Below them on the far side, the slope dropped into scrub and thin forest, the trees stunted by contamination but alive, their leaves beginning to turn with the early autumn. The air was cooler here, cleaner, the System's reclassification perimeter ending somewhere on the ridgeline behind them.

Maren turned back. From the ridge he could see the whole valley — the Crook, or what had been the Crook. The camp was gone, buried under quest-light filaments. The lean-tos were shapes in the glow. The bodies of the dead sin-eaters were somewhere down there, covered in the same cold light that covered everything.

On the northwest ridge, a single figure was still fighting. Maren couldn't see the details — his eyes were wrong, watering from contamination, and the distance was too far. But he could see the movement, the trained economy of a man who knew how to hold a position, and he could see the smaller shape behind the man, crouched, no longer fighting.

*The boy is alive.* Aldric. Quiet. *Caul is holding the ridge. The creatures are — they are slowing. The reclassification appears to be concentrating on the valley floor rather than the high ground. If Caul can maintain his position for another few minutes, the spawning rate should—*

"Should what?"

*Should normalize. The System does not sustain maximum response indefinitely. It — it corrects, and then it settles. Like a fever.*

The entity pulsed inside Maren. Enormous, restless, fed by what he'd consumed, pressing against his ribs and his skull and the spaces between his thoughts. He held it. He didn't know how. He didn't know how long.

Thessa stood beside him on the ridge, looking down at the valley with an expression Maren now recognized. He'd seen it in the mirror, years ago, the first time he'd understood that the villages he'd been pretending to serve were living in contamination he'd left behind. The expression of someone watching the cost of what they are.

"Three of them," he said. "Because of me."

"Yes." Thessa didn't soften it. She didn't qualify, didn't contextualize, didn't offer the kind of structural absolution that would let him slide the weight sideways. She said *yes*, because it was true, and then she said nothing else, because nothing else was required.

Below them, the valley hummed. The quest-light pulsed in slow waves, mapping and remapping the contaminated ground. The threat creatures circled the camp's remains with their wrong-jointed gait, looking for heat signatures to read. Caul's sword caught the early-autumn light on the far ridge — a small, bright movement in a landscape that the System had remade in its own image.

The last thing Maren heard before they descended the far slope was Caul's voice, distant and wrecked and clear, speaking not to his surviving party member or to Maren or to the creatures but to the interface itself, the System that had been the architecture of his grief and his duty and his faith.

"You did this." Quiet enough that Maren shouldn't have been able to hear it, but the valley's acoustics carried sound the way its soil carried contamination — faithfully, indifferently, without regard for who was meant to receive it. "You sent us here and then you — you did this."

The hum swallowed the rest. Maren turned and followed Thessa down the far side of the ridge, into the thin trees where the contamination was old and quiet and the autumn light came through the stunted canopy in long, low shafts that didn't warm anything they touched.
