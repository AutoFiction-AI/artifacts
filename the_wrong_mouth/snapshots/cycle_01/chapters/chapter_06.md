# Chapter 6

The bread sat in Maren's hand like a stone. Yesterday's bread — the crust had gone the color of old bark and the inside was dense and cold, the kind of cold that comes from being carried against a body all night and still not warming. Derry had torn off the worst of the heel, the part that had gone greenish where the cloth had been damp, and handed the rest over without looking at Maren's face.

They were sitting by a stream maybe half a mile off the road, in a spot where the bank had been cut back by spring flooding and then left alone. Birch roots clawed out of the exposed dirt, pale as tendons. The water was shallow and moved without much purpose, collecting in a flat pool before finding its way through a gap in the rocks and continuing. Late summer light came through the canopy in uneven pieces. It was a good place to stop. It would have been a good place to eat.

Maren brought the bread to his mouth and his throat closed.

Not dramatically — no gagging, no heave. Just a quiet, total refusal. The muscles in his neck went tight and his jaw locked and the bread touched his lips and his body said no. The smell of it — yeast and flour and the faint sourness of age — hit his stomach and his stomach moved in a slow, oily roll and he put the bread back down on his knee.

His hands were still shaking. Not much. A fine tremor, the kind you had to look for, the kind you could hide by pressing your palms flat against your thighs. He'd been doing that since yesterday, pressing his hands into things. The bark of a tree when they'd stopped to piss. The fabric of his bag when they walked. His own knees, now, the bread balanced on top of his left thigh, untouched.

*You should eat,* Aldric said.

The voice was flat. Not the usual delivery — no lecture underneath, no implicit comparison to the dietary habits of men who had served the Order with discipline and purpose. Just the statement.

Maren didn't answer.

*Your body requires fuel. The tremor will worsen without caloric —*

*He knows,* Vess said. *Leave it.*

A silence inside his skull. The two of them pulling back to their separate corners, or whatever the equivalent was when three people occupied the same head. Maren could feel them like pressure changes — Aldric's disapproval a dull weight behind his right eye, Vess's watchfulness a cooler thing, lower, settling somewhere near the base of his skull where his neck met his shoulders.

Derry was eating. Methodically, without apparent enjoyment, tearing pieces off his half and putting them in his mouth and chewing and swallowing. His jaw worked with the steady, mechanical patience of a man who had spent his life doing necessary things without passion. He sat with his legs stretched toward the water, his boots unlaced, the sweat stains on his shirt dried to pale maps of salt. His face was red from the walking and from the heat and from something else that Maren would not look at directly.

"Pell counts everything now," Derry said.

Maren looked at him.

"She's — I don't know when it started, but it's everything. The steps to the door. The chickens. She counted my buttons the other morning, stood there in her nightshirt and pointed at each one and said the number." He tore another piece of bread. "She gets stuck on seven. She can do one through six and then she says seven and then she says seven again and then she says eight. But the second seven — she knows it's wrong, you can see it on her face, she just can't —" He chewed. Swallowed. "Lenna thinks it's because I taught her the song wrong. The counting song. I probably did. I can't remember which one has seven in a different — anyway."

"That's good," Maren said. His voice came out strange. Thin, like he was talking through cloth.

"Yeah." Derry pulled at the bread. "And the garden — you know Lenna tried herbs this year? Not the useful ones, not the ones you cook with. The other kind, the ones the apothecary buys. She read somewhere that fennel grows in anything, which is — I mean, I could've told her, fennel's a weed where I'm from, my mother couldn't keep it out of the onions. But Lenna planted it in that little bed by the south wall, the one that gets no sun because of the shed, and she was so — she had this idea about selling it at the Silt market. Dried fennel. Three coppers a bundle."

Maren watched Derry's hands on the bread. The way he held it was the way he held everything — firm, careful, the grip of a man who'd spent his life picking things up and putting them down. Turnips. Fence posts. His daughter.

"It didn't grow," Derry said. "Or it grew about an inch and then just sort of — stopped. She'd go out every morning and look at it. Just stand there. And I didn't say anything because I'm not — I mean, you know Lenna, she doesn't want to hear it from me." He ate. "But she's going to try tomatoes next year. She told me before I — before we left. She said, 'When you get back, I'll have tomatoes.' Like it was a promise she was making, but to the garden, not to me."

*His wife is growing produce in shade,* Aldric said. *That is not going to —*

*Shut up,* Vess said.

The stream made a sound like someone running their thumb along the edge of a bowl. Maren listened to it and to Derry's chewing and to the nothing that was happening inside his own body where hunger should have been. He could feel the absence of appetite like a shape — a hollow where the mechanism of eating normally sat, gummed up and stuck.

"And the cow," Derry said. He said it the way he always said it, with the particular exhausted affection of a man who has been bested by livestock. "She got out again. The day before we — the day before. I spent an hour and a half walking her back from Garron's field. An hour and a half. She doesn't run, that's the thing. She just walks. Slowly. In the wrong direction. With this look on her face like she's — like she's doing it on purpose but she wants you to know she doesn't care if you know."

Maren almost laughed. It came out as a breath through his nose, a small convulsion that died somewhere in his chest, but Derry heard it and glanced at him and something in Derry's face opened a crack. Not a smile. An easing.

"I reinforced the fence three times," Derry said. "Three times, Maren. And she just — she finds the one spot. Every time. Like she's testing the whole fence with her face and she just knows where the —"

He stopped talking. He was looking at Maren's untouched bread.

"You should eat," he said.

"I know."

"You really should."

"I know. I'm going to."

Derry didn't push it. He went back to his own bread and for a while they sat there and the stream moved and the light shifted in the canopy and the day happened around them the way days do when nothing is required and nothing is resolved. A bird did something in the birches — Maren couldn't see it, just heard the branch bounce and resettle. The air smelled like warm dirt and water and, underneath that, the faint sweetish ozone that meant quest-light was somewhere nearby. He'd smelled it his whole life and never known what it was until Aldric told him, four days ago, in a voice that made the information sound like an indictment.

*We need to discuss direction,* Aldric said.

Maren closed his eyes.

*The main road is untenable. We were visible leaving the road yesterday and anyone who finds the — who finds what happened will have a trajectory. There is a route through Selwick Pass that avoids the main road for approximately —*

*Wrong,* Vess said. *Selwick narrows at the bridge. One approach, no cover. Any tracker with half a class would position there.*

*I am not suggesting we linger at the bridge. I am suggesting the pass itself provides —*

*Terrain advantage doesn't mean anything when he can't fight.*

The word sat in Maren's skull. Fight. What had happened yesterday — his body moving without him, the dead hero's weight pulling at Maren's blade as Maren's arm, which was Aldric's arm, which was nobody's arm, drew it free — that wasn't fighting. He didn't know what it was. An event that had happened inside his body while he watched from somewhere behind his own eyes.

*He fought adequately,* Aldric said, and the correction in his tone was careful, almost gentle, which was worse than his usual disdain. *The combat instincts are sound. With practice —*

*He doesn't need practice. He needs to not be in situations where combat is the variable. We go east, stay off the roads, move at night when the interface is harder to read at distance. We find another quest pool. He eats it. We see what happens.*

*And if what happens is another fragment? Another consciousness? At what point does the accumulation —*

*That's not how it works.*

*You do not know how it works. None of us knows how it works. That is precisely my —*

*Your point is that we should understand before we act. My point is that understanding is a luxury he doesn't have. There are heroes behind us who aren't going to pause for his moral education.*

Maren opened his eyes. The stream was still there. The birch roots were still there. Derry was lacing his boots back up, his thick fingers working the leather through the eyelets with the patience of a man who laced boots every morning of his life and would lace them every morning until he was dead.

"Hey," Maren said.

Derry looked up.

"Tell me about Pell again."

Something happened in Derry's face — a complicated thing, several expressions arriving at once and then settling into one that Maren couldn't have named if pressed. Gratitude, maybe. Or just the relief of being asked to talk about something that was real and good and had nothing to do with any of this.

"She's doing this thing now," Derry said, "where she puts all her shoes in a line by the door. Not just her shoes. Everyone's shoes. She'll take mine and Lenna's and line them up smallest to biggest, and if you move one she'll put it back. She's very — she has opinions about the order. Strong opinions." He pulled the lace tight. "She's three. She's three years old and she has opinions about shoes."

Inside Maren's skull, the argument continued — Aldric making a case for finding the sin-eater community at the Crook, where proper practitioners could examine the unclassified entity and possibly stabilize it; Vess countering that approaching a gathering of sin-eaters with a bounty that size was painting a target on every practitioner in the valley. They were both right. That was the problem. Aldric's version of right was moral and long-term and required Maren to submit to examination by people who might decide the safest option was to consume him. Vess's version of right was tactical and immediate and required Maren to keep eating quests until he was strong enough that the bounty stopped mattering, which might never happen, or might make him something worse than what he already was.

He let them argue. He watched Derry lace his boots and he listened to the part about Pell and the shoes and he held the bread on his knee and he tried, again, to want to eat it.

His hands were still shaking.

---

The quest-light was in the water.

Maren saw it the way he always saw it — a faint shimmer, like heat haze, except the air wasn't hot enough and the shimmer moved against the current rather than with it. It pooled in the flat stretch of the stream where the water slowed, collecting in the still places behind rocks and in the eddies where fallen leaves turned in slow circles. To Maren it looked like moonlight would look if moonlight had weight and opinions about where to gather. He'd seen it a hundred times. A thousand. In ditches outside dungeon mouths, in the corners of rooms where someone had died, in the air above the ceramic bowl when he'd performed his fake rituals and nothing had answered.

He was standing at the edge of the stream because he'd gone to wash his hands and then forgotten to wash them. The water moved over his boots — he'd stepped in without thinking, and the cold was a clean, specific thing against his ankles, and he'd just stopped. The light was doing something in the pool. Twisting. Catching on itself.

"There's quest-light in the water," he said. Quietly, because Derry had gone to refill the water skins upstream and Maren wasn't really talking to Derry.

*I see it,* Aldric said.

A pause. Usually the reading came fast — the paladin's narration of what the interface laid over the world, the data that everyone else saw and Maren never had, delivered with the clipped rhythm of a man cataloguing someone else's mess. Threat assessment, residual quest class, contamination grade, probability of hostile emergence. The information had been useful and humiliating in roughly equal measure.

But Aldric didn't start reading.

*It is a residual pool,* he said. *Low-grade. Passive. No active quest structure, no threat emergence within — I would estimate none.* Another pause, longer. *The light is refracting through the water in a way that — I have not seen this particular configuration before. The interface shows standard residual markers, but the visual phenomenon is — separate from the data. The data says it is ordinary. The light suggests otherwise.*

Maren watched the shimmer. It moved the way light moves through glass that isn't quite flat — bending, stretching, pulling itself into shapes that had no name and didn't need one. Where it caught on the surface tension of the water it made something he could almost call beautiful, except that word had never had much use in his vocabulary and he didn't trust it now.

*The way it moves,* Aldric said, and his voice was different. Quieter. Stripped of the cadence he used for reports and corrections and the careful articulation of his ongoing disappointment. *It looks as though the light is trying to become something. Not a quest, not a threat. Something that does not have a classification. It simply moves through the water and it is — it is quite —*

He stopped.

Maren stood in the stream and the cold water ran over his boots and the quest-light turned in the pool and for a moment neither of them said anything. Not the loaded silence of disagreement or the pointed silence of withholding. Just quiet. The two of them — the con man and the dead paladin — looking at the same thing from different sides of the same skull, and the thing being, for once, not a problem to solve or a danger to assess or a failure to name.

The light twisted. It caught something in the rocks — a vein of quartz, maybe, or just a wet edge — and flared briefly, a pulse of warmth that Maren felt on his face the way you feel a candle when someone opens a door.

*It is beautiful,* Aldric said.

He said it the way he said things that cost him: completely, without qualification, and then not again.

Vess said nothing. Whether she could see through Maren's eyes the way Aldric described seeing, Maren didn't know. She hadn't offered, and he hadn't asked. Her silence now was neither agreement nor dismissal. It was the silence of someone watching someone else have a private moment and choosing not to be in it.

Maren stood in the water until his feet went numb. The light continued its slow, purposeless work in the pool. Upstream, he could hear Derry filling the water skins, the leather gulping and then the sound of him shaking the last drops off and capping them. Ordinary sounds. The sounds of a man doing a small task well because it was the task in front of him.

He looked down at his hands. They were in the water now — he'd let them hang at his sides and the stream had taken them, the cold wrapping around his fingers, his wrists, pulling the heat out of him in a way that felt less like discomfort and more like an offer. His hands were still shaking, but the water made the shaking harder to see, and that was enough. That was something.

He bent down and scooped water to his mouth and drank. It tasted like stone and cold and the faint sweet chemical edge of whatever quest-light left behind when it sat in water long enough. He drank again. His throat opened for the water the way it hadn't opened for the bread. It wasn't food, but it was something going in instead of everything coming up, and his body accepted it with the desperate, uncritical gratitude of a system that had been locked for a full day.

When he walked back to where they'd been sitting, Derry was repacking the bag. The bread was still on the ground where Maren had left it. Derry looked at it, looked at Maren, and put it in the bag without comment.

"East, I think," Maren said. "Off the road. Vess says there's better cover."

Derry nodded. He didn't ask who Vess was. He'd stopped asking about the voices after the second day, the way you stop asking about a neighbor's dog that barks all night — not because you've accepted it, but because the asking doesn't change anything.

*Acceptable,* Vess said.

*I did not agree to east,* Aldric said.

*You don't have legs,* Vess said. *You agreed when he did.*

They walked. The stream fell behind them and the quest-light stayed where it was, turning in the water, becoming nothing, meaning nothing, and Maren thought about it for a long time after they'd gone — the way it had looked, the way Aldric's voice had sounded saying the word beautiful, as if the word were something he'd put away in a drawer years ago and had just found again, surprised that it still fit in his mouth.

Derry talked as they walked. About the cow, again — a different escape, this time involving a neighbor's cart and a turnip field and a complicated chain of events that ended with Derry standing in the rain holding a rope attached to nothing because the cow had slipped the knot. About Lenna's plans for the front room, which involved moving a wall that Derry had built with his own hands three years ago and which he maintained was structurally important and which Lenna maintained was in the way. About Pell's new word, which was "no," and which she deployed with the conviction and frequency of someone who had just discovered the concept of refusal.

Maren listened. He let Derry's voice fill the space that the bread couldn't and the argument in his skull wouldn't, and he walked and he listened and the day went on and nothing happened and nothing was resolved and they were still alive, which was the only thing that mattered and the thing that mattered least.
