# Chapter 8

The road past Silt smelled different. Fresh contamination had a sharp ozone-sweetness Maren knew well. He'd walked through enough of it faking his way across Fenhold's farmland. This was older, settled into the dirt like grease in a cookpot. Decay with a sweetness underneath, and under that a mineral flatness that stuck to the roof of his mouth. He breathed through it because there was no alternative. The road went forward and the smell went with it.

Scorch marks darkened the road stones every few hundred paces now. Some old enough that grass had crept into the blackened edges, others fresh — the surface still glossy, still faintly warm underfoot in the afternoon heat. Heroes had been through here. Were through here regularly, if the layering was anything to go by. Maren stepped around the marks without thinking about it, the way you step around puddles. The hum was louder out here, a low steady drone that sat behind his teeth and wouldn't leave.

Derry walked ten paces ahead, his pack hitched too high on his shoulders. He'd been adjusting the straps since they'd left the outskirts of Silt — pulling at the leather, letting it go, pulling again — and Maren recognized the gesture. It was the thing Derry did when he wanted to talk but hadn't decided what to say yet. The strap adjustment was a placeholder for conversation. It meant he was thinking about Pell, or Lenna, or the cow, or all three, and wasn't sure Maren could handle hearing about any of them right now.

He was probably right.

*Your pace has dropped,* Aldric said. *We are losing daylight.*

Maren didn't answer. He was watching the quest-light in the ditch — a thin sheet of pale luminescence pooled in the low ground the way rainwater pooled after a storm, except rainwater didn't glow and wasn't warm. He could see it even without the interface. Everyone could. The difference was that the System-sighted saw it with data overlaid — quest details, threat levels, completion percentages — and Maren saw the raw thing. A soft, faintly golden shimmer, like light caught in shallow water. It was beautiful if you didn't know what it meant.

He'd been thinking about Thessa. The insults had landed where they belonged, heavy and specific and earned. Eleven years of contamination in named villages, children growing up in the residue of quests he'd only pretended to clear. That weight sat where it sat. He wasn't going to put it down.

The other part, though. The warning. Unclassified entities didn't last. The System corrected them. Sixty years since the last one, and nothing left of the host. Thessa had said it the way she said everything — clinically, as if the fact of his approaching dissolution was a drainage problem and she was describing the pipe.

Which meant whatever he was carrying was on the same clock he was.

"Vess."

No response. That was standard. She treated the first use of her name as a notification, not a summons. She'd respond when she was ready, or when the silence had lasted long enough to constitute acknowledgment.

"The contamination in that hollow. The quest I — the one that brought you in."

*What about it.*

"What was it? The quest itself. Before it went bad, before it pooled — what was the quest?"

The pause lasted six steps. Maren counted them. Vess's pauses were measured things — assessment, the silence of someone deciding how much information to declassify.

*A bounty quest,* she said. *Anchored to the holdings of a minor lord. Torren Blackhall. He held land east of the Silt road, maybe two days' walk from where your hollow was. He'd purchased quest access for the territory around his estate.*

"Purchased."

*Landholders can buy quest generation for their territory. The System creates quests, heroes come through, spend money, generate trade. Blackhall's holdings were close enough to the dungeon approaches that the purchase was cheap. Standard practice for minor nobility.*

Maren didn't know that. Eleven years of performing a System role and half the basic mechanics were news to him. The fraud had been so thorough that its gaps were showing up now like cracks in plaster — everywhere, and all at once.

"So the quest was his. Generated on his land. And it went bad?"

*It didn't go bad. He used it.*

"Used it how?"

*There were tenant villages on the eastern edge of his holdings. Forty or fifty families, mostly agricultural labor. Timber workers, some of them. They'd disputed his cutting rights for two growing seasons. There was a boundary disagreement — whose forest, whose trees, whose profit. The kind of thing that goes to a magistrate in most holdings. Blackhall didn't take it to a magistrate.*

Derry had stopped ahead, waiting where the road bent around a stand of scrub oak. His silhouette was a stocky question mark against the yellowing sky.

"You coming?" he called.

"Yeah. Just — give me a minute."

"You're talking to them again."

"Yeah."

Derry nodded. He sat down on a flat stone by the road's edge and pulled his water skin from his pack. He drank, wiped his mouth, drank again. He didn't ask what the voices were saying. He'd stopped asking after Silt.

Maren kept walking, slower now, closing the gap between them but not all the way. Close enough that Derry could see him. Far enough that talking to himself looked like mumbling instead of conversation.

"Blackhall killed them," he said to Vess. Not a question.

*He hired mercenaries. A company of eight. They went through the eastern villages over three nights. The quest activity on his land generated enough System noise — bounty notifications, threat assessments, the normal chatter of an active quest zone — to mask the contamination signatures of what happened. Anyone checking the area afterward would read the deaths as quest casualties. Dungeon-adjacent attrition. Common enough that no one investigates.*

The words arrived in Vess's usual register — flat, precise, billing by the syllable. She could have been describing supply routes or weather patterns. That was her voice. The voice of a woman who'd been trained to separate information from feeling, to deliver operational data clean.

"Fifty families."

*Approximately. The contract didn't specify an exact count. I wasn't hired for the aftermath. I was hired for Blackhall.*

"The Nightguild."

*Yes.*

"You were going to kill him."

*I was going to complete the contract. A client in one of the surviving settlements had reached the guild through the standard channels. Payment was adequate. The target was accessible. I had his schedule within a week of deployment. I had the estate mapped in ten days. The purchased quest gave me operational cover — I could move through the contamination zone without triggering secondary System alerts because the zone was already noisy with quest activity. The same trick Blackhall used to hide the killing. His own purchased cover, turned against him.*

There was something in the cadence that Maren couldn't place. Vess's voice was as flat as ever, but the sentence had a structural wrongness, like something built with load-bearing walls and hollow ones mixed together.

"What happened?"

*I was inside the quest's operational radius. Moving through the contamination perimeter at the edge of the estate. Two days from completion. The approach was clean. Everything was — the approach was clean.* A pause that wasn't tactical. *And then I was inside you. The interval between those two states isn't present. I don't remember the consumption.*

"A gap."

*The memory isn't degraded. It's absent. The distinction matters. Degraded memory is fog — I had a head injury at fifteen, lost three weeks of training, and those weeks feel like looking through dirty glass. The details are present but distorted. This isn't that. This is a room that's been emptied.*

Maren reached the bend in the road. Derry was still sitting on his stone, water skin balanced on his knee, watching a beetle navigate the road dust between his boots. He glanced up when Maren stopped.

"Sausage?" Derry said. He held up a leathery coil. "Got it in Silt. Vendor charged too much but it's not bad if you pull the casing off."

"Maybe later."

"You look like you're about to be sick."

"I'm fine."

"That's what you said before the ditch outside Fenhold."

"Different ditch."

Derry didn't push. He put the sausage back in his pack and went back to watching the beetle. It had reached a scorch mark and was navigating the glossy black edge with the careful deliberation of something that didn't understand the obstacle but was going to get past it anyway.

Maren sat down on the road's edge, close enough to Derry that the silence between them was companionable rather than strained. The quest-light in the ditch beside him glowed faintly. The hum droned. The sun was low enough that the scrub oak cast long shadows across the road, and in the shadows the scorch marks were invisible, which was a small, stupid mercy.

"Vess."

*Yes.*

"You had a sister."

The silence that followed snagged hard. It had weight in it, real resistance. Vess had hit something she couldn't step around.

He hadn't planned to say it. It came from a conversation days earlier — one of the fragments' arguments, Vess and Aldric sniping at each other while Maren tried to eat, and she'd said something sharp about the Order of the Veil and then, in the same flat register, had mentioned a sister. One word. Then she'd moved on. Maren had filed it automatically — the con man's reflex, cataloguing every detail because detail was currency — and now the word had come out of him like something he'd been holding in his mouth.

*I had a sister.* A beat. *Have. Had. The tense is unclear.*

"What was her name?"

*I —*

The pause stretched past anything Vess usually allowed.

*It starts with — a. Or — an open sound. Two syllables. The first one opens and the second —* She stopped. Started again. *I said it every day. I knew it better than Blackhall's name. It isn't here.*

Maren's hands were flat on the warm road stone. He pressed them harder against the surface. The stone was rough, gritty, real.

*I can give you the distance from Blackhall's bedroom to the servants' staircase,* Vess said. *Fourteen feet. I can give you his steward's name and his breakfast preferences and the rotation schedule for his perimeter guard. I can tell you how long to keep a wire tight after a man stops kicking, because stopping and dead aren't the same thing. All of that is here. Complete. My sister's name isn't.*

*She is describing selective absence,* Aldric said. His voice had gone stiff — stiffer than his usual formality, which for Aldric meant the architecture had gone load-bearing. He was processing.

"I didn't ask you."

*No. But the pattern is relevant.*

"In a minute."

"Vess. The massacre. When you — when you talk about the families. The fifty families. You don't sound —"

*Angry. I know. The contract flagged personal motivation as a risk factor. I accepted it anyway. Below-rate fee. This one was personal. The villages were —*

She stopped hard. The sentence hit a wall and died there.

*I can feel it start,* she said. *The beginning of the feeling is there. Then it cuts off. I took the contract for a reason I can document and can't feel.*

The beetle had reached the other side of the scorch mark. Derry was still watching it, seemingly absorbed, but his jaw was set in the way it set when he was pretending not to listen to something that frightened him.

Maren breathed. The contamination smell was in his mouth and in his throat and the hum was steady and the quest-light in the ditch glowed its soft, indifferent gold.

Maren had spent days listening for what Vess could do in a fight. It took this to hear what had been done to her.

*I have them too,* Aldric said. Quiet. Formal. The voice of a man filing a report he didn't want to file. *The gaps.*

---

They camped in a hollow off the road where the scrub oak thinned enough to see the sky. Derry built the fire — small, careful, the fire of a man who'd been sleeping outdoors for a week and had gotten efficient about it. He cut sausage with his belt knife, ate slowly, stared at the flames with the particular distance of someone whose thoughts were two hundred miles away and centered on a girl who was learning to count.

He offered Maren a piece. Maren took it. The casing was tough and the meat was too salty and it was fine.

"Tomorrow's worse," Derry said. "More patrols. Some of the roads have checkpoints, or they used to — my cousin went through here four years ago and said there were heroes just sitting at the crossroads, scanning everyone who passed."

"Scanning."

"Checking profiles. Class, level, party affiliation. The whole thing. And you've got —"

"Nothing."

"I was going to say a situation." Derry cut another piece of sausage and inspected it in the firelight. "You've got a situation."

"I've got a situation."

"Right. So tomorrow, you're the cousin. Quiet. Head injury. I'll — we'll figure it out." He ate the sausage. "Lenna always said I was a bad liar but honestly I think I'm all right when it's — you know. When it matters."

"You're a terrible liar."

"Yeah." He almost smiled. "Yeah, I know."

The fire popped. Something moved in the scrub oak — an animal, probably, or the wind rearranging branches — and Maren felt Vess's tactical assessment ripple through his awareness, a brief sharp scan that catalogued the direction, distance, and threat probability of the sound before dismissing it. Automatic. A reflex from a woman who couldn't feel her own anger but could calculate threat vectors in her sleep.

They sat without talking. Derry finished his sausage, wiped his knife on his trousers, folded it, and put it in his pocket. He leaned back against his pack and closed his eyes. Not sleeping yet — Maren could tell from his breathing — but making the shape of sleep, the way you make the shape of something when you want it to come.

"Aldric."

*Yes.*

"The gaps."

The pause was long enough that Maren almost spoke again. But Aldric's pauses were construction. He was building the answer to his own specifications before he'd release it.

*My service record is complete,* he said. *Fourteen years with the Order of the Veil. I can provide the name of every member of my cohort, every deployment, every site investigation. My training in System theory is intact. The research Commander Dellan authorized — the investigation into structural anomalies in the System's deeper architecture — I remember the commission, the scope, the findings.*

"But."

*I remember the dungeon. The one where I died. I remember the briefing, such as it was. The approach march. The entry. The first chamber — standard layout, moderate contamination, residual quest activity from a previous clearing. I remember the hum there. It was different. Lower, more sustained. A harmonic I had not heard in any previous site.*

He paused again. When he continued, his syntax was tighter, more clipped, the formal architecture contracting under weight.

*I remember reaching the second chamber. The wall. Then the stone ceased to be stone and the System's architecture showed through. Bare structure beneath the interface. The frame under the overlaid data. I remember seeing it. I remember that I understood something about what I was seeing. I remember the understanding arriving, the shape of it, the magnitude. It was —*

He stopped.

Aldric completed every sentence. It was a defining feature of his voice, as fixed as Vess's flat delivery or Derry's trailing qualifications. When Aldric started a sentence, it ended. Properly, grammatically, with the finality of a door closing. That was who he was. A man of complete sentences.

*I do not remember what I understood,* he said. *I have tried. There were structures on the wall — lines, or something that — I can see the start of the pattern and then it is not there.* His voice tightened. *I can recite the first chamber. Thirty-two feet by twenty-one. Contamination, fragment signatures, all intact. The understanding I reached in the second chamber is gone. The memory has a clean edge. Nothing past it.*

"Like Vess's."

*Yes. Fragment memory does degrade — I have experienced it in peripheral details, things that blur over time. This is not degradation. The edge is too clean. Something was there, and it was taken.*

Derry's breathing had evened out. He'd fallen asleep with his head on his pack and his mouth slightly open. In the firelight his face was slack, younger, scrubbed of the worry that had been tightening it since they'd left Fenhold. He looked like what he was — a farmer, thirty years old, who should have been home.

*My death,* Aldric said. The word came out with the specific precision of a man who had trained himself never to flinch from a report, even when the report was about himself. *The understanding I reached, and then my death. Both absent. Both removed with the same precision. If the dungeon collapsed on me — which is what the Order's records state — then the memory of that collapse has been excised. If something else happened —*

He didn't finish. A second unfinished sentence. For Aldric, that was a building falling.

*If something else happened,* Vess said, *then the removal of the memory is itself evidence that the death wasn't what the records claim.*

*Yes.*

"The System," Maren said. He said it aloud. Derry didn't stir.

Neither fragment answered, but neither needed to. It was the thing that classified everything, assigned quests and generated bounties, and structured the architecture of the world that everyone except Maren could see. It was the thing that had put a bounty on Maren's head because it couldn't classify what he carried.

If the System was the one doing the cutting, then it was following a rule Maren couldn't read. It was too deliberate for randomness and too strange for anything simple. Vess had lost her sister's name, the feeling that sent her after Blackhall, the interval where she died. Aldric had lost the understanding he reached in that chamber and the death that followed it. Maren couldn't tell whether the pattern was intimacy, danger, or some colder rule that made both losses look alike.

"There could be more," Maren said. "Things that were taken that you don't know were there. You can only see the gaps in things you remember having. If a whole category of memory was removed —"

*Then we would not know it was ever present,* Aldric said. *That is correct. The observable gaps may be a fraction of the actual editing.*

The fire was low. Maren put another stick on it — a piece of scrub oak that was dry enough to catch quickly. The new flames threw shadows across Derry's sleeping face and across Maren's hands, which were rough and dirt-lined and holding a stick and shaking slightly, though not from cold.

He was carrying two people. Damage had gone through them, maybe deeper than any of them could see, and they were still themselves inside it.

Vess, who couldn't feel her own anger and couldn't retrieve the name she'd known better than her own.

Aldric, who couldn't remember dying and couldn't reach the thing he had understood in the chamber where he died.

Maren sat with them. The hum droned in the dark, steady and low, and the quest-light in the ditch beyond the hollow reflected the stars — a second sky, fainter, pooled in the low places of the world. The contamination smell had settled into something almost ignorable, the way any constant discomfort becomes background if you sit with it long enough.

Derry snored once, softly. The sausage he'd been saving had slipped from his pack and lay in the dirt near his boot. Maren picked it up and brushed it off and put it back. A small thing. The kind of thing you did for someone without thinking about it. He had no idea whether small things like that survived whatever happened to the dead in contaminated ground.

He thought about Thessa's warning and the clock he was on. He thought about the correction coming. He thought about the two people inside him, already altered in ways he could see and probably more he couldn't, and about how little that told him about what a full correction would do.

*You should sleep,* Vess said.

"Yeah."

*Your body will function at approximately sixty percent capacity tomorrow if you don't.*

"I know."

*Then sleep.*

He didn't. He sat with the fire going to coals and the stars turning overhead and the hum in his teeth, and he thought about what it meant to carry people who'd been cut and not know the rule behind the cuts. He thought about the System out there in the dark, vast and invisible to him, and about how little it took to disappear inside it. He thought about his hands, which had faked a hundred rituals and killed a man and failed Thessa's test and were now, apparently, the only hands left to do anything for these two people.

He didn't sleep. But he stayed.
