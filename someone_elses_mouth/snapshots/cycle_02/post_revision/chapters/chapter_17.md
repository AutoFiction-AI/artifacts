# Chapter 17

Debt got loud the first full week after she stopped speaking to June. It sounded like closed-caption work when that was the only thing standing between Lena and rent.

The source files themselves were only voices: a pharmaceutical webinar full of bright fake certainty, a church Christmas pageant recorded on somebody's phone, a regional used-car ad whose sound bed kept swallowing the consonants whole. The noise came from the labor of forcing them into shape. Her old USB foot pedal clicked every time she backed up two seconds. The radiator under the window knocked like a pipe wrench against bone and still did not put enough heat into the room to matter. The space heater by her ankle smelled faintly singed. The tailor shop downstairs had already gone dark, but steam still rose through the floorboards in little damp breaths, carrying up hot cloth and starch. Her headphones pinched the cartilage behind her ears until it felt bruised.

She had a yellow legal pad on the desk with four numbers written down in block capitals.

RENT 1125

STATE 240

VISA 70

PECO 86

The amounts had once looked like categories. In December they looked like instructions.

At 12:41 a.m. the client for the webinar kicked back her file with a note asking for cleaner speaker IDs and tighter timing around audience laughter. She would lose eighty-five dollars on the job because of the revision. She read the email twice, then once more because stress always made her stupid in one exact way: she started believing a sentence might change if she returned to it carefully enough.

She went back into the file. A man with a polished middle-aged baritone was explaining patient trust. A woman on the panel said "authenticity markers" three times in seven minutes. Lena slowed the audio to eighty percent and listened to them smile with their mouths. She typed [light laughter] and hated herself for the brackets, for the neatness, for being paid to make this kind of bullshit sound easier to live with than it was.

Her phone vibrated once against the desk and skidded into the legal pad.

She looked because some reflex in her still believed a late vibration might carry want instead of damage. The screen lit her hand from below.

JUNE SALAZAR sent you $300.

There was no note, just the app's clean green banner and the amount, cheerful as a holiday coupon.

Lena sat very still. The webinar kept running in one ear because she had forgotten to pause it. A man somewhere inside the headphones said, "What we're hearing from consumers is--" and then the sentence drowned in a wash of applause that had probably been sweetened in post.

It was three hundred dollars, too small for justice and big enough to change Thursday.

She put the phone face down and kept working. She tagged a hymn lyric. She fixed a timestamp. She corrected a line where a child had sung "manger" as "danger," then left it "manger" because accuracy was not the same thing as honesty and the client had not hired her for honesty. By 2:10 her wrists had gone hot inside the sleeves of her sweatshirt. She stood up, crossed the four steps to the sink, and drank water out of a jelly jar cloudy with old detergent. When she came back, the phone was still there, dark now, as if it had not done anything.

She opened the app anyway.

The pending transfer sat in the center of the screen, with Accept and Decline beneath it.

Her checking account, displayed one line above it, held $143.18.

She did the arithmetic because she could not stop doing it. If Farah gave her Saturday, that was maybe one-fifty before taxes if the day ran long. If the pageant client paid on time, another two hundred and change by Monday. The state payment could slide another week if she wanted the penalty notice to keep breeding in the mailbox. PECO could wait until the red print got meaner. Mrs. Alvarado had already knocked once this month and then pretended she had only been dropping off junk mail. Lena did not want to find out what the second version of that knock sounded like.

There ought to have been a cleaner feeling available to her than the one that arrived. She wanted rage, or principle, or something that stood upright.

The apartment answered first: cold window glass at her shoulder, draft under the sill, old coffee thickening in the mug, the ache in the base of her thumb from keying timestamps, the fact that the tailor would unlock downstairs at eight and the day would begin asking for money in voices that had bodies attached to them.

Refusal gave her exactly one thing: a cleaner story about herself while she got poorer inside it. The wire, the ring, the months she had spent shaping her life around a man built out of June's theft and her own appetite would stay gone either way.

She pressed Accept.

The app gave her a small green check and a pulse of brightness like she had won something.

Lena laughed once, a hard ugly sound that scraped her throat. Then she moved the three hundred straight into the account she used for rent before she could start thinking about where June's hands had been when she sent it. Typing the transfer amount felt like touching the same hot pan twice because the first burn had not taught her enough.

She finished the webinar. She finished the pageant. At dawn she sent both files, scrubbed the metadata one last time because she no longer trusted herself to have done anything right the first time, and lay down on top of the blanket with her clothes still on.

Sleep came in strips. SEPTA rumbled somewhere beyond Broad. A scooter coughed at the corner. Somebody downstairs rolled up the tailor-shop grate. She was awake again by nine-thirty with one side of her face creased from the blanket seam and a taste in her mouth like old pennies.

Farah texted at 9:42.

Can you come in at noon. Holiday cases just landed and Luis called out.

Lena texted back yes before her body had time to object.

The shop was warmer than her apartment and smelled like citrus peel, wet cardboard, and the mineral sting of spilled white wine drying on concrete. Farah had on a men's gray cardigan with one elbow shiny from wear and a strip of clementine pith stuck unnoticed to the cuff. Her silvered temples were damp from hauling boxes in from the sidewalk. She did not ask Lena whether she had slept.

"Dolly's by the Lambrusco," she said. "If your back goes, I can't afford the paperwork."

"You know how to make a girl feel wanted."

"You're wanted. By the entire state of Pennsylvania and also by twelve customers who think prosecco appears by prayer."

That was how Farah did care when she knew you were too raw for pity: she gave you a task with a joke welded onto it and trusted the task to carry the rest.

Lena spent four hours breaking down cardboard, price-gunning sparkling rose, and carrying cases from the narrow back room to the holiday display by the front window. The weight steadied her better than sleep would have. By the third trip her forearms were humming. By the fifth she had sweat cooling between her shoulder blades under the sweatshirt. Farah's holiday playlist kept trying to turn the room into something glossy and forgiving, then losing the fight to the compressor in the cheap beverage fridge.

At three-thirteen, while Lena was crouched by a stack of Cava with a box cutter between her teeth, her phone buzzed again in her apron pocket.

She knew before looking that it would not be anybody good.

It was not June. It was Mrs. Alvarado.

Need rent by tomorrow morning, mija. Plumber came. I can't float everyone.

Mrs. Alvarado did not usually use exclamation points. That was almost worse.

Lena looked at the text, then at the glass storefront where her own reflection floated over the winter beer display: hair half-fallen from its knot, box-cutter nick on her thumb, sweatshirt front dusted with cardboard grit. She had the three hundred. She had two hundred and some change coming from caption files if the clients behaved like people instead of portals. She had maybe ninety in cash from the tips jar Farah used to split on Saturday nights and had let her take early twice this fall.

She still felt behind in a way that made language stupid, behind on rent, behind on taxes, behind on becoming the kind of woman who could afford disgust.

Farah came up behind her carrying a case against her hip. "What happened?"

Lena showed her the text.

Farah read it and snorted through her nose. "Everybody's got a plumber this month. Go on your break and get a money order before the check-cashing place closes."

"I can wait till tomorrow."

"You can also get pneumonia on principle. Go now."

The refusal rose in her throat out of habit, quick as a cough. She set the case down before it reached her mouth and went for her coat.

The check-cashing place on Passyunk had a line almost to the door and a television in the corner playing a daytime court show with the volume off. Lena stood between a man buying scratch-offs and a woman in pink scrubs counting folded twenties into piles on the counter. The transfer from June had already settled. It lived inside her balance now, indistinguishable from caption money and shop cash except in the place where she knew.

When her turn came, she asked for a money order for eleven hundred and twenty-five dollars and watched the clerk stamp it with bored force. The carbon paper smell, the ink, the old Plexiglas clouded by a thousand breath marks: all of it felt more honest than the app had.

She carried the money order three blocks to Mrs. Alvarado's house and slipped it through the mail slot because she could not bear a conversation on top of the transaction. As she was turning away, the door opened anyway.

Mrs. Alvarado stood in quilted house slippers and an Eagles sweatshirt with the collar stretched wide enough to show one bra strap. Her penciled eyebrows gave her a permanently skeptical look even when she was trying to be kind.

"I was not stalking you," she said.

"Good, because I'd have to charge more."

That got a brief smile. Mrs. Alvarado took the money order and glanced at the amount. "January goes up twenty-five. I told everybody in November."

Lena had read the notice. She had put it under a magnet shaped like a tomato and then taught herself not to see it.

"I know."

"I'm not being a bitch," Mrs. Alvarado said. "The heater in the first-floor unit ate six hundred dollars."

"I know."

Mrs. Alvarado looked at her for one beat longer than landlord business required. "You look tired as hell, Lena."

There was nothing to do with that except laugh a little. "That's because I respect the season."

On the walk back to the shop, she felt the absence of the money order in her hand more strongly than she had felt the money in her account. Rent had been paid. That fact should have relieved something. Instead it made room for the next fact. January went up twenty-five. The state payment still sat on the legal pad. Visa still waited with its monthly little mouth open. June's three hundred dollars had gone where it needed to go, and now Lena had to live with the shape of that.

By close she had sold six cases of champagne to men who called every bottle "bubbly," one frantic aunt who needed orange wine because her nephew's girlfriend was vegan and "aggressive about it," and a couple buying the cheapest pinot grigio in a quantity large enough to signal a family event nobody wanted. She loaded two leftover baguettes and a bruised pear into her tote because Farah said she would only throw them out.

Back in the apartment, Lena took the legal pad and crossed out RENT 1125 with such force the paper tore under the pen tip.

Then she wrote JANUARY 1150 beneath it and kept going.

The day she gave up the trailer keys, the shore looked like a place somebody had forgotten to finish.

The winter drive to Wildwood Crest took less than two hours because the parkway had emptied of everybody who treated the coast like joy. Gabe drove with both hands on the wheel and a paper cup of gas-station coffee cooling untouched in the holder between them. Glitter clung under one of his thumbnails; one of the twins must have used him for a school craft and he had not noticed. Every time he shifted gears, the pale band on his ring finger flashed where his wedding band usually sat during work.

He had asked once, when they left South Philly, whether she wanted music.

"No."

"Good. Truck radio's possessed."

That had been the only negotiation they needed for the first hour.

The trailer park in December was all shut blinds, chained grills, and little aluminum porches gone mean with wind. Somebody had left a plastic snowman staked into a patch of dead grass three lots down; its smile had faded to a grayish smear. The air smelled of salt and damp plywood. Lena got out of the truck and the cold came straight through the denim of her jeans like the weather had hands.

She still had the keys on Rosa's old shell keychain, the one with a fake pearl glued to the center. She had not noticed until that morning that the pearl was missing now, leaving only a yellowed ring of glue like an eye after the lid had gone.

Gabe came around the hood and looked at the trailer. "Feels smaller every time."

"That's grief, I think."

"I thought grief was bigger."

"No. It's the stupid part where I still think she's gonna yell at us for letting all the cold in."

He looked at her sideways, maybe checking whether that was a joke. It was not, exactly. He let it go.

Inside, the place was stripped enough that every sound came back at them. Their shoes clicked on the vinyl. The trailer still carried its same old body odor of salt, mildew, old sunscreen, and warmed plastic, but now the smells had nowhere to hide. The curtains were down. The framed beach print over the banquette had gone with the last cleanout. All that remained were pale rectangles on the walls where objects had once blocked the light, four coat hooks by the door, and one coffee mug Lena had somehow missed in the top cabinet. WHITEHORSE PIKE CASINO NIGHT, it said in faded red letters.

She stood with the mug in her hand too long. It had the dead weight of a thing that had survived her mother by accident.

Rosa had drunk Bustelo out of chipped diner cups, not novelty mugs. The casino night one probably belonged to some neighbor or to some year nobody remembered right. It was possible to inherit a whole place and still not know which objects had ever really belonged to the person you missed.

"Trash?" Gabe asked.

Lena set the mug on the bare counter. "Yeah."

The realtor was meeting them at noon with the buyer's final paperwork, but Lena wanted ten minutes inside before anybody arrived to call the place "the unit." She walked the four paces to the back bedroom. Without the bedspread and the tote bags and Rosa's stack of paperback saints, the room looked almost fake, like a sample trailer on a lot. She touched the indentation in the wall where the old headboard had rubbed for years. In the little bathroom the medicine cabinet mirror showed her face in a harsh square: tired eyes, hair frizzed by the drive, mouth flattened into something not quite anger anymore.

She opened the cabinet out of reflex. The shelves were empty. One rust stain marked the spot where Rosa's cheap metal razor cup had sat for too long.

In October, cleaning the place out with June, she had kept thinking meaning would rise off the objects if she touched enough of them: the old cards, the tomato knife, the half-flat beach balls gone tacky in the closet. But there had been too many things and then too few. Inventory missed the point. Grief worked by ambushes, through a grocery list, a voicemail, the way Rosa had always hooked the dish towel through the oven handle twice instead of once for no reason anybody could ever defend.

Now there was almost nothing left to ambush her with. There was only space.

She went to the little front window and looked out at the lane between trailers. Off-season weeds had pushed up through the gravel. Somebody across the way had wrapped their porch railing in green Christmas lights and never plugged them in. She tried to feel the thing ending. She tried to find some clean line between before and after, mother and no mother, inheritance and aftermath.

What she felt instead was the cold from the glass and, beneath that, an absurd practical thought: she still had to stop at CVS on the way home because she was nearly out of ibuprofen.

Gabe was in the kitchenette testing the faucet one last time. "No drip," he said.

"Thrilling."

"I aim to bring sparkle."

He shut the water, then leaned one hip against the counter and looked at her with the patient wariness he had used ever since he learned the truth and stopped being her teasing little brother long enough to become family in the original, older sense. He was protective now, not teasing, and it still made her tired.

"You don't have to do all the talking when this guy gets here," he said.

"I know."

"I can do my electrician face."

"Your electrician face is just your regular face when you haven't eaten."

"That's rude and accurate."

He rubbed at the glitter under his thumbnail with the edge of his key. "Girls made reindeer antlers out of pipe cleaners last night. I was apparently structural support."

There it was, the small human thing that belonged to Gabe and nobody else: he always showed up to help carrying some leftover evidence from the life that had pulled him away from home an hour earlier. Today it was gold glitter. Last month it had been a sticker on his work boot from a pediatrician's office. The week before, it had been a braid elastic around his wrist because one of the twins had decided his wrist was safer than her own.

Lena looked at his hand and felt the simple ache of loving a person who had not betrayed her. The ache did not make a speech out of itself. It just sat there, warm and unhelpful.

At noon the realtor arrived with the buyer, a compact man in a navy fleece vest whose nails were rimmed dark with plumber's putty. He introduced himself as Sal and kept clearing his throat before each sentence as if sound cost him a little. The paperwork happened at the banquette because there was nowhere else flat enough.

"You'll want copies emailed?" he asked.

"Sure."

"And the park office has already got the transfer notice, so once I take the keys we're clean."

We're clean. There it was, that little language people used when they wanted a transaction to feel like detergent.

Lena signed where he pointed. She initialed here, here, and here. She watched her own name accrue itself in blue ink until it looked like evidence from another woman's life. Sal slid one page toward Gabe by mistake and then apologized when Gabe said he was only there to drive and carry whatever still needed carrying.

"Good brother," Sal said.

Gabe gave him a flat little nod that meant he did not intend to become friendly in under ten minutes.

When it was done, Lena reached into her coat pocket and took out the shell keychain. The keys made a mean small sound against each other. She put them on the table.

That was supposed to be the part that mattered, the handoff, the last practical token. Something in her had prepared for a blow, or for tears, or for one final unreasonable wish that Rosa would come back from the dead long enough to complain about strangers in her kitchen.

The room stayed flat.

The keys looked cheap on the table. The fake pearl was still missing. One of them had a strip of pink nail polish near the teeth where Rosa used to color-code them and then forget the code. Sal picked them up, thanked her, and tucked them into the same folder as the paperwork.

That was all.

She left, and the trailer stayed what it had become: a narrow little place by the shore, useful once, then emptied, then sold. That whole piece of family life ended with a folder, a throat-clear, and a man in a fleece vest asking whether she had any questions.

She did have one, and it came sharp and stupid and late: what, exactly, had she thought ownership was going to preserve. The rooms were already stripped. Rosa was already dead. The keys had promised one narrow right: to come back and stand inside the remainder.

"No questions," she said.

Outside, Gabe loaded the one leftover folding chair into the truck bed. The wind off the bay shoved at the hem of Lena's coat. Sal and the buyer walked away talking about winterizing the pipes before the hard freeze.

Gabe shut the tailgate. "You good to roll."

She looked once more at the trailer and saw, through the front window, only her own reflection laid over the empty interior.

"Yeah," she said. "Let's go."

On the drive home, they stopped for sandwiches in Somers Point because Gabe said if he kept driving on coffee alone he would end up killing them both in a Wawa parking lot. The sub shop was too warm. Somebody had hung paper snowflakes in the window, and one of them had folded wrong and looked more like a wound than a decoration.

Gabe set the paperwork folder between the ketchup bottle and the napkin dispenser. "You want me to hang on to this till you can look at it without wanting to hit somebody?"

Lena looked up. "About the folder or the fleece vest?"

"Dealer's choice."

She looked at the manila edge, at ROSA CORDERO printed three times on the closing copy in a font too clean to have known her. "No. If I let it out of my sight, it'll start feeling like somebody else's mother."

He nodded and pushed the folder toward her tray.

Under the carbon copies sat the line she'd already memorized: NET PROCEEDS TO SELLER $18,600. It looked huge until she started assigning names to it. Twelve hundred had to go back into the tax reserve she'd blown open for Eli. January rent waited with its new extra twenty-five. The rest was supposed to be cushion, the unsexy kind that kept a heater, a tax notice, or a cavity from turning into catastrophe.

By the time they crossed back into the city, one corner of the folder had gone soft inside her tote from where her hand kept finding it. The habit changed before the skyline did. What remained was paperwork, eighteen thousand six hundred dollars, and one decision she trusted more than any neat story about endings: she was not going to let June's fraud finish by eating through Rosa's trailer too. If June wanted to pay her back, June could hit rent and card minimums and tax scraps first. Rosa's money would stay separate until Lena decided otherwise.

Farah fed people the way other people set alarms: not because it solved anything, but because things got worse when you skipped it.

The week before Christmas, after a Saturday shift that had left Lena smelling like cardboard and spilled syrah, Farah locked the front door, flipped the sign to CLOSED, and told her to put the register count aside.

"I'm not paying you to become a Victorian orphan in front of me," she said. "Go wash your hands."

In the back room a dented rice cooker sat on the counter beside a foil tray of chicken legs roasted with onions and too much black pepper. Farah had changed into house slippers patterned with tiny lemons, one heel crushed flat because she always wore them half-stepped. She was tearing cilantro over a bowl with efficient annoyance.

"You cooked here?"

"I assembled dinner in a place with electricity. Don't romanticize me."

Lena washed up in the tiny sink. Her hands stung where the cardboard had dried them raw along the knuckles. When she came back, Farah had already made two plates.

"Sit."

"You say that like you're my parole officer."

"If you were on parole, I would at least know who to yell at."

Lena sat. The rice was hot enough to steam her face. The first bite hurt the roof of her mouth and then settled in her stomach with an almost medicinal heaviness. She had not realized until then how close she had been to going home and eating half a baguette over the sink again.

Farah leaned against a stack of beer flats with her own plate and watched her take the next bite. "Better."

"You're being weird."

"I'm being correct."

The bell over the front door rattled even though Farah had locked it. A second later somebody knocked in a short, apologetic rhythm.

Farah jerked her chin toward the front. "That's probably Mateo. I told him he could grab the charger he left."

Lena's fork paused halfway to her mouth.

Farah saw it and did not soften. She rarely did. "He's sixteen, not a carrier pigeon. Finish chewing and act like a grown woman."

Mateo came through the curtain from the shop floor with his backpack hanging off one shoulder and a school hoodie zipped up over a T-shirt that said ROBOTICS IS A TEAM SPORT in cracked white letters. One cuff was dark and shiny where he had worried it between his teeth. Beat-up headphones rested around his neck, one side held together with black electrical tape. He stopped when he saw Lena.

"Oh," he said.

Farah pointed with her fork. "You can say hello in this establishment."

"Hi, Lena."

"Hey, Teo."

He stood there another second, thin and folded into himself despite how much taller he had gotten this fall. Then Farah shoved a third plate toward the edge of the counter.

The sight of him tightened something low behind Lena's ribs. June was not in the room, but pieces of her kept showing up anyway: the electrical tape around the headphones, the chewed cuff, the guarded way Mateo stood as if he had already learned adults only called you in when the air was bad. Lena had known him since he was a skinny ten-year-old falling asleep on two pushed-together folding chairs at block parties. She hated that this mess had reached him before she had even figured out what name to give it.

"Eat before you go."

"I came for the charger."

"You can leave with both. Miracles happen daily."

Mateo made the expression he always made when adults were being impossible in a way he secretly liked. He set down his backpack and sat on the overturned milk crate by the door.

For a minute the room held only eating sounds: fork on foil, the fridge motor, muffled traffic out on the avenue. Lena did not know where to rest her eyes. They kept landing on Mateo's taped headphones, then on the rice, then on Farah, who was pretending not to referee while in fact controlling the entire oxygen supply.

Mateo broke first.

"I didn't know," he said to his plate.

Lena put her fork down.

He kept looking at the rice. "Not like that. I knew she had some weird thing running on her laptop. I knew she was being... off. I didn't know it was you."

His voice had the usual teenager's instinct to make every sentence smaller than the feeling under it. Then, because the subject mattered, he kept going.

"Everybody keeps talking around me now. That's the worst part. Not even the part where they know something happened. It's that they decide I can't hear if they lower it half an inch." He finally looked up, quick and direct. "Mrs. Kelly at the laundromat asked if I was staying with my mom again. Like she was checking inventory."

Farah swore softly under her breath.

Lena felt something in her chest shift, not toward forgiveness, not anything as grand as that. It felt more like the angle of weight in a box when you realized somebody else had taken hold of the other side without asking your permission.

"I'm sorry," she said.

Mateo gave a little shrug that did not dismiss it so much as admit there was nowhere to put it. "Yeah."

Farah pointed at him with her fork. "How was the math test?"

He blinked. "What?"

"The math test you pretended all week not to care about."

"I got an eighty-eight."

"See. Society advances."

That pulled half a smile out of him. He ducked his head over the plate and ate faster, like now that the worst sentence was out he had a right to be sixteen again for at least ten minutes.

Lena watched him and remembered him at twelve, all elbows and solemn eyes, building a fort out of folding tables at Saint Rocco night while June yelled at adults about charcoal timing like the block would collapse without her. She remembered him asleep on June's couch with a geometry book open on his chest. She remembered how many normal, ungenerous, unstaged kinds of care had been real in June's life. That was part of what made the thing unforgivable. June had done it with human feeling fully intact. She could take care of one life with both hands while poisoning another on purpose.

Her phone buzzed on the counter beside the rice cooker.

Nobody reached for it. The sound sat there.

Then the screen lit.

JUNE SALAZAR sent you $250.

Below it sat an unread text preview.

I'm not asking you to answer. I'm sorry. I'll keep sending what I can.

Lena stared at the words until they blurred slightly at the edges while the room around her stayed brutally ordinary. The chicken skin had gone cold on her plate. Farah's slippers rasped once against the floor as she shifted her weight. Mateo fished a grain of rice from the sleeve of his hoodie with the concentration of a surgeon.

Farah did not ask what the screen said. "If that's her, let it sit for a second before you decide anything," she said.

Mateo's eyes flicked up, then away. He knew whose name would do that to a room.

Lena picked up the phone. The transfer waited, pending. The text remained unread unless she chose otherwise. She could feel the old desire for an argument trying to wake up in her, that exhausted, useless appetite to pin June to the wall of language and make her say the exact sentence that would finally match the damage. June had already burned through that currency. What remained was the new amount on the screen, another small bucket from the arsonist, and the closing-sheet figure she had memorized in Wildwood: $18,600, the last room Rosa had left her translated into digits. She was not going to let that number keep paying for June if June still had money to send. Money could move on one track. Contact could stay dead on another.

She accepted the transfer.

Then she locked the screen without opening the text.

Farah nodded once, not approval exactly, more like recognition. "Good. Now eat before that turns into plaster."

Lena put the phone face down and took another bite. The rice had gone warm and a little clumped at the edges. The chicken was salty, pepper-heavy, a little dry. It tasted like a meal made in the back room of a neighborhood bottle shop by a woman who had spent all day moving cases and still understood that bodies required maintenance whether feelings cooperated or not.

Mateo licked pepper from his thumb and looked at Lena carefully. "You don't have to, like, talk to her because of me."

"I know."

"She keeps acting like if she gets the right amount together it'll..." He stopped, searching. "I don't know. Un-stupid it."

Farah snorted. "That is not a verb."

"It should be."

"A lot of things should be."

Lena looked at Mateo, at the taped headphones, the shiny cuff, the tiredness he carried too carefully for somebody his age. She thought about the attendance office, the laundromat woman, the way adults' disasters always drafted children into cleanup work they had never volunteered for.

The closing table in Wildwood had taught her at least one useful thing: money and access were not the same bargain. A transfer could clear without giving anybody the right to walk back into the room.

"I'm not talking to her," she said. "That's separate."

Mateo nodded, and the relief on his face was complicated enough to hurt. He looked relieved that he did not have to broker anything, relieved that the rules were clear, and maybe relieved that one adult in the situation was finally saying what would happen and then actually meaning it.

Farah took Mateo's empty plate and stood. "Good. Since we've established boundaries, one of you is taking home this extra rice and the other is breaking down that cardboard stack before I report both of you to the state."

"You talk to the state a lot," Mateo said.

"The state and I are in a serious relationship."

"Seems toxic."

"That's because you're observant."

He smiled outright then, quick and young. It changed his whole face.

They cleaned up without making an event out of the cleaning. Mateo broke down boxes with the same grave efficiency June had always brought to neighborhood chores, and the resemblance hit Lena in flashes she could do nothing about. Farah packed rice into a deli container and shoved it at Lena with two bruised mandarins from the front display. Somebody outside revved a scooter too hard at the light. A bus sighed to the curb. The city kept performing the same cheap rough music it had played before Eli, during Eli, after Eli.

When Lena finally picked up her tote, the unread text still sat beneath the accepted transfer. She did not open it. She did not delete it either. She left it there to cool.

At the door, Farah said, "I need someone Wednesday if the distributor screws up again."

Lena adjusted the tote higher on her shoulder. Her forearms still ached from the cases. Wednesday meant pallets, invoice bullshit, and hours paid in something other than June's guilt. It also meant a room where people used their own names.

"I'll do Wednesday," she said.

Outside, the air had gone knife-cold. Mateo pulled up his hood and gave her a small two-fingered wave before turning toward the bus stop. Farah stood in the doorway with her lemon slippers and her crossed arms, making sure they both actually left with food. Lena headed north toward Moore Street carrying leftovers, mandarins, and the next shift already nailed into the week.

Her phone buzzed once in her bag as she reached the corner.

The buzz told her enough: apology, explanation, maybe another sentence trying to turn ruin back into language. She kept walking. In a few blocks she would be home to the pageant revision, the tax portal, and the cold apartment. Whatever June had to say could stay shut in the dark of the bag against Lena's hip. Wednesday belonged to Farah's shop.
