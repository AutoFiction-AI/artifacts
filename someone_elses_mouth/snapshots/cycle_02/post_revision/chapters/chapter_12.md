# Chapter 12

June had texted that they'd figure it out. Lena spent enough of the night turning Eli's thread into evidence that by the next morning, with rain pasting free-pizza flyers flat to the South Philadelphia sidewalk, she took that to mean June's car keys. She went down June's basement steps with her phone in one hand and the screenshots still open long enough for the rain to dot the glass silver.

June's place always felt like the underside of the block: cooler, dimmer, full of cords and old machine heat. A fan buzzed in one corner for no reason Lena could identify except habit. Mateo was at the folding table in a gray Central hoodie with his beat-up headphones around his neck, picking a gummy bear off the W key of a school Chromebook with the edge of a library card. June stood at the counter in work boots and black socks, one lace trailing, tearing open junk mail with the nicotine-yellowed side of her thumbnail.

She looked up once. "You look bad."

"Thanks."

"You want coffee or the truth."

"I want your car keys."

Mateo stopped worrying the gummy bear and looked between them without moving anything else in his face.

June laughed, quick and automatic. "For what."

Lena held the phone out. Eli's message sat there in the same patient cadence that had kept working on her even after it started to chill her. "Long wet load-in. Harborlight's floor is murdering my knees. If I survive tech, I'm stealing you Sunday." Below it was the earlier one from Tuesday with the too-specific little detail that had done the damage: "The space is this old annex off Ocean. Smells like dust and salt and somebody's bad coffee." He had sent a picture after that, a black dance floor rolled halfway out over scuffed wood, cinderblock painted cream, one red EXIT sign.

"I'm going down there," Lena said. "Wildwood, Crest, wherever the hell this is supposed to be. I just need to see the building."

June set the junk mail down. "Now."

"Yes, now."

"In this weather."

"It's rain, not a biblical test."

Mateo glanced at the basement window where water was crawling down the glass. "Could be both."

June pointed the torn envelope at him without looking. "Not helping."

He lifted one shoulder and went back to the Chromebook.

Lena heard herself getting too formal and corrected it before the sentence finished. "I know this is unhinged. I do. I know if I say it out loud it sounds like I'm one step from making a wall of yarn in my apartment. But he told me Tuesday at three twelve that he was at a dance residency through Sunday, and if that's true then there is a place. There has to be a sign or a flyer or a bored woman at a desk who has seen his face once. I don't need to catch him. I just need one thing that isn't a phone."

June leaned back against the counter. She was still smiling a little, but it had been reduced to maintenance. "Lena. Surprise-inspecting your boyfriend at work is not proof. It's chaos. It's also, for the record, exactly the kind of thing that makes people lie harder."

"Then he can be mad at me from inside a real building."

"Or he can be in rehearsal and not answer because adults have jobs."

"Then I'll know there's a rehearsal."

"You can just call him."

"No." Lena rubbed rainwater off the phone with the heel of her hand and only smeared it. "If I call him, he'll talk. That's the whole problem. He'll say something warm in that voice and ask me why I sound tired and suddenly I'm apologizing again."

June's face did a small shut thing. Lena would have missed it if she did not know it so well from years of watching June hear bad news, bullshit a landlord, keep her temper with a copier repair line. When June was actually stressed, she got tidier. The jokes came off first.

"What did Farah say?" June asked.

"I didn't say Farah said anything."

"You didn't have to."

That was June too, the remembered fact offered like a receipt. Lena hated how much comfort that trick had given her over the years. She hated more that it still worked for half a second.

"Farah said if a place exists, it has a phone number," Lena said. "I know that's the reasonable move. I'm not in a reasonable mood."

"No kidding."

Mateo looked up again. "If you go, don't take Forty-Two all the way. It backs up where it narrows. Fifty-Five is ugly but faster."

June stared at him. "You have algebra."

"I also have ears."

"You have school."

"I'm leaving in twenty minutes." He slid the gummy bear free and flicked it into the trash with a tiny, satisfied snap. "Also if the thing is fake, Google won't help anyway. It'll just show you wedding venues and a haunted mini golf place."

Lena laughed before she could stop it. The sound came out wrong, too high and thin. Mateo looked embarrassed by having caused it and hunched over his computer again.

June picked up her phone, put it down, then picked up the keys from the chipped bowl by the sink. "This is stupid."

"I know."

"This is how people end up on neighborhood Facebook pages with a Ring screenshot and a caption like LADIES STAY SAFE."

"Probably."

"And if he's there, if you find out this is all normal, you're buying me lunch and gas and the extra toll because you decided to go full detective in a nor'easter."

"It's October. It's not a nor'easter."

"Look outside."

Lena did. The narrow strip of concrete at the bottom of the basement steps had gone black with water. The gutter was choking on leaves. Somewhere up on the street a truck hit a pothole deep enough to make a splash that sounded like a sheet dropped on tile.

June said, "Give me ten minutes."

"For what."

"For shoes, a charger, and because Mateo still needs lunch money unless your investigation budget covers that too."

It should not have mattered, but Lena felt the small flush of shame anyway. June still had a real life standing around her in damp socks: the folded utility bill by the fruit bowl, the cheap deli turkey in the fridge, Mateo pretending not to listen because he had learned that from her. It would have been easier if June looked cleaner inside the suspicion. Instead she looked exactly like herself.

While June went into the bedroom, Mateo shut the Chromebook and reached for his backpack. "If it's fake," he said without looking up, "don't let her keep making it sound complicated."

Lena blinked. "What."

He shoved a notebook into the bag. "Adults do that when they already know something is bad. They add words until it's not bad anymore."

"Teo," June called from the bedroom.

"What? I said if."

He zipped the bag. His forehead had broken out again, angry little red bumps across the hairline. There was a geometry quiz folded in the front pocket, the corner marked with a C in red pen and then a smaller note beneath it offering corrections for half credit. He saw Lena looking and flipped the pocket closed with a shrug that was more annoyed than ashamed.

"I'm working on it," he said.

"I didn't say anything."

"You did your face."

June came back in a black rain shell, car charger looped around two fingers, winding the cord tight around her knuckles as if she could get the morning under control by force. "All right. Move before I remember I've still got standards."

On the way out Mateo said, "If you stop for food, don't get anything from the first Wawa off the expressway. Their hot case tastes like wet keys."

June pushed the door open with her hip. "A poet."

The car was a silver Corolla with one missing hubcap and a phone mount suction-cupped to the windshield so long it had left a pale ring on the glass. The passenger seat held three reusable grocery bags, a rolled-up hoodie, and a folder stamped KEYNEST in blue. Lena shoved the bags to the floor and climbed in. The seat was cold through her jeans. The inside of the car smelled like peppermint gum, old french fries, and the damp wool of June's rain shell.

They drove without music through wet block after wet block, wipers thocking time. At Broad the light caught them long enough that Lena could see herself in the side mirror: hair knotted badly, mascara shadow under the eyes, mouth already set for an argument she could not yet name.

June merged south and said, "So what's the plan here. You want to roll up on this man with your phone out and say hello, can somebody verify my romance."

"I want to see Harborlight or whatever the place is called."

"That was not the question."

"Then no. I don't want to roll up on anybody."

"Good, because that's how you get a cop called on you by a nineteen-year-old intern with a lanyard."

Lena looked at the phone again. She had screenshotted everything before she came over: the message about the old annex, the one about tech, the picture of the floor, the little throwaway complaint about coffee that had been convincing precisely because it was so stupid. A man lying for money would invent emergencies, not bad institutional coffee. A real person complained about coffee.

Except a fake person could do that too. That was the point. That was the whole grotesque trick. It had made her feel known in the exact grain of her life and now every grain was suspect.

At the entrance to Fifty-Five, traffic slowed to a crawl under the rain haze. June drummed once on the steering wheel, stopped herself, and said, too casually, "Residency can mean a lot of things, you know. Half the arts world is smoke and nouns. It could just be a rented room with a grant name."

Lena turned and looked at her. "You know a lot about dance residencies all of a sudden."

June kept her eyes on the road. "I know a lot about people making small things sound expensive."

"That wasn't what I said."

"No, but it's what you meant."

The rain thickened. Tractor trailers threw up curtains of dirty spray that smacked the windshield and then ripped away. Beyond them the marshland flattened out gray-green and drowned-looking.

Lena put the phone facedown in her lap. She could feel it there like a second pulse.

"If this is nothing," June said after a while, "if the place exists and he's just not answering because he's busy, you need to decide whether you want to be with somebody or audit him."

Lena stared out at the highway signs blurring past. "If it exists, I'll apologize."

"And if it doesn't."

The answer stuck somewhere behind Lena's teeth. She kept her eyes on the highway signs until they smeared into green ribbons and still could not tell which version would be worse.

* * *

The first address took them to a low tan building two blocks off Ocean that looked like it had been designed by someone angry at windows. BEACH TAG OFFICE was stenciled on one side in flaking blue paint. On the other was a smaller metal sign that said BOROUGH STORAGE. A chain hung through the glass handles of the front doors. Inside, under buzzing fluorescent lights, Lena could see folding tables stacked on their sides and a bulletin board with one crooked flyer for a rabies clinic.

June killed the engine and leaned forward as if a better angle might produce a man, a rehearsal, a single fact.

"Maybe they use the service side," she said. "Maybe they load in around the back."

Lena did not move. "To what."

"These places get called weird shit. Annex, office, civic center, whatever. He said annex."

"He said Harborlight. He said a residency. He said dancers."

"And that's an annex."

"It's a beach-tag office."

Rain hammered the roof hard enough that the whole car sounded tinny and enclosed, like they were inside a kicked bucket. June reached for the umbrella wedged behind her seat. It was the kind you bought from a pharmacy and hated instantly.

"Come on," she said. "Let's at least see."

They crossed the lot with the umbrella listing sideways between them, mostly protecting neither. The puddles were deep enough to fold icy water over the tops of Lena's sneakers. Up close the building got worse. One window had a handwritten notice taped inside: CLOSED FOR SEASONAL OPERATIONS. Another had a flyer for flu shots from the previous year. The cement planter by the door held two cigarette butts, a dead seagull feather, and a drowned yellowing mums arrangement gone stringy with rot.

Lena put her hand flat to the glass anyway. Her reflection, warped by rain, leaned back at her like somebody drunk.

"No music," she said.

"Because it's locked."

"No scuff marks. No cases. Nothing."

June peered through the side panel. "One floor photo doesn't buy us the whole room."

"Why are you defending a building."

That got June quiet.

Lena stepped back. Across the street an enormous fiberglass crab above a seafood place grinned through the weather with one claw chipped off. Beyond it motels sat shut up for the season, plastic lawn chairs stacked in white towers and chained together. She had been down here with her mother enough times in October to know how the shore smelled when the money went home: wet plywood, fryer grease that had soaked into walls all summer, salt pulling at metal. It should not have felt humiliating. It was just rain and closed businesses. But shame made everything public. Even the gulls sounded like they were laughing at her from a roofline.

Back in the car June said, "Show me the other thing."

"What other thing."

"You said he mentioned coffee."

Lena pulled up the thread. June held out her hand for the phone without thinking, then seemed to realize the gesture and waited. Lena gave it to her anyway. June scrolled with her mouth set and read aloud, "Old annex off Ocean, bad coffee, floor's decent, the director wears scarves like she's in a foreign film."

She handed it back. "Okay. Then maybe he was down here once. Maybe he grabbed coffee, took the floor picture, and kept talking like he was still working out of it."

"That is a very furnished maybe."

"I'm trying to keep up with the weather and your mood."

The diner was half a mile away, exactly where Eli had once said he was grabbing lemon pie after rehearsal because the dancers were all hungry and impossible. Lena had remembered that because she had liked the phrase hungry and impossible. It made the room around him feel populated. It made his life extend past the edge of her screen.

Blue Gull Diner sat at the corner under a faded sign with one gull missing an eye. The blinds were down. Brown paper covered the lower half of the windows. Inside, chairs were stacked upside down on the tables. A handwritten sign on the locked door said SEE YOU MEMORIAL DAY and below that, in smaller pen, TIPS MAILED FRIDAY IF THE OWNER STOPS BEING CHEAP.

Lena stood on the slick concrete apron staring at it while rain ticked off the aluminum awning.

"Well," June said. "That's rude timing."

Next door a bait-and-tackle shop was open under a sign shaped like a marlin. Buckets of green crab sat by the door in black water. A woman in a red fleece vest was behind the counter with lilac reading glasses hanging on a beaded chain and one cuff darkened where she had clearly just reached into a tank. She looked up at them with the expression of someone who had not asked for tourists and knew exactly how much they were going to cost her.

June gave Lena a little chin lift. Go on, then.

Lena hated her for it.

Inside, the place smelled like bait brine and coffee that had been reheated past dignity. The woman kept one hand on a styrofoam cup and waited.

Lena said, "Sorry. Weird question. Do you know if there is some kind of dance residency around here? Harborlight, maybe. Or a place people call the annex."

The woman blinked once. "A dance what."

"Like a rehearsal space. Program. Arts thing."

The woman took a sip of coffee, still looking at Lena over the rim. "Honey, the only arts thing next door is when somebody from Philly throws up in the hydrangeas."

June made a noise that could have been a cough.

Lena felt heat start behind her ears despite the wet. "Okay. Sorry. My, uh, boyfriend said he was working down here."

"Doing what."

"Sound design."

"For dancing."

"Yeah."

The woman looked past Lena out at the rain, considering the whole stupid coast. "Nope."

"There isn't anything."

"There is a ballroom class for widows at Saint Ann's on Thursdays, and there was a Zumba lady in Rio Grande for about six minutes before she ran off with a plumber. That's what I've got."

June said, lighter than necessary, "It could still be one of those donor weekends. You get six people in a borrowed room and everybody calls it a residency because retreat sounds too much like a church basement."

The woman shifted her coffee to the other hand. Her wedding band had been cut off and soldered back together crooked. "Private where. In October. You think rich people come here to make dance."

"I don't know what rich people do," June said.

"Neither do I, but if they were here I'd hear about it because they'd complain the coffee sucks."

There it was, the coffee detail coming back to Lena stripped of magic, just a local woman making the same joke because it was an obvious joke to make about this town. She thanked her, or thought she did. The words felt wet and useless in her mouth.

Back in the car she shut the door too hard. June started the engine and let the defroster roar for a minute.

"Okay," June said. "So. Not there."

"Not anywhere yet."

"Right."

The phone sat in Lena's lap, screen gone black. She could have unlocked it and found Eli's face in two seconds. She could have played one of the voice notes where he said her name low, like he was already in the room. Instead she looked through the windshield at the empty diner, the sign about tips, the rain making the parking lot look oiled.

"This is humiliating," she said.

June watched the defroster push a clear circle through the fog on the windshield before she said, "It's only humiliating if you decide it is."

Lena turned to her. "I'm standing in a bait shop asking strangers if my boyfriend has a workplace."

"Okay. Fair."

The municipal building was farther inland, a squat brick rectangle between a pharmacy and a dentist with a giant inflatable pumpkin collapsing gently in the rain by the curb. Inside, Lena's sneakers squeaked on the waxed floor. The lobby smelled like bleach and the sweet rotten note of wet umbrellas. A woman at the desk wore a fleece vest over a Christmas T-shirt even though it was barely mid-October, and she kept straightening a paper clip while she talked on the phone about a soccer permit.

Lena waited until she hung up.

"Hi," Lena said. "I'm sorry. I'm trying to find a program and I keep getting nowhere. Do you know if there's a Harborlight dance residency in town? Maybe at an annex building near Ocean."

The woman looked at her, then at June, then at the rain flattening itself against the glass doors. "Harborlight."

"Yes."

"No."

"Not now or not here."

"Not now, not here, not in summer either." She clicked to a calendar on her computer, then dragged a paper binder toward herself anyway as if for ceremonial emphasis. "We have line dancing at the senior center on Tuesdays, rec basketball sign-ups, and a trunk-or-treat in the church lot next week if nobody steals the extension cords. That's the arts calendar."

June tried again. "What if somebody booked it under an event company or a donor's LLC. What if the load-in was small, there were no signs, and they paid cash to the building."

The woman gave her a flat look over the top of the monitor. She had silver glitter on two fingernails and a nicotine patch visible where her vest gaped. "If somebody rented private space for a week and called it a residency, that would still not make it a town program. And if they wanted sound permits or load-in or parking for any amount of equipment, they would come through me or code. Nobody has. Nobody named Harborlight, either."

Lena heard herself say, "So there isn't a venue by that name."

"No."

"Or an annex."

"Not unless you mean the old tax office and that place is locked up tighter than my sister's liquor cabinet."

June gave a short laugh that died in the room.

The woman softened half an inch. "You want the Chamber office in Cape May maybe. They know about the artsy people."

"He said Wildwood," Lena said.

"Then Wildwood should know about it."

"And they don't."

"Apparently not."

Lena thanked her and walked out before the woman could turn kind. Kindness would have finished the job.

Outside, the rain had thinned to a mean steady drizzle. The air tasted metallic, like the weather had scraped its teeth on the ocean and come back inland. June followed her down the steps and stood beside her in the lot while cars hissed past.

"Lena."

"Don't."

"I'm saying the lie could still be smaller than a whole invented man."

"Smaller how."

June tucked her hands into the pockets of the rain shell. "For him dressing the work thing up."

Lena let out a laugh that came out mean. "You hear yourself, right."

"People lie about jobs when the real one sounds small. They take one decent room, one paid day, and spin a whole week out of it because nobody wants to say they were hauling cases for a church gala."

"So that's your good version."

June thumbed the key fob hard enough to make the Corolla chirp behind them. "My good version is maybe he didn't want you showing up after D.C. Maybe he wanted one part of his life that wasn't under a microscope."

"I didn't put him under a microscope. I wanted to meet him one time in three months. Then I wanted the story to line up with the world."

"You reverse-searched his face."

"Because his face turned into a drummer named Asher Quinn."

June looked away, out toward the pharmacy lot where a shopping cart rolled loose and bumped a curb.

"Okay," she said. "Okay. I'm not saying this looks good."

"It looks insane."

"I'm saying it doesn't have to mean nothing is real."

Nothing is real would've been easier to hear from anybody else. From June it landed wrong, too exact in the place she was trying not to look. Lena turned toward the street so she would not have to watch June say another clever thing with that careful mouth.

Cars sprayed past. Somewhere farther off, toward the boardwalk, a loose sign banged metal against metal in a steady, stupid rhythm. Lena pictured Eli's floor photo again: the rolled marley, the red EXIT sign, the cream cinderblock. It could have been anywhere. It could have been a high school gym, a rehearsal room in a church basement, a file somebody had bought for six dollars off the internet. She had loved the image because it placed him. Now all it proved was that an image could have corners.

June said, quieter, "Let's get out of the rain."

Lena gave her only the back of her wet coat and started toward the car because there was nowhere else to go.

* * *

June took them to the supermarket on the edge of town with no discussion, just a left turn off the boulevard and then another into a lot full of wet carts. The big red sign hummed in the mist. An inflatable pumpkin sagged by the entrance with one arm collapsed, permanently hailing nobody.

"I need groceries," June said when she parked. "Mateo texted. We're out of everything."

Lena stared through the windshield. "Now."

"Yes, now. People still eat when their love lives are a landfill."

"You can go in."

June killed the engine. "Fine."

She sat there a second longer, fingers still on the key. Then she said, not looking over, "Come inside or don't. I don't care. I'm not leaving you in my car to spiral hard enough to crack the glass."

Go fuck yourself pressed hot at the back of Lena's teeth. She got out and followed June across the lot under the pharmacy umbrella that kept trying to invert.

Inside, the store was overheated and smelled of floor cleaner, seafood, and the sugary rot of overripe peaches. Endcaps had already turned to Halloween: miniature chocolates, paper skeletons, an entire pallet of pumpkin-spice coffee pods. June moved fast with a basket over one arm, practical as always, grabbing bread, eggs, sliced turkey, cereal, the banana yogurt drinks Mateo liked when he was too anxious to eat anything that required chewing. She added dishwasher pods, a family-size bag of pretzels, and a box of generic tampons without pausing to conceal any of it.

Lena wandered two aisles over and then back again. The fluorescent light made everybody's skin look faintly refrigerated. A child in a raincoat licked condensation off a freezer door while his father compared prices on frozen shrimp. At the front of the store, by the checkouts, a metal bin held ten-pound bags of ice in blue plastic.

She took one before she thought about it.

It was pure shore habit. Rosa's trailer ice maker had given up years before Rosa died, and every time they left the supermarket Lena or her mother would grab a bag on the way out and sling it into the cooler between the beach chairs and the folding table. Don't come back empty-handed, Rosa used to say, even when what she meant was don't come back without the thing I forgot.

Lena stood there holding the cold, sweating weight against her hip and hated herself for still having hands that remembered.

At checkout June looked at the ice and did not comment. The cashier, a woman with a neck brace under her sweatshirt and two tiny gold studs in one nostril, rang it through with the rest.

"Four twenty-nine," she said, then glanced at the bag and added, "That thing leaks if you baby it."

Outside, the drizzle had thickened again. June popped the trunk, set the groceries inside, came around to the driver's seat, and turned the key.

The engine clicked once. Then again, smaller and meaner.

June tried a third time. Nothing but the choked rattle of a battery giving up with attitude.

For one long second neither of them moved.

Then June put her forehead against the steering wheel and said, with no style left in it at all, "Fuck me."

Lena was still standing beside the car with the ice hugged to her coat. Meltwater ran down her sleeve and into her cuff. One of the plastic grocery bags had cut a red line into the base of her fingers.

"Did you leave the lights on," she asked.

"No."

The dashboard glowed accusingly. June looked at it, looked away. "Maybe."

Lena laughed. She could not help it. It came out ugly enough that June turned.

"Don't start."

"Start what."

"This."

Lena lifted the bag of ice half an inch, as if presenting evidence. "I'm standing in supermarket drizzle holding ten pounds of sentimental ice because my fake boyfriend does not have a workplace and now your car is dead. I think this has actually started."

June got out hard enough to rock the car. "Pop the hood."

"I don't know where that is in your stupid car."

"It's under the dash."

"I know where it is in cars in general. I don't know where it is in your stupid car."

That made June stop, one hand already under the steering wheel. For a second Lena thought she might laugh. Instead June yanked the latch and went to the front.

The hood came up with a cough of old metal. Rain beaded on the engine cover. Lena stood uselessly beside the passenger door while carts clattered toward the entrance and back. Water dripped from the bottom seam of the ice bag onto her sneaker, then through the canvas to her sock. Across the lot a man loaded paper towels into a pickup bed with the grim concentration of somebody moving bricks.

June said, without looking up, "Do you still have that portable charger thing from your mom's trailer."

"In my apartment. Extremely helpful."

"I was asking."

"It sounded a lot like blame."

June straightened. Her wet hair had started to curl at the temples. "Everything sounds like blame to you right now."

"Maybe because we drove two hours to verify a sentence and the sentence lost."

"I said I know."

"Do you."

June shut the hood carefully, too carefully. "What exactly do you want me to say, Lena."

The cart kid came over before Lena could answer. He was maybe nineteen, in an orange poncho two sizes too big, with a healed skate scar cutting white through one eyebrow. He pushed a string of wet carts with one hand and took in the open hood, the dead car, Lena with the ice, June with her face set like she might punch weather.

"You need a jump?" he asked.

June exhaled through her nose. "If you've got one."

"Manager keeps a battery pack. Hold up."

He jogged back toward the store, poncho flapping around his knees. Lena watched him go because it was easier than looking at June.

After a minute June said, lower, "He could still be real."

Lena turned her head very slowly. The sentence used the same carpentry as *don't do the story where*: take her shame in both hands, narrow it, hand it back in a shape she could survive. That was the first sickening part. June's defense of him sounded like him.

June kept going, tidier now, every word set down like it was going to be audited. "He could've lied about the residency because he needed a story big enough to keep you from driving down here. He could be broke. He could've grabbed one floor photo from one job and leaned on it for a week. He could've cut a better life together out of scraps."

"Cut a better life together."

"You know what I mean."

Rain found the seam of Lena's collar and slid cold between her shoulder blades. June wasn't talking like somebody guessing anymore. She sounded like somebody explaining acceptable losses.

"No." She did, too well, and that made the word come out younger than she meant. "I actually don't anymore."

June's jaw worked once. "People make themselves look better online."

"People use old gym photos online. They don't invent an annex."

"He didn't invent your calls."

That should've been comfort. Instead Lena felt her stomach tip. Calls were not a defense unless June had already decided what counted as a person.

"No," Lena said. "Somebody made those."

That landed between them and stayed there, not yet accusation, no longer harmless. June's eyes flicked to her and then away.

The cart kid came back with a black jump pack and a middle-aged assistant manager in a store vest carrying an umbrella that was useless against the wind. The manager had one thumbnail painted glitter green and the rest bare. He clipped the leads on, told June to try it now, and the Corolla shuddered awake on the second turn.

"Don't shut it off till you're basically in Pennsylvania," he said.

June thanked him with a sincerity that would've charmed plenty of people. It did not charm Lena. The men headed back toward the store through the rain, already talking about a delivery truck.

June lowered the hood, got in, and left the engine running. Lena put the ice in the footwell because there was nowhere else to put it without touching June's groceries. She climbed in and shut the door. The car filled with heat that smelled faintly burnt.

For a while June drove with both hands at ten and two like a student driver taking a test. The groceries rustled every time they turned. One of the yogurt drinks rolled in the back and thudded softly against a loaf of bread.

At the first light out of the lot June said, "I'm not defending him because I'm stupid."

Lena looked out at the wet neon of a liquor store, the CLOSED flag plastered to its pole. "I didn't say you were stupid."

"No. You just make the air in the car say it for you."

"Then maybe crack a window."

June's laugh this time was one dry note and done. "Jesus."

They passed the turn that would have taken them toward the trailer park and kept going. Lena watched the sign disappear in the side mirror with a little stab of relief so sharp it embarrassed her. She was not ready to drag her mother's place into this too. She had already brought the ice.

June said, "You know what I think."

Lena kept her eyes on the side mirror until the trailer sign vanished completely.

"I think if he's lying, he's lying from underneath something. Money, probably. Shame. Maybe he thought if he told you the real job you'd hear the whole rest of his life in it and bounce."

The wipers slapped back and forth. A gull rose out of a puddled lot and then vanished in the rain.

"He got me to send money because of a smashed van window and a payroll freeze and his sister's ruined apartment," Lena said. Her voice came out steady enough to pass for normal, which was worse. "At a certain point shame and business start wearing the same coat."

June's fingers tightened on the wheel. "I know."

That was the last full thing either of them said for a long time.

The silence did not arrive all at once. It came in pieces, taking the spots where their friendship usually lived. The comment about the billboard lawyer with the spray tan. The automatic ranking of shore grocery stores. The shared disgust when they passed a pickup with truck nuts shining wet as cherries. Any other drive, one of them would have said something. Any other drive, the other would have built on it. Now the words rose and died before they crossed the space between them.

By the time they hit the expressway, the car had become all mechanics: wipers, engine, the soft slosh from the half-melted ice at Lena's feet, the papery shift of grocery bags every time June changed lanes. Lena kept her phone facedown in her lap. It buzzed once somewhere near Deptford, a short bright insect sound, and neither of them looked at it.

Rain blurred the brake lights ahead into red smears. June reached to turn the heat down, then left her hand on the knob too long, as if she had forgotten what came after that motion. Lena watched the road signs count them north.

At the toll plaza June handed over cash without asking Lena to dig in the cup holder the way she usually did. The receipt fluttered onto the console and stayed there, dampening at one corner from the wet air. A song came on the radio low enough that only the bass made it through. June switched it off.

The city thickened around them by degrees: chain stores, gas stations, the first dirty walls of warehouses, a SEPTA bus hissing at a stop with its doors open to the rain. Home was coming. Lena did not feel relieved. She felt as if she had carried something heavy all the way to the shore only to discover it was hollow and then had been stupid enough to carry it back.

When they got off Broad and into the neighborhood grid, June missed the easy left she always took toward Moore.

Lena heard the signal clicking and said nothing.

June corrected at the next corner. "Sorry."

Lena looked at the wet windshield, at the pale ring where the old phone mount had marked the glass, at June's hands on the wheel, and kept her mouth shut.

That more than anything seemed to land.

The rest of the ride passed in the sound of rain and the patient engine, each block bringing them deeper into streets they both knew too well to need directions. When June pulled up to Lena's block, silence sat between them like a third person who had finally been invited in and had no intention of leaving.
