# Chapter 5

By four o'clock the next afternoon Lena had already changed the sheets she'd twisted around her thighs the night before, shaved both legs past the point of common sense, and burned a narrow stripe of skin above her lip with a wax strip that promised salon results and delivered a hot paper tear. The date was not until seven-thirty. Eli was meeting her at a wine bar off Passyunk with mismatched stools and good fries, and she had told herself three different times that she was not acting like a woman about to be inspected by God.

The apartment kept the late-summer heat the way a grudge kept shape. Her box fan shoved the smell of hot dust and old headphone foam from the desk to the bed and back again. Below her, in the tailor shop, somebody dropped a metal hanger and swore in Spanish. Out on Moore Street a scooter coughed past, then another. Every sound came up through the floorboards thin and cheap and neighborly. The noise should have anchored her in the block. Instead she kept picturing the whole street looking up while she put on mascara.

She tried to work until five and got through eleven minutes of a caption file before realizing she had typed the same speaker identification twice and missed an entire sentence because Eli had sent a photo of his hand wrapped around a coffee cup with the caption leaving load-out in an hour, don't let me be underdressed next to you. He was good at that, laying a light touch over a live wire. He could flirt without sounding like he had set out to flirt. He could make attention feel like weather, like something she had not caused but had somehow earned.

She texted back, `Too late. I'm wearing the bishop's hat and carrying a scepter.`

He sent a voice note almost immediately. Even now, weeks in, the little triangle of it on Signal could tilt her pulse. His voice came through with traffic hiss behind it and a hollow clang she pictured as road case lids. "I'd still be into it. I respect pageantry. Also, for the record, I've seen you in a T-shirt with one sleeve rolled weird because you were doing dishes one-handed, so I don't think you can lose this one."

He had remembered that from a call three nights earlier, when she had balanced the phone on the counter and rinsed plates while he described a venue in D.C. with a dressing room that smelled like wet velvet. He remembered everything. That was the trick of him, though it never felt like one. He returned her life to her slightly altered, made shinier by the fact of his noticing. Even her laundry had started to seem like evidence of a person worth touching.

She stood in front of the mirror wedged between the kitchenette and the closet and tried to see what he would see when the screen was gone and the whole body had to arrive at once. On video she had angles. On video she had the mercy of framing, the ability to prop the phone, lean a shoulder, decide where light should hit. In person there would be the walk toward each other, the first second of recognition, the moment his face either did or did not brighten. She had thought about that second enough to hate herself a little.

Her black wrap dress lay on the bed, rejected. Too obvious. Too much like admitting she had changed the sheets for a man who had not even had his hands on her yet. The dark green sleeveless top stayed. Jeans, too. She chose the soft black bra with the underwire that no longer stabbed her in the left side and underwear that matched only in the forgiving sense of both being black. She put lotion on her knees. She put it on her elbows. She even rubbed a little on the scar on her left knee, as if he would notice it first thing and decide what kind of woman she was from there.

At five-thirty she stripped the bed again and remade one corner because the fitted sheet had come loose. That embarrassed her more than shaving had. She wasn't bringing him home. She knew that. She knew exactly how adult women were supposed to act about first dates after thirty-five, after divorce, after three weeks of hearing a man's voice in your ear and one explicit night on video that had ended with your thighs sticky and your whole body shocked by its own willingness. She knew all of it. Still, the clean towels were folded at the foot of the bed. Still, she had moved the laundry basket into the closet and shut the door on it with her hip.

At six-ten she sat at the tiny table with one sandal on and one sandal off, eating salted almonds she could not taste because she had read somewhere that drinking on an empty stomach made you look older by midnight. She was about to put on the second earring when Eli called.

It wasn't a text or a voice note. It was a call, his face there immediately, jostling in and out of frame.

"Hey," she said, already smiling at the mess of him, because he was outside somewhere loud and his hair looked flattened on one side. "You alive?"

The smile on his face was wrong. It arrived and then fell apart before it was fully built. Behind him she saw cinder block, a wedge of white van, light too hard to be evening. He had one earbud in and one hanging loose against his collarbone, the way he always wore them when he wanted to hear both her and the room. His thumb was scraping the torn skin beside one fingernail.

"I'm so sorry," he said.

The room didn't literally change temperature. That would have been easier. The fan still pushed hot air across her shins. A kid outside still screamed something about a foul. But inside the call a new pressure came down, clean and heavy.

"What happened?"

He tipped the phone and for a second all she got was glittering safety glass across a van seat and a black rubber weather strip hanging loose like torn licorice. Then his face again. "We were loading out. Ten minutes, maybe twelve. Somebody smashed the side window and took one of the cases. Not the whole van, thank God. Just the Pelican with the wireless packs and my interface and one stupid iLok that apparently runs half my week. I came back and it looked like a craft project in there."

He gave a little laugh at the end, automatic and bad. There was a red line across the heel of his hand where he'd cut himself on glass.

"Jesus." She was on her feet without deciding to stand. "Are you okay?"

"I'm fine. Pissed off, but fine. Nobody got hit. It just..." He dragged the back of his wrist over his mouth. "I'm at this garbage auto-glass place off Columbus now. And I can't make tonight. I hate even saying it like that. I hate it."

She looked down and saw the single earring still in her fingers. One sandal. Bare left foot on the peeling linoleum. Her whole body had been pointed toward seven-thirty for the last six hours and now had nowhere to go.

"Okay," she said, too quickly. Then, because that sounded like somebody giving him permission to disappoint her, "I mean, fuck, Eli, okay. Obviously. We can do another night."

"Len." His voice dropped, closer to the mic. "Don't do the nice voice with me. I know what tonight was."

That almost undid her. She sat back down because otherwise she would have had to brace herself on the table. "It's not a fake nice voice."

"Maybe not fake. Generous, then." He looked away from the screen, toward somebody yelling in the distance, then back. "I was really looking forward to seeing your actual face in three dimensions. I had a whole plan to pretend I wasn't nervous and then probably fail in under a minute."

She laughed once. The sound felt exposed. "You could still come later."

He shut his eyes for half a second. "I can't. They've got to vacuum out the whole door and tape it if the glass guy can't do it tonight, and I'm trying to figure out what I can borrow for tomorrow so I don't eat the job. I smell like hot wires and windshield glue. This is not my best debut."

"I don't care how you smell."

"I do." He smiled again, thinner this time. "I don't want our first real meeting to be me picking tempered glass out of my ass in a parking lot."

His joke should have broken the tension. Instead the parking lot rushed in around him: cut hand, hot-wire smell, tempered glass in his ass. He stopped seeming curated and started seeming stupidly real. Only an actual man with an actual body in an actual bad night would say ass to a woman he wanted and still sound embarrassed about disappointing her.

She asked what had been taken, and he told her, slower now, as if naming it made the loss solid: two receiver packs, the interface, cables he could replace without crying, the key with the software license on it that would make him want to strangle himself. The nouns meant enough to her by now. He had spent so many calls narrating the petty mechanics of load-ins and bad monitors and the particular misery of untangling cable somebody else had wrapped wrong. She could picture the case foam with the neat cutouts. She could picture his hands laying things in.

"Did you call the police?"

"Yeah. Guy took a statement like I was interrupting his smoke break. Venue manager filed one too." Somebody shouted behind him and he turned his head. "Hang on."

He looked back at her immediately, almost guilty for the second of inattention. "I gotta grab this. Len, I'm sorry."

"I know," she said.

The screen went black.

***

When the screen went black, the apartment got louder. She stood in the middle of it with her phone in one hand and the second earring in the other and felt foolish in a way that was not clean enough to call humiliation. Humiliation would have meant the story was already over. This was worse. This still wanted something.

She put on the other earring.

She left both on for maybe six minutes, standing at the sink with the tap running for no reason. Then she took them off, lined them up on the table, and read the two messages he had sent in the meantime.

need ten. auto-glass guy talking like i'm made of money

and hey. eat actual food. i'm serious

She should have let the second one soften her. Instead it irritated her enough that she called him back.

This time he answered from inside the van. Clear plastic had been taped over the missing window and breathed in and out with the night air. He looked grayer, the adrenaline burned off.

Outside, a SEPTA bus sighed at the corner. Somebody thumped down her stairs, then back up, then laughed. She looked at the clean line of her top in the dark window, at her own shoulder hovering over his face on the screen. "So what, you just need the window fixed? Or what?"

He let out a breath through his nose. It didn't feel like a performance. If it was one, it was beautiful work. "The window, yeah. And I can borrow one pack for tomorrow if I put down money. That's the part that's making me want to head-butt a wall. I can cover some of it. I'm not in rags over here. It's just a really ugly week for it."

"How ugly?"

"Lena."

"What?"

He watched her for a beat, the way he did when he wanted to make silence feel gentle instead of loaded. "Eat something, okay? You sound shaky."

The corner of her mouth twitched before she flattened it out. Caretake first, deflect second, answer third if the room required it. Usually she loved that about him. Right now it made her want to reach through the phone and grab the front of his shirt.

"I ate almonds."

"That's not food."

"Eli."

He glanced offscreen again. A forklift beeped somewhere behind him. When he looked back he looked tired in a way she had not seen before, not deeper exactly, just less arranged. "If the glass gets done tonight and I can charm the rental guy, I'm short three-fifty till Friday. Which sounds disgusting when I say it out loud, so I would love to rewind the last five seconds of my life."

There it was, worse than an ask in a way: a number with a date attached, hanging between them like a damp towel.

She thought of her checking account. Rent already gone. Electric due next week. A client invoice still outstanding because one producer believed paying on time was a moral weakness. Three hundred and fifty dollars was two long Saturdays at Farah's and one emergency evening restocking Rioja while her feet swelled in her shoes. It was also, if she let herself look at it that way, less than two percent of the trailer money sitting in savings with her mother's name still ghosting the paperwork in her head.

"I could send that," she said.

He stared at her.

"Len, no."

"I'm serious."

"So am I." The joke dropped out of his voice completely. "Absolutely not. I'm not having our first in-person night turn into you bailing me out because my week went to shit."

"You wouldn't be turning it into that. I would."

"That's not better."

She stood again, because sitting made her feel pinned. "It's a bridge. You just said till Friday."

"Yeah, and that means I can survive till Friday like every other idiot freelancer in America."

"You don't have to perform being noble for me."

His face changed at that, not much, but enough. Shame and pride crossed each other so fast they almost looked like the same thing. "I'm not performing. I'm trying not to be the guy who misses a date and then somehow ends up with your money."

It hit low in her ribs because it was exactly the right thing to say. It protected her. It also left the door open with almost painful courtesy.

Neither of them said anything for a second. She could hear street noise through his mic and her own fan chopping the room behind her into uneven pieces. Her earrings sat on the table like two tiny dropped screws.

"Okay," she said finally. "Fine. I'm not shoving anything at you right this second."

He rubbed the back of his neck. "Thank you."

"That wasn't me agreeing with you."

That got a real smile out of him, tired but real. "I know. I can hear the difference."

"Tell me what you ended up wearing tonight," he said after a moment, softer. "So I can hate myself accurately."

She looked down at the dark green top, the jeans, her bare feet on the linoleum. Desire and humiliation met so neatly in her chest it made her a little sick.

"Maybe later," she said.

"Fair." His mouth softened. "I'm sorry."

"I know."

When the call ended she stared at his thread until the words blurred. A minute later another message came through: his Cash App tag, then beneath it, don't, seriously. The warning only turned the screw. Worse, somehow, than if he'd asked outright.

Then she grabbed her bag and went to June's.

***

The air outside was barely cooler than the apartment and felt greasy on her upper lip where the wax strip had taken too much skin. Half the block was on stoops. A little girl in pink clogs dragged a jump rope through the gutter. Somebody had a game on loud enough that the announcer sounded like he lived inside the laundromat wall. Lena walked past all of it in the outfit she had chosen to be looked at in and hated every reflective surface that caught her.

June's place was three blocks and a turn away, basement stairs dropping beside a rowhouse with a dead planter on the stoop and a bike frame chained to the rail. Cooler hit her first when she opened the door, then nicotine, then the sharp plastic smell of overheated chargers. June's apartment always felt like a bunker somebody intelligent had been forced to improvise in. Cords. Old laptops. A folding desk crammed with two monitors and a chipped mug full of pens. A box fan shoved in the corner like an afterthought.

June sat in her rolling chair with one knee up, tearing a Wawa receipt into perfect little squares while something loaded on-screen. She always did that when she was thinking, reducing paper to neat confetti with nicotine-stained fingertips until the desk looked like it had lightly snowed. She had changed into basketball shorts and a faded union T-shirt and was eating dry Honey Nut Cheerios from a ceramic ramekin because, she claimed, milk after dark was demonic. Her dark hair was twisted up with a pencil.

She took one look at Lena and stopped chewing. "Jesus. He missed all that?"

Lena shut the door harder than she meant to. "Don't be funny."

"I'm not being funny. I'm being outraged on your behalf." June set the ramekin down. "What happened?"

Lena gave her the chronology too exactly. Six-fourteen call. She'd been about to leave. No, technically she had one shoe off, one on. Van window smashed after load-out. Pelican case gone. Auto-glass place on Columbus. Three-fifty short till Friday if the glass guy came through and the rental guy didn't act like a prick. She heard herself talking and knew she sounded like she was trying to make a police report convincing.

June let her get through it. Only then did she ask, "He told you three-fifty exactly?"

"Three-fifty. Exact. It wasn't a pitch."

"I didn't say it was." June picked up another piece of receipt. "What got taken?"

Lena told her.

"And the venue's not covering anything?"

"There'll be insurance or whatever eventually. He needs to float the ugly part now."

"Eventually," June said, flat. "Great word. Love eventually."

Lena took the only other chair, which had a sweatshirt on it and one dead AA battery in the seat crease. She moved both to the floor. "Can you not do that?"

"Do what?"

"That voice."

June pinched the next strip of receipt between two fingers and forgot to tear it. Her eyes moved from Lena's mouth to the smeared edge of her eyeliner and back. "I'm asking regular questions."

"You're asking them like I'm dumb."

"No." June leaned back and the chair complained under her. "If I thought you were dumb, I wouldn't bother with the questions."

June could get away with saying that because she used the same tone other people used for pass the salt. Lena rubbed her thumb hard across the inside of her wrist where lotion still made the skin feel coated. "He didn't ask me for money."

"Okay."

"He actively said no."

"Okay."

"And I said I could send it and he said no."

June took a Cheerio from the ramekin, pointed it at her like a tiny coin. "So what's the problem?"

Lena hated how cleanly that landed. "The problem is I still want to."

June crunched the Cheerio, thinking. "How much do you want him to have and how much do you want not to be the woman who sat at home in a full face because a man had a disaster?"

June had gotten so close to the center of it that Lena actually laughed, once and sharp. "Fuck you."

"Probably a mix, then."

"You are such an asshole."

"And still useful." June swiveled toward her monitor long enough to smack a key before the screen went dark. "Look. If you want me to say don't do it, I can say don't do it. Don't do it. There. Clean."

Lena stared at her.

June shrugged. "What, you wanted a TED Talk?"

"I wanted..." Lena stopped. She had wanted something embarrassingly specific. She had wanted June to say that helping was generous and adult and not the kind of thing women got conned into by accident. She had wanted absolution dressed as advice. Saying that out loud would have made it too naked to keep.

"You wanted me to tell you what kind of person it makes you," June said.

Lena looked up so fast it felt like getting caught.

June saw that and exhaled through her nose. "Sorry. That came out meaner than I meant it."

"No, it came out exactly how you meant it."

June pushed the chair back a little and sat straighter. The joke left her voice. When that happened she did not sound colder. She sounded cleaner. "Three hundred and fifty dollars is not a symbolic amount of money for you, Len."

"I know what three hundred and fifty dollars is."

"Do you, tonight?" June asked.

The room tightened. Somewhere overhead the people in the upstairs apartment dragged something heavy across the floor. The box fan clicked on one side with every rotation. Lena could smell the stale sweet cereal on June's breath, the cigarette smoke in the curtains, the electrical hotness of an overworked power strip. Everything in the basement was material. Nothing floated.

"I know exactly what it is," she said. "It's not a fucking GoFundMe. It's a bridge till Friday."

"For him."

"Yes, for him."

"The man you haven't stood next to yet."

There it was again: not cruel, not even wrong, just placed where it would do the most work.

Lena folded her arms, then unfolded them because that felt adolescent. "You're acting like I offered him my Social Security number and both kidneys."

"No. I'm acting like once you float him three-fifty, his name doesn't come up clean on your phone again." June kept her eyes on Lena and tapped the edge of Lena's bag with one fingernail.

That irritated Lena because it was good. She could feel June slotting her into place with it.

"You think he's lying."

"I think men tell the truth and still make a mess with your money every day of the week." June reached for the receipt pile again, then stopped and flattened her palm on the desk instead. "I don't know him. I know timing. I know panic costs extra. I know if a rental guy smells desperation he'll charge for the air in the room."

"He didn't ask."

"I heard you."

"So then why are you doing all this?"

June's mouth tightened at one corner. "Because if you hit send, I want you doing it awake. Not because I patted your head first."

Lena looked away. The monitor had gone to a screensaver of dark shapes sliding over darker shapes. Her own face in the reflection looked harder than she felt.

"What would you do?" she asked.

June snorted softly. "I would steal copper out of an abandoned building before I asked a person I wanted to fuck for bridge money."

Despite herself, Lena laughed. "You're disgusting."

"Correct." June leaned over, snagged the ramekin, and held it out. "Cheerio?"

"No."

"Suit yourself." She ate one, considered, then said, "But you're not me. Thank God for the city. If you want to help him, help him. Just don't wake up tomorrow acting like you only missed dinner. You'll be out three-fifty too."

Lena looked down at the phone in her lap and could feel the fabric of her top sticking between her shoulder blades.

"He said Friday," she said.

"Then Friday matters." June pointed toward Lena's bag with her chin. "If you send it and Friday comes with some fresh bullshit attached, clock that. Don't help him and then help him lie to you about it."

Lena was quiet.

June tilted her head. "How much were you thinking?"

"Three-fifty."

One of June's brows went up. "He said three-fifty."

"I know what he said."

"Okay." June let that sit for a beat. "So it's not just a bridge."

Shame came up fast enough to feel like heat. "Fuck off."

"I'm being serious."

"So am I."

June nodded once. "Then be serious all the way. Look at the amount before you hit it and call it yours."

That pissed Lena off because it was accurate and because accuracy from June had always felt a little proprietary, as if she had rights to Lena's motives by seniority. "You don't get to sound like you're above this. You've handed men cab fare and rent and cigarettes and whatever else because you felt bad for them."

"Yeah," June said. "And every single time I started checking the clock like a bill collector."

Silence again, not hostile yet, just loaded. The fan clicked. Upstairs somebody laughed at a joke from a TV audience. June reached up and removed the pencil from her hair. Her dark hair fell down over one shoulder in a tired wave, and for a second she looked younger, more like the girl Lena had known at twelve, ferocious and underslept and ready to tell a nun exactly where to shove her opinion.

"I don't think you're stupid," June said, quieter. "I think you're lonely and he's had a bad night and those are dangerous ingredients together."

Lena swallowed. June should have sent her back from the edge. Instead the edge came into view, clear and manageable. Dangerous ingredients. Fine. Adults used dangerous ingredients all the time. Adults burned the garlic a little and kept cooking.

"He's not using me," she said, hearing as soon as it left her mouth that nobody had asked.

"I didn't say he was."

"You meant it."

June's gaze slid past Lena to the dark monitor. She worried the softened edge of the receipt with her thumbnail before she answered. "Maybe I meant I don't know yet."

That was probably the most honest answer available, which made it useless.

Lena pulled out her phone.

June did not move. She did not lean in to see the screen or tell her not to. She took another Cheerio, bit it in half, and looked toward the dark monitor as if she had already said what she needed to say.

The gesture irritated Lena almost as much as if June had grabbed her wrist.

Eli's name sat at the top of the thread above the last message he'd sent ten minutes earlier: you okay? even now, even in the middle of his own mess, checking on her. Her throat tightened, but not with some generic movie ache. It was more specific than that. It felt like the stubborn rubber edge of a jar lid that had started to give. She could almost hear the seal breaking.

She opened her banking app. The checking balance looked smaller for being spoken aloud by the screen: $1,142.38. Rent gone. Phone bill still pending. A client payment overdue. Three hundred and fifty dollars would leave enough, technically. Enough was such a filthy word.

"You don't have to do it from checking," June said without looking at her. "You've got the trailer money."

Lena looked up.

June shrugged one shoulder. "I know. I'm not breaking news."

The mention of it made Lena's stomach turn. The trailer money lived in her head in stacked forms: Rosa's old vinyl cushions, the smell of mildew and sunscreen, the little dent in the screen door where her mother used to kick it shut with both hands full. She did not want Eli anywhere near that money, not even conceptually, not yet.

"I'm not touching savings for this."

"Good," June said. "Then you'll feel it."

"You are incredible."

"I know."

Lena went back to the phone. Her hands were steady. That offended her. She opened Cash App and pasted the tag he'd sent after their call. Eli Ward resolved under the little profile circle. don't, seriously sat right below it, which only made the idea settle more deeply.

She typed `350.00`.

The screen asked if she wanted to pull it from her linked checking account. Once it went through, it went through.

She thought of the date that had been supposed to start twelve minutes ago. By now the wine bar would be filling, somebody else's elbows on the chipped counter, fries going limp in a metal basket. She thought of him in a parking lot full of safety glass and black tape. She thought of Friday.

She hit Pay.

Cash App flashed green. A check mark bloomed, then confetti. `$350.00 sent to Eli Ward.`

For a second nothing happened. Then his thread jumped.

Len.

Another bubble.

Jesus Christ.

Then the voice note icon.

She pressed play.

His breathing came first, rough with movement, then his voice, lower than before. "I told you not to do that." A pause that sounded like him looking up at the sky or the roof of the van or whatever hard thing was nearest. "Thank you. Fuck. Thank you. I'm paying you back Friday even if I have to sell blood and one decent kidney. I hate that you did this and I also..." He exhaled. "I don't have a good sentence yet. Let me have a good sentence later. Please get yourself a drink anyway, okay? Even if it's just on your couch being furious at me."

The note ended there with all the splinters still on it, none of the prettier finish he usually knew how to lay on. The rough edge bought more of her trust than a smoother thank-you would have.

Then another text bubble came through.

tell me when it lands

June held out her hand and Lena, without thinking, passed over the phone. June read the short text bubbles, listened to none of the note, and handed it back.

"Well," she said.

"Well what?"

"He answered fast."

Lena slid the phone into her bag. The zipper caught in the lining and she had to tug twice to free it. June bent to gather the little white receipt squares she'd knocked off the desk, dropping them one by one back into the empty ramekin. Neither of them said anything until the fridge motor kicked on and off again.

"He'll send it back Friday," Lena said.

"Maybe." June stood, stretching until her shirt lifted and a pale strip of stomach showed above the shorts. "Maybe he will. Maybe he'll get weird. Maybe he'll love you forever and you'll tell this story at your disgusting wedding. I don't know." She nodded at Lena's bag. "Keep the receipt."

Lena's mouth gave up and bent. "You're unbearable."

"And yet here you are." June went to the fridge, opened it, stared in, and came back with two cans of lime seltzer because there was apparently nothing else in there worth the trip. She handed one over. "You're not telling Gabe."

It wasn't a question. Lena bristled, then stopped, because she already knew June was right.

"No."

"Farah either."

"Obviously not."

"Good." June cracked her can. "Gabe'll make it a whole man-trust seminar and Farah will ask for his social, blood type, and dental records."

"She would not ask for his dental records."

"She would if she thought he had decent teeth."

A real laugh broke out of Lena, and the relief behind it hit hard enough that her eyes stung. She tipped the seltzer can to her mouth before anything wetter could happen. It tasted aggressively of fake lime and aluminum. She looked around the basement apartment at the cords, the screens, the little paper squares June had torn from the receipt until they formed a drift near the keyboard. Real life sat everywhere in the room. Bills. Dust. Sugar cereal. Work tabs waiting to be reopened. Her own night had become part of that mess now.

After a while she stood to leave. The outfit felt different on her body, no longer anticipatory, not exactly wasted either. Just reassigned.

"You sure you're okay?" June asked, and for once the question was plain enough that Lena couldn't hear the angle in it, if there was one.

"Yeah." She adjusted the strap of her bag. "I mean. No. But yeah."

June nodded like that answer was respectable. "Text me if he says anything weird."

"He won't."

"Sure."

At the door Lena turned. "Thanks."

June lifted her can in a tiny salute. "Go wash your face. You're gonna sleep in all that and wake up furious."

Outside, the block had thinned a little. Night had come down properly, though the brick still breathed heat. Lena walked home with the can sweating in her hand and her phone heavy in her bag.

In the apartment the clean sheets waited under the fan's crooked wind. She set down the can, took off her sandals, and pulled the transfer receipt back up without turning on the overhead light. She sent the screenshot he'd asked for, then left the transfer receipt open beside her on the sheet while the room darkened and the fan worried one corner of the pillowcase loose again.
