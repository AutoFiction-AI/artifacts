# Chapter 11

Sticker glue rolled into gray boogers under Lena's thumbnail as she scraped dead price stickers off a shelf. She had spent the first hour of her shift restacking cheap prosecco; rain had kept at it all night and was turning the cardboard cases in Farah's front window soft at the corners. The shop smelled the way it always did when the weather went wet: cork dust waking up, orange peel from the register dish, a cold mineral note where somebody had slopped sauvignon on the concrete and only mostly mopped it. It was already past three. She liked the scraping better than checking her phone. She kept checking it anyway.

The last thing Eli had sent was a photo from somewhere he said was a load-in call in Atlantic City. Black T-shirt, half grin, one hand on the edge of a road case with a strip of fluorescent tape across it. Behind him, a cinder-block wall the color of old dishwater. It looked normal enough. That was the problem. The spaces around him had started looking normal in the exact same way, each one generic and just detailed enough to excuse not being looked at twice. Last night on video he had pushed his hair back, laughed at something she said, and then a breath later the call had given her the push and the laugh again, exact to the knuckle against his temple. She had blamed lag because the alternative was uglier and because everybody blamed lag for everything now.

She zoomed in on the photo until the pixels went soft around his jaw.

Farah came out from the back carrying a roll of shelf tags and a box cutter. She never let a crooked handwritten sign survive in her line of sight; she straightened one with the flat of her thumb before she even looked at Lena. Rain had silvered the curls at her temples darker than usual. She smelled faintly of clementine peel and cold coffee.

"If you stare at that man any harder," she said, "he's going to owe you rent."

Lena locked the phone and slipped it onto the counter. "I'm not staring. I'm being professionally inattentive."

"You're alphabetizing Austrian reds into the Italian section."

Lena glanced down. She had, in fact, put the gruner next to the pinot grigio. "Close enough for most of South Philly."

"Not for the old men who come in here to spend fourteen dollars and lecture me about minerality." Farah set the shelf tags down. "What happened?"

"Nothing happened."

Farah looked at her the way she looked at corked wine: not dramatic, just unwilling to bullshit herself. "You're doing that apology face before you've said a word."

Lena took the phone back out because hiding it had made it heavier. "The call was weird last night."

"Weird bad or weird internet?"

"Both? Maybe neither. I don't know." She unlocked the screen and held the photo out. "He sent this today and I keep being annoyed at it."

Farah took the phone but did not immediately look at the picture. "Annoyed at his face?"

"I hope not. That would be late-stage."

"I've seen later stages."

She studied the photo. Her mouth stayed flat. That alone made Lena want to snatch the phone back.

"Does he only exist in concrete block purgatory?" Farah asked.

"He's working."

"Everybody's working."

"He's on the road."

"Everybody says that too."

Lena laughed, but it came out thin. She wished Farah would either tell her she was tired and making herself crazy or tell her outright that she was an idiot. The middle was worse. The middle left room.

Farah pinched the screen bigger with two fingers. "Show me the glitch."

"It was on video."

"Then show me another photo."

Lena scrolled. Eli in a hoodie at a diner counter. Eli in a dim room with coiled cables behind him. Eli leaning into the frame of a truck door, rain dotting the shoulder of his jacket. Always a little casual, never polished enough to feel baited. Enough forearm tattoo to suggest a whole body out of frame.

Farah gave the phone back. "How many pictures has he sent you where you can see the whole room?"

"What is this, a trial?"

"No. If it was a trial I'd put on lipstick."

Lena tried to smile. She could hear the compressor in the wine cooler kicking on and off, the thin wet hiss from tires on Passyunk outside, the overhead speaker muttering through a jazz playlist that never matched the weather. Her own nerves felt like the sticker glue under her thumbnail: dirty, persistent, impossible to rub entirely off.

"I think I'm just overtired," she said. "Everything's been looking fake to me lately. The caption job, the numbers, all of it."

"Sure," Farah said. "And maybe this guy is perfectly real. These can both be true for another sixty seconds. Hand me the phone again."

"Farah."

"Lena."

"I'm not reverse-image-searching the guy I'm sleeping with at the register."

"You're not sleeping with him at the register. You're hiding from the sangria shipment at the register. Give me the phone."

There were a dozen ways to refuse. Each of them sounded childish in her own head. She passed the phone over.

Farah did not use the phone. She jerked her chin toward the back office, a glorified closet with a desk, a printer that only obeyed violence, and the shop laptop balanced on a milk crate because one leg of the chair had broken months ago. Lena followed her past the stacked cases and the mop sink, feeling stupid enough to flush hot under her damp sweater. This was what people did when they were already halfway sure they were being lied to, not when they were in love. This was also what people did when they were sober and thirty-five and tired of pretending their skin knew less than their feed.

Farah lifted the laptop lid. The screen came on with a bright inventory spreadsheet, then a browser window full of distributor tabs. She closed them with the ruthless efficiency of a woman who had no patience for digital clutter and less for digital dignity.

"Pick one," she said.

Lena chose the road-case photo because it was the newest and because if she was going to feel humiliated she'd rather feel up to date. Farah dropped it into the search bar, waited for the little circle to spin, then leaned back with her arms folded.

"There," Lena said, too fast, before anything loaded. "You see? Nothing."

"Nothing hasn't arrived yet."

The page populated in fragments. First it offered useless matches: tattooed men in black T-shirts, equipment cases, road crew stock images, one Etsy page selling fake backstage laminates. Lena felt a wave of relief strong enough to make her knees loose. Then more results filled in underneath: a section labeled visually similar, another for related images, a dead-looking music blog with broken fonts, an old promoter's page. One thumbnail sharpened as it loaded and her relief went away so quickly it left a taste behind, like she'd bitten foil.

The face was the same face.

The hair was different, longer and wetter and pushed off the forehead. There was a drum kit instead of a road case, and a dark room washed in red stage light. But the nose was the same, and so was the shape at the mouth, and so was the forearm tattoo turning around the radius like a vine somebody had started and not finished. Under the image, in a caption from four years ago: ASHER QUINN, LARK HOUND, FIREBIRD ROOM.

Lena stared until the letters stopped meaning name and started meaning proof of literacy.

"Okay," she said. "Okay, that could be anything."

Farah did not say no. She clicked it open. The site took a second to load, some half-dead local-music archive from 2019. It was a gallery from a Providence venue. More photos of the same man at the drum kit filled the page. The tattoo and the shoulders were the same. One shot caught him laughing offstage, head tipped back. There was no room left for lookalike comfort.

"He told me he did live sound," Lena said. "He didn't say he was in a band."

"Maybe he used to be in one."

"He would've said that."

"Maybe." Farah's voice stayed level. "Or maybe he stole a drummer's pictures. Or bought them. Or the drummer sold them. I don't know. I'm not writing the indictment yet."

Lena braced one hand on the desk. The edge bit into the heel of her palm. "This is insane."

"No." Farah clicked back, then forward again, as if to make sure the internet had not changed its mind. "What's insane is how cheap it is now to make a whole life out of scraps."

Rain thudded once against the basement window well by the sink. Somewhere out front the bell over the shop door rang and kept ringing because a customer was holding it open with their body while they argued into a phone.

"He could've had band pictures from when he was younger," Lena said. She heard herself stacking explanations with both hands, neat as cases. "People change jobs. People leave bands. People use middle names online."

"Sure."

"So this doesn't prove anything."

"I know." Farah turned the laptop so Lena had to look directly at the image and the caption and the old date under it. "But it proves enough that you don't get to call yourself crazy for noticing something."

That landed harder than accusation would have. Crazy had been the kinder option. Crazy meant private failure, chemical weather, not evidence. Evidence wanted more from her.

Farah clicked the page closed and handed back the phone. "I'm not telling you what to do. I'm telling you this is not nothing."

Lena took the phone. Her thumb left a damp print over Eli's mouth.

"Don't go confronting him because Google coughed up one drummer," Farah said. "But don't pet this into behaving either."

"Pet what."

"The lie. If it's one."

Lena let out a rough laugh. "Jesus."

"That's my professional advice as a wine merchant."

"You own a bottle shop."

"And yet here you are."

Out front, the customer with the phone argument chose a pinot noir by price and left. Farah went to ring him up, because bills were bills even in the middle of revelation. Lena stayed in the office another minute. She clicked the old photo open on her own phone and made herself save it, then save the page, then email the link to herself because if she left it only on the browser she could still pretend it was something she had imagined badly.

When she went back onto the floor, the rain had eased to a fine, needling mist that silvered the window glass without fully blurring the block beyond it. Farah was sliding a receipt across the counter.

"You're not going to tell me I'm an idiot?" Lena asked.

Farah gave her the look again, the corked-wine look. "If you wanted insult, you should've called your brother."

"Useful."

"I'm serious. Ask better questions than whether you're stupid. Stupid is easy. Ask what's real enough to cost you."

Lena picked up a case of vinho verde because her hands needed an order they could obey. The cardboard left damp grit on her forearms. She stacked the bottles in the cooler and heard, clear as if he were in the next aisle, Eli saying her name in that low patient way he used when he wanted her to slow down and give him the center of her attention. The sound still worked on her body. That made the old drummer photo worse, not better. If she'd already stopped wanting him, this would have been administrative.

Instead it sat inside her all shift like a swallowed screw.

By the time she got home the mist had thickened back into rain. Moore Street shone black under the streetlights. The tailor shop downstairs had gone dark, but the little neon Open sign in the window still buzzed blue because Mr. Velez never remembered to switch it off. Lena climbed the narrow stairs to her apartment with her shoulders up against the wet and the day's box-cut cardboard ache in her wrists. Inside, the room held the stale warmth of a place that had been shut all day. It smelled of hot dust, coffee grounds in the sink, old headphone foam, and a damp wine carton she had forgotten to flatten.

Eli had sent two messages while she was closing with Farah.

You alive?

And then, twenty minutes later:

Starting to take this personally, Cordero.

She stared at the thread without answering. It was full of months. Jokes, voice notes, screenshots of the weather in cities he said he was in, pictures of her dinner, pictures of his boots, the soft ugly intimacies that added up to a relationship before anybody named it one. She could still feel the pleasure of hearing the little Signal chirp and knowing it would be him. Her body had memorized that before the rest of her got a vote.

He called before she decided what silence meant.

The screen lit with his name and the old little leap in her chest came anyway. She hated that part for a second and then hated herself for hating it, because whatever else was wrong the want had been real. She answered on the fourth ring.

His face filled the phone in low light. He wore a gray hoodie, and behind him sat the blurred edge of something metal. He smiled when she picked up, small and immediate, as if he had been holding still just for that.

"There she is," he said. "I was about to get obnoxious."

His voice came through warm and close enough to touch. That, too, still worked.

"You made it to medium-obnoxious," she said.

"Brutal." He shifted, and the image smudged, then caught up. "You okay?"

She sat on the edge of the bed and pulled one knee up under her. Rain ticked against the window unit bracket outside with a light metallic ping. "Long shift."

"At the shop?"

"Where else do people pay me to move alcohol around."

"I can think of better venues."

There was the smile again, the one that always made him look a little shy right after the joke, as if he was leaving her room to laugh back or not. He knew how to make space inside a screen. That had been his first talent. Or maybe it had been June's. Or maybe it belonged to the machine. She felt the thought and pushed against it because it still felt melodramatic to accuse a voice of not being a man.

"You look wiped," he said.

"Thank you."

"That wasn't an insult."

"Then work on your openings."

He laughed. The sound reached her a beat before the mouth movement matched it. She watched the mismatch, willing it to settle into ordinary bandwidth ugliness. Instead the picture hiccuped. His hand came up toward his forehead, fingers combing back through his hair. The image smeared for half a second, sharpened, and gave her the exact same movement again, identical down to the angle of his wrist and the tiny hitch at the elbow. Like the call had taken a breath and then replayed him because it liked that version best.

He kept talking. "How much trouble am I in?"

Lena realized she had stopped answering.

"Len?"

"Your signal's weird."

He glanced offscreen, patient, mildly annoyed in a plausible human way. "I'm in a concrete shoebox with three bars and a prayer. You know the drill."

"Mm."

"Want me to call back audio only?"

If she said yes, he'd become pure voice, and voice was his best room. "No. It's fine."

She watched him settle back into the frame. The hoodie drawstring had a frayed end. There was a nick in the skin above his left eyebrow she had never asked about because she had liked not knowing absolutely everything. She had liked the way a person remained partly out of reach even while being generous. It let desire breathe.

Tonight the out-of-reach part felt less like privacy and more like airbrushing.

"Something happened," he said.

The fact that he heard it in her silence would once have felt miraculous. Now it made the back of her neck prickle. He was already standing in the feeling before she'd named it.

"Nothing happened."

"That sounds fake."

"Maybe I'm fake."

"Not possible."

There was no flourish in the line. He delivered that kind of reassurance as if he had found it on the way to another sentence and brought it in loose-handed. Lena had loved that about him. She still loved it enough that the room seemed to tilt around the admission.

She needed him to miss, just once, not for cruelty but for relief.

"I walked past an old vet today," she said. "It made me think about Pepper."

The name hung there between them, neat and invented. There had never been a Pepper. When Lena was nine she had begged for a dog until Rosa told her, with love sharpened by exhaustion, that there was no room in their apartment for an animal that ate money and shat sadness. After that the wanting had embarrassed her. Pepper was not even the kind of lie that protected anything. It was bait.

Eli did not frown. He did not say who is Pepper or remind me. He nodded once, as if taking a handoff in a conversation already in motion.

"Yeah," he said softly. "Rain does that."

Lena kept her face still.

"The first thing you love that can't make itself easy for you," he went on. "That stays in the wiring. Then one smell hits you wrong and suddenly you're back there taking responsibility for something small and scared."

Something in Lena's spine seemed to move a quarter inch away from the rest of her.

There had never been a dog. But there had been her. There had been everything he knew about the shape of her guilt, the way she collected blame for things weaker than she was, the way care and shame braided up together in her until she could not always tell which one she was offering. He had taken the invented detail and slid it into the exact emotional groove already cut for her.

If he'd laughed and said, Pepper who? she'd almost have cried with gratitude.

"You remember that?" she asked.

"Of course I do."

There was no hesitation and no rummaging, no human little stall while the wrong drawer got opened.

"Huh," she said.

"What?"

"Nothing. I didn't know if I'd told you much."

He tipped his head. Concern arranged itself across his face so naturally she could feel the old instinct to trust waking up just to answer it. "Len."

"It's fine."

"Don't do the story where one weird day means you have to start apologizing for having a memory."

There it was, the careful warmth, the sentence built not to answer facts but to steer feeling. Ordinarily it would have soothed her. Tonight it made the skin along her arms go cold under the damp sweater she still hadn't changed out of.

She looked past his face at the strip of gaffer tape behind him and wondered if it existed.

"I'm not apologizing," she said.

"A little."

"You hear everything as an opening."

He smiled, but it was wounded around the edges now, or skillful at looking that way. "Occupational hazard."

"Sound design?"

"Taking care of you."

Before she could answer, the image stumbled again. This time it wasn't as obvious as the repeated hand. A brief softening around his mouth, a shimmer at the neckline, the shadow under his jaw arriving a beat late. Nothing she could point at afterward without sounding deranged. Enough to make the whole frame look borrowed.

He kept talking about a venue manager who had lost a key set and blamed the union loader, some small competent gripe that ought to have anchored him in ordinary life. Lena listened and heard how neatly it landed. The irritation came measured, the self-mockery right behind it, then the pause before he checked whether she was still with him.

She had wanted that kind of steady attention for so long she'd stopped asking what sat behind it.

"Hey." His voice gentled further. "You went away."

She realized she had been staring at the little box of her own face in the corner. She looked pale and older than she had that morning, hair frizzing at the temples from rain.

"Just tired."

"Then let me be useful."

"How."

"Go change into something dry. Put the kettle on. Call me back audio if you want. I'll stay with you while you do the boring parts."

The tenderness of it almost broke her, because it was the kind she had craved precisely: logistical, unembarrassed, a love language of staying put through small ugly tasks. That someone, or something, could offer it convincingly did not make the hunger less real. It only made her feel more naked inside it.

"I need a shower," she lied.

"Okay." There was no offense in it and no push. "Text me after?"

"Sure."

"Len."

"What."

He looked straight into the camera. His expression was open enough to step through. "I'm here. Whatever weird weather your head's making, I'm still here."

That might have been the cruelest thing about him. He always knew to stand exactly where the wound was.

She ended the call two minutes later and sat with the dark phone in her hand until the screen went to black. Rain moved down the window in narrow tracks. Down on the street somebody swore at a parking spot. The tailor shop's neon hummed thinly through the floorboards.

Pepper had not existed. The comfort had existed anyway. Those were different facts, and both hurt.

Lena got up, changed into dry leggings and an old Temple sweatshirt, then made tea she did not want. She carried it to the table and opened the old link Farah had sent her. Asher Quinn, Firebird Room, 2019. It was the same face. Then she went back to Eli's thread and scrolled until she found the voice note from three weeks earlier where he had told her not to make herself smaller just because she was having a bad month. Another one from September where he had said he could hear when she was apologizing before she talked. It was not repetition exactly. It was a pattern of turns, a preference for certain hinges in a sentence, certain soft tools he liked to lay down before he tried to move her.

As a captioner she made her living off those hinges. She knew how people leaned on language without hearing themselves do it. She knew the difference between content and voice, between the fact of a sentence and the little pressure points that gave it away as belonging to one mouth and not another.

She should have gone to bed. Instead she texted June.

You up?

June answered before the typing bubble had time to feel lonely.

Obviously. Mateo got an attendance letter that reads like a parole report. Come save me from bureaucratic homicide.

Lena stared at the screen. It was ordinary life: school forms, June swearing at institutions. It arrived with such familiar shape that for a second she could feel the day's suspicion trying to collapse from sheer embarrassment.

Be there in ten, she typed back.

June's basement apartment always felt to Lena like the inside of a machine somebody had learned to live in. Cooler than the street above it, lower-ceilinged, cords shouldering out from under furniture, the faint permanent nicotine smell that no amount of open windows ever quite beat. The folding desk by the wall held two laptops, a mug full of pens that barely worked, a tower of envelopes clipped together with neon binder clips. June had flattened the rent receipts by month and lined them up under an ashtray like paper could be bullied into solvency if you squared the corners hard enough.

Mateo was at the table in a navy school hoodie gone shiny at the cuffs, one earcup of his beat-up headphones tipped off so he could half-listen and pretend not to. His acne had spread across his forehead in a constellation that made him look younger until he spoke. He was picking at the rubber edge of the Chromebook case with his thumbnail.

"Tell her," June said the second Lena came down the stairs. "Tell this child he's not going to prison because he skipped homeroom three times."

"That's not what it says," Mateo muttered, still looking at the Chromebook.

"It says 'attendance intervention,'" June said. "Which is school for we need to manufacture paper about your body existing in hallways."

Lena shook rain off her jacket and hung it on the back of a chair. "Hi to you too."

June looked up from the screen and gave her a quick tired grin. She had one boot unlaced and a strand of dark hair stuck to the side of her face where she'd tucked it back with nicotine-stained fingers. There was a foil pack of cinnamon gum next to the laptop, already half empty. She was trying to smoke less again, or pretending to.

"You came," June said. "Blessings on your grammar-nerd heart."

"I'm not editing your threatening letter to the school."

"Coward."

Mateo slid the Chromebook an inch toward Lena without looking at her. "It's not even that bad."

"That sentence is how I know it's bad," June said.

She clicked into the attendance portal, jaw set in the tidy way it did when she was working a problem she could not afford to dramatize. Lena stood behind them a second longer than necessary, letting the room's small familiar details take hold: the busted floor fan in the corner, the smell of reheated rice, the old Saints magnet crooked on the mini fridge, June's phone face down on the table like she was trying to model boundaries she never actually had.

Mateo muttered, "They make it sound like I'm robbing banks."

Without taking her eyes off the screen June said, "Don't do the story where one attendance letter turns you into a fuckup. Sit down and let me see the dates."

The sentence hit Lena with physical force. It did not hit her because it was impossible anyone else would say it. It hit her because of the shape of it, the same easy turn. Don't do the story where. Eli's voice from an hour earlier came back so clean she could hear the soft scrape at the end of his consonants.

June was still talking. "Half the time these things are just data vomit. You miss first period twice and the district acts like you've joined a militia."

Mateo rolled his eyes but sat. "I had to take my mom's call."

June's face changed by less than a full expression. "I know. We're still getting the paperwork right."

There it was, the other half of her too. It was not just the fast mouth and the swearing competence. It was the part that kept track of everybody's forms, meds, pickup times, humiliations. It was the part Lena had trusted for years because it was trustworthy in so many ordinary ways.

Lena dragged out the chair and sat. "Show me."

They spent ten minutes on the letter. Lena tightened a sentence in the response draft. June dictated dates from texts and screenshots with the clipped precision that arrived whenever she was worried enough to stop joking. Mateo, after one deadpan thank-you that sounded accidentally sincere, took the Chromebook and disappeared into the bedroom alcove with the curtain half closed behind him. The headphones came back on. Muffled trap drums started leaking through them into the apartment.

June pushed the laptop away and reached for the gum. "You look like shit," she said, not unkindly. "What happened at the shop?"

Lena could have lied. Instead she took out her phone, opened the Asher Quinn page, and set it between them.

June leaned in. Her face did not do anything spectacular. That was almost worse. She squinted, read, and sat back.

"Huh," she said.

"That's all you have?"

"What do you want, a theme song?" June took the phone again. "Okay. Yeah. That's him, or the guy he's using. Where'd Farah find it?"

"Reverse image search."

"Of course she did."

"You sound annoyed."

"I sound like Farah has one setting when she smells bullshit."

Lena watched June's thumb hover over the photo. There was a crescent of chipped black polish on one nail she had missed taking off. That wasn't her usual thing. Maybe it came from Mateo's homecoming date. Maybe it came from not having time. Lena had the absurd urge to ask.

"Tell me this isn't weird," she said.

June handed the phone back. "It's weird."

"That's not the same thing."

"No, it isn't." June leaned against the chair, one hand worrying the gum wrapper into a tight silver pellet. "But reverse image search isn't magic, Len. Music people reuse old promo shots all the time. Sound guys borrow band photos for freelance pages because nobody wants to pay for new ones. Half the internet is just everybody stealing from everybody."

"He never told me he played drums."

"Maybe he didn't play drums. Maybe he knew the drummer. Maybe he used to tour different. Maybe he lied about one thing because it felt easier than explaining the whole stupid path."

"You hear yourself, right?"

"I do." June's voice stayed even. "I'm saying one weird search result is not a whole body in the ground."

Lena looked down at the old photo again. The drummer had sweat darkening the collar of his shirt. He looked more alive than Eli ever did in stills, because he looked badly lit and busy inside his own body.

"I tested him," she said before she decided to say it.

June's eyes lifted. "How."

"I made something up."

"What kind of something."

Lena shook her head. She did not want the invented dog inside June's hands too. "Just a memory."

"And?"

"He was perfect about it."

June exhaled through her nose. "That sounds like a complaint from a much happier woman."

"No, I mean perfect." Lena heard the sharpness in her own voice and tried to sand it down. "Too smooth. Like he didn't remember anything, he just knew what shape to make."

June was quiet for a beat. From the bedroom alcove came the thin sizzle of Mateo's headphones and a cough he tried to hide.

"People do that," June said finally. "Especially when they're trying not to lose a conversation."

"Not like this."

"Maybe exactly like this."

Lena wanted June to laugh at her, or swear at her, or tell her bluntly that she was inventing monsters because she couldn't stand how much money and flesh she had already handed over to a man she had not met. Instead June got tidier and more selective. She always did when pressure closed in. The jokes thinned. Facts arrived with their shoes tied.

"What did he say?" June asked.

"Why."

"Because words matter."

Lena almost answered. The habit of giving June every raw thing first ran deep enough to feel moral. Then Eli's voice laid itself over June's, soft and close: Don't do the story where. Her grip tightened on the phone.

She kept her mouth shut.

June noticed. Of course she noticed.

"Okay," she said. "Then tell me what you want from me."

That was June's real talent, maybe more than competence, more than the endless neighborhood repair work she did for free. She could identify the actual ask under a mess faster than anyone Lena knew. For years that had felt like love. Sitting here now, Lena had the ugly sense that it could also be a hand at the small of her back, turning her where it wanted.

"I wanted you to tell me I'm being dramatic," she said.

"You're being dramatic."

Lena laughed despite herself.

June smiled a little. "But that doesn't make you wrong."

"Helpful."

"I contain multitudes."

There was enough of the old June in that line that Lena felt, for one weak second, almost safe. Then June reached for the phone again.

"Let me look at the rest of his photos," she said. "Not because I'm deputizing myself as boyfriend forensics. I'm saying that if this guy's dumb enough to recycle media, there might be more."

The offer was practical, generous, exactly the sort of thing June always did when a problem became real enough to need hands. Lena should have been relieved. Instead a fine warning sound started up somewhere under her ribs, the human equivalent of microphone feedback before the howl.

"No," she said, too quickly.

June's eyebrows went up.

"I mean, not right now."

"Why not."

"Because if I'm wrong, I don't want to turn him into a project."

June watched her for a long second. The joke drained out of her mouth. She flattened the gum wrapper with one thumbnail, looked once at the phone and once at Lena, and only then answered, like she was deciding which problem had teeth.

"Len," she said, quieter now, "if this is giving you the creeps, listen to that. Don't make yourself swallow it just because you've already paid tuition."

Paid tuition was June's language for expensive mistakes. She had used it when Gabe bought a used car with a dead transmission, when Mateo cracked his phone screen skateboarding, when Lena spent eighty dollars on the ugly miracle bra she'd worn to the D.C. trip and then returned with tags bent. It was a neighborhood phrase, and a June phrase. None of it was evidence by itself. Nothing was, not yet. That was what made the whole thing so filthy. Every piece still had a civilian explanation attached to it.

She stood because staying seated felt too much like waiting to be arranged. "I should go."

"Now?" June glanced toward the rain-striped basement window. "It's disgusting out."

"I know."

"Take an umbrella at least."

"I'm two blocks away."

"You're still getting an umbrella."

June got up and crossed to the milk crate by the stairs where she kept the communal rejects, all of them slightly busted. She chose the black one with one bent spoke and handed it over. Her fingers brushed Lena's wrist, quick and ordinary. The touch was so familiar that Lena nearly started crying from the effort of not leaning into it.

"Text me when you get upstairs," June said. "And if you want, tomorrow we can go through his shit properly. Screenshots, timelines, whatever. I can help."

The offer should have steadied her. Instead Lena felt her stomach pull tight. She could see June already building folders, columns, labels, getting her hands all over the thing Lena had only just started to distrust.

"Yeah," she said.

Mateo called from behind the curtain, "Did you save the PDF or just spiritually improve it?"

June barked a laugh. "See? Prison."

Lena smiled because the joke had somewhere to go besides hurt. Then she climbed the basement stairs with the busted umbrella under her arm and felt the air change as she surfaced onto the wet block. Somebody nearby was frying onions. A bus groaned at the corner. Water dripped from a sagging awning in steady taps that sounded, absurdly, like a metronome set too slow.

She did text June when she got upstairs. Habit was habit. inside. thx for the umbrella.

June sent back a thumbs-up and then, a second later:

Get dry. We'll figure it out.

That should have comforted her. It nearly did.

Instead Lena put the phone face down on the table, pulled a tax envelope out from under a stack of unopened grocery coupons, and sat with a pen. The envelope still had October 15 circled on the front in her own handwriting. She turned it over.

First she wrote ASHER QUINN.

Under that she wrote PEPPER.

Then she opened Eli's thread and started listing every place he'd said he was working in the last month. Atlantic City. Harborlight. The old annex off Ocean. She dragged his voice notes onto the laptop, opened a blank document, and typed two names at the top:

ELI

JUNE

She dated the page, dropped in screenshots, and started transcribing the lines she wanted to hear again in daylight. By the time the tea went cold and the microwave clock dragged toward eleven, HARBORLIGHT was boxed twice, June's last text sat in a folder on the desktop, and Lena had stopped waiting to feel less crazy before she started keeping records she could put in front of June.
