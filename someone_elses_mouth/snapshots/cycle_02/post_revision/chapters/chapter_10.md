# Chapter 10

At 8:41 her phone lit on the pillow beside her face. Three missed calls. Two emails. One text from Eli sent at 2:13 a.m.

The rain had started before dawn and kept finding new surfaces to worry at. It ticked on Lena's window unit, hissed in the alley behind the tailor shop, ran down the brick across from her in long black seams that looked, half asleep, like somebody had put the whole block under subtitles and then let them blur.

`sorry baby. hit a wall. didn't mean to vanish on you`

She had fallen asleep with one earbud still in, waiting for the call he'd said he could make after load-out. She remembered his last message before that, a picture of coiled cable on a concrete floor and the toe of his boot, and then nothing. She had lain awake anyway, stupidly alert, the body still keeping time for somebody who was not there. When sleep finally took her it had done it all at once, face mashed into the pillow, lamp still on, the half-done caption file open on the desk by the window.

The missed calls were from Nora Patel at BlueArc Learning. The first voicemail had come in clipped and courteous. The second had lost the courtesy.

Lena sat up too fast, the earbud falling into the blanket, and opened the newest email.

Subject: URGENT - Reel 4 export issue

She did not need to finish reading the message to know she had fucked herself. The client had uploaded the training package before final QA because they were behind and trusted her. Reel 4 was offset almost two seconds after minute eighteen. Speaker IDs had slipped. Worst of all, one caption in the warehouse safety module now read:

Do stand beneath a suspended load.

Lena had dropped the `not`.

It sat there on the screen with obscene confidence, a tiny missing word with a whole skull under it.

She got out of bed and crossed the four steps to the desk. The apartment smelled like damp dust and old coffee. Yesterday's jeans hung over the chairback, still wet at the cuffs from the walk home. Her keyboard had a crescent of bread crumbs in it. She rubbed at the pale band on her right hand where her mother's ring had been for years and then wasn't.

Nora's second email was already there beneath the first.

We pulled the file from the portal. Please confirm immediate correction. Rush bonus removed and QA recovery hours deducted from invoice. We are moving next Thursday's live webinar to another captioner.

Revised payment: $412.

The file had been worth $585.

One hundred seventy-three dollars gone because she had stayed awake for a man with a beautiful voice and then slept through a client's call like a college freshman with pink-eye and no alarm clock. She stared at the revised number until it stopped looking like money and started looking like accusation.

There were nine days until October fifteenth. Four thousand two hundred still owed between federal and state. Three thousand one hundred living on the Visa statement like mold. The trailer money was already bitten into. The ring was gone. Another Wednesday live webinar was gone too, which was worse than the docked invoice because BlueArc's live work paid fast and never all at once enough, which made it useful.

Her phone buzzed again. Eli.

`You awake now? Want me to call?`

Lena typed `rough morning` and deleted it. She typed `I missed a client call because I was waiting for you to maybe love me in real time` and deleted that too. What she sent was:

`fixing work shit. talk later`

Three dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again. Then nothing.

She opened the caption software, loaded the project, and put her headphones on. The waveform rolled out across the monitor in neat blue hills. The instructor in the video was a cheerful man in an orange vest standing in a warehouse aisle, wagging a hand at a camera nobody would remember. Lena watched his mouth move and heard the disaster she had sent into the world. The captions followed him drunk and haunted. At 00:18:42 the line hit exactly where it should have and still said the wrong thing.

Do stand beneath a suspended load.

"Jesus Christ," she said to the empty room.

Rainwater tapped the metal lip of the window unit in a rhythm almost but not quite like a notification. She fixed the missing word, retimed the drift in Reel 4, corrected a speaker ID she'd somehow slid from Narrator to Supervisor and back again, exported, checked, exported again. Every minute she wasn't looking at her phone she imagined it lighting. Every minute she looked, it stayed dark.

At 9:26 Nora finally called back. Lena answered before the second ring.

"Hi, Nora. I'm so sorry."

Nora did not waste time telling her not to apologize. "I need the corrected package in twenty minutes and I need to know this isn't where your bandwidth is right now."

Lena looked at the room around her, at the damp socks on the floor, the uncovered mug, the tax envelope under the lamp. "It isn't," she lied.

"Okay. Because the client is already jumpy. That safety line was bad."

"I know."

"And the missed call didn't help."

Lena swallowed. The rain on the window got louder for a second, as if the block had leaned in. "I know."

Nora exhaled through her nose. She did not sound cruel. She sounded worse than cruel: tired. "Send it. Then take the rest of the day and get yourself right. I like your work. I don't like scrambling around your work."

"You won't have to."

"Let's both hope that's true."

When the call ended Lena sat very still for a beat, the headphones pressing warm dents above her ears. She delivered the corrected package at 9:43. At 10:11 Nora confirmed receipt with a terse `Got it.` No smiley, no thanks, no note about next week except the one already made.

She opened her spreadsheet and moved numbers around the way people in bad marriages moved furniture. If Farah could give her Saturday and maybe one emergency shift this week, that was another hundred forty or so. If she paid only the card minimum and let the interest chew. If BlueArc still released the $412 by Monday. If Eli paid back any of it soon, which was a sentence she did not let finish itself in her head because it sounded too much like the kind of sentence women in her family ended up saying in kitchens with broken linoleum.

Her phone buzzed at 10:19.

`shit. i'm sorry you're getting hit from all sides`

Then, a minute later:

`Take a breath for me. Eat something?`

A month ago that would've cracked her chest open. This morning it felt like one more task arriving through glass. She was hungry, yes. She also needed twelve hundred dollars to stop October from getting personal.

By noon the rain had settled in for the day. The block below her window looked half erased, umbrellas moving like dark punctuation. Lena texted Farah to ask if she needed help at the shop.

Farah answered in under a minute.

`Three to close if you're not dead. If you're dead I still need three to close but standards change`

Lena went to shower. The water in the old pipes shuddered before it came through. She stood under it longer than she should have, letting it hit the back of her neck while the apartment cooled around her in damp degrees. When she got out, Eli still had not answered the `later` she had sent.

She put on dry jeans, a sweatshirt that smelled faintly of cork dust from the shop, and walked out into the rain with her hood up and her phone zipped into the inside pocket of her bag like a thing she didn't trust herself around.

"You look like hell," Farah said when Lena came in.

The bottle shop smelled more alive in bad weather: cork dust and wet cardboard, cut citrus, concrete, the sharp mineral stink of white wine somebody had mopped and not fully won against. Umbrellas dripped by the door. Condensation filmed the cooler glass. Wet leaves kept sticking to the mat every time it opened.

Farah was behind the register in a faded forest-green sweater, sleeves shoved to the elbows, silver showing clean at her temples. She had a deli container of orange peels open beside the card reader and was dropping the clean strips into it one by one as she worked, saving them for the syrup she made every winter while complaining that everybody on the block wanted sugar and nobody wanted tannin.

"Love that for me."

"I didn't say ugly. I said hell. Take the Slovenian orange to aisle three before Mr. Cardenas decides to die waiting."

Lena laughed because that was the price of entry with Farah: you had to keep the joke moving or earn concern. She shrugged out of her wet jacket, hung it in the back, and started hauling the case across the narrow floor. Her forearms already felt full of old static from the morning. The cardboard softened under her hands where the rain had found the seam.

The afternoon came at her in little neighborhood pieces. Mr. Cardenas wanted the cheap Montepulciano but called it the red with the saint on it. A younger woman in scrubs stood in front of the canned cocktails for five whole minutes and bought the one with a cartoon shark because she said it looked honest. Lena rang up a six-pack as a single and had to void it. She reached for the wrong shelf tag twice. Every time the phone in her apron buzzed, heat moved through her ribs before she even checked whether it was Eli.

At four-thirty Farah came around the counter carrying two paper cups of over-steeped black tea and jerked her chin toward the back room.

"Five minutes."

"I'm fine."

"No, you're vertical. Different category."

They stood in the back by the stacked cases and the old hand truck with one wheel that always complained. Rain drummed on the alley door. Somebody in the shop laughed too loud at their own joke and then apologized to nobody. Farah handed Lena a cup and leaned against the counter that held the pricing gun, the broom, and a bowl of clementines with soft spots.

"What's the damage."

Lena wrapped both hands around the tea. "Work."

"Work is not damage. Work is weather. Damage is what weather does to your roof."

"Then my roof."

Farah watched her over the rim of her cup. Her eyes were dark and dry and not interested in theater. "You want to be cryptic, go write songs. I'm asking plain. Is this tax-face, family-face, or man-face."

Lena looked down at the tea. The surface trembled with the building every time the front door opened. "Mostly work. I blew a file this morning. Lost money."

"How much."

"A hundred seventy-three and a webinar."

Farah hissed once through her teeth. "Fuck."

"Yeah."

"That because you were tired or because you were being stupid."

The question landed exactly where it was aimed. Lena let out a small laugh that wanted to become something else. "Can it be both."

"Usually is."

Farah set her cup down. "Did this guy keep you up."

There it was. It was not an accusation. Farah was taking inventory.

Lena could have told the truth in a dozen partial ways. That Eli had said he would call and then hadn't. That she'd still waited. That the waiting itself had started to feel like a second shift, unpaid and somehow more compulsory than the one that bought groceries. That she had already sent him money she no longer had. That she had started timing her body around pings.

What she said was, "He's got a lot going on."

Farah made a face. "Everybody's got a lot going on. I've got distributor reps trying to sell me pumpkin-spice prosecco like they're not ashamed to have tongues. That doesn't mean I get to ruin your Tuesday."

"Wednesday."

"See. This is what I mean. You're down in the screws."

Lena smiled despite herself. "I fucked up. It isn't all him."

"I didn't say all." Farah folded one orange peel in half before dropping it into the container. "I'm asking whether this man makes your life bigger or just busier."

Lena looked at the damp concrete. There was a flattened wine label stuck near the drain, one of the expensive Burgundies Farah cursed under her breath whenever customers asked for something smooth under fifteen dollars. "Busy week," she said.

Farah let that sit. She was good at silence when she wanted something from it. Finally she said, "Okay. Busy week. Useful options. You can take more shifts if I've got them. You can ask me for an advance and I can say yes or no like adults. You can tell me to mind my business and I'll probably mind seventy percent of it. What you can't do is start moving through the shop like a ghost and think nobody notices."

Lena held the tea tighter until it almost hurt. "I don't need an advance."

"I didn't ask what would flatter you."

"Farah."

"Len."

For a second she thought she might say enough to stop carrying it alone. She could not say everything, because everything would change the room. The first transfer. The second. The ring. The weird hollow way Eli's tenderness now arrived right on time and somehow late. She imagined the words in Farah's back room among the broom handles and extra invoices and the case of bargain cava nobody wanted, and she could not bear the sound they would make once out in the air.

So she took the mercy Farah had left for her.

"It's taxes and work," she said. "And rain. That's the whole glamorous story."

Farah studied her face one last time. Then she picked up her tea. "Fine. Then take home the lentil soup in the fridge before it turns into a science fair, and when your phone buzzes out there maybe don't look like it just called you ugly."

"That's very specific advice."

"I own a retail business. Specificity's all I've got."

They went back out front. The shop was busier now, people buying midweek consolation in screw-tops and six-packs. Lena moved through the rest of the shift fast enough to be useful. She lifted cases, rang people up, wiped the neck of a bottle a kid had knocked from the cold shelf without breaking. She was good at work when she was in it. That was part of the humiliation. She had built a life out of being dependable in rooms where nobody said thank you beautifully. She had let a voice on a phone make her sloppy inside the only competence she could reliably sell.

At close Farah shoved a quart container into her tote.

"Soup. Eat it."

"Yes, chef."

"Don't call me that. I sell pinot grigio to men in Eagles jackets."

Lena zipped the tote and pulled on her damp jacket. Farah was already at the register, counting twenties with the same expression she used for grief and inventory. Lena stood there half a second too long.

"What," Farah said without looking up.

"Nothing."

"Right. Go home before I start being kind in a way you'll hate."

Lena went out into the rain with the soup knocking against her thigh and her phone still unhelpfully silent in the pocket of her sweatshirt.

"She's in a mood," Mateo said when Lena came down the steps that evening.

Water had gathered in the crack by the bottom stair and turned the landing slick. June's basement smelled like wet concrete, cigarettes, and burnt toast. Somebody upstairs was running a vacuum in the slow brutal lines of a person who had given up on tidiness and was settling for revenge.

Mateo sat at the folding table in the main room with his school hoodie on and his beat-up headphones in his lap. The foam on one side had split clean down the middle. He was wrapping it with black electrical tape so neatly it looked factory, head bent, one knee jiggling under the table. A district Chromebook glowed in front of him beside a mug full of pens and a spoon standing in a puddle of instant ramen broth.

"Helpful," Lena said.

"I know."

June's voice came from the kitchenette sharp and low, the voice she used when somebody on the other end had already exhausted the free version of her patience.

"No, I heard you the first time. I'm saying the form was turned in. I'm looking at the copy right now. If your office lost it, that's not suddenly a character-building exercise for me."

She came around the corner with her phone pinned to her ear and a cigarette unlit between two nicotine-stained fingers. She wore black work pants, unlaced boots, and an old gray T-shirt with a faded Sixers logo cracked across the chest. Her hair was clipped up badly. On the little desk by the wall, three laptops were open: one with a KeyNest dashboard full of maintenance tickets and little red timers counting response windows down, one asleep, one awake and face-angled away so Lena could only see shifting blocks of text and a soft pulse of notifications in the corner.

June saw Lena, lifted one finger, and kept talking.

"Then email me the policy. No, not tomorrow. Tonight. If you're gonna say 'district policy' like God came down and wrote it in toner, you can at least send the PDF."

She hung up before the other person could perform their last line, tossed the phone onto the couch, and looked at Mateo.

"If I find out you handed that script to anybody besides the one dumbass who asked, I'm leaving you at school."

Mateo did not look up from the headphones. "He said it was just for the bibliography formatting."

"And then half the class had it."

"That's not my fault. That's distribution."

"Jesus Christ."

Only then did June look properly at Lena. The irritation in her face loosened, not all the way. "Hey. Sorry. Come in before the stairs sue you."

Lena held up the container from Farah. "Peace offering."

"Soup from the queen herself." June took it and peered through the lid. "Lentils. She thinks I'm iron-deficient."

"Are you."

"Who isn't."

While June carried the soup to the counter, Lena clocked the rest of the room: extension cords looped from an overloaded strip, a coffee mug held loose screws and dead lighters together like they were the same species, and an envelope from Mr. DeLuca sat under the ashtray on the windowsill with FINAL NOTICE stamped in red and bled soft by damp fingers. The dehumidifier in the corner groaned and clicked. On the desk, the KeyNest dashboard was all red timers and unit numbers. The angled laptop beside it flashed a notification preview and went dark when June reached over and killed the sound with two hard taps.

Lena nodded toward the Chromebook. "Bibliography formatting."

Mateo finally looked at her, acne bright on his forehead, headphones still in his hands. "It was a little script. Their assignment was to fix citation spacing by hand in a spreadsheet. That's not learning. That's hazing."

"And now the vice principal thinks he's running a criminal syndicate," June said. "Tea."

"I made the citations right."

"Not helping yourself."

A laugh slipped out of Lena before she could stop it. Mateo had that teenage way of sounding half absent until the subject mattered and then suddenly precise enough to be annoying. He went back to the headphones, jaw tight.

June took a pack of cigarettes from the counter, thought better of it, and shoved it back. "You want coffee? Tea? I've got exactly one beer and I'm saving it for after I call DeLuca back and after KeyNest quits pretending seven minutes is a humane response window."

"I'm okay."

"You look bad."

"You're really getting flattering in your old age."

"No time for lying tonight."

The angled laptop chirped again, thin and needling. June crossed the room in three quick steps, touched the keyboard harder than she needed to, checked the red timers on the KeyNest screen while she was there, then glanced at the damp FINAL NOTICE on the sill as if it might have multiplied while her back was turned.

Mateo said without looking up, "That thing's been doing that since lunch."

"Then it's committed," June said.

"You keep turning it away every time I look up."

"Because privacy is still legal in Pennsylvania."

To Lena she sounded not secretive exactly. Just overfull. Like there were too many tabs open behind her face.

June came back and stayed standing, one boot tapping once against the floor. "I've got DeLuca wanting eighteen hundred by yesterday, the district acting like Mateo launched a cybercrime ring, and KeyNest timing my bladder like it's a luxury item. So if you came here to confess to murder, rank it."

Lena leaned against the doorway instead of sitting. It made leaving easier if she needed it. "I blew a file this morning. Lost a hundred seventy-three and a live webinar."

June's mouth flattened. "BlueArc?"

"Yeah."

"Shit."

"Yeah."

June sat on the arm of the couch but kept one foot planted, like she expected another interruption before she could finish the sentence. She dragged her hands down her face. When they fell away, her mascara had shadowed at the corners and her eyes stayed bright in the mean, overused way of somebody too tired to soften. "That's real money."

Mateo snorted softly at something on his screen, then stopped when June looked over.

Lena said, "I was up late."

June's eyes came back to her face. "Waiting on him again."

Lena shrugged. "Waiting for him, mostly."

June leaned forward, elbows on her knees. The ashtray shifted on the sill when the upstairs neighbors thumped overhead. "Okay. Do you want me to be nice or useful."

"Dealer's choice."

"Useful. If somebody caught me on video right now, they'd think I hated them. I don't. I'm just one landlord threat, one school call, and one KeyNest timer past normal. Sometimes tired strips the charm clean off a person. That's all I'm saying."

Lena looked at the angled laptop, then back at June. "He's been weird."

June's expression changed by maybe half an inch. "How weird."

"Not weird weird. Just slower. Like he'll say he can call and then I wait around and he can't. Or he answers, but it feels..." She stopped. Stress always made her want to overexplain the timeline until the timeline itself sounded insane. "Off by a beat. I don't know."

June rested her chin on her fist. "Could be something. Could be he's tired in a boring human way. The guy's been doing load-ins, his sister's still a mess, and every competent person gets turned into free labor by their whole extended bloodline. Exhaustion can make people sound fake."

"I know tired."

"So do I." June flicked her fingers toward the room: Mateo, the notices, the ticking timers on the KeyNest screen. "Sometimes all it is from the outside is less sparkle and worse timing."

There was no cruelty in it. That made it harder to argue with.

Lena said, "I just keep feeling like I'm waiting to be resumed."

June's mouth tightened in sympathy or something close enough. "Then stop standing there on pause for him."

"Easy for you to say."

"No, actually, easy for me to say is the whole point of friendship." June stood, but another pulse hit the angled laptop before she reached the stove. She checked whatever had surfaced there, swore once under her breath, then forced herself away and started ladling Farah's soup as if she had been handed a task and needed movement more than appetite. "Eat something. Take a shower if you haven't. Don't interrogate him because you had a garbage day and now your skin is on inside out. Let him come back toward you."

Lena watched her. "You make that sound simple."

"It's not simple. It's just better than turning yourself into customer service for a relationship."

Mateo let out a laugh he tried to hide in his shoulder.

June pointed the soup spoon at him. "You don't even have a relationship. You have malware and opinions."

"That script was elegant."

"You're about to elegantly email an apology to the vice principal."

The old laptop on the desk lit again, silent now. Just a pulse. Then another. June saw it and ignored it too carefully.

Lena took the bowl June handed her. The lentils smelled like cumin and lemon and Farah's whole theory of mercy. "How bad is DeLuca."

June rolled her eyes toward the windowsill. "He wants the back eighteen hundred and now he's talking about another hundred twenty-five starting November because the upstairs washer died and apparently my rent is a mood ring for his appliances."

"Jesus."

"Yeah." June blew on the soup. "KeyNest keeps cutting response windows too. Seven minutes, ten minutes, like tenants only lock themselves out when I'm already sitting upright in a clean room with my shoes on. Everybody wants machine speed and human warmth. Pick one."

She said it bitterly enough that Lena almost apologized for bringing her one more need. Instead she stood there with the hot bowl in her hands and understood, dimly, that other people's lives had kept happening while she was staring at a screen waiting to be chosen by hers.

June softened first. She had a way of doing that, dropping just enough sympathy into the room that Lena felt mean for wanting to push any harder.

"Listen," she said. "If he calls tonight and it still feels off, ask for something concrete. A date, a plan, an answer that has a shape. But don't go looking for blood because you had one bad Wednesday. You'll find what you came for."

Lena nodded. The advice sounded sane enough to live inside.

As she left, Mateo finally looked up from his apology email. "For what it's worth," he said, not quite meeting her eyes, "people get laggy when they're doing too many things at once."

June cut in before Lena could answer. "Great. He invented human behavior at sixteen."

Mateo shrugged and went back to typing.

Outside, the rain had thickened from steady to committed. Lena pulled her hood up and walked home through water that had found the low parts of every cracked sidewalk square. Her phone stayed quiet all the way back.

***

Eli called at 12:37 a.m.

Lena had done almost everything June told her to do, which annoyed her by working. She had showered. She had eaten the rest of the soup with a heel of bread standing over the sink. She had answered one email from Nora with the required amount of humility and no extra. She had not texted Eli after coming home. She had even put the phone face down while she folded laundry off the chair and gave up halfway through because folding one person's laundry at midnight felt like parody.

When the screen finally lit, her stomach still went quick and stupid anyway.

She took the call on video before she could talk herself out of it. Her room was dark except for the streetlight leaking through wet glass, which left her face mostly a suggestion in the corner of the screen. On his side, a cinder-block wall went muddy under bad light. Rain rattled the window unit hard enough now and then to make the whole frame tick.

"Hey," Eli said, and then again, softer, "hey."

He had a way of breathing in through his nose before he said her name, a little warm sound like somebody stepping into bathwater. "Lena."

"Hi."

"You awake or did I just commit a crime."

"Little of both."

His laugh came low and tired. "Yeah. That's fair. I'm sorry. Today got away from me in a really ugly way."

"You don't have to do the whole apology package. I know you've got shit going on."

"I still left you hanging."

She turned onto her back and looked at the ceiling she knew too well. The plaster above the bed had a water stain shaped like Florida if Florida were trying to disappear. "You did."

Silence, but not empty silence. The kind with furniture in it.

Then Eli said, "Talk to me. What happened with work."

She told him enough, though not every detail. The missed calls, the wrong export, the dropped `not`, the docked pay, the webinar gone. She heard herself become exact about chronology in the middle of it and hated that too. At some point she realized she was gripping the edge of the blanket like it was the thing keeping her from sliding somewhere.

When she finished, Eli swore softly. "Fuck. Baby."

The old tenderness arrived anyway.

"I know," she said.

"No, I mean it. That's brutal. One stupid file and suddenly everybody acts like you're a public hazard."

"I did tell a nation of warehouse guys to stand under a suspended load."

He laughed, right where a laugh belonged. "You did, and I love that you're making me laugh at OSHA crimes."

She smiled in the dark. It hurt a little, how immediate the relief still was.

"Hey," he said. "Put both feet on the mattress."

"What."

"Do it. You're curling up so hard you're gonna throw your back out, and I can hear that rain beating the shit out of your window unit."

Lena obeyed before she had decided to. "Okay."

"Good. What's in the room besides your own head."

She closed her eyes. Rain. Somebody's TV through the wall. A late bus dragging water. Her own refrigerator motor. Him.

She got to three and stopped.

"Rain," she said. "Bus. The apartment next door pretending nobody can hear their game show."

"Yeah." His voice gentled. "That's your room. Stay there a second. You already went back and rescued the `not`. You don't have to keep working the shift from bed."

Something in her chest loosened. Something else stayed braced. The line flashed overbright, the way a favorite song sounds wrong under grocery-store fluorescents.

"You there," he said after a beat.

"Yeah."

"Come closer."

"You're bossy for four inches of glass," she said. Still, she propped it higher against the pillow so more of her face caught the light.

"There you go."

She'd heard that little drop on `there` before. August maybe. D.C. Maybe some Sunday after Gabe's kids had gone sticky and loud in the background. She couldn't pin it. That was almost worse.

He said, "Did you eat the soup or did you just haul it home like a good-luck charm."

"Soup."

"Good girl. Farah didn't send that over for decoration."

Usually that would have gone through her like current. Tonight it landed half an inch to the left, not enough to refuse but enough to feel the miss.

She said, because she did not know how else to get at what was wrong, "You sound far away."

Another furnished pause. Then: "I'm far away."

"You know what I mean."

"I think so." He took a breath. "I'm worn thin tonight, Len. That's true. But I'm here. I want to be here."

She listened to the rain hit the metal lip outside her window. "Okay."

"No, really. Look at me."

"I am. You keep freezing."

He made that low laugh again. "Fair. Okay. Then listen to me. One shit Wednesday doesn't turn you into a public hazard. You're the woman who re-exports a whole reel because one speaker tag feels off. You're not sloppy. You're fried. And soup's fake food unless Farah bullied bread into you."

She smiled despite herself. "Farah would kill you for that."

"Farah terrifies me."

"You've never met her in person."

"Exactly. Mystery increases power."

On the screen he pushed his hair back, laughed, and then the video handed her the same gesture back again, the knuckle landing at his temple in the exact same spot. She told herself it was lag because the alternative was uglier and because everybody blamed lag for everything now.

Ordinarily that would've settled her.

Instead she started listening ahead of him. When she said she felt like the apartment had gotten smaller lately, he answered before the sentence had even finished settling. "Open the window an inch and let the rain in."

She did it anyway. Damp air shouldered in at once, cool and metallic. Rain smell, brick smell, the block washed to its bones.

"Better," he said.

"A little."

"Good. Stay with me there."

Cool air moved through the room. His next line came right after it, neat as if the opening had been waiting.

He said, "You know what I love about you," and her body went ahead of him, braced for the inventory: her work hands, the way she listened, the mean little joke she made when she needed reassurance without asking for it. He'd hand it back polished and warm enough to wear.

"What," she said.

"That even when you're wrecked, you're still trying to make the story accurate."

It was a beautiful answer. It was even true.

She rolled onto her side and pressed her forehead to the cool wall. "I should sleep."

He did not push. "Okay."

"Okay?"

"Yeah." Softly: "You don't have to hand me the clean-copy version tonight."

The phrase nicked the same spot behind her ribs.

"Call me tomorrow?" she said.

"Of course."

The answer was there too fast, like he'd had it loaded before she asked.

After they said goodnight she left the phone on the pillow and did not move for a long time. The apartment had gone cold in damp increments. On the desk, the tax envelope leaned under the lamp. Her wet cuffs from earlier still hung over the chair. Somewhere down on Broad a bus hit standing water and sent up the long shhhk of a city being dragged through weather.

Eli had made her feel better. That was still true. But afterward the room felt damp with him, his voice in the lampshade, the tax envelope, the wet cuffs sagging over the chair. Not a door opening. More like humidity getting into paper and wood while the rain kept worrying the walls, swelling the ordinary a little out of true.
