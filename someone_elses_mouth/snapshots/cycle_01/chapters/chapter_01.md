# Chapter 1

By five-thirty Lena had corrected "public housing" back from "pubic housing" three times and decided the man in the training video deserved whatever humiliation the software tried on him next. Sweat kept sliding down the side of her ribcage and sticking her tank top to the chair. The box fan in the window shoved hot air from Moore Street straight at her face, then clicked on the turn and shoved it back again. It did not cool anything. It only moved the heat around so she could feel it in shifts.

Below her, the tailor shop rolled toward closing. Steam hissed once through the floorboards. Somebody laughed on the sidewalk. A SEPTA bus dragged itself up Broad with that worn metal complaint city buses had when August had baked them too long. Lena kept her headphones on and listened to the councilman in the file say community investment with a mouth full of saliva. The waveform swelled blue and green on her monitor. She backed up four seconds, fixed a dropped not, put the period outside the quotation marks because the client wanted that for some insane reason, and drank coffee that had gone sour and skin-warm in the mug.

The quarterly estimate sat on the table behind her under a saucer with a crack through it. She did not need to turn around to know the number. She had written it twice in black pen and once again in red as if color might make it smaller.

$4,200 by October 15.

That gave her six weeks, if she was being generous about what counted as a week.

Her phone lit once beside the keyboard and then again. They were not calls. It was app noise. She had shoved Hinge into a folder with banking, parking tickets, and the PECO app, as if romance and utilities belonged under the same little square of forced optimism. The screen flashed a partial message from a man named Chris who had opened with Nice smile. He had no chance of a response, and she resented him anyway for existing in the same minute as the tax bill.

There was a knock that was not really a knock. June hit the door twice with the side of her fist, then once with her heel.

"If you're dead, I'm eating both orders," she called.

Lena paused the file. "Come in, then, vulture."

June came through the door backward with a sweating plastic takeout bag hanging from one wrist and two fountain sodas cradled against her chest. She was compact and fast even when the heat should have slowed her, dark hair frizzing loose at the temples, work boots unlaced at the top. Her fingers were yellowed from cigarettes in a way she never bothered to hide. She set the drinks down, shook out the takeout containers, and pocketed the extra chopsticks without looking at them. June stole spare chopsticks from every restaurant in South Philly because, according to her, forks vanished in a one-bedroom but chopsticks respected the home.

She sniffed once. "Jesus, Len. It smells like hot dust and burnt headphones in here."

"That's the smell of literacy."

"That's the smell of your fan trying to sue you."

Lena pushed the headphones down around her neck. The skin beneath them felt tender, dented. "I've got twenty-eight more minutes on this file."

"Then you'll eat while I judge you." June slid one container toward her with a plastic fork anyway. Cold sesame noodles, cucumbers gone slick in the heat, dumplings already sweating through their wrappers. They were better than cooking and better than pretending she was going to cook.

Lena took one bite standing up. Peanut, vinegar, too much garlic. It made her realize how hungry she had let herself get.

June leaned over the desk, reading the waveform like she could make sense of it by spite alone. "What's it today?"

"Municipal broadband training."

"Bleak."

"He said nonprofit like he was chewing on a wet sock."

June barked out a laugh. She had a good laugh when she forgot to aim it. "You couldn't pay me enough to hear men explain the public good."

"You literally work in property tech."

"Exactly. I hear worse men for more hours."

She said it lightly, but there was the little edge under it, the work edge, the one that had gotten sharper over the past year whenever KeyNest came up. Lena knew the outline: after-hours tickets, fake emergencies, clients who thought every locked-out tenant was a crisis God had assigned to June personally. She also knew June did not want pity unless it came disguised as mockery, so she only tipped her chin toward the soda.

"Diet," Lena said.

"You're welcome."

They ate with the fan chopping the room into warm gusts. Lena stood to keep herself from sinking into the chair and losing another hour. June wandered the apartment the way she always did, not nosy exactly, just impossible to keep still. There was not much to wander. The bed took up one wall, the table another, and the stacked storage bins against the closet had turned the place into a careful version of crowded. June checked the freezer, made a face at the tray of old ice, picked up the tax paper from the table, and looked over at Lena.

"You still owe the feds forty-two hundred."

"Federal and state," Lena said. "Pennsylvania likes to feel included."

"Right. And the card?"

"Still there."

June tapped the paper against her thigh. "You still have the Wildwood money."

Lena swallowed before answering. Even the phrase made her think of fiberglass walls heating up under sun, her mother's old floral towels turning stiff with salt, the sour-sweet smell of sunscreen that had lived in that trailer long after Rosa stopped going to the beach. Money from the sale did not feel like money. It felt like the last object in a room after somebody died, except the object was a bank balance and people treated you like you were lucky.

"I've got eighteen-six that already belongs to other things," she said. "Taxes, rent if work gets weird, maybe the luxury item of not panicking every time a client takes three extra days to pay."

June's face changed in a way so slight most people would have missed it. It was not envy. It was not calculation, not nakedly. It was more like the brief stillness she got when a screen finally loaded and the answer on it mattered.

"Okay," she said. "So. We don't let you light any of that on fire."

"We don't let me do what, exactly?"

June was already reaching for the phone on the desk. "Give me this."

Lena caught it first. "No."

"Yes."

"I'm not doing profile triage with you right now."

"You absolutely are. Look at this place. You're one tax notice away from marrying your own fan."

"At least it shows up."

June's mouth twitched. "That's exactly why I need to intervene."

Lena should have said no again. She knew it. Hinge had been living on her phone for months in the same dead zone as meditation apps and dentist reminders, an idea of action that let her avoid actual action. Sometimes she scrolled it while waiting for microwave rice and felt nothing except a low, stale meanness. There were men holding fish. There were men holding children who were not theirs. There were men who used the phrase fluent in sarcasm and looked like they sweated cologne. It all made her feel both superior and already rejected, which was efficient if nothing else.

June held out her hand. "Len."

"You get ten minutes."

"I need twelve."

"Ten."

June took the phone and sat on the edge of the bed as if she had been invited to do this by a panel of experts. She squinted at Lena's existing profile and let out a long disgusted breath.

"Oh, this is grim."

"Fuck you."

"No, listen. I'm helping. First problem, these pictures all look like you're apologizing for being visible."

"That's my face."

"That's your divorced face. Different thing."

Lena leaned against the desk with her fork in one hand and felt the old irritation rise, familiar enough to be almost companionable. June had known her since twelve, since plaid skirts and cafeteria pizza and those years when every humiliation arrived in public and then kept arriving. There were things June could say to her that would have sounded unforgivable from anybody else.

"Delete the black dress one," June said. "You look like you volunteered for a fundraiser you hate."

"It was a fundraiser I hated."

"Exactly."

She swiped through Lena's camera roll with unnerving speed. It was not random speed. It was selection speed. She threw out the church-step picture where Lena's ex-husband's shoulder still remained at the edge like an unfinished amputation. She tossed the blurry beach shot from Wildwood because grief sat in it too plainly. She dismissed a selfie from the winter because Lena looked thin-lipped and careful. Then she stopped at a photo Farah had taken in the bottle-shop stockroom, Lena laughing with a case of cava balanced against her hip, her mother's signet ring catching the fluorescent light.

"This one."

Lena looked over. "I'm shiny."

"It's August. Everybody's shiny. You look strong."

That hit harder than pretty would have. June seemed to know that too.

"Also this one," June said, selecting a picture from Gabe's backyard where Lena had one knee tucked under her on a folding chair, head thrown back at something off-camera, hair half fallen from its knot. "And one face-forward picture where a man can verify you're not an AI-generated aunt."

"Beautiful. Exactly the dream."

"Shut up."

June changed the prompts next. Lena had written hers like disclaimers. June deleted almost all of it and started over with two thumbs moving so fast the screen blurred.

"What are you putting?"

"Better things."

"That's not an answer."

June read aloud as she typed. "'Freelance captioner. If you eat chips into a microphone, I will subtitle you as a hate crime.'"

Lena laughed despite herself. "That's aggressive."

"That's memorable."

"I'm pretty sure Hinge has community standards."

"Good. You'll look rebellious."

She flicked to the next prompt. "'A fact about me that surprises people: I can carry a case of wine and hold a grudge at the same time.'"

"Jesus."

"What?"

"You make me sound competent."

"You're competent. That's half your problem."

June looked up then, quick and intent. The teasing had thinned out. "The rest of the problem is you keep offering men your caution like it's a prize. Nobody wants to date your caution."

Lena went still around the fork. Heat pressed in through the window screen. Down below, somebody slammed a car door and two dogs answered from different blocks.

"I wasn't aware we were doing a seminar," she said.

"We're not." June's tone stayed even. "I'm saying if you want somebody to actually notice you, you have to let them notice the part that's alive. Not the part that already filed the paperwork."

For a second Lena hated how close that came to true. She hated it more because it came from June, who still moved through the world as if every room had been built a little wrong and she could fix it by shoving harder on the correct hinge.

June finished the last prompt and handed the phone over. "Read."

Lena read.

The profile did sound like her. Not exactly her daily self, not the version sitting in a hot apartment with peanut sauce on her wrist and an estimated tax sheet under a cracked saucer. But it sounded like a self contiguous with her, a self who might still exist under the account balances and freelance revisions and the years since the divorce had hardened into a story nobody asked follow-up questions about anymore. It was a sharper self, one less interested in preemptive defeat.

"It's obnoxious," she said.

"It's a dating app. That's the native language."

"The hate-crime line might get me arrested."

"Then you'll finally meet people."

Lena sat back down at the desk. The chair seat was hot through her shorts. "I hate that you're good at this."

June shrugged, but there was pride there, open and unhidden. "Systems are systems."

She said it like a joke. Maybe it was. But again Lena felt that small chill under the heat, not fear exactly, only the sense of June locking onto something with more focus than the thing seemed to deserve.

"You're doing this for Mateo too," Lena asked, "or only for the elderly."

"Mateo's sixteen. He doesn't need help meeting terrible people."

"Fair."

June snatched up her soda, took a swallow, and winced. "Christ. Warm already."

"Welcome to my climate."

She reached over Lena's shoulder before Lena realized what she was doing and hit the final button.

The profile went live.

"June."

"What, you were going to pray on it?" She stood, gathering containers with the efficiency of somebody who never believed in leaving one mess when she could start managing the next. "Leave it up for a week. If a man says he likes hiking, ask him where. If he says everywhere, he's lying. If he sends a fish picture, block him on behalf of women. If he opens with hey beautiful, the Lord has spared you."

"That's ten different instructions."

"I contain multitudes."

At the door she paused, one hand on the frame, unreadable in that tired way she got when work and nicotine and unspoken things had thinned her down to essentials.

"Len," she said. "Try not to act like this is a dare. Let it be nice if it's nice."

Then she was gone, her steps quick on the stairs, the building swallowing her into its usual evening noises.

Lena looked at the phone in her hand. Her own face looked back from the app, brighter than she felt, the caption joke sitting there with stupid confidence, visible and available. The word available made her skin itch. She set the phone facedown on the desk and put the headphones back on too fast, as if work could reseal the room around her.

It almost did.

For fourteen minutes she managed to stay inside the file. The councilman became a woman from procurement. The woman from procurement became a slide deck voice-over with a hard g in strategic. Lena fixed a dropped article, moved a comma, trimmed dead air. Then the phone buzzed once against the wood and kept buzzing, two short pulses in a row, and the sound went straight into the back of her teeth.

She took the headphones off again.

* * *

There were three new likes, one rose from a man named Darren whose entire profile seemed organized around smoked meats, and one match.

The match was Eli Ward, twenty-nine.

His first picture was on a loading dock, black cable looped over one shoulder, one forearm braced against a road case. Dark hair that needed cutting, two days of stubble, no visible gym mirror in sight. The second was less flattering in a way Lena trusted more: him in a cramped booth beside a lighting console, looking down at something out of frame with the kind of concentration that rearranged a face. The third was at what looked like somebody else's wedding, thrift-store suit, crooked tie, paper cup in hand, smiling like whoever had taken the picture had interrupted him midway through a bad opinion.

One prompt said he worked in live sound and spent too much of his life in rooms where everybody blamed the speakers for human choices. Another said he kept motel sewing kits in his backpack because buttons never came off on days when stores were open. That detail stopped her longer than the pictures did. It felt stolen from a real life in the way most profiles tried and failed to imitate.

The message under his name read: "Be honest. When somebody chews directly into the mic, do you caption the chewing or let God handle it."

Lena sat down slowly.

Outside, a scooter went by with music clipping through cheap speakers. She could hear the tailor shop grate rattle down all the way now. The room had darkened without permission. On her monitor the paused transcript waited with the blinking cursor of a patient enemy.

She typed back before she could decide whether she should.

"God is busy. I bill by the minute."

The typing dots appeared almost at once. They came too fast, she thought, and then she only thought that because she was already interested.

"That feels ethical," he wrote. "I do sound for a living and I still think panel moderators should have to pass a licensing exam."

She smiled despite herself.

"What kind of sound?"

"Mostly theater, some touring, some random civic nightmare gigs when rent gets personal. You really caption stuff like that all day?"

"I caption whatever lands in the portal. Today I spent fifteen minutes deciding whether a city employee said 'broadband corridor' or 'broadband warrior.'"

"Which was it?"

"Corridor. Sadly."

"Warrior would've justified the taxes."

That was the first thing. It was not that he made a tax joke. Anybody could do that. It was that he had moved one inch past the caption prompt and into the life under it without sounding like he was collecting data off a clipboard. Most men on the app either asked nothing or asked questions so nakedly strategic they might as well have come with checkboxes. Here is where I pretend to care about your work. Here is where I flatter your intelligence. Here is where I suggest drinks.

Eli wrote like he had actually heard the room she was in.

She let herself look back at his pictures. In one, a strip of green tape was looped around two fingers like a ring. In another, there was a coffee stain on the cuff of his shirt. He was handsome, yes, but not in the plasticky way that made her back away before she was even done being impressed. He was handsome in a way that still had laundry and cords and bad fluorescent light in it.

"You don't look old enough to be this disgusted with your profession," she typed.

"I started young and have range."

Then, after a beat:

"Also the wine-case picture is unfair."

She stared at the screen.

"Unfair how?"

"You look like you could carry me and my road case up two flights without losing the argument."

Lena laughed out loud, alone in her apartment, and immediately looked around as if somebody might have caught her. The line was flirty. Of course it was flirty. But it had landed in a place generic handsome did not know existed. It had gone straight to competence, to the body she used for work and lifting and getting through a life nobody was carrying for her. June had chosen that picture because June knew what men saw. Or knew what Lena wanted seen.

The thought should have made the whole thing feel staged. It did not. It only made her thumb hover harder over the keyboard.

"This is a very elaborate way to ask me to help you move."

"Absolutely not. I would never waste forearm privileges on furniture."

She sent back a laughing face and hated herself a little for it. Then she hated the hate. It was exhausting, the way every small pleasure in her life seemed to arrive already carrying its own rebuttal.

The conversation kept going.

He asked what kind of headphones she used and did not act shocked when she answered with the actual model. He said he had once miked a middle-school production of *Guys and Dolls* where one boy had swallowed half his lines because he was chewing cinnamon gum against direct orders. She told him about a pastor's anniversary video that had been ruined by somebody's bracelet hitting a table every eleven seconds. He said the worst noise on earth was sneakers on a stage catwalk at two in the morning. She said no, it was a wet mouth six inches from a cheap lapel mic. He conceded too gracefully to count as surrender and then circled back with a better question: what did she put on after work when she'd spent all day listening to other people talk.

That one took her a second.

She looked toward the fan. She looked at the half-finished file on the monitor. She realized she had not added a caption in eleven minutes.

"Depends how bad the day was," she wrote. "Sometimes silence. Sometimes old salsa. Sometimes I leave the fan on and let it impersonate a helicopter until I hate myself."

"That fan sounds serious."

"It clicks every rotation like it's making a threat."

"Now I love it."

She should have gone back to work. She did go back to work, technically. She hit play, corrected a sentence, set an inaudible marker where the speaker coughed. But every time the phone lit she reached for it before she had even formed the thought to reach. The app began dividing the room into before-message and after-message. The file kept losing to a stranger.

At seven-thirty June texted her.

"Any disasters yet."

Lena looked at the message, then back at the open Hinge thread. She typed, "Nope," and did not mention Eli. She was not sure why. Maybe because naming him to June would make him feel like a skit. Maybe because she wanted one thing that had not already passed through June's capable hands.

Eli asked what her neighborhood sounded like through the open window. She told him: buses, scooters, somebody's aunt yelling up a stoop, a kid bouncing a basketball too long after dinner, the tailor shop gate, the old couple next door whose television believed in maximum volume. He answered with the backstage equivalent from wherever he was working that week: flight cases, bad coffee, a drummer practicing fills nobody had requested, one actor doing breathing exercises like a public grievance. He did not overshare. He did not perform loneliness. He simply kept matching the level of detail she gave him and returning it altered, a little more alive.

By eight-fifteen the file was still open and the sky outside the window had gone the color of dirty dishwater. Lena ate the last dumpling cold and did not remember doing it. Her apartment had shrunk all summer into a place where work happened and bills waited. Now, with the phone in her hand, it kept making new little doors.

He sent: "I'm about to get yelled at by a stage manager. Do you hate voice notes from strangers?"

Lena sat very still.

She did not hate voice notes from strangers. She hated them from men, sometimes. Voice notes had a way of arriving already convinced of themselves. They came in too loud, too casual, too eager to make her host them.

She typed, deleted, typed again.

"I hate bad ones."

"Reasonable standard."

"You think you're offering a good one?"

"I can try. No legal guarantees."

She looked at the message long enough for the screen to dim.

If she said yes, it would be a small thing. Smaller than a date, smaller than a number, smaller than those first stupid overthought drinks where two people asked each other what neighborhoods they'd almost been priced out of. But it would also not be small at all. A voice was different. A voice put a body on a sentence whether the body deserved to be there or not.

The cursor blinked inside the text field.

"Okay," she sent.

Three dots appeared. Then nothing for almost a full minute, long enough for her to feel mildly stupid, long enough to reopen the work file and stare at the word corridor as if it had become profound.

Then the Hinge notification came through: 1 voice note.

* * *

She did not play it right away.

That was pride, mostly. It was also caution dressed up as routine. She saved the open file, closed the laptop on the unfinished transcript, took her soda to the sink, and ran water until it came up only lukewarm instead of hot. She washed peanut sauce off her fingers. She tied her hair back again because it had fallen loose. She moved around the room with the phone faceup on the table, each step arranged to prove to nobody that she was not waiting.

Outside, the block had entered its night shift. The corner store bell kept dinging. Somebody somewhere was frying garlic. A couple argued in short furious bursts on the sidewalk and then stopped so abruptly it was worse than if they had kept going. The fan clicked and turned. Clicked and turned.

Lena took the phone to bed because the chair had become hostile. She sat with one leg folded under her, sheet sticking to the backs of her thighs, and tapped play.

His voice came in close, closer than anything about the app had any right to be.

"Hey, Lena. This is me proving I'm a person and not a customer-support team. I'm on a loading dock in Wilmington, so if you hear metal crashing, that's just three guys losing an argument with a ramp."

There was metal in the background, a hollow bang, somebody swearing, his laugh under it. It was not a performance laugh. It was a low, rough little break in the sentence like he had turned his head before it could fully happen.

"Your fan sounds exactly like an old follow spot trying not to die," he went on. "I mean that respectfully. Also, anybody who eats chips into a microphone should have to pay you directly. You don't have to answer in voice if that feels cursed. I just wanted to say hi like a person."

It ended there. There was no extra chuckle, no push.

Lena looked at the screen for a second, then hit play again.

The second listen was worse.

Or maybe it was better. It was worse for her, better for him.

The speaker sat against the groove her headphones had pressed into the side of her head all day, and his voice fit there too easily. The fan kept clicking. A siren passed somewhere farther off. The whole apartment stayed exactly what it had been ten seconds earlier, hot, cramped, smelling faintly of dust and cold noodles and the tailor's starch downstairs. But the scale of it changed. His voice made the room feel occupied in a new way, not physically, not enough for her to mistake anything so badly, only acoustically, intimately. As if a second set of attention had leaned in under the ordinary noise and stayed.

She played it a third time because at that point she was already committed to losing the argument with herself.

There were other men she had spoken to since the divorce. A contractor from Fishtown who wanted to discuss crypto at brunch. A divorced dad from Jersey who cried into a second beer before she had finished the fries. Two coffee dates so polite they felt like paperwork. None of them had made her body recognize anything before her mind could distrust it. None of them had sounded amused and patient and a little tired in a way that made tiredness feel like a shared language instead of a private failure.

He had not even said anything extraordinary. That was part of it. He had said hello, joked about the fan, let the note stop. There was no soulmate garbage in it, no You seem different, no gross confidence. Just a voice pitched low and warm enough that when he laughed again on the replay, Lena felt the sound almost physically, a small brush at the tender place under her ear where the headphones had rubbed all day.

Hinge was not built for this kind of waiting. The app sat there bright and marketlike, still full of little icons and options and the implication that everyone remained replaceable until proven otherwise.

She typed: "Okay, you do in fact sound like a person."

He answered fast enough to feel eager, not fast enough to feel on top of her.

"Huge relief on my end."

"Wilmington is random."

"Wilmington is where regional theater sends you to learn humility."

"I'm from South Philly. Humility is available locally."

"I believe that instantly."

She smiled again. The room kept smiling with her, which was embarrassing. She set the phone on the pillow, then picked it back up before another message could come in and pretend she had not been about to read it anyway.

"This app is going to eat the note if I keep replaying it," he wrote a minute later. "If you want, I'm better on Signal."

There it was.

It was the exact little threshold she had known was coming.

Lena stared at the message until the fan completed three more clicking rotations. Off-app did not mean nakedness. It did not mean trust. It meant a number in a contact list, a thread that could appear in daylight beside Gabe and Farah and PECO fraud alerts. It meant waking up and maybe seeing him there before coffee. It meant the possibility of continuity, which was what made it dangerous.

From the apartment next door came the old couple's television, game-show applause spilling through the wall in a tinny burst. Her tax estimate still sat on the table with the cracked saucer. The unfinished file still waited on the laptop. All the practical parts of her life remained exactly where she had left them, lined up with their mouths open.

She thought of June saying, Let it be nice if it's nice.

She thought of the voice note again, that brief low laugh under the metal crash, the respectful joke about the fan. She thought of how quickly she had wanted to hear it a second time.

Then she typed, before caution could redraft the sentence into something safer.

"Yeah. Signal's easier."

She added her number.

Once sent, it sat there in the thread looking more intimate than a whole naked photograph would have, a strip of digits, a gate she had unlatched herself.

For one terrible second nothing happened.

Then Signal lit up at the top of her screen with a new message request, his name and the same half-smiling picture from the wedding.

"Hey," he wrote there. "Less cursed app. Still me."

Her stomach did a small unhelpful turn, not from fear exactly but from recognition. Anticipation had texture. It had weight. It took up space in the room with the fan and the hot window and the bill on the table. It made the apartment feel less like a sealed container for work and debt and more like the first minute before somebody arrived.

Lena set the phone on the pillow beside her instead of on the table with the tax paper. When it buzzed again through the thin cotton case, the sound went straight into her thigh.

She let it.
