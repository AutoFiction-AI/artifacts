# Chapter 16

June's basement windows glowed under the sidewalk grates, a flat yellow that made the apartment look half sunk into the block. Lena stood in the little concrete well with her coat collar up and the folded bank statement cutting into her palm through the paper bag she'd used as a folder. The cold had found the gap at her wrists. She could smell fryer grease from Snyder, wet leaves mashed into the curb, and the sweet stale nicotine that always leaked out of June's place when the door opened.

She knocked once because anything softer would have been absurd.

Mateo opened the door with a jeweler's screwdriver between his teeth. He had one side of his headphones split open in his hand, the black foam peeled back, tiny silver screws laid out on an overturned takeout lid in a neat row. He wore his school hoodie and socks on the concrete. His forehead had a fresh scatter of acne across it. He blinked at Lena, took the screwdriver out of his mouth, and said, "Hey, Len," like there was still a version of the evening where she was just dropping by.

Behind him June was at the folding desk in her work boots, one knee tipped against a crate of cables, the blue-white light of two monitors flattening her face. She looked up. Her expression did not do anything dramatic. It went still, then useful.

"You could've answered a text," she said.

Lena stepped inside. The apartment was cooler than the hallway, always cooler, because the place sat half underground and the radiator banged more than it heated. Cords climbed the wall in black loops. An empty mug ring marked the desk beside a closed laptop that was not the same one Lena had opened during the storm, but the sight of it made her stomach give a small ugly twist anyway, as if the whole room had learned to mimic a trigger.

"Teo," June said, not looking away from Lena, "go ask Gus if that left ear still cuts out. You can use his soldering iron if you need."

Mateo glanced between them. He was smart enough not to ask. He slid his feet into sneakers without untying them, scooped up the headphones and the lid with the screws, and paused at the door.

"You still coming by Sunday?" he asked Lena. It was not suspicion in his face, not yet. Mostly it was habit. Sunday dinner. People orbiting each other on old schedules.

The question landed in the place Eli's voice had occupied for months, that soft organized place in her. Lena felt it make contact and die there.

"Not tonight," she said.

Mateo nodded once. "Yeah. I got that."

He slipped out. The door shut. The room got smaller immediately, as if he had taken the last ordinary piece of air with him.

June pushed away from the desk. "If this is about you going through my laptop-"

"No." Lena's voice came out flat, almost calm. "It's about you saying it."

June looked at the paper bag in Lena's hand, then back at her face. "You know it already."

"I know what I saw." Lena set the bag on the table and pulled out the folded printout, more because she needed her hands to be doing something than because she needed proof. The bank transactions were highlighted in dull pharmacy yellow. She had circled dates at four in the morning with a pen that kept skipping. "I want you to say what he was. Out loud. With your mouth."

June did not sit. She hooked one thumb in the back pocket of her jeans, the old stance she used when a landlord or a school office or a drunk cousin needed handling. "Who else knows?"

Lena laughed once, with no humor in it. "You don't get that first."

"Len."

"Don't."

The name, shortened in June's voice, hit with all its old permissions. Lena could hear twelve years old in it, twenty-three, thirty, every dumb emergency where June had shown up with a wrench or a car or a spare charger or the exact stupid thing Lena had forgotten. She hated that the sound still knew where to go in her. She hated even more that June had known that would still be true.

June's gaze shifted to the printout. "You highlighted the transfers."

"I highlighted the money because the sex was harder to hold in my hand."

That landed. June went quiet in the way she did when she was most dangerous, not larger, not louder, just cleaner. The joke left her face completely.

Lena stepped closer. The room held traces of June everywhere: laundry detergent fighting a losing battle with smoke, a pair of Mateo's beat-up cleats under the milk crate by the door, a stack of KeyNest paperwork clipped with a pink binder clip, the sour metallic smell of old electronics warming themselves. It made no difference. It made everything worse. This was not a lair. It was the room where June had made coffee and complained about clients and asked Lena if she wanted arroz con gandules from her aunt's place. It was the room where the thing had lived.

"Say his name," Lena said. "Say what you built."

June looked at the closed laptop, then back at Lena. "I built Eli."

Lena had known that sentence already. She had known it in the storm light on June's screen, in the file tree, in the notes that had taken her body and her habits and broken them into usable pieces. Still, hearing it enter the air in June's own voice was like hearing a floorboard crack under full weight. Something operational in her really had stayed alive until now. It died.

June kept talking because silence had stopped helping. "I set the Hinge account up. I wrote the first messages. The first voice note too."

Lena nodded once. "Keep going."

"A lot of the ordinary chat could run once the memory was good enough. I stepped in when it needed steering."

"Meaning?"

June's mouth tightened. "Meaning first contact. Meaning when you got suspicious. Meaning D.C. Meaning the money."

The radiator knocked in the corner, a dull pipe sound that used to make June roll her eyes and smack it with the side of her boot. Lena waited for the old impulse to say something about how the building was trying to murder everyone. Nothing rose. The room did not belong to habit anymore.

"The calls?" Lena asked.

"Some were live. Some were scheduled. Some were him running on what I'd already fed it."

"And the video."

June took half a second too long. "Mixed."

Lena stared at her.

June made herself say it. "I tuned that too."

The chair edge pressed a white-hot line into the backs of Lena's thighs. She had not realized she'd sat down. She could see, with nauseating clarity, the little note she had glimpsed in the dashboard during the storm. Praise forearms. Do not overdo waist compliments. Ask before telling her to tilt the phone lower. Recall scar on left knee only after she thinks he has forgotten it. She had not even needed to read the whole card for it to brand itself into her. June had built a way to arrive at her body through its shames.

"Did you watch me?" Lena asked.

June's answer came careful, which made it worse. "Not all of it."

Lena let out a sound that might have been a laugh if a laugh could curdle before leaving the mouth. "You don't get points for that."

"I know."

"No. You don't." Lena leaned forward. "You don't get to say that like we're on the same page now. You don't get to act like there was a clean part and a dirty part and you only stood in the doorway for the dirty one. There wasn't a doorway. You built the room."

June took that. Her face did not soften. It got more exact.

"At first," she said, "I thought it would be a week. Maybe two. I thought I'd prove a point, then kill it."

"What point?"

June's eyes flicked up, dark and tired and unreadable in the old familiar way Lena had once trusted. "That the guy you wanted wasn't a miracle. He was a set of inputs."

Lena felt heat rise under her coat, not from anger exactly, something meaner and more humiliating. "So I was homework."

"No."

"Then what?"

June's jaw moved once. "A joke first. Then an experiment. Then proof."

The words came out so clean they were almost bureaucratic. Lena hated them on sight.

"Proof of what?"

"That if somebody knew you well enough, they could build the thing you kept waiting for." June said it without flourish, like she was naming a bad price on a repair. "That all that bullshit about chemistry and fate and finally being seen wasn't magic. It was pattern recognition and timing and knowing when to shut up."

Lena looked at the desk, at the loops of cable ties, at the dark rectangle of the closed machine. She remembered Eli's voice in her ear after midnight, how patient it had sounded, how unembarrassed by tenderness. She remembered arranging her day around notifications, the private way she'd smiled on buses, in the bottle shop storeroom, in the bathroom mirror with her shirt half on because a message had arrived and she wanted one more minute before she had to leave for work. She remembered the wet red dress in D.C. clinging to the backs of her thighs in the station bar. She remembered lying on top of her covers with her phone balanced against a pillow while she spread her legs for a man who was also, in pieces, this room.

June watched her remember it and said, quieter, "I didn't think you'd go that far with him."

Lena looked up so fast her chair scraped.

"Don't," she said.

"I'm not saying that to make it better."

"Then why are you saying it?"

June held her eyes. "Because it's true."

"It's also useful." Lena stood again. The paper statement shook in her hand now, the cheap office paper already softening where her palm had dampened it. "You don't get to do that thing where you slide one true thing under the door and hope I won't notice the rest of the room."

June's nostrils flared once. "I know what I did."

"Then say more than the software version." Lena took a breath and heard it catch on the old damp in the walls. "Say it in a way that has a body in it."

For the first time since she'd opened the door, June looked away. Not long. Long enough for Lena to see the nicotine stain on her index finger when she rubbed her mouth.

"I built him," June said again, stripped down now. "I matched you with him on purpose. I wrote the things that got you off the app. I kept him alive when he should've died. I handled D.C. I wrote the money asks. I knew what you'd answer to, and I used it."

There it was. Not complete. Enough for the first cut.

Lena did not feel bigger for hearing it. She felt the way she'd felt on the train back from Washington, staring at her reflection over the black window while her phone lit up with apologies from a man who had never had lungs. Not shocked anymore. Just trapped in the fact.

June said, very low, "Who knows?"

"Farah." Lena folded the statement once, hard. "Gabe too."

Something finally crossed June's face then, small and involuntary. Not guilt exactly. Calculation meeting cost.

"Anybody else?"

"My nieces asked at dinner why Eli hadn't called in a while." Lena heard her own voice turn careful, the dangerous careful she got into when she was hurt enough to stop wasting words. "I stood there with mashed potatoes in my hand and told them there was no Eli. So, yeah. More than two people know."

June closed her eyes for a second. When she opened them, the tired unreadable look was gone. In its place was the one Lena had seen aimed at cable companies, at school administrators, at men who thought they could underpay her. Pure inventory.

"Okay," June said.

Lena almost slapped her for the word. Okay, as if there were a form to fill out next, a sequence. Instead she said, "No. We're not at okay. We're at why."

June gave a short nod. "Then ask it."

Lena looked at her best friend, or the woman who had been that name until the storm, and understood that the first part was over. The fact had been spoken aloud. The cleanest lie in the room was dead. What was left was the dirty part, the part with motive and money and the old contract between them that neither of them had ever named because naming it might have required changing it.

She pulled the other chair out with her foot and sat down again.

"Fine," she said. "Let's do that."

* * *

The room did not get bigger once the truth was in it. If anything it shrank around the words, around the desk and the radiator and the cracked linoleum by the sink. June crossed to the little basement window and shoved it up two inches. Cold air slid in with the smell of wet brick. She tapped a cigarette from a soft pack, looked at Lena, then put it between her lips without asking permission. The lighter sparked. For a second her face glowed from below, all bone and fatigue.

Lena used to know the entire map of June's moods from the way she smoked. Fast drags meant panic. Half a cigarette abandoned in the sink meant she was about to leave and fix somebody else's emergency. Slow, measured, almost mean pulls meant she'd made up her mind about something and didn't care if anyone liked it. Tonight the drags were even. Inventory drags. Accountancy.

"You asked why," June said. Smoke thinned out at the cracked window. "You want the order or the mess?"

"The mess." Lena leaned her forearms on the table. The laminate was cold through her coat sleeves. "If you give me one reason, I'll know you're lying again."

June gave the smallest hint of a smile, not warm, not pleased, more like acknowledgment of a fair condition. "Fine."

Lena unfolded the bank statement and flattened it between them. The yellow highlighting looked cheap and childish in the bad kitchen light. "Start with the money."

June looked down. "Three eighty-two."

Lena's stomach turned.

"September fifteenth," June went on. "Cash App. The smashed van window and the missing gear. Then six forty, September twenty-ninth, after D.C., for Noelle and the hotel story. Then two nine forty on October tenth. Wire."

Lena had known the numbers. She had cross-checked them in bed, in the bath, on Farah's back-room milk crate with her phone brightness turned down. Hearing June recite them from memory made them intimate again in a way she hadn't prepared for. They lived in June's mouth too. Of course they did.

"You remember that exactly."

"Yeah."

"Because it was my money."

"Because I remember numbers."

"Don't get cute."

"I'm not being cute." June ash-flipped into a mug with a chipped Flyers logo. "You asked."

Lena pressed one finger to the highlighted lines until the paper bent. "Which asks were you writing yourself?"

"All of them."

The answer came so fast Lena almost missed it.

"All."

"The first one was me. D.C. was me. The big one was me." June took the cigarette out of her mouth. "That kind of shame doesn't automate well."

Lena sat back. The chair wobbled. "Jesus Christ."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Don't make that your shocked face. You saw the dashboard."

Lena laughed at her then, a raw stupid sound. "My shocked face." She rubbed her eyes with the heels of her hands until color flashed. "I've got about six faces left, June. Be grateful you got a reusable one."

June let that pass. She kept one hand curled around the cigarette, ash lengthening without falling. "The first transfer happened because you offered before he finished refusing. I was broke and you put it on the table. That's the ugly truth. There wasn't a speech around it."

"How broke."

June looked at her. Lena held the stare until June answered.

"DeLuca had eighteen hundred in back rent and fees on me."

That figure Lena knew too, now. She had found it in the logic of everything after the fact, in the late notices open on the desk during the storm, in the way June had tightened through October. Still, hearing the landlord's name made the theft local, almost neighborly. Her money had not floated away into scam abstraction. It had crossed three streets and turned into another month of June keeping a key.

"And the rest."

"The six forty after D.C. went to PECO and groceries and the card minimum because everything was due at once. The wire paid DeLuca, killed the loudest piece of the Visa, bought Teo a winter coat because he'd grown out of the last one and was acting like he hadn't, and kept the phone on."

Lena stared at the chipped mug full of ash. A coat. Electricity. Fees. Groceries. All the dull nouns that kept a life from humiliating itself in public. None of them made her feel better. They made the betrayal feel more domestic, which was worse.

"And what did you buy just because you wanted it."

June's eyelids lowered. "A pair of sneakers."

The answer was so fast, so unvarnished, that Lena looked up.

"What."

"Black Nikes. Outlet. Seventy-eight dollars." June met her eyes. "You asked for the mess."

The room held for a second on that stupid specific number. Seventy-eight dollars. Not some operatic waste. Not diamonds. Sneakers. The exact kind of purchase that happened when someone had been holding their breath over bills so long they mistook any new object for relief.

Lena thought of her mother's ring sliding across the pawn counter under fluorescent light, the man with the loupe barely looking at her face. She had told herself that day it was temporary, that the ring was metal and the man was need and she was being adult about a crisis. Seventy-eight dollars of her had walked out on June's feet.

"Did you look at my bank app too?" Lena asked. "Was that in the system? Trailer-sale cash. Tax bill. Which day my balance looked soft enough to push?"

June did not flinch. "I didn't need your login. I had you. You told me what you were scared of. You told me what was earmarked and what wasn't. You told me how much the trailer was clearing for and how guilty you felt touching it. You said the numbers out loud in this room."

The words landed harder than if June had hacked her. Lena had given it, because people did that with best friends. They said, I have eighteen-six after closing and four-two has to stay untouched for taxes and if I touch it I am stealing from my future. They said those things while eating Chinese takeout out of the cartons. They said them because speaking a number to someone you trusted could make it feel less like a number and more like a life.

"So I did the work for you."

"Yes."

The yes was too simple. Lena felt the cheap table edge under her fingers and imagined driving it through the wall. Instead she said, "Okay. Money. You were broke, and I was there. That's one part. Not enough."

June smoked, then pinched the ash off with her thumbnail. "No. Not enough."

"Why start it."

June looked toward the desk again, toward the closed machines. "You remember that night you let me rewrite your profile."

"Obviously."

"You kept saying the same thing." June's voice went flatter, imitating Lena's old complaints without doing an impression. "'I don't need gorgeous. I just need somebody who pays attention. Somebody competent. Somebody who doesn't make tenderness feel embarrassing. Somebody who sounds like he lives in a body and a city and not in a podcast.'" She looked back at Lena. "You kept making it sound like this impossible species. Like men were too stupid or too selfish or too scared to learn you that well."

"That justifies none of this."

"I didn't say it justified it."

"Good."

June stubbed the cigarette out and immediately lit another from the butt, a habit Lena had always found revolting and weirdly efficient. "At first I wanted to see if you were right. If the thing you wanted was actually rare or if it was just detail. Timing, memory, restraint. I had the tools. I had the source material. I had you talking in my kitchen like a manual with feelings attached."

"So joke, experiment, proof."

"Yeah."

"And when did it stop being that."

June took a long drag. "When it worked."

Lena waited.

"When you answered the first voice note like you'd been waiting on it longer than a week. When you started checking your phone in the middle of other people talking. When you smiled at nothing and then lied about what you were smiling at." June blew smoke toward the crack in the window and missed. "When I realized I could get the temperature right."

That phrase made Lena's skin feel too tight. "The temperature."

"Yeah." June's eyes came back to her face, steady now, almost cold. "How long to wait. What kind of joke to make. When to send a voice note instead of text. How much care you would believe before it tipped over into bullshit. If he complimented your face first you brushed it off. If he mentioned your hands after a long caption file you melted. If he asked questions too directly, you got suspicious. If he remembered something sideways, like your mother hating navels and insisting on the cheap oranges, you gave him another week for free. I kept learning it."

Lena had to look away. The sink held two bowls with dried rice welded to them. There was a magnet shaped like a cartoon tomato on the mini-fridge, chipped at the top. Mateo's algebra packet sat half under the salt. So much life still in the room. So much dumb ordinary evidence that people ate, forgot homework, bought discount produce, smoked by cracked windows. It did not belong next to what June was saying, and that was exactly why it did.

"You make it sound like tuning a radio."

"Sometimes it felt like that."

"And that felt good."

June did not answer.

"Say it."

June's mouth tightened around the cigarette. "Sometimes, yeah."

Lena could not sit still. She got up and walked the six available steps between the table and the desk, then back again. On the desk was a roll of gaffer tape, a dead succulent in a yogurt cup, a receipt folded into quarters. Any one of those things could have existed in the same air as their friendship before. Now every object seemed contaminated by its proximity to the machinery of attention.

"Not the money," Lena said. "Not the proving-something bullshit. The part where you kept doing it because it felt good. What exactly felt good."

June watched her pace. "You really want that answer."

"I came over for the answers you don't want to hand me."

June put the cigarette down in the mug. "It felt good to get it right. It felt good to make something that could move you that cleanly."

"Move me."

"You know what I mean."

"No, I want your words."

June's face changed, just slightly. The jokes had been gone for a while. Now even the defensive ease left. What remained looked close to shame, but Lena did not trust the resemblance.

"It felt good," June said, each word set down separately, "that I knew how to get to you."

The room was very quiet. The radiator had gone silent. Someone upstairs dragged a chair over bare floor. On the block outside a scooter coughed past and faded.

"Through a fake man."

"Yeah."

"Through my phone."

"Yeah."

"Through my body."

June's throat moved. "Yeah."

Lena stopped pacing. "Did you get off on it."

June looked at the mug, at the smoke still climbing off the crushed end, then back at Lena. She did not reach for a lie.

"Sometimes," she said.

There it was. Not a confession shaped for absolution. Not tears. Not a movie. Just a door opened onto something Lena would now have to live with.

She put both hands flat on the table because she needed the weight of them somewhere. The cheap laminate was sticky near the sugar jar. Her palms made soft peeling sounds when she shifted them. She remembered Eli's voice going low and patient on video, the way he had told her to slow down, the way she had obeyed because being guided had felt like rest. She remembered the pulse of humiliation afterward and the deeper pulse under that, the one that had felt like gratitude. Somewhere in the next room of reality June had sometimes been the one holding the switch for that.

"You could've just wanted me," Lena said. The sentence surprised her coming out. "You could've just been jealous. You could've just been broke. Any one of those would've at least had a shape. You had to pick the ugliest version of all of them at once."

June gave a tired little shrug that was not dismissal, more like reluctant agreement. "Yeah."

Lena laughed again, smaller this time. "You are unbelievable."

"No," June said. "I'm very believable. That's part of the problem."

Lena looked up sharply. "Do not get smart with me."

"I'm not." June leaned back against the sink, arms folded. "You want the rest? Fine. The money started as opportunity. I was underwater and you offered it. After D.C. it got meaner."

Lena went still.

"Meaner how."

June rubbed her thumb over the lighter wheel. "Because you still stayed."

Lena stared at her.

"You stood in that bar in that red dress you were gonna return, you got humiliated in public, and you still came home defending him. You sat right there on that chair and told me he was ashamed, that Noelle needed help, that sometimes real life was ugly. You kept making room for him." June's voice did not rise, but something in it sharpened to a blade. "And part of me looked at you and thought, of course. Of course you get to turn this into another story where your hurt makes everybody lean in closer."

For a second Lena could not connect the words. Then the sentence found all the old hooks in her.

"There it is," she said quietly.

June gave a tight nod. "Yeah."

"Say that part better."

"You always got to be the one people softened around." June was looking straight at her now. "When your marriage blew up, everybody acted like you were made of glass for a year. When your mother died, it was casseroles and careful voices and people asking what you needed. When you screwed up work because you were tired or sad or all over the place, there was always some reason anybody decent was supposed to understand. You got to be a person with context."

Lena felt the words like a row of fingers pressing bruises to see which one still hurt.

June kept going. Stress always made her tidier. She was almost painfully articulate now. "When I got behind, I was a mess. When I got sharp because I was tired, I was an asshole. When I couldn't pay rent, I got a fee notice taped to the door. When Teo's school called, they didn't ask if I needed anything. They asked if I understood policy." She looked at the statement between them. "Even your bad luck came with an asset attached. You lost your mother and she still left a trailer that could turn into cash. I missed two months and got DeLuca."

The trailer. The ring. The whole filthy arithmetic of grief and money. Lena felt something in her recoil so hard it was almost physical pain. Not in her chest or throat. Lower. In the gut, where shame and rage used the same wiring.

"You think my mother dying made me lucky."

"I think even your losses had witnesses." June flicked the dead cigarette into the sink. "Mine came with late fees."

Lena's hands curled. The table edge dug crescents into the underside of her fingers. "So you built a boyfriend and charged me for the difference."

June did not answer.

"No, say yes or no."

A beat.

"Yes."

The bluntness of it almost cleaned the air. Almost.

Lena nodded slowly. "And all those years of showing up. The rides. The fixing shit. Mateo's school forms. My fan. Gabe's sink. Everybody calling you because you knew how to solve things. You kept score."

June's laugh was small and ugly. "Everybody keeps score."

"Not like this."

"Not like this, no."

"You could've told me you were angry."

June looked genuinely tired then. "To what end."

"To the end where I knew."

"You knew some of it."

"Not this."

"No." June's mouth flattened. "Not this. But you knew you liked me useful."

Lena opened her mouth and then shut it. The sentence did not land clean because part of it was true. June had been the one who knew how to reset the breaker, how to talk to a mechanic without getting sold bullshit, how to translate a school notice, how to keep a family barbecue from turning into a fight. Lena had relied on that the way people relied on streetlights, not maliciously, just by letting them be there until they forgot somebody paid the bill.

June saw the hit and kept her foot on it, because of course she did. "You got to need and I got to handle. That was the arrangement, even when nobody said it."

"Arrangement." Lena tasted the word and nearly spit it out. "You say that like I signed something."

"You didn't have to sign it. You just lived inside it."

Lena stood so fast the chair tipped and clattered against the cabinet. The sound made both of them glance toward the door where Mateo had gone, then back at each other.

"You do not get," Lena said, each word clipped but complete, "to drag one true ugly thing out of our friendship and use it like a permit for what you did."

June straightened. "I'm not asking for a permit."

"Good, because there isn't one."

"I know that."

"Do you." Lena righted the chair with her foot. "Because this whole night you keep handing me pieces that are true and then looking at me like maybe if enough of them are true, the shape they make stops being monstrous."

June's face went blank. "I know it's monstrous."

"No. I think you know it's effective." Lena heard herself getting too exact and let it happen. She wanted the dates and nouns tonight. She wanted nothing hazed over. "You liked that you could build a person out of stolen video and my own stupid disclosures. You liked that you could test him and adjust him. You liked that you could watch me answer. You liked that the first money ask worked. You liked that the second one worked after D.C. because it meant I was still in. You liked being right about me."

June let out one breath through her nose. "Yeah."

The naked agreement enraged Lena more than denial would've.

"And what, exactly, were you right about."

June crossed her arms tighter. "That if somebody paid attention hard enough, you'd call it love."

Lena stared at her. Then she nodded once, sharp. "There. That's the line you wanted all along."

"No."

"Don't back off now."

"I'm not backing off." June pushed away from the sink. "You want the ugliest line? Fine. Part of me wanted to see if the thing you kept waiting for was actually just obedience with better timing. Part of me wanted to know if I could build it before some real guy stumbled into your life and got credit for understanding things I had understood for years."

The sentence hung there, heavier than either of them moved to catch.

Desire was in it, but not the clean kind. Not a hidden-love reveal that would let anyone rearrange the story into tragedy. It was possession without tenderness, envy with a pulse in it, the ugly thrill of authorship crossing into appetite. Lena felt sick with how much of it made sense and how little that mattered.

"You should hear yourself," she said.

"I do hear myself."

"Do you hear how pathetic that sounds?"

June's expression changed again, a flicker of temper at last. "You're not going to stand in my kitchen and call me pathetic like you haven't spent four months crying over a workflow."

Lena took one step forward. "A workflow you wrote."

"Yeah."

"A workflow you used to make me come on camera."

June's face shut. "Yeah."

"A workflow you used to get money out of me."

"Yeah."

"A workflow you fed with things I told my best friend."

June swallowed. "Yeah."

Lena let the silence after that do some work. She could hear water knocking in the pipes. She could hear a television upstairs, muffled game-show applause. The block did not care that her life had just been reduced to a set of admissions in a basement apartment. Outside somebody laughed too loud. A delivery truck idled at the corner.

When she spoke again, her voice was lower.

"Listen carefully. I'm going to say this once."

June nodded.

"You owe me the money. All of it. Every stupid dollar. The three eighty-two. The six forty. The two nine forty. Anything else I find later. You owe me for the sneakers too."

One side of June's mouth twitched, not toward humor, more toward flinch. "Fine."

"That's debt," Lena said. "That's not conversation. That's not repair. That's math."

June stayed still.

"You don't come by my apartment. You don't fix anything in it. You don't leave food at my door. You don't ask Gabe how I am. You don't show up at Sunday dinner and act like this is some bad month we're all going to get through if we keep our voices low."

"I wasn't planning to."

"I don't care what you were planning. I'm telling you the terms."

June's chin lifted by a fraction. "Okay."

Lena almost snapped again at the word, but she was past the point where another explosion would buy her anything. She pressed on.

"If Farah sees you, that's between you and Farah. If Gabe sees you, same. If you want to tell Mateo some version that lets him sleep, do that. I'm not going to stand in a doorway and help you with it."

At Mateo's name, something finally cracked in June's posture. Not collapse. Just a strain at the edges.

"He'll ask where you've been," June said.

"Then tell him I know what you did."

"Len-"

"No. You don't get to use me to cushion it." Lena picked up the folded statement and shoved it back into the paper bag. "You tell him whatever level of truth you can live with. That part is yours."

June rubbed a hand over her face. "I'm not asking you to cushion it."

"Good."

Lena got her coat straight, found one sleeve inside out, fixed it with fingers that were less steady than she wanted. June watched her. The space between them was crowded with old muscle memory: June tossing Lena her scarf on the way out the door, Lena reaching blindly for the pack of gum June always had in her jacket, the thousand stupid civilian intimacies of a friendship long enough to stop noticing itself. Every one of them felt like an exposed wire now.

June said, "I can start sending it back this week."

Lena looked at her.

"Do that," she said. "But don't confuse that with me taking you back."

June's eyes lowered for the first time all night. "I won't."

Lena believed she believed that. It made no difference.

At the door she stopped, not because she wanted one last line, not because there was anything left to wring from the room, but because the block outside waited on the other side of the thin metal, and once she stepped through it she would be carrying this into weather and traffic and the tailor shop awning and Farah's register and Gabe's kitchen and every other place June's name had once moved easily. Severance was not clean. It had to pass through shared geography.

She turned back.

June was standing by the sink with the cigarette burned to a long bent column between her fingers, ash threatening the floor. The smoke curled around her face and climbed toward the window. She looked older than thirty-five in that second, not because guilt had transformed her, not because anything grand had happened, but because exhaustion finally had room to show.

Lena thought, against her will, of all the times June had driven across the city at midnight because somebody's radiator died or somebody's kid needed picking up or Lena herself had called crying from a parking lot and June had come without making a lesson out of it. The thought arrived like an old reflex and she let it arrive. Letting it exist was not forgiveness. It was just part of the mess. June had really loved her in some forms. June had also done this. Both things sat in the room together, refusing to simplify each other.

"You should've just told me you hated me," Lena said.

June's face twisted, quick and involuntary. "I didn't hate you."

"No," Lena said. "You just built a man to prove it."

She opened the door before June could answer that, if June had any answer. Cold air hit her eyes immediately. The concrete steps were damp. Somewhere farther up the block Mateo laughed at something through the wall or maybe in the hallway above, the sound thin through old plaster and still recognizably his. Lena climbed out of the basement well one hand on the rail, the paper bag rustling against her coat. The street looked exactly like itself. Cars at the curb. Dark tree pits. A string of weak Christmas lights in Mrs. Padilla's front window already dropping one bulb every other night. No one had marked the sidewalk to show where a friendship ended.

June did not follow.

At the top of the steps Lena stood for a moment with the cold moving through her coat and under it, down to the skin. She could feel the folded statement under her arm, the highlighted numbers still there, stupid and real. If June sent money, Lena would take it. Rent was rent. Taxes were taxes. But the person on the other end of those transfers would not be her friend.

That was debt now. Nothing else.
