# Chapter 3

By the time Lena turned onto Gabe's block, the foil pan under her arm had warmed through the dish towel and her phone had buzzed six times against the back pocket of her jeans, quick trapped-bee hits she could feel all the way into her hip. The street already had enough noise without Eli adding his own private frequency to it. Somebody was arguing through a first-floor screen door about the Phillies bullpen. A scooter tore past too fast for the block and made a mother on a stoop yell after it. From half a block away Lena could hear one of Gabe's girls doing the long theatrical cry that meant she was not hurt at all, only offended in public.

She stopped under the sliver of shade from a sycamore and checked the phone anyway.

`You there yet?`

Then, thirty seconds later:

`Tell me what the house smells like.`

Then:

`Actually don't. I'll go insane and eat venue almonds for dinner.`

It was not even a real joke and it still got her. The smile came before she could manage it. She had changed her shirt twice for Sunday dinner at her brother's house, which was humiliating enough, and put on mascara like Gabe's twins were going to file a report if she looked tired. June would have called her on it. Gabe would have called her on it louder.

She typed, `Almost there. Sauce. Child sweat. Lemon Pledge probably.`

The three dots came back so fast it made something warm and cheap unspool under her ribs.

`Perfect. I want the full census later.`

She shoved the phone away before she could reread that into meaning and mounted Gabe's stoop with the pan on one hip. The rowhouse door was open to the metal security gate. Heat and tomato and child soap came straight out at her. The house looked the way it always did on Sundays, as if it had been losing a fight with objects for hours and was somehow winning anyway. Tiny sneakers under the radiator cover. A pink plastic doctor kit on the floor by the umbrella stand. A damp beach towel hanging over the banister because one of the girls had insisted on making a fort with it and then abandoned the project halfway through. There was never a clean blank stretch of floor in Gabe's place. Something always got there first.

"If that's another vegetable," Gabe called from the kitchen, "turn around and go home."

Lena stepped inside and kicked the door nearly shut with her heel. "It's a vegetable. I brought it because I love your children and want them to live long enough to resent you."

Gabe leaned out from the kitchen in a gray SEPTA T-shirt gone darker at the chest and collar with sweat. He had a wooden spoon in one hand and a pencil behind one ear, like he had been taking notes on the stove. His face had the permanently tired drag it always got by Sunday afternoon, but he was grinning. "My children eat red sauce. That's a vegetable. Ask a court."

"Ask a cardiologist."

"I don't trust rich people doctors."

"You say that every time and then you take their prescriptions."

"That's because I contain multitudes."

He took the pan from her anyway, peered under the foil, and made a show of disappointment. "Zucchini. Jesus Christ."

"You can say no thank you and die younger."

From the dining room archway, Marisol said, "Put it down before you drip on my floor, Gabe."

Marisol sat cross-legged on one of the dining chairs with a cordless trimmer in her hand, shaving a snag off the edge of a bright orange press-on nail. She had done a box color on her hair and wrapped it in a silk scarf printed with chains and lemons, though damp strands had already escaped at the nape of her neck. Her reading glasses were on top of her head for no reason Lena had ever understood. She never read in them. Tati leaned against her knee in a bathing suit top and shorts, solemnly applying a sticker to her own elbow. Mina had red rain boots on bare feet and the wet shine of recent hand-washing all over her cheeks.

"I mopped twice," Marisol said. "Today I refuse to watch tomato footprints happen in real time."

"That's fair," Lena said.

The booted twin looked up at her with the grave authority only six-year-olds and judges possessed. "Tati cheated."

"I didn't cheat," said Tati, still working the sticker onto her elbow. "You cried first."

"That's not about cheating."

"It's about winning."

Lena bent to kiss both heads because not doing it would have started another argument. The girls smelled like Johnson's shampoo and the faint sweet rot of dried juice. They hit her on the way back up with the full soft weight of children who assumed her body was a public utility. It landed somewhere low in her chest, that casual claim. Gabe's whole house did that. It pressed people into one another until privacy seemed like an expensive taste.

"Where's June?" she asked, because the air felt wrong without the extra noise June usually brought.

Gabe rolled his eyes and went back into the kitchen. "Late. As always. She texted Marisol something about Staples and ice and Mateo needing sneakers apparently this minute."

"His foot grew again," Marisol said. "That kid is built like a weed. Also your brother killed the fan in the front room."

"The fan was making a death noise at me," Gabe called.

"Because you hit it."

"I adjusted it."

"With your foot."

The box fan in the front window sat at an angle, unmoving, one side of the plastic grille caved in. The room without it felt close and wet. Sweat gathered under Lena's bra line, in the bend of her elbows, under the cheap metal of her ring. The radiator cover was warm from trapped sunlight. Somewhere upstairs a cartoon shrieked from a television nobody seemed to be watching.

Her phone buzzed again.

This time it was a photo from Eli: his hand holding a paper cup of coffee in front of what looked like a black-painted backstage hallway. No face. Just wrist, cup, a strip of silver rings on a case handle in the background, and the caption: `Ten more minutes and I'm yours.`

She looked at it long enough for Marisol to catch her.

Marisol tipped her head. "There he is."

"Shut up."

"I'm not saying anything."

"Your whole face is saying something."

"My whole face says lots of things. I'm very expressive."

One of the girls climbed into Lena's lap before she had actually committed to sitting down. The other followed because being left out of somebody else's body was intolerable. Lena let herself get pinned there on the dining chair, both small knees digging into her thighs, and tried not to check the phone again while the house worked around her. Gabe stirred sauce and cursed at the oven door. Marisol shouted upstairs for nobody to jump on a bed, even though both twins were visibly downstairs. Somebody in the alley behind the house dropped a glass bottle into recycling with a sound like a snapped bell.

This was the part Lena missed without admitting she missed it. Not motherhood, not exactly, though sometimes that too in ugly little flashes she never looked at straight. She missed being expected somewhere noisy. She missed the way Gabe's place never asked whether she wanted touching before it handed her three different kinds. Her apartment had room to think in, and room to think in could become its own kind of sickness if she let it.

The security gate banged. June came in backward with two bags looped around one wrist and a twenty-pound bag of ice balanced against her hip. Mateo followed with a shoe box under one arm and a case of seltzer hugged to his chest. He had his school hoodie on despite the heat, hood down, beat-up headphones hanging around his neck with one earcup patched in silver tape. Acne had broken out hard across his forehead and he seemed newly angry at his own height, as if the extra inches had arrived without asking. A black composition notebook stuck out of the back pocket of his shorts, pages warped from use.

"Nobody helps around here?" June said. "Love that for you all."

"You're late," Marisol said.

"Correct. And still carrying civilization in both hands."

June dropped the bags on the table, took one look at the dead fan, and clicked her tongue. "Who kicked it?"

"It was already dying," Gabe said.

"That means you kicked it while it was dying."

Mateo put the seltzer down carefully, as if he did not trust glass near this family, and said, "She made me buy a screwdriver set at Staples because she said grown men shouldn't be allowed near electrical items if they're related to us."

"I said some grown men," June said.

"You looked right at Gabe."

"Because visual aids help."

Even Marisol laughed at that. June set the ice by the sink and fished a tiny yellow-handled screwdriver out of the Staples bag. She crouched in front of the fan with her knees apart and the silk of her blouse sticking to her spine, moving with the quick irritated economy Lena had watched since they were thirteen and June had been the only girl in class who could fix the chain on a bike with a house key. Her nicotine-stained fingers worked the bent grille loose. She leaned her head close to the blades, blew away a clot of gray lint, and said, "Teo, freezer. Then wash your hands and rinse that basil. Gabe, get me a butter knife. Not that one. The one you did not already put in your mouth."

"Bossy," Gabe muttered, but he handed her the clean knife.

Mateo paused on his way to the freezer. "Do I also get paid in vibes or is that just family policy?"

"You get dinner," June said.

"Same thing, basically."

He disappeared into the kitchen. A beat later Lena heard the freezer door thump, then Mateo saying to one of the twins, "If you lick the bag of ice again, you're drinking hot juice like a pioneer."

The box fan coughed once, rattled with offended drama, and then spun into life. A strip of cooler air came off it thin as mercy but real. It moved the damp hair off Lena's neck. Marisol put a hand over her heart as if June had performed surgery.

"Marry me instead," Marisol said.

"Tempting," June said without looking up.

Then she stood, wiped her hands on the sides of her jeans, and finally took Lena in properly. Her eyes flicked once over the mascara, the shirt, the phone clenched a little too hard in Lena's hand.

"You look nice," June said.

"Don't start."

"I didn't start. I said you look nice."

"Which in your mouth means I obviously tried."

"You obviously did try. That's not illegal."

The girls had migrated toward the revived fan and were holding their mouths in front of it to make robot voices. Gabe barked at them not to stick crayons in the grille. Marisol got up to rescue the salad from whatever was happening in the kitchen. In the temporary cover of everybody moving, June hooked two fingers in Lena's elbow and steered her toward the narrow strip of space between the fridge and the back door.

"Breathe," June said quietly. "It's dinner, not a parole hearing."

"I'm breathing."

"You're auditing your own breathing. Different thing."

Lena huffed once through her nose. Up close June smelled like cigarettes ghosted under drugstore jasmine body spray and the freezer-cold damp of the ice bag. Familiar enough to knock Lena down into herself a little. "Is it weird if he calls?"

"At a table full of people who already know you have a phone?" June said. "No. It's only weird if you make it weird."

"Helpful."

"Len. Gabe would have mocked any man you brought home even if he came with a deed and a saint's blessing. Let him have somebody to bounce off. He'll live."

It was ridiculous how much that helped. June had always been able to tune a room for her, take the shriek out of it and leave the useful noise. When they were teenagers she could walk Lena into a party and tell her in under ten seconds who was safe, who was lying, who was so drunk they would cry in half an hour. She did it now with one dry sentence and a thumb over the tomato stain Lena had not noticed on the side of her pan towel, rubbing it off against the sink.

"Also," June said, stepping back, "if he sucks on speaker, you'll know faster."

"Jesus."

"That was me being practical."

"You're enjoying this."

"A little," June said. "You're cute when you pretend you aren't losing your mind."

Before Lena could answer, Mateo came back from the sink carrying a fistful of basil and the shoe box under his other arm.

"Do I put these anywhere," he asked, lifting the sneakers, "or are they ceremonial."

"Front closet," June said.

"That closet's a war crime," Marisol called from the kitchen.

"Then kick it in and make room," June called back.

The house answered with laughter, spoon clatter, the fan's steady chop. Lena's phone buzzed again. Eli this time: `Outside now. Call whenever. No rush.`

No rush was one of his better tricks. He never said do you miss me right now, do you want me enough, are you thinking about me instead of whoever is in front of you. He made room and let her hurry into it herself.

She locked the screen and slid the phone facedown onto the counter.

For ten whole seconds she felt almost normal.

***

They ate at a table technically built for six and currently holding eight bodies, three serving bowls, a sweating pitcher of tap water, the dead and then resurrected fan moving one corner of the room, and two coloring books nobody had succeeded in confiscating from the twins. Gabe's sauce had gone deep and dark from the pot, sweet first and then the sausage heat after. Marisol had made a salad with shaved fennel because she got on seasonal kicks and liked converting people against their will. The zucchini Lena brought sat mostly untouched except by June, who put a disapproving amount on Gabe's plate whenever he looked away.

Lena spent the first ten minutes pretending she could eat normally while Eli's last text waited in her pocket like a held hand. One of the girls kept kicking the table leg in an absent rhythm. Mateo, seated sideways at the end because there was no formal place left for him, ate with one headphone on the table beside his plate and the other still around his neck. He had a habit of straightening the fork and knife so they lay parallel after every few bites, then messing them up again without noticing. Marisol pointed out twice that he was hunching and twice he said he was not.

"You want another meatball, Teo?" Gabe asked.

"I want school to get canceled forever," Mateo said.

"Not what I asked."

"Then no."

"He'll eat half a loaf of bread in secret in an hour," June said.

"That's slander," Mateo said.

"That's eyewitness testimony."

Gabe wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, saw Lena glance toward her pocket, and grinned. "All right. Enough of this. Is Phone Romeo calling or not?"

Both girls lit up instantly.

"Phone Romeo," one of them repeated, delighted.

"Phone Romeo!" said the other, because nothing mattered more to twins than synchronized mockery.

"Can you not call him that," Lena said.

"You bring a man to Sunday dinner by Bluetooth, you get what you get," Gabe said. "Call him."

Marisol snorted into her water. "If you don't call him soon she's going to grind a groove in the table with her knee."

June, chewing, said, "That's true."

"Everybody shut up," Lena said, which only made the girls laugh harder.

Her hand was damp when she pulled the phone out. She did not want to examine why. Not here, with the sauce smell sitting thick in the curtains and Gabe reaching for more bread and one twin using a carrot stick as a wand. She hit call before she could rethink the whole idea.

Eli picked up on the second ring.

"Hey," he said, and even through the speaker the low warmth of his voice changed the room for her. "Am I interrupting crimes?"

"Only dinner," Lena said.

"Worse. I should have worn a tie."

Gabe pointed at the phone. "This Gabe?"

"That's me," Eli said. In the background there was a hollow bang, maybe a loading door, maybe a case getting dropped. He raised his voice a little over it without losing the easy tone. "Hi, Gabe. Sorry to crash the meal."

"You're not crashing till you do it in person," Gabe said. "Right now you're a podcast."

Eli laughed, not too hard. "Fair. Then I'm honored to be your least educational one."

June lowered her eyes to her plate to hide a smile. Mateo did not hide his.

"What are you feeding her," Eli asked. "She already sent me the smell report but I don't trust her to classify sausage accurately."

"Excuse me," Lena said.

"That's slander against my Italian side," Gabe said, though Lena had never once heard him claim an Italian side before. "We're having gravy. Meatballs, sausage, salad nobody asked for, and Lena brought zucchini because she hates joy."

"Zucchini is joy if you do it right," Eli said.

Marisol leaned toward the phone. "Finally, a civilized person."

"Marisol, right?" Eli said. "Thank you for taking my side before you've even seen me."

"I respect anybody who'll eat a vegetable in this house."

"Then I'll eat two and make eye contact."

It was skillful, but not slick enough to announce skill. That was the thing. He did not come in swinging. He met the room where it was, quiet enough that people leaned a fraction toward him instead of shrinking back. Lena could feel Gabe testing for cheese and not finding any obvious amount.

The twin in the red boots had abandoned her chair and was now standing with both palms on the table, speaking straight at the phone. "Do you have tattoos?"

Lena nearly dropped her fork. "Mina."

"What?" Mina said. "I'm asking."

Eli did not miss a beat. "Yeah, I do."

The other twin popped up too. "How many?"

"Two good ones and one I got at nineteen because I thought being stupid made me interesting."

Gabe barked a laugh. "Now that's honest."

"What's the bad one," Mateo asked, before Lena could process that he had chosen to join the conversation at all.

"A compass that points west no matter what," Eli said. "Which would be poetic if I had any idea where west was."

"That doesn't even make sense," Mateo said.

"Exactly."

One of the girls shrieked with delight. "I want a mermaid tattoo."

"No tattoos till you're thirty," Marisol said.

"Thirty is old."

"Good," Gabe said. "Stay scared."

The questions came loose after that the way they always did once children decided an adult was worth the effort. Did Eli have a dog. No, but he kept trying to befriend every ugly venue cat available to him. Did he play guitar. Not well enough to make anybody suffer through it at dinner. Did he know Taylor Swift. Personally, no; spiritually, against his will, yes. Could he hear the fan. Absolutely, and it sounded like it had survived battle.

"June fixed it," Lena said before she could stop herself, sudden and proud the way people got about tiny domestic competence.

"Of course she did," Eli said. "June saves another household."

"You hear that?" June said, lifting her glass. "Public record."

"Write it down for tax purposes," Gabe said.

Lena watched the pleasure move around the table in small ordinary ways. Marisol smiling despite herself when Eli complimented the fennel. Mateo actually looking at the phone when Eli asked what he was dreading most about school and Mateo, after pretending not to care, said, "Group projects," with a level of disgust that made Eli say, "Correct answer," like he had won something. The girls abandoning interrogation long enough to yell over each other about whose birthday came first and insisting Eli guess. Gabe leaning back in his chair, arms folded, face easing from brotherly suspicion into the more dangerous thing, acceptance.

"So what do you actually do," Gabe said eventually. "Lena says sound designer, which means nothing to me."

"Mostly I make other people's messes less embarrassing," Eli said. "Live shows, recorded stuff, whatever pays. If a singer breathes like he's dying into a mic, I'm there. If a room hums like it hates God, I'm there. If somebody put an outlet in the stupidest possible place, I curse a little and keep moving."

Gabe snorted. "Now you're talking my language."

"Electrician solidarity?" Eli asked.

"SEPTA. Which means everybody blames me for power, delays, weather, and their own bad life choices."

"Then yes. Solidarity."

"He comes home mad at wires," Marisol said.

"Wires deserve it."

"Tell him about the guy who microwaved foil at the depot," Lena said.

Gabe groaned. "Why are you giving away my set?"

Eli said, "No, I need that story. It sounds healing."

And Gabe told it, because of course he did, with hand gestures and the exact proud disgust he gave every story in which another man had behaved like an idiot in a professional setting. The table leaned with him. Even the girls listened for almost twenty seconds, which in that house counted as reverence. Eli laughed in the right places, then asked one follow-up about whether the guy at least had the shame to deny it. Not too much. Enough.

Lena let herself watch instead of performing not-watching. That was the dangerous part too. Not the call itself. The way it made a shape for future around the table without asking permission first. Eli on speaker now, Eli maybe on a folding chair in this same room later, paper plate in hand, Gabe giving him shit over beer, the girls climbing him like public stairs, Marisol deciding in one look whether he was useful or decorative. It arrived whole enough that her body believed it before her head got to vote.

"When are we meeting you for real?" Marisol asked, because she had no patience for fake-casual social choreography.

Lena's face went hot.

Eli answered before she could. "Soon, I hope. I owe Gabe a proper union story and I owe your daughters evidence that I'm not just a voice in a rectangle."

"And the tattoo," Mina said.

"No tattoo," Marisol said.

"Maybe one glimpse of the stupid compass," Eli said. "Educational purposes."

"I support education," Gabe said.

"Traitor," Marisol told him.

One of the girls had somehow ended up with a meatball in her fist. The other had drawn a green spiral over half a coloring-book page and onto the table. Mateo took the phone from Lena without asking and angled it so Eli could see the drawing over the rim of the water pitcher.

"You should know what you're signing up for," he said.

"That's important consumer information," Eli said.

"She did a horse," Mateo said.

"That's not a horse," said the artist twin, outraged.

"It's emotionally a horse."

Eli laughed again, that soft quick breath of it. "I can tell. The horse has seen things."

The girls exploded. Even Lena laughed hard enough to bend. She had heard him be funny before, but alone with her, in the sealed little tunnel the two of them kept making. Here it landed differently. It had witnesses. It bounced off the walls and other people's mouths and came back altered, proven. Gabe was still smiling when he reached for the phone to hand it back.

"All right, podcast," he said. "You can stay on probation."

"High honor," Eli said. "Thank you for letting me interrupt."

"Next time you come hungry," Marisol said.

"Gladly."

Then, a fraction quieter, for Lena only though everyone could hear it if they wanted, he said, "Tell me later how the room sounded once everybody calmed down."

It was such a small thing. Sounded. Not looked, not went. Lena felt it anyway, the private note tucked inside the public one, the reminder that he knew where her mind lived. She swallowed and said, "Okay."

When the call ended, the room did not deflate. That might have been the worst part, later. It stayed warm and easy, as if a real person had just stepped outside to take another call and might come back in any minute. Gabe tore bread in half and tossed Lena one piece.

"Annoying," he said.

"Who?"

"Him. In a good way."

Marisol nodded. "Normal enough to be dangerous."

"What does that mean?" Lena asked.

"It means you like him more now because I said that."

The girls had moved on entirely. "Phone Romeo has a stupid tattoo," one of them sang.

"Phone Romeo doesn't know west," the other added.

Mateo, deadpan, said, "That part honestly tracks."

June caught Lena's eye across the table. She did not say I told you so. She did not have to. She lifted her brows once, small and private, and Lena felt herself answering with a look that probably gave away too much.

She ate the rest of her plate with the loose dizzy happiness of somebody who had gotten away with bringing a fragile thing into public and watched it survive.

***

The neighborhood had gone from white-hot to merely sticky by the time they left. Gabe pressed leftovers on them in mismatched containers because he always cooked as if weather and appetite were theoretical. Marisol sent half the fennel salad with Lena whether she wanted it or not. The girls tried to follow everyone onto the stoop barefoot until Gabe hauled them back by the elbows and threatened a bath with no cartoons. The fan could still be heard from the sidewalk, chopping away in the front room like proof of June's usefulness.

Mateo carried the seltzer June had somehow forgotten and the empty shoe box flattened under one arm. At the corner he stopped and pushed one earcup up.

"I'm going to Lopez because she wants cilantro too now," June said.

"You're going now?"

"It's two blocks."

"It was supposed to be before dinner."

"And then your uncle killed a fan."

Mateo looked at the sky as if requesting patience from a higher office. "Fine. But if she only has the sad cilantro left, I'm not taking blame."

"Nobody is prosecuting you over herbs."

"That's not how this neighborhood works."

June snorted. "Take twenty."

He took the bill, hesitated, then said to Lena, "Your guy was okay."

It sounded, from Mateo, almost extravagant. Before she could answer he added, "The compass tattoo is stupid, though," and headed toward the bodega with his shoulders folded in, composition notebook thumping lightly against his back pocket.

Lena watched him go. "He likes Eli."

"Mateo likes very few people on purpose," June said. "Take the win."

They turned toward Moore Street together, leftovers swinging between them. Warm air rose off the pavement in slow dirty breaths. People were back out on stoops now that the sun had dropped: old men in sleeveless undershirts, three teenage girls sharing one bag of chips, a woman in rollers smoking and yelling at somebody inside without real malice. A SEPTA bus groaned at the light. Somewhere close, somebody was frying garlic. Lena's skin still held the house on it, sauce and kid shampoo and the metallic cold brush of the revived fan.

June nudged her with one shoulder. "Your boy did good."

"He's not my boy."

"No? Whose is he?"

"Don't be gross."

"I'm being exact."

Lena laughed in spite of herself. The leftovers thumped against her leg. "You heard the call. He was just being normal."

"That's what I said. He was normal in a very efficient way."

"What does that mean?"

"It means Gabe handed him a test and he didn't act like he was taking one."

Lena heard the small barb in it and felt herself go instantly defensive. "Maybe because he wasn't. Maybe he's just easy with people."

"Maybe," June said.

"Why are you saying it like that?"

"Like what?"

"Like there's a hidden camera and he's secretly an actor."

June lit a cigarette off the stub of another one cupped in her palm, the flame briefly showing the tiredness in her face. "Len, relax. I'm complimenting him. A lot of men would've come on too strong. He didn't. That's good."

"Okay."

"Also he laughed at Gabe's depot moron story at the right places, which is advanced work."

"That's because it was funny."

"It was barely medium funny."

Lena stopped at the corner while a car nosed too far into the crosswalk. She could feel herself smiling again anyway, unable not to. The whole walk seemed to have taken on a thin lifted quality, as if the night had been aired out from inside. "He asked about the room after," she said.

"I heard."

"No, I mean he got what I'd want to tell. Not just what happened. The sound of it."

June looked sideways at her. "That did seem to hit you pretty hard."

Lena stared ahead at the bodega awning flapping once in the warm wind. There it was, the tiny opening, and because it was June she stepped through before she had sorted whether she should. "It did."

They crossed. Halfway down the block a sprinkler somebody had rigged to a hose leaked over the sidewalk in a fine slant, not enough to cool anything, just enough to freckle their shins. June hopped the wet patch without breaking stride. Lena walked through it. The water smelled faintly of rubber and old pennies.

"Why?" June asked.

Not nosy. Not gentle either. June at her best always asked like she expected a usable answer.

Lena shifted the containers in her arms. "Because they heard him."

"Groundbreaking."

"Shut up."

"I'm listening."

Lena blew out a breath. "No, seriously. They heard him and it wasn't embarrassing. Gabe didn't do the face."

"What face?"

"The face where he thinks I'm about to ruin my own life and he has to act like a comedian so I don't notice."

June's mouth flattened around the cigarette. She knew the face. She had seen it through the divorce, through the stupid months after when everybody handled Lena like a box with cracked dishes inside.

"Tonight he was just..." Lena searched for it and hated that it mattered enough to make the sentence difficult. "He was just my brother meeting somebody I like. That was all. Nobody was bracing."

June said nothing.

"And the girls liked him," Lena went on, because stopping now would have looked more embarrassing than continuing. "Mateo liked him. Marisol liked him, which I honestly trust more than Gabe. It made it feel less like I made him up in my apartment because I got lonely."

There. Ugly enough to be true.

June exhaled smoke through her nose. "You didn't make him up."

Lena gave her a look. "I know that."

"Do you."

"I hate you."

"No you don't."

She did not. That was part of the problem with June, with all the years packed into her. June could say the meaner version and it still felt like care because Lena knew how much of her life June had already carried with no audience. Rides home from bad jobs. Advil after migraines. Three straight nights on Lena's couch after the divorce when Lena kept waking up angry and ashamed and unable to stop talking. June had seen the least flattering shapes of her and stayed anyway. That kind of history made certain kinds of danger almost impossible to imagine.

They turned onto Lena's block. Her building sat above the dark tailor shop, second-floor windows open because the heat left no good choices. The night smelled like old brick, hot trash, basil from somebody's container garden, and the sugar leak of a busted fruit bag in the alley. June flicked ash into the gutter.

"How serious is this getting," she asked, casual enough that it nearly passed.

Lena looked at her. "What do you mean."

"I mean is this fun, or are you already reorganizing the furniture in your head."

It was too exact. That was June again, good enough at naming the motion before Lena herself finished making it. She could have laughed it off. She could have said obviously not, it has been what, a week and change, get a grip. Instead she heard herself say, "I don't know."

June waited.

"No, that's a lie," Lena said. The truth was in her mouth before she had sanded it down. "I think it might be bad."

"Bad how."

"Bad like..." She shifted the foil container against her ribs, felt sauce warmth still trapped in it. "Bad like if he keeps feeling this real I'm going to build a lot around him very fast."

June's face did not change much. It never had to. Tired dark eyes, cigarette ember, the unreadable set her mouth took when she was listening hardest. Lena pressed on because the words had broken loose now.

"Tonight made it worse," she said. "In a good way. I know how that sounds."

"It sounds normal."

"It does not feel normal."

"Why not."

Lena laughed once, no humor in it. "Because for two years everybody has politely stopped asking if there's anybody. Gabe asks about work. Marisol asks if I'm eating. Farah asks if I'm sleeping. Nobody asks if there's anybody because the answer kept being no and then it got rude to keep poking it. Tonight there was actually someone to hand them. Somebody who fit in the room. Do you know how sick that made me feel, how much I liked it?"

June took the cigarette out of her mouth. "That isn't sick."

"It felt a little sick."

"That is because wanting things always embarrasses you right before you get them."

Lena looked up at her apartment windows. Dark except for the small blue pulse of her router on the desk by the front room window, visible from the street if she leaned right. Inside it would still be too hot. The bedsheet would still cling. The sink would still have the coffee mug from this morning with the spoon welded to the bottom by dried sugar. But now Gabe and Marisol and the girls and even Mateo had heard Eli's voice. He existed in other people's ears. The feeling of that moved through her slow and strong, closer to relief than giddiness, though it had some of that too.

"I think I could get in real trouble with him," she said quietly.

June's gaze held on her a beat too long for a joke. Then she smiled, small and crooked, and bumped Lena's arm with the back of her hand.

"Then maybe don't tell him about tonight like you're filing wedding banns," she said. "Make him miss a little."

"You are such an asshole."

"And yet I'm right."

Lena smiled despite herself. "Maybe."

"Maybe nothing. Go upstairs. Text me when Lopez only has corpse cilantro so I can blame Mateo."

"You are sending a sixteen-year-old out for herbs at nine-thirty."

"I bought him sneakers. That's called governance."

Lena snorted. June started back toward the corner, smoke trailing behind her in the damp air. Halfway down the block she lifted a hand without turning around.

Upstairs, Lena let herself into the apartment and stood for a minute in the stale heat, leftovers in her arms, listening to the room take her weight. The place felt smaller after Gabe's house, as it always did, more deliberate in its loneliness. She set the containers on the table, kicked off her shoes, and checked her phone.

There was a text from Gabe already.

`okay fine. podcast can come around`

And under it, from Eli:

`You sounded happy.`

Then:

`I liked hearing you in there.`

Lena sat on the edge of her bed without meaning to and read both messages twice. Sauce and fennel and other people's detergent still clung to her shirt. Outside, a bus sighed at the corner and somebody laughed loud enough to bounce off the brick opposite her window. She typed back to Eli, deleted it, typed again.

For once the apartment did not feel like the only place where he existed.
