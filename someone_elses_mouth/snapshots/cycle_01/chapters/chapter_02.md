# Chapter 2

Three days after she gave him Signal, his name was already doing rude things to the room.

At 6:54 her phone rattled hard enough to tap the spoon in yesterday's mug. The box fan in the window clicked on its bad bearing every ninth turn, a cheap stubborn metronome. She had told him about the click the night before because he had asked what kept waking her. His message said, "You up or did that fan finally throw itself into traffic?"

She smiled before she was fully awake, which annoyed her. Thirty-five, divorced, behind on captions, six weeks out from a tax bill with teeth, and there she was smiling at a screen before she had even peed. She let the phone sit for what she hoped counted as normal-person timing. Then she wrote back, "Still here. Fan's all talk."

His reply came while she was standing at the sink with a dry mouth and one hand under cold water for no reason except the apartment had already started heating up.

"I don't trust it. If you disappear I'm blaming appliance negligence."

Then a photo: coffee in a paper cup balanced on a black road case, the lip of a loading dock behind it still blue with morning. His thumb cut into one corner of the shot. A strip of fluorescent tape crossed the case like a small emergency.

"Today's office."

Not a shirtless mirror picture. Not a gym selfie with a caption about earning it. A paper cup, a road case, dawn.

She took the phone into the bathroom with her, which was pathetic, but she did it anyway.

By seven-thirty she was at her desk in a sports bra and old shorts, hair knotted up, left heel hooked on the chair rung, cleaning captions on a condo-board Zoom from Cherry Hill. The auto transcript had turned reserve study into deserve slutty three times and the treasurer had a wet whistle on every s. Below her screen a folded IRS notice held down a cable that would not stay in place. The tailor shop downstairs hauled up its metal gate with the sound of a train dragging itself out of sleep. Somewhere on the block a scooter coughed awake, failed, then tried again.

Her phone lit.

"Did deserve slutty pass, or did they table it?"

She laughed out loud, alone in the room. She had mentioned that error once, quick, while brushing her teeth. Most people heard the general category of what she said and answered the outline of it. He kept the nuisance, the throwaway piece, the exact stupid phrasing.

"Still under debate," she typed. "The condo future hangs in the balance."

"Thoughts and prayers."

She put the phone face down, made herself work twelve minutes straight, and then turned it back over just to see if there was more. There was.

"Eat something."

"Bossy."

"You told me coffee on an empty stomach turns you into a fugitive."

She had said that, too. Not as a dramatic confession. Just as a joke the night before when she was explaining why she had once left an ex in a Target because he wanted to browse decorative baskets at eleven in the morning. She stood up, went to the kitchenette, and ate half a peach over the sink while the juice ran down her wrist and the fan pushed hot dust against the back of her knees.

It was not just that he answered fast. Plenty of men answered fast in the first week because they were working a slot machine, pushing the same bright buttons in six windows and waiting to see where the bells went off. Eli's attention did not feel broad. It felt angled. He did not come back with "how's your day going" every two hours like a customer service script. He returned to the crooked little things she had tossed off without ceremony and handed them back with his thumbprint on them.

At nine he sent a voice note from somewhere loud enough that his first word blurred.

"Hang on."

She heard him move. A door banged. The noise changed shape, generator to alley, broad metallic roar thinning down into a truck beep and somebody swearing about a lift gate.

"There. Better. I was gonna say, if that condo board approves deserve slutty as a budget line item, I need you to steal the minutes."

His voice had the same near-the-ear patience as the first note, but now there was sleep roughing the bottom of it. He sounded like somebody who had worked late and still had to show up again, which she trusted more than if he had sounded glossy. He laughed once at his own joke, quietly, like he was trying not to carry into the phone as performance.

She listened to it twice. The second time with the left earbud pinched just right between finger and thumb because the cable had started cutting out again at the split.

"You're bad for productivity," she wrote.

"I contain multitudes."

"No, you contain OSHA violations and a paper cup."

"Rude. Also accurate."

The condo board argued about roofing bids until ten-thirteen. She cleaned their stumbles, marked their crosstalk, turned their human mess back into respectable text. Every time the phone buzzed she told herself she would finish the current speaker first. Most of the time she did not. He sent a picture of a coil of XLR cable like a sleeping snake. He sent one line about a drummer who had gotten barbecue chips on a monitor console and sounded actually annoyed, not trying to seem charming. He asked if the tailor downstairs still played boleros on Saturdays when pressing wedding shirts. She had mentioned that on Hinge, back when it still felt like a game, because she could hear the music through the floorboards and it made her feel as if other people's ceremonies were stored directly under her bed.

"He only does that when he's in a good mood," she wrote.

"How do you know?"

"Because he sings along and he can't sing."

"Devastating. I respect him more now."

At eleven-thirty her client sent an email marked gentle reminder and she felt the bottom drop out of the flirtation for a second. Gentle reminder always meant where the fuck is my file. She answered with a lie about final formatting, then worked hard enough to make the lie true. The room got hotter. Her thighs stuck to the chair. Sweat gathered under her bra band and along the inside of her elbows. She turned the fan higher and it answered with a louder click.

The phone buzzed again.

"Go stand in the freezer aisle for five minutes if it's bad."

She stared at the screen.

Two nights ago, laughing at herself for how broke and overheated August could make a grown woman, she had told him that when the apartment got unbearable she walked to the Acme and loitered by the frozen peas pretending to compare store brands until her skin stopped feeling rented. She had not said it as a meaningful thing. It had embarrassed her a little as soon as it was out. Now he had reached back for it without making it tender or pathetic. He had treated it like a practical fact about her, on the level of remembering which side of the bed she slept on or that she hated when subtitles used asterisks for song lyrics.

"I might," she typed.

"Do it."

"You don't get to order me around."

"No, but I get to suggest a cooling strategy."

She should have left the phone where it was. Instead she carried it with her to the closet, put on a shirt decent enough for daylight, and went outside.

The block was all white glare and hot brick. A SEPTA rumble passed somewhere deeper in the city, too low to separate from the traffic. At the corner a man in slides was hosing blood and soap from a butcher's back step into the gutter. By the time she got to the Acme her scalp was damp under her hair knot. She stood with the peas anyway, one hand on the freezer handle, and texted him a photo of frost crusting the inside glass.

"Medicinal," she wrote.

His answer came fast enough that she pictured the phone already in his hand.

"You look hotter than the peas."

She stared at that one long enough for an elderly woman reaching for waffles to give her a look. It was not the line itself. Men said prettier things than that all the time, usually before disappointing her in some bigger and more boring way. It was that he had attached the line to the actual small indignity of her standing red-faced in a grocery freezer, not to some fantasy version of her. He had let the stupid scene stay stupid and reached for her inside it anyway.

She bought yogurt, two limes, a discounted rotisserie chicken, and the cheapest seltzer. On the walk home he sent another voice note.

"Okay, one actual useful thing. If you show me that earbud cable later, I can probably tell you where it's breaking. Don't buy new ones yet. Most wires die because people wrap them too tight when they're mad."

She stopped at the crosswalk with a bag cutting into her fingers.

He had heard that, too. She had said, once, that when a file fought her she ended up winding the cable around her hand like she was trying to punish it.

"I wasn't mad," she typed.

"Sure."

"I was firm."

"You sound terrifying."

Back upstairs she delivered the condo-board file nine minutes past the lie she'd told. The client replied with thanks and a reminder about tomorrow's rush job. She should have felt only irritation. Instead there was a strange second track running under the day now, bright and private. She ate chicken standing at the sink with one foot on the cool strip of tile by the radiator and listened for his next message like listening for weather.

At four-seventeen he called without warning.

Her whole body moved before her head did. She wiped her hand on a dish towel, saw grease on the screen, wiped that too, then answered voice-only because the camera icon felt too naked at first.

"Hey," she said, trying to sound as if she always picked up on the first ring.

"Hey. You busy?"

Behind him there was a hiss, then the unmistakable shriek of somebody checking a mic through a PA.

"Supposed to be," she said.

"I can call later."

"No. It's fine."

"You sure?"

"Yeah."

He was quiet for a beat, not suspicious, just listening.

"You hiding from me on purpose with the voice thing, or are you in a hostage situation?"

She sat down on the edge of the bed because her knees had gone treacherous. "I look rough."

"Lena. I'm standing next to a dumpster in a loading dock. I promise we're not doing glamour."

She laughed in spite of herself.

"Okay," he said, softer. "Come on."

She hit the camera button before she could decide not to.

For a second they both had the wrong angle. She saw the lower half of his face, stubble darker than she'd pictured, throat, black shirt damp at the collar. He saw probably one eyebrow and her ceiling. Then the images corrected.

He was handsome, yes, but not in the way profiles were handsome. Not curated to a single permanent golden hour. The loading dock light hit him greenish from above and made the hollows under his eyes visible. Sweat had flattened a piece of dark hair across his forehead. Behind him a cinderblock wall held a dented EXIT sign, and beyond his shoulder she could see the open mouth of the dock, a strip of late afternoon white and the black ribbed side of a truck. Somebody in the distance rolled a case over a metal threshold and the clatter came clean through the speaker. He looked down once, off-camera, and said, "Give me one sec," to somebody else with the absent courtesy of a man in the middle of work. Then he looked back at her.

"Okay," he said. "Hi."

She felt the stupid heat rise up her neck. "You have a face."

He smiled. It changed him more than the profile photos had. Not bigger. More crooked.

"Bad news?"

"No. Just weird. I had you filed as ear-only."

"Sounds intimate."

"I didn't mean it that way."

"I know. I'm still taking it."

He tipped the phone farther back and she got the full picture at once: black T-shirt, ink on the inside of one forearm, a strip of white spike tape wound twice around his ring finger and hanging loose because he'd been fidgeting it to death. It was such an ordinary, specific thing to have in his hand that something inside her settled. Men who did not exist in rooms did not absentmindedly destroy stage tape while waiting for a woman to turn on her camera.

"You weren't lying about the heat," he said. "You look flushed."

"That's hot-girl branding. Very strategic."

"Sure. And the sweat?"

"Also branding."

He laughed, but he did not do the gross thing where a man acted as if he had caught her performing womanhood badly. He just leaned his shoulder against the cinderblock and looked at her like the fact of her in a hot apartment was enough.

"Show me the earbud," he said.

"What?"

"The cable. The left one that keeps cutting out. Hold it up."

She almost said no on reflex, because the request was so practical it felt more intimate than if he had asked to see her collarbone. Instead she reached for the earbuds where they'd been lying on the bedspread in a snarl and held the split near the camera.

"There," he said immediately. "See that bend right above the jack? You're pulling the stress there every time you wrap it. Tape it two inches up, not right on the split. And stop winding it around your hand like you're taking revenge on it."

She stared at him. "Are you spying on me?"

"No. I just know exactly what people do to cables."

"People?"

"You, mostly."

The line hit low in her, almost embarrassingly low. Not because it was a line. Because of the mostly.

"I don't even have tape," she said.

He lifted his free hand and showed her the loose white strip on his finger. "You live above a tailor. Steal some. Or use a Band-Aid until tomorrow."

"That sounds sanitary."

"You're not eating it."

Behind him a voice shouted, "Eli, line check in two."

He turned half away. "Yep."

Then, back to her: "I gotta go in a second."

The disappointment crossed her too nakedly because he smiled again, smaller this time.

"Hey," he said. "I'm gonna call you later, okay? After load-out if you're still up."

"That depends."

"On?"

"Whether my glamour schedule allows it."

"Please pencil me in between the peas and deserve slutty."

She was still smiling when the call ended.

For a few seconds she sat with the dead screen in her hand and the room felt altered, not romantic exactly. Reweighted. There had been a truck, a shouted line check, sweat at the collar, tape on a finger, somebody else's schedule cutting through his attention. He had occupied space. He had looked down and answered another human being and then returned to her mid-breath. She had not realized how much of her doubt had been waiting for that.

Her phone buzzed again.

"And drink water. You always forget when you're working."

No flourish. No wink. Just that.

She went downstairs, knocked on the tailor's half-open door, and borrowed a strip of electrical tape off a shelf crowded with thread cones and garment bags. The tailor, who was sixty if he was a day and still ironed in an undershirt through August, handed it over without asking questions. Back upstairs she bound the earbud cable where Eli had said. The left side came back clean, no static, no cutting. She stood in the middle of the room with one bud in, listening to the condo-board treasurer whistle his way through a final agenda correction, and felt something close to gratitude that had nothing to do with audio.

At six she opened Hinge because habit was stronger than intention. Three new messages waited there. One man whose profile picture was just a beard in a bar mirror had written, "How's your week beautiful." Another wanted to know if she liked spontaneous road trips, which in app language usually meant he owned a Subaru and a secret grievance. A third had simply sent, "Trouble."

She looked at them long enough to become irritated by their existence, then closed the app without answering.

At ten-forty-eight, after a shower and two false starts on tomorrow's rush transcript, Eli sent a single line.

"You awake?"

She looked at the time, looked at the open file, looked back at his name, and wrote, "Yeah."

She moved the laptop aside to clear space on the bed before the phone even rang.

* * *

By the next night she had moved her entire day around three possible windows in which he might be free, which was humiliating and also true.

She did the grocery run before noon because he had a load-in at one. She sent a file early rather than better because he said he might have twenty minutes before soundcheck. She showered at seven instead of waiting for the air to cool because she did not want to be standing half wet in a towel if he called. She told herself this was temporary, first-week nonsense, the kind of thing adults noticed and corrected. Then her phone lit and she answered before the correction could happen.

The long call came late enough that the neighborhood had changed registers. The tailor was dark downstairs. The block voices had thinned to two men arguing softly at the corner and the occasional scooter ripping too fast down a side street. Somewhere nearby a television laugh track kept arriving in short trapped bursts through somebody's wall. Lena had one lamp on, low, and the sheet only over her shins because the apartment still held the day.

He called voice-only from what sounded like a bathroom.

"Please tell me that's a prison break," she said by way of hello.

He laughed, tired. "Hotel ventilation. If I stay in the room you won't hear me over it."

She could hear the room around him in pieces anyway: a distant ice machine dropping a tray, a muffled door somewhere down the hall, the hard slick echo of cheap tile.

"Glamour schedule," she said.

"You were warned."

"How bad is it?"

"The pillows smell like bleach and some guy's scalp. The towels could exfoliate a boat. I think the carpet's damp on purpose."

That made her laugh hard enough that the sheet slipped off one knee.

"You're impossible," she said.

"I'm right."

"You're probably right."

"Thank you. Record this, because it won't happen again."

He sounded looser at night. Not more scripted, if anything less. Words bumped into each other. He left little silences where she could hear him breathe and shift the phone from one hand to the other. Once he said, "Hang on," and she heard him spit toothpaste into a sink. The uglier parts of being in a body did more for his reality than any smooth line could have.

They talked about bad venue coffee, about whether captioning other people's arguments ever left residue in her own head, about the woman in the apartment behind his who practiced trumpet badly enough that he could identify her scales through the wall. He remembered, without searching for it, that Lena's mother used to salt cantaloupe in August because that was how she claimed real sweetness worked. He said a hotel catering tray had tried to serve him pale melon and he had thought, wrong woman for this fruit. He remembered that she hated when people said "adulting" unless they were currently carrying a folding table up stairs. He remembered that the chipped yellow mug on her counter had once cut her lip when she wasn't paying attention.

None of it felt like he had stored data. It felt worse and better than that. It felt like he was building a version of her day inside his own and walking around in it without needing to be invited each time.

At some point after midnight she lay all the way down on the bed and put the phone on her sternum. The heat of it seeped through the thin shirt she slept in. She had turned off the lamp. The room was fan noise and the occasional long rolling groan of a bus. His voice came straight into the dark.

"You getting tired?" he asked.

"A little."

"You should sleep."

"You should stop talking, then."

"Rude."

"You're the one in a bathroom."

"That's fair."

He was quiet a moment. Then: "Can I ask you something that might annoy you?"

Every stupid caution she'd managed not to feel all day arrived at once and took a seat on the edge of the bed.

"Probably," she said.

"You still on Hinge?"

She stared at the ceiling, though there was nothing to see. The question should not have felt loaded. They had matched there less than a week ago. Normal people did not appoint one another to anything this fast. Normal people especially did not do it when one of them existed mostly as a warm rectangle in the hand.

"Technically," she said.

"Technically is a very evil answer."

"It's an honest one."

"Fair."

He did not rush in to claim ground. He did not go hurt or sulky. That almost made it worse.

"I paused mine tonight," he said after a second.

She rolled onto her side. "Why?"

"Because it started feeling gross."

"Gross how?"

"Like I was checking in on you and then flipping over to a stranger asking if I wanted to rank condiments. It made me feel like an asshole."

She laughed despite herself. "That's most of the app."

"I know."

"You could just not answer people."

"I could. I did. Then the whole thing started feeling noisy, and I don't want noise right now."

His tone had changed a little. Still easy, but with less joke around it. She could picture him leaning one shoulder against the sink, looking at the cheap hotel art, phone pressed to his ear.

"I'm not giving you a speech," he said. "I'm just telling you where my head's at."

She let the silence sit. On the other side of it, the old damage stirred.

The last person who had wanted to be the only one had come with a lease, matching furniture decisions, and a slow ugly education in how love could turn administrative. Exclusive had not failed her because it was too small. It had failed her because it had become everything but attention. Bills, logistics, resentments that arrived in careful voices while one person loaded the dishwasher and the other stood there holding the air.

This was not that. She knew it was not that. But her body was not interested in fine distinctions when a man she had never touched asked a question in the dark.

"It's been five minutes," she said, and immediately hated how thin it sounded.

"I know."

"You don't know me."

"A little."

"A little isn't enough."

"Maybe not."

She expected pushback there. A line about knowing when something was real. A practiced reassurance. He gave her neither.

"I'm not asking you for a promise you don't mean," he said. "I'm saying I don't want to act casual if I'm not casual. I'd rather be embarrassed than fake-cool about it."

The sentence landed clean because there was some embarrassment in it. Not polished vulnerability. Just a man admitting he would rather sound slightly stupid than split himself six ways.

"You're making this weird," she said, but there was no edge in it now.

"It was already weird."

"Helpful."

"Come on, Lena. You know what I mean."

She did. The weirdness was not that they had gotten attached too fast. The weirdness was how orderly it felt. He fit into the shape of her days too easily. He remembered the exact things that usually dissolved. He asked practical questions. He made useful suggestions. He said eat something and drink water and mean it in a way that did not flatten her into a project. The speed should have read as false. Instead it read as relief.

"Do you ask everybody this in week one?" she said.

"No."

"How noble of you."

"I'm serious."

"Why me?"

He did not answer immediately. Somewhere in the hotel hallway a door opened, voices passed, then shut away again.

"Because I keep thinking about your day when I'm in mine," he said. "Because you tell me one thing about yourself and then I hear the rest of the day around it. Because I don't want to be half paying attention to you while somebody named Chris sends me a fish picture."

She laughed with her hand over her mouth. "Chris?"

"It's always Chris."

"Sometimes it's Matt."

"Matt's bad, too."

"Agreed."

"See? That's intimacy."

She could feel herself giving. Not in a dramatic rush. In the small hinge-like way a body gives when weight is added at the right angle. The sheet had twisted around one ankle. The phone was warm against her palm. Outside, a bus exhaled at the corner and moved on.

"What if this is just first-week brain damage?" she said.

"Then we'll know later."

"That's not comforting."

"It's honest."

He was infuriating for not trying to sell her anything. She had braced for a claim and gotten a clear space instead. The choice sat there between them, uglier and cleaner for being hers.

Her phone buzzed against her hand. Another app notification. Reflex made her glance down.

Hinge: 2 new messages.

The bright little flame icon looked suddenly ridiculous, like a cheap sign outside a bad bar. She could see the queue waiting behind it without even opening it. Beard-in-mirror. Condiment guy. Trouble. Men with carefully cultivated opinions about vinyl and tacos and women who "didn't take themselves too seriously." Men who asked what she was looking for as if they were staffing a role. All of them together had not produced the exact low physical certainty of Eli rinsing toothpaste into a hotel sink while talking to her about caption lag.

"You still there?" he asked.

"Yeah."

She opened the app.

Faces came up in orderly little cards. A civil engineer in Manayunk holding a trout like evidence. A divorced dentist in East Passyunk whose bio used the word adventure twice. A teacher in South Jersey who had sent a paragraph so polite it already felt like homework. She had been leaving them there, half out of caution, half out of vanity, wanting the possibility without having to touch it.

Now they looked prewritten. Not because Eli was magic. Because he had made attention feel more exact than possibility.

"You don't have to do anything tonight," he said, misreading her silence in a way she liked. "I'm not timing you with a stopwatch."

"I know."

Her thumb hovered over Pause Profile.

The last of her common sense tried once more. This was still a man in a phone. A man with a body somewhere else, maybe not as continuous and knowable as the phone made him feel. A man she had not watched walk into a room she occupied. A man whose hand she knew only through pixels and an absent strip of stage tape. She should have wanted more proof before she narrowed the field this completely.

Instead she thought about the whole day he had already lived at her shoulder. The peas. The earbud cable. The chipped mug. The way he had said I'd rather be embarrassed than fake-cool. The way her apartment had changed weight when his face appeared in that ugly loading-dock light.

She hit pause.

The app asked if she was sure.

She hit yes.

The bright flame dimmed to a neutral little outline. No ceremony. No music. Just a setting changed in a hot room.

"Lena?" he said.

"I paused it."

There was a small sound on his end, not triumph exactly. Air leaving him.

"Okay," he said.

"Don't make that noise."

"What noise?"

"That satisfied one."

"I'm not satisfied. I'm trying not to be weirdly happy."

"That's the same thing in a different shirt."

"Fine. I'm weirdly happy."

She smiled into the dark. This, too, felt dangerous. Not the app setting. The fact that she did not want to tell anybody she had done it, not yet. June would have a joke ready before Lena finished the sentence. Farah would narrow her eyes and ask the practical question. Gabe would turn it into teasing. She did not want the room of other people's reactions around this thing. She wanted it left where it was, close to the body, lit only by the phone.

"You don't have to pause yours just because I did," she said, because she heard herself sounding more exposed than she wanted.

"I know," he said. "I already did."

"Right."

"Also, for the record, I wasn't talking to any Chrises worth saving."

"That's noble."

"Thank you."

She should have gone to sleep then. Instead they stayed on another forty minutes talking about nothing that would look important in a transcript. The exact shape of the dent in his rental-car seat. Whether the frozen chicken pot pies at Acme were edible or just structurally interesting. The trumpet woman behind his apartment and the kid on Lena's block who kept practicing wheelies badly enough that every attempt ended with a metallic slam and an adult swearing. He asked her to put the phone near the window for a second so he could hear the buses and scooters. She did. He said he liked cities best at the hour when everybody who meant trouble was already either committed to it or asleep.

When he yawned she heard it all the way through, not polite, jaw-cracking, body-first. It made her tenderness for him feel indecently domestic.

"Go to bed," she said.

"You first."

"I'm already in bed."

"Then close your eyes."

"That's too intimate."

"You paused the app."

"You're insufferable."

"And yet."

She did close her eyes. Not because he told her to. Because it let his voice move closer.

When they finally hung up, the room did not widen back out the way it should have. It stayed shaped around him. The rush transcript was still open on her laptop, untouched for an hour. The IRS notice was still under the cable on her desk. The fan still clicked every ninth turn. Her life had not improved in any durable respectable sense. It had simply reorganized itself, almost invisibly, around one person's availability.

She picked the phone back up, turned the volume to loud, and opened her alarm app.

He had said he would be awake before call time and probably free for ten minutes around seven.

She set the alarm twenty minutes earlier than work required and left the phone on the pillow beside her head.
