# Chapter 8

By five o'clock Moore Street had been turned into extension-cord country.

Somebody from the parish had dragged out the folding tables with the old bingo-night legs that never locked straight. Somebody else had zip-tied orange sawhorses across both ends of the block and declared that official enough to stop traffic. Aluminum trays sweated onto card tables. Coolers bled through their seams. A little plaster Saint Rocco, protected by a cloudy plastic hood that made him look like he had been shipped through weather, waited on a plywood platform in front of the tailor shop downstairs from Lena's apartment. His dog looked cheerful in a way that bordered on insulting.

The block smelled like lighter fluid, charred peppers, and the first spoiled inch of ice water at the bottom of a beer tub. Kids were already running under the tables as if the event were not the event at all, only the chance to be under adults' feet without getting yelled at immediately.

Lena stood on a chair and taped down the paper runner while her phone knocked against her thigh through the pocket of her shorts. Every time it moved, she told herself she would not look. The whole point of tonight, she had decided around noon and then re-decided around three, was to be on her own block like an actual person instead of a woman waiting for a vibration, instead of a woman trying to erase Union Station with fresh evidence.

The chair wobbled. June caught the back of her calf with one nicotine-rough hand and steadied it without looking up from the grill.

"If you crack your head open before the saint gets outside," June said, "I'm not writing the incident report."

She had a black Sharpie tucked behind one ear, a grease pencil in the apron pocket she had stolen from somewhere, and a tank top already darkened at the ribs with heat and smoke. People kept calling her name from three directions at once, and somehow it never made her sound harried. It made her sound central.

"Junie, where's the extra propane?"

"June, can you look at this outlet?"

"June, tell him he can't put the raw skewers next to the brownies, what the fuck."

June pointed the tongs at a teenager Lena did not know, then at the extension cord wound near the church donation jar, then at a dented milk crate that apparently contained salvation in the form of matches, zip ties, and two unclaimed phone chargers.

"Propane's behind the ice, outlet's dead because you're using it wrong, and if the brownies taste like cumin that's between you and God," she said. "Mateo. Milk crate."

Mateo came slouching over from the curb in a navy school hoodie he refused to retire even in September. The foam on one side of his headphones had split and was shedding black flecks onto the neck of the sweatshirt. He hooked the milk crate with one sneaker and dragged it over without taking the hood off.

"Love being conscripted into church labor," he said.

"You're not church labor," June said. "You're free labor."

"Cool distinction."

He said it flat, but he was already crouching to untangle the cords, long fingers moving faster than Lena would have expected from the posture. Sixteen lived in him the way static lived in an old radio. He looked half switched off until a thing interested him, then all the current went to the same place at once.

Across the street Gabe came through the barricade carrying a forty-pound bag of ice against his shoulder like it had personally offended him. One twin trotted ahead in sparkly jelly sandals with a paper crown slipping over one eye. The other had a crooked butterfly painted across her face and held three glow bracelets on one arm like inventory. Gabe's SEPTA T-shirt was dark under the arms; the strip of paler skin where his wedding band usually sat flashed every time he shifted the bag.

"Tell me this saint has permits," he called.

"He's grandfathered in," Lena said.

One twin pointed at the plaster dog. "Why does the dog get to come?"

"Because he's essential personnel," Gabe said.

The second twin looked at Lena with the cold seriousness children reserved for the truly important administrative failures of life. "Can we have soda before dinner if it's for church?"

"That's not how church works," Lena said.

"That's exactly how this block works," Gabe said.

Farah arrived ten minutes later with a canvas tote cutting into one hand and a plastic tub of ice balanced on the other hip. She always seemed to have just stepped out of work, even when she had clearly gone home and changed, because the smell of citrus peel and cork dust followed her like a personal weather system. Tonight she had her silver hair twisted up with a pencil and wore a men's Phillies windbreaker over black linen pants, as if she had no intention of trusting the forecast or the event.

She set the tub down by the beer cooler and looked inside.

"You people did all this and bought Lite," she said. "That's a moral failure."

"It was on sale," Lena said.

"So are cough drops."

Farah took a lemon out of her tote, tossed it once in her palm, and went hunting for a knife as if she had been put on earth to rescue every public gathering from itself at the level of garnish.

Lena's phone buzzed again. This time she looked.

Load-in ran long. I can call around seven if that still works. Send me the saint when he comes out.

There was nothing wrong with the message. Nothing even slippery about it. Still her body took it in with the same quick stupid relief that had been wrecking her concentration for weeks. She thumbed the screen dark before Gabe or Farah or one of the children could clock her face doing that face.

June did clock it. June clocked almost everything.

"Seven's better," she said, flipping peppers with the speed of someone shuffling paperwork. "Father whoever doesn't start the walk till then anyway. Put him on when Rocco comes out. He'll get the whole disaster."

Lena looked at her. "You think?"

June gave her a quick sideways smile, already reaching for the long lighter. "Len. Half the point of a neighborhood ritual is making absent people look guilty. We can fold him in."

It was so practical the way she said it, like she was talking about where to set the potato salad so it would stay out of the sun. Lena felt some private knot ease under her ribs. She had spent the week since D.C. answering small questions about the trip in a voice she hoped passed for casual. Fine, he got caught up with his sister. No, it was not a whole thing. Yes, it sucked. Tonight had been turning, in her head, into another test Eli could fail by sounding too polished or too eager or too much like a man who existed only when she held the phone. June made it sound simpler than that. He could just call. The block could happen around him. People could meet him in the shape he fit.

"Hear that?" Gabe said, passing behind her with the ice. "Your mystery man is finally on the schedule. Girls, be normal."

"We're normal," said the butterfly-faced twin, which was such a false statement that even Mateo laughed.

By six-thirty the street had thickened into itself. Aunts who did not live on the block anymore arrived carrying foil trays and opinions. Old men in folding chairs started policing the grill from fifteen feet away. Mrs. Alvarado, Lena's landlord, came downstairs in a rhinestone visor and informed everyone that nobody was to leave grease on the sidewalk in front of her building because rats had rights too, apparently. Kids melted water ice down their wrists. Somebody's Bluetooth speaker played old dance tracks until the church volunteer with the procession sash made them cut it when the saint was due out.

June moved through all of it as if she had eight hands and no resentment about using any of them. She got the dead outlet working by making Mateo run the cord through Mrs. Alvarado's front window instead. She reassigned the skewers when one tray started going dry. She sent Gabe to pick up another bag of charcoal and talked an old woman out of putting actual votive candles under a plastic folding table.

"I love her," Mrs. Alvarado said to Lena in a conspiratorial stage whisper, nodding at June. "That one can run FEMA."

Lena laughed because the alternative was to stand there too moved by something as stupid as that.

When the little procession party finally came down from the parish hall with Saint Rocco on his plywood platform, the whole block pinched narrower to make room. Four men took the corners, grunting more from performance than strain. Dollar bills fluttered from ribbons pinned to the plastic hood. The saint's dog came first around the hood's curve, then the saint himself, squeezing between a parked Honda and Mr. Ricci's pickup with about an inch to spare.

"Call him now," June said, and shoved the power bank into Lena's hand.

***

Lena hit video before she could give herself a reason not to. The screen jumped, then found Eli in a smear of backstage dark resolving into cinderblock, coiled cable, the line of his jaw shadowed with stubble. A black work shirt, sleeves pushed up. He was somewhere noisy but not too noisy. He always seemed to find the pocket of sound that made the illusion intimate.

"Hey," he said, smiling when her face appeared. "Tell me I didn't miss the dog."

"You missed the dog by three seconds," Lena said, and turned the phone outward.

"Absolutely unacceptable," he said. "I want a replay."

The nearer twin, the one with the crown falling off, grabbed Lena's wrist with sticky fingers and shouted directly at the screen.

"He has sores on his leg because he had plague."

Gabe barked a laugh. "Jesus Christ."

Eli did not flinch at the volume. "That's useful intel, thank you. And honestly more than Lena usually gives me."

"I'm right here."

"You're withholding saint facts."

The butterfly-faced twin shoved into frame to correct the record. "He has a dog because a dog brought him bread."

"That's a solid union story," Eli said.

Something about the way he said it, not too broad, not pandering, let the joke land without turning the children into props. The girls immediately accepted him as a person worth yelling at.

"Show him the money," one of them demanded.

Lena walked with the crowd as the saint came past the tailor shop window, dollar bills flickering against the plastic. Farah leaned into frame over Lena's shoulder, lemon knife still in hand.

"So you're the traveling man," she said. "Your first useful contribution can be explaining this beer selection."

Eli took one look at the tub behind them. "My guess? Nobody wanted to admit how much ice costs."

Farah gave one short, surprised laugh through her nose. "All right. He knows arithmetic."

Gabe elbowed Lena hard enough to move her half a step. "Ask him if he's bringing better next year."

"I'm hearing next year already?" Eli said.

"Careful," Gabe said, crowding into frame with the toddler-sized solemnity of a man about to perform skepticism for his sister's benefit while also enjoying himself. "This block annexes people fast. My daughters already think you're in the television."

One twin corrected him. "Phone, Daddy."

"Same disease," Gabe said.

Eli looked at the girls, then back to Gabe. "That's fair. If I don't show up in person soon, I assume they get to repossess something."

"Your kneecaps," said the butterfly twin promptly.

"Reasonable. Keep the paperwork ready."

The girls dissolved. Even Mrs. Alvarado, who had drifted close enough to hear, laughed and fanned herself harder with the parish program.

The procession reached the middle of the block, where the saw-horse spacing got stupid. One of the men carrying the platform swore because the plastic hood scraped lightly against a side mirror. The whole crowd made the same sound at once, a neighborhood gasp that was mostly delighted to have a near-disaster to react to.

"That saint's brushing the Honda," Eli said. "This is either faith or terrible zoning."

This time Farah laughed out loud. Gabe did too. Lena had to press her lips together, the pleasure of it sharp and almost embarrassing. He had not arrived trying to own the room. He had arrived like someone who understood rooms existed.

Mateo appeared at Lena's elbow with the portable speaker under one arm.

"Can your boyfriend tell these idiots to point this away from the brick?" he asked, not looking at the phone.

Eli tilted his head. "Let me hear it."

Mateo held the speaker up. The marching-band recording coming through it sounded like it had been packed in wet towels.

"Yeah," Eli said. "It's bouncing right back at the wall. Pull it out six inches and tilt it toward the street. Also that thing's too low."

Mateo gave him a suspicious glance, then did exactly that. The sound improved enough that even the old men noticed.

"Huh," Mateo said.

"I cherish your respect," Eli said.

"Don't push it."

But he said it with the faintest corner of a grin, and Lena saw June see it too from the grill, a quick unreadable flick of attention before she turned back to the skewers.

For the next twenty minutes the phone passed through the block like a borrowed relative. One twin tried to feed Eli half a hot dog through the camera and got mustard on the screen. Eli thanked her for the offering. Gabe asked him what kind of work actually kept a man in black T-shirts in rooms with cables all his life, and Eli described load-ins and bad acoustics and venues built by people who clearly hated sound without ever sliding into lecture. Farah asked him whether he at least drank decent beer off the clock. He said yes, but never when it was free, which made her snort into her plastic cup.

Most of what won them was smaller than that. When the girls started chanting over each other, he picked one and then the other instead of trying to charm both at once. When Mrs. Alvarado launched into the history of the saint as if she had personally reviewed the plague, he listened long enough to make her feel heard and then said, "I'm going to shut up and watch, or she'll smite me through Lena's data plan." When the music started up again, he asked Lena to turn the camera outward and let the block be louder than him.

He modulated. That was the thing. He kept meeting each person at the size they offered him, neither bigger nor brighter. Lena had not known until that moment how much she had dreaded the opposite.

By the time the saint finished the short solemn loop and got set back down near the tables, people were talking to Eli as if they had already agreed on his basic reality. Gabe held the phone while Lena fixed plates, telling him which house used to belong to which old drunk. Farah told him he was still under review. One of the twins asked whether he could come for Halloween and see her witch costume, and he said, "Only if I get to be judged harshly by both of you," which satisfied her completely.

June threaded through with a tray of skewers balanced on one forearm. "Move," she said to nobody and everybody.

Then, to Lena, with a glance at the battery percentage, "Take him while you eat, before this phone dies and he becomes a ghost again."

"Jesus," Lena said.

"What? It's a church event."

Lena took her plate and the phone and moved toward the stoop outside the tailor shop. Eli's face steadied there, no longer jumping with everybody else's arms. Behind him somebody shouted for a monitor check.

"You good?" he asked.

She looked back at the block. June at the grill with smoke turning her outline soft. Gabe crouched to wipe mustard off one twin's chin with a napkin already ruined. Farah slicing lemons into plastic cups because if she had to drink bad beer she was going to improve it by force. Mateo pretending not to help Mrs. Alvarado stack extra chairs and helping her anyway. The saint under his plastic hood, looking slightly smug.

"Yeah," Lena said. "I'm good."

"Looks like home."

The word landed harder than it should have. She covered it by biting into a skewer too hot in the middle and burning the roof of her mouth.

"Elegant," Eli said.

"Shut up."

"No chance."

She smiled at the plate so nobody across the block would see it and read too much into it, though that fear was already starting to feel old. The block had just spent the better part of half an hour reading him in anyway. Let them.

Farah wandered over long enough to steal one of Lena's peppers.

"He survived inspection," she said to Lena, low enough that it did not become public theater. Then, to the phone: "Don't get cocky."

"I wouldn't dare," Eli said.

"Good answer."

She moved on. Lena watched her go, absurdly grateful. Farah did not hand out approval cheaply. You had to give her something shaped like competence or truth, preferably both.

The night thickened after that in the way good block parties did, losing all visible structure while remaining somehow held together by coolers and aunties. People ate standing up because the chairs were always claimed. Somebody produced a tray of store-bought cannoli and got immediately insulted for it. The girls ran until their jelly sandals slapped dust up onto the paper tablecloths. June ended up with a grill brush in one hand and a beer in the other, fielding three separate conversations without losing the thread of any of them. More than once Lena caught someone reaching for June's elbow before they reached for the problem itself.

Useful and loved, Lena thought, then felt strange about how much tenderness was in the thought.

She drank two bad beers because they were there and because the first one turned the whole block a notch softer around the edges. It did not make anything blurry. It made things softer. She let Eli drift in and out as the night demanded it. He disappeared for ten minutes to handle something on his end and texted back exactly when he said he would. He let the girls yell good night at him without dragging the exchange out. He told Gabe he would come see the block for real once the run broke. He told Farah he accepted her review process. He asked Mateo one question about school and, when Mateo answered with a shrug and "It's school," left it there.

Lena kept waiting for the wrong note. Something canned. Something too eager. Something that would make her have to translate him for the people whose opinions had started mattering more than she wanted to admit. It did not come.

When the real dark set in and somebody lit the strings of old festival bulbs across the middle of the block, June finally killed the grill. The saint had been returned to the parish hall. The paper runners were translucent with grease. Streetlight and bulb-light worked together badly on everyone's faces, flattening some people and making others glow. Kids began crashing in stages.

Gabe got one twin onto his shoulder and took the other by the hand, the paper crown now crushed into a cuff around her wrist.

"Tell phone man he's invited to Halloween if he brings his own chair," he said.

"Why his own chair?" Lena asked.

"Because I'm not losing another one to this family."

The butterfly twin pointed at the phone. "And candy."

"And candy," Gabe said.

Farah collected her tub with the severe competence of a woman who planned to leave no useful object behind for freeloaders.

"You heading up?" she asked Lena.

"In a minute."

Farah's eyes flicked to the phone, then back to Lena's face, measuring something and deciding not to make trouble out of it. "Drink water before you get romantic," she said.

"That's disgusting."

"You know I'm right."

She walked off toward the corner, windbreaker tied around her waist now, calling over her shoulder that she was taking the decent opener because nobody on this block deserved it.

Mateo hauled a trash bag toward the sawhorses with the air of a prison trustee. "This is all from one night," he muttered. "You people live like raccoons."

"You ate four skewers," June said.

"I'm talking structurally."

***

Lena stayed on the stoop when most of the crowd thinned. Her sandals had raised two bright dents across the tops of her feet, so she slipped them off and let the warm concrete take her soles. Somewhere farther up the avenue a siren stretched and folded away. The block, after hours of being louder than any one person, began separating back into units: front doors, voices on steps, a television through a screen, the drag of chairs over pavement.

Eli called again when he was properly free. The background behind him had changed. Now there was the inside of a van or maybe a loading bay, a strip of sodium light and the metal lip of a road case. He looked tired in a way Lena had come to trust, not because tiredness proved anything but because she had spent too long learning the shapes of his face when the day got hold of it.

"Hey," he said, softer this time. "You made it through church lawless."

"Barely."

"You sound like smoke."

She rubbed her fingertips over the front of her shirt, gritty with ash and paprika and some sticky sugar one of the girls had transferred onto her without permission. "I probably am smoke."

June was two stoops down coiling extension cords, the Sharpie still behind her ear like the night had marked her for management and refused to let go. Mateo was helping Mrs. Alvarado fold chairs, which he would deny if accused. Lena could feel June's attention touch over here and then leave, not nosy exactly, not generous either. It felt watchful. It felt protective, if she wanted to be flattering about it. It felt like something else if she did not.

"You okay?" Eli asked. "You went quiet."

She looked at the emptied middle of the street where Saint Rocco had stood an hour ago, dollar bills fluttering under plastic like trapped moths. The dress from D.C. rose up in her mind with such stupid clarity she could have touched the paper tags still hidden under the seam. Union Station had left a public bruise. Tonight had laid a warm hand over it. It had not healed it. It had covered it enough that she could breathe without pressing on it every second.

"Yeah," she said. Then, because the bad beer had lowered the gates just enough and because the block around her had spent the evening making him ordinary, she added, "No. More than okay, actually."

He waited. That was one of the things he did best. He could make a silence feel invited instead of abandoned.

"I needed this tonight," she said. "I know that sounds ridiculous."

"It doesn't."

"It does a little."

"A little, maybe."

She laughed. Across the street Mrs. Alvarado was giving Mateo directions on how to stack chairs like she was teaching him a lost liturgical art. June bent to pick up a zip tie, straightened, looked over once. Lena could not tell whether June had heard the conversation so far or whether Lena was just hearing herself through June already and getting self-conscious.

"After D.C.," Lena said, more quietly, "I had this gross feeling that if I didn't get you into my actual life, all of it was going to start feeling fake around the edges. Not you. Me. Like I was the idiot generating it."

The call hit a tiny snag. Eli's face held for half a beat, one eye squinting in frozen grain, then caught up.

Lena's stomach dropped hard enough that the stoop seemed to tip with it. She almost laughed and said never mind. She almost reached for some smaller sentence, one that would not leave a mark.

Then his voice came back.

"I get that," he said. "I liked hearing your block. I liked hearing the girls bully me and your brother talk shit and whoever kept yelling about charcoal. It made me feel less like a voice you keep in your pocket."

The line should have sounded corny. Maybe it was corny. But it was corny in the exact shape of the ache she had been carrying around, and that was what undid her. The neighborhood noise had thinned enough now that her own voice felt exposed on the stoop. Voices carried too well on Moore Street in warm weather. Everybody knew that. Mrs. Alvarado knew it. June knew it. Lena knew it and said the next thing anyway.

"I love you," she said.

Nobody dropped anything. There was no choir of gasps. There was just the scrape of one folding chair leg over pavement somewhere behind June and the quick hot pressure of blood rising into Lena's face because the sentence was now in the world, not just in the safe bad wiring of her head.

The call lagged again, less than a second. It lasted long enough to hurt.

Then Eli said her name first, which helped.

"Lena."

She gripped the phone harder. The power bank cord brushed her wrist like a live thread.

"I love you too," he said. "And you don't have to sound like you're confessing through a screen door."

She laughed once, helplessly, because of the church line in it, because of the relief, because she had in fact sounded like somebody trying to confess behind a lattice.

"You heard that, huh?"

"A little."

"Fuck."

"No," he said, warm and immediate. "No, I loved it."

Behind June, Mateo looked over from the chairs with the blunt incuriosity of a sixteen-year-old who knew adults were always saying humiliating things into phones and preferred not to learn specifics. Mrs. Alvarado definitely looked over. That was unavoidable. Her visor caught the streetlight and flashed.

June came back up the sidewalk carrying the last loop of extension cord over one shoulder. She did not interrupt. She only paused near the stoop long enough to unplug the power bank from the wall and hand it to Lena, her face unreadable in the porch light.

"Seven percent," she said.

Lena could not tell if June meant the battery or her remaining dignity.

"Thanks," Lena said.

June's mouth moved like she might make a joke out of it, then did not. She went inside the tailor's side door to return something to somebody's back room, leaving the smell of smoke and metal with her.

For a second Lena felt absurdly bare. Then Eli said, "You still there?"

"Yeah."

"Good."

He did not overwork it after that. He asked how much cleanup remained. He made her show him the street one more time, the abandoned cooler, the saint gone, the paper tablecloth snapping at the corners. He told her he wished he had been there to carry chairs and get judged by the women. She told him Mrs. Alvarado would have failed him on principle. He said he respected her standards.

When the battery warning finally flashed red, Lena ended the call before it could die on her. She sat another second with the dark screen reflecting only the block lights and her own face, thinner and more tired than she ever liked in a phone camera, but lit from somewhere real all the same.

June came back out with the empty tray from the brownies and set it on the table to dry.

"Well," she said.

Lena looked up. "Well what?"

"You said it loud enough for a practicing Catholic to count it."

Heat climbed up Lena's face again, but this time it came with laughter. "I hate you."

"No you don't."

"Not tonight."

June nodded once, as if that answer fit somewhere she had already cleared room for. "Take your shoes before somebody steals them."

Across the street Mrs. Alvarado called from her stoop, not even pretending she had not heard enough to qualify as audience.

"Tell your boyfriend next year he brings better beer."

Gabe, halfway to the corner with one sleeping child on his shoulder, lifted his free hand without turning around. "And candy."

From farther down, Farah's voice cut back through the dark. "And proof of decent parking habits."

The whole block, apparently, had revisions.

Lena picked up her sandals and stood barefoot on the warm sidewalk with the phone cooling in her hand. "I will," she called back before she had time to think about what kind of promise that was.

Nobody treated it like fantasy. That was the thing. Nobody on Moore Street answered her as if she had said maybe or someday or there is this guy online. They answered her as if Eli were already in rotation now, another person to feed, mock, test, and expect. By the time June killed the last light string and Mateo dragged the sawhorses toward the curb, his name had somewhere to live besides Lena's apartment.
