# Chapter 17

The Haviland Innovation Atrium smelled of lemon polish, espresso, and pastry that had been thawed somewhere else. By nine in the morning sun through the glass walls laid a hard white shine across the concrete floor and made the donor banners look more expensive than cloth had any right to look. The room had been built to flatter engineering. Every cord was hidden. Every table edge matched. Even the fingerprints disappeared.

Leela stood behind a retractable wall near the catering entrance with a paper folder tucked under her arm and her laptop open on a high-top table that was supposed to hold sparkling water. Her right wrist ached inside the brace. The folder held twelve printed packets, the public version of what Marisol had checksummed into a drawer in March, because Omar had insisted that if the wireless failed or somebody decided to be brave in theory and useless in practice, paper still counted as technology. Her phone showed three threads open at once: Omar, June, and Nina Solis from the graduate workers union, who had the encrypted archive and instructions to send it wider if all of them lost their nerve at the same time.

June arrived first, coming through the service door with a paper cup of coffee she did not seem to want. Her blunt-cut hair had been hacked shorter again, the line at one side a little crooked. She stopped long enough to flatten the cup between her hands and drop it into the trash before she said, "How many backup plans are we on now."

"Three send paths, two physical packets, and Nina if both of those fail," Leela said. "Four, if you count the timed link."

"That one doesn't count. Timed links are for people who still believe in elegant solutions."

She had nicotine gum in one pocket and her yellow legal pad under the other arm. Leela had not seen the pad in weeks. The sight of it made something tighten and then loosen in the same place. June noticed her looking and shifted the pad against her ribs as if it were not evidence but an ordinary object, grocery list, meeting notes, nothing worth building a future around.

Omar came in a minute later in a navy shirt so crisp it made the atrium's student-volunteer polos look damp. He always dressed more carefully when he was frightened; fear showed first in his jaw and then, if it kept going, in the speed of his hands. Today his hands were quiet. He set his phone on the table between the packets and Leela's laptop.

"I scrubbed the sponsor list against the faculty list," he said. "No duplicates except Gloria and the dean's liaison. The email is drafted. If their network blocks the attachments, I send the drive link. If that fails, I tether."

"And if you don't send anything," June said, not looking at him, "Nina does."

"Yes."

"Good. I like plans that assume character defects."

Ben appeared so suddenly that Leela understood she had been listening for him. He had one of the department's volunteer lanyards on over a pale blue shirt that still held the vertical fold line from the package. Somebody in events had given him a polo to wear and he had draped it over his arm instead, not willing to look that temporary. His sneakers were immaculate. There was a crescent of dried toothpaste near one cuff, as if he had dressed in a hurry and only caught most of himself.

"I came straight over," he said. "I just need to make sure we're still on the same page, because the way June was texting last night made it sound like there was maybe a version where this turns into me being the center of the story, and I did do what you asked, I just can't be the whole public face of it. That was never the agreement."

"Nobody asked for your face," June said.

"That's not what I said."

"It's what I heard."

Leela closed her laptop because the screen glare was making her irritable. "You're not the only release path. That's the point. If you walk out right now, the documents still go."

Ben flinched as if she had slapped him with something harder than the sentence. He looked younger immediately, the way he always did when approval left the room. "I'm here," he said.

"Then stay here," June said. "You don't get points for standing upright."

Omar slid a printed sheet toward Ben. It was the event program with Mohit's speaking slot circled in blue pen and three times written beside it.

"When he starts the Ruderal transition slides, I send," Omar said. "If Gloria or events cuts the projector before then, Leela hands the packets out. If Mohit tries to move this into a side room before there are witnesses, Nina comes forward. If he asks you a direct question, you answer only the question."

Ben stared at the page as though it might become simpler if he refused to blink. "You keep saying Ruderal out loud like that's not insane."

June gave him a flat look. "It stopped being insane when he built a company out of our work."

He turned to Leela. "Are we sure she has to be in this version."

"I'm standing right here," June said.

"I know. I just mean the public piece. Because if he pulls her into it, he's going to make it look personal. He's good at that. He'll make it look like this is about the grievance and Baltimore and all the weird interpersonal stuff, not the documents."

June's face did not change. "You don't know enough to use Baltimore as shorthand."

Leela stepped in before Ben could apologize badly and make the air worse. "The documents go whether June speaks or not."

June looked at her then, properly for the first time that morning. There was less anger in it than there had been in March. That made it more difficult to bear.

"I didn't promise you anything," June said.

"I know."

"He already gave me my off-ramp. Clean little meeting, nice person from industry, whole speech about how I was too smart to tie myself to other people's procedural mistakes. If this turns into him pointing across the room and asking me to help him keep the lie tidy, I want everybody to understand that I know exactly what it costs if I don't."

No one said anything for a moment. Out in the atrium a student volunteer laughed too loudly at something an administrator had not meant as a joke. Plates knocked against one another in the catering kitchen. Through the wall Leela could hear the microphone being tested: one, two, thank you, one, two.

Ben swallowed. "I'm here," he said again, softer this time, as if saying it to himself.

Leela thought: this was what trust had become, not faith or repair but an engineering solution to mutual fear. It meant assuming every person in the room might freeze, defect, vanish, decide the institution was too large and their own life too small, and building anyway.

"All right," she said. "Then nobody is indispensable. That's the only reason this works."

June barked a laugh without humor. "Bleak. Accurate. Fine."

Omar nodded once. "Then we should take our places."

He picked up his phone. Ben picked up the volunteer polo as though he had just remembered he needed a costume. June tore a fresh piece of nicotine gum from the foil and tucked the wrapper into her back pocket instead of throwing it away. Leela slid three of the packets into her tote and felt the paper weight settle against her hip. It felt absurdly small. Months of fear, theft, shame, the archive she had named `holdout_set` on a winter afternoon with numb fingers and a heater rattling in her apartment, reduced to toner and staples.

"One more thing," Ben said, stopping at the door.

Nobody encouraged him.

"If this works," he said, "what actually happens to us."

June pushed the door open. "If you wanted a reassuring meeting, you should've stayed with him."

She went out into the light.

Leela followed more slowly. There was no answer better than that.

* * *

By ten-thirty the atrium had filled with the exact mix of people Haviland liked to photograph: men in soft blazers without ties, women in excellent shoes, trustees wearing badges as if they had invented sponsorship, faculty who had remembered to become genial for one morning, undergraduates standing beside poster boards with the fixed smiles of students who had been told opportunity might walk by in loafers. On the far wall, three large screens looped the same department reel: drone footage of campus, a robot hand sorting colored blocks, a line of code scrolling in blue light for no reason except that somebody in communications thought code looked like progress.

Leela stood near a pillar wrapped in pale oak slats and held a paper plate she had no intention of eating from. The mini quiche on it had gone leathery under the heat lamp. She could feel the packets in her tote whenever someone brushed past her. Every accidental touch made her think security had arrived, though there was no reason for security yet. Her body had been preparing for official punishment for so long that it did not know what to do with an hour in which she was the one who had set the timing.

Across the room, Omar took up position beside a student poster about municipal data governance that nobody had read twice. He looked attentive in the ordinary way: one hand in his pocket, the other curled around his phone, listening to a trustee say something about responsible innovation. Only Leela could see how hard he was holding still.

June stayed near the coffee station at the back. From a distance she could have been mistaken for someone helping with logistics, compact and unsmiling and dressed for utility rather than display. The legal pad was gone from sight. That frightened Leela more than if June had held it up like a weapon. It meant June had not chosen yet whether it was evidence or just paper.

Near the glass doors, Nina stood under a donor banner in a borrowed blazer over her rain shell, visitor badge clipped crooked, looking like she was trying to pass for somebody's late-arriving policy guest. Leela let herself look at her only once. That was enough.

Ben moved through the room with a box of presentation clickers and adapters, trying so hard to look useful that usefulness itself started to look suspicious. He laughed when people laughed at him. He bent too quickly when anyone older asked for something. Once, as he crossed in front of Leela, he mouthed, still okay? without slowing down. She did not answer.

Then Mohit took the stage.

He did not look hurried. He never did. His sleeves were rolled once to the forearm, exact and unrumpled. His expensive glasses caught the light and made the pause before his first sentence feel practiced rather than vain. The opening slide behind him showed an aerial map of a floodplain rendered in Haviland blue and white, the title in thin clean font: CIVIC ROBUSTNESS IN THE WILD.

"Thank you for spending your morning with us," he said, and the trustees in front flattened their programs on their knees while a sponsor near the aisle tipped her face toward the stage, already willing to be persuaded. "We're trying to build systems that survive contact with reality. That sounds obvious, but most machine learning still treats the world as a laboratory that ought to behave."

A few people smiled.

"Our lab has spent the year asking a harder question. What would it mean to train both models and researchers not for ideal conditions, but for adversarial ones? Not for consensus, but for the public mess we actually inhabit?"

At the coffee station June's mouth thinned to a line. A trustee near the front was already nodding. Adversarial, public mess: the language that made strategy sound like courage and cruelty sound like pedagogy. Leela felt the old disgust rise with the older, more humiliating recognition underneath it. He was good at this. He had always been good at it. If she had met him only here, only in this room with the lake light turning the glass walls into a soft gleam and the trustees nodding before the end of his sentences, she would have liked him too.

He moved through the first slides with the fluent pace of someone who understood exactly how long a room could be asked to admire a graph. He thanked students without naming them. He thanked sponsors by name. He used care language and market language in alternating turns, a braid he had spent years perfecting.

"One of the things we've learned," he said, "is that resilience cannot be outsourced to goodwill. You have to design for friction. You have to invite critique early enough that it can still improve the work."

At the back of the room June shifted her weight from one foot to the other. Omar glanced down at his phone once and looked back up.

Mohit advanced to a slide about translational pathways, the phrase glowing in white over a chart of pilot partnerships. He spoke about local government collaborations, licensing possibilities, the moral duty to move tools out of the lab and into institutions that could use them. He did not say Ruderal yet. He did not need to. The sponsors at the center tables had already put on their bright, acquisitive faces, the expression money wore in academia before lunch.

Omar pressed his thumb to the screen.

Nothing changed immediately. The projector still glowed. Mohit's voice kept its even warmth. But ten seconds later a trustee in the front row frowned at his phone. A sponsor beside him did the same. Then someone near the back opened a laptop. Leela heard the thin flock of new-message chimes go off around the atrium, each one small enough to be deniable and together loud enough to alter the air.

Mohit kept talking for one more sentence. "What we're showing today is not just technical performance, but a culture of disciplined mutual challenge-"

He saw Gloria Fisk, halfway to the stage, reading from her phone with her mouth set in a line that meant process was no longer hypothetical.

His pause was almost invisible. That was part of the skill.

"It appears," he said lightly, "that some of you have received an internal packet stripped of context. Since transparency is obviously a value in this room, let me address that directly before rumor has a chance to do what rumor does."

A sponsor lowered her phone. The dean's liaison let his packet rest against his thigh instead of opening it. Of course they did.

"Doctoral mentorship in a fast-moving lab produces partial documents," he said. "You're seeing pieces of red-team exercises, contingent funding discussions, and draft planning notes. We deliberately pressure-test one another's work here. We also discuss scarce resources honestly. If isolated emails now look contradictory, that's because students experience the same evolving situation from different vantage points and private coaching rarely reads well once selectively forwarded."

He smiled, not broad enough to become defensive, just enough to suggest mild regret at everyone else's confusion.

"I would ask that you not mistake academic rigor for misconduct simply because somebody has discovered that screenshots travel faster than trust."

Leela watched the reassembly happen in real time: the donors relieved to hear there was a reasonable adult story available; the faculty calculating how ugly it would be if the unreasonable version proved true; the administrators reaching with their eyes for the nearest smaller, more containable interpretation. Even she felt it, for one hateful second, the desire to let his version win because his version preserved the shape of the world.

Gloria reached the edge of the stage. "Mohit," she said quietly, but the microphone caught it anyway. "Perhaps we should pause here."

"Of course," he said. Then, turning back to the room with that same composed generosity: "I'm happy to answer brief questions now and move the rest into a more appropriate setting. There's nothing here that can't be explained."

That line nearly did it. Leela saw shoulders lower. A man from a foundation board slipped his phone back into his jacket. One of the student volunteers took a dutiful step toward the sponsor cluster as if the whole thing might still be managed into a coffee break.

Leela set her plate on the nearest high-top before the quiche could slide off. Grease streaked her thumb. She pulled the top packet from her tote, crossed into the aisle, and handed it to the woman in the cream blazer with the green scarf.

"Before this gets reduced to a private mentoring dispute," she said, louder than she meant to, "page four is my ablation table. It's from an unsubmitted November run. The orange condition is wrong because it's the draft version from before I fixed it. Page seven has the clone path from the lab server into Professor Saran's Ruderal folder. That's why we printed these."

She gave the next packet to the dean's liaison, then held a third out toward a trustee who accepted it because refusing would have been more conspicuous than taking it.

"If you want a smaller room after that, fine," Leela said. Her voice sounded steadier than the inside of her ribs. "But not before the people already holding this know what they're being asked to ignore."

The woman in the green scarf flipped to page four and stopped touching her neck.

"Professor Saran," she said, eyes still on the paper, "was that work licensed to Ruderal Analytics or not."

Ruderal Analytics cut through the atrium cleaner than any accusation could have.

Mohit turned to her as if grateful for the chance to be precise. "Ruderal was an exploratory translational vehicle. The deck in circulation was a draft mock-up assembled from approved aggregate results to test external interest. Under ordinary university policy, materials developed inside the lab fall under institutional research governance. No student-owned intellectual property was transferred. Anybody familiar with early-stage research translation will understand how ordinary that is."

He said it smoothly enough that ordinary almost sounded factual. The phrase university policy landed exactly where he had aimed it. One of the faculty sponsors lowered his packet a few inches. Gloria lifted a calming hand.

"We are not adjudicating an ownership dispute in the atrium," Gloria said. "Mohit, Leela, June, and Ben, come with me. We can review the documents upstairs with the appropriate offices."

He looked past Gloria toward June beside the coffee urn before anyone moved.

"June," he said, gentle as a mentor checking whether a student wanted to add nuance. "You were part of some of those translational conversations. If it'd be helpful, you can clarify that these were mock-up materials, not a production claim on anyone's dissertation."

Two trustees who had been half turning toward the corridor stopped with their programs still folded in their hands. Near the coffee urn, somebody set down a cup too hard. The spoon inside rang once and kept trembling.

June put her coffee down on the floor. She wiped her hand once on the side seam of her pants. When she spoke her voice was so flat with fear that it sounded calm.

"No," she said. "I can't clarify that, because it isn't true."

June stepped away from the coffee station and into the open aisle between the sponsor tables. She did not hurry. Leela thought, wildly, absurdly, of all the times June had walked across the lab with a plate of takeout balanced on one hand, swearing at benchmarks and bad code and people who called anything lightweight when somebody else had to carry it. Now she moved like somebody carrying something that would break her if she gripped it wrong.

"I found `ruderal_pitch_v3.pdf` in Baltimore in November," June said. "Not in some hypothetical future deck. In a real folder, on a real laptop, while you were off taking side meetings during the conference you were supposed to be at with us."

Nobody interrupted her.

"Slide fourteen used my evaluation figure and Leela's ablation table from a run we hadn't submitted anywhere yet. I know it was ours because the orange condition still had my bad label on it. I mistyped the seed, got the wrong shoulder on the bar, fixed it later, and the wrong version is still in that deck. That's not approved aggregate anything. That's a stolen draft."

Her hand went into the pocket with the nicotine gum and came back out with a folded sheet torn from the yellow legal pad.

"And since he likes context so much, here is some. November sixteenth, Baltimore, 5:40 p.m., outside Ballroom C, he tells me translational visibility matters more than authorship timing and that I should trust him to place the work correctly once the market is clearer. February nineteenth, in his office after Ben forwarded the grievance, he offers me an introduction if I stop attaching myself to this. Those are my notes, because he changed the story every week and I got tired of pretending memory was enough."

She looked at Mohit then without pleading or fury, only with the directness she reached when she was done spending language on style.

"Don't use me to clean it up."

For the first time since taking the stage, he seemed to misjudge the faces in front of him by a fraction. It was barely visible, just a tightening at the corners of his mouth and a new stillness around the eyes.

"June," he said, "I think you're collapsing several separate conversations."

"No," she said. "I'm finally lining them up."

He turned quickly, not to her but to the rest of them, trying to make her line sound emotional and his sound structural.

"This is exactly what I mean by partial records. A student under stress may reasonably overread a draft diligence exercise, especially when personal grievances are already attached to it."

Gloria took another step toward the aisle. "All right. Enough. We are not finishing this here. Relevant parties, with me now."

Two of the student volunteers started drifting forward again, relieved by the return of instructions. Ben did not move. He stood by the projector cart with the spare clickers in one hand and the volunteer polo balled under the other arm, pale under the atrium light.

"Ben," Leela said.

He looked at her the way people looked at stairwells during fire alarms, as if he might still be told there was a better exit.

"Tell them where he had you bring the hard copies."

His mouth opened. Closed. The woman in the green scarf held her packet half-folded. Gloria kept one hand lifted and still. Even the volunteers stopped drifting. Under that silence, he finally spoke.

"I printed it," he said, the second word cracking. "In November. Before Baltimore was over, actually. You emailed it to me and told me to bring hard copies to your hotel for a breakfast thing with two people from industry. I thought there was permission. You said there was permission."

Mohit's head turned toward him with almost paternal disbelief. "Ben, slow down. You're describing a sponsor breakfast. Preliminary decks get shown in translational meetings all the time. That is not the same as transferring student rights."

"It had the Ruderal logo already on it." Ben was talking too fast now, words bumping into one another. "I remember because I thought the logo looked kind of bad and I asked if somebody in design was fixing it and you said not to worry about the logo, worry about getting the color copies because investors hate grayscale. That's what you said."

The woman in the green scarf folded the packet shut with a neat, offended precision. "If this deck went to outside diligence before title was clear," she said, "our counsel will need written provenance before anything connected to Ruderal moves another inch."

That sentence froze the volunteers better than Gloria had. Nobody went upstairs.

The dean's liaison's phone buzzed. He checked the screen, then looked at Gloria instead of Mohit. "Research Integrity is asking whether external circulation is confirmed," he said. "If it is, OIT wants a preservation ticket opened before Professor Saran or anyone else touches Keating again. If he walks upstairs first, chain of custody becomes its own problem."

Gloria kept her face arranged, but Leela saw the tendons pull tight along her jaw. "Then we open the ticket in Conference B and take statements there," she said. "Everyone else can step back and let the appropriate offices do their jobs."

"No," the woman in the green scarf said. She had the packet pressed flat against her ribs now, not reading it, not surrendering it either. "If our counsel is about to get involved, I'm not watching this disappear into a side room and come back out as a misunderstanding."

Nina was already moving before she spoke, her bent visitor badge flashing once under the lights as she came in from the back. "I represent the graduate workers union," she said. "The best next step is preservation on the record, in front of the witnesses who have already received aligned documents. Not a side conversation that produces a newer, cleaner story."

Gloria stared at her. "This is not a union matter."

"Graduate labor and supervisory misconduct in a funded lab are exactly a union matter."

Leela watched Gloria absorb the sponsor, the faculty, the students, the packets now open in three different sets of hands, June's legal pad, Ben's face gone waxy with panic, Mohit's expression collapsing inward toward pure calculation. She watched the arithmetic finish.

When Gloria spoke again, the cushioning had dropped out of her voice.

"All right," she said. "Because disputed student work appears to have been circulated externally, and because preservation has to start now, effective immediately Professor Saran is on administrative leave pending formal investigation. You are not to contact students about lab business, access Keating systems, or remove research materials from your office until counsel and OIT complete preservation. Nobody deletes anything. Nobody edits anything. If you have documents relevant to this matter, you send them to my office and to Research Integrity by close of business."

The microphone carried every word into the glass and back.

Someone near the catering table let out a little breath that sounded almost like a laugh and then covered it with a cough. One of the trustees took his badge off and folded the lanyard in half, because apparently even shock could become manners.

Mohit did not raise his voice. That would have been unlike him, and anyway he did not need it.

"Gloria," he said, "you're making a procedural decision in the middle of a coordinated retaliation campaign."

"Then you can make that argument after the records are preserved."

"You are rewarding theft of internal communications."

"I'm stopping you from supervising students this afternoon. The rest can wait."

His eyes moved once across the room and landed on Leela, only for a second but long enough. She could not read the expression fully because he was too practiced, but she recognized its structure: calculation under insult, the immediate search for survivable lines of control. For a second she had the deranging thought that he was already advising himself through it.

Then Gloria gestured to the dean's liaison and an IT staffer who had materialized near the stage, and the spell broke for everyone at once. The open house became an event again, but now it was an event that had failed in public. Sponsors clustered. Faculty performed concern. The undergraduates by the posters stared openly because nobody had yet taught them the adult version of not seeing.

June bent, picked her coffee up from the floor, and threw it away.

* * *

By noon the atrium smelled less like pastries and more like cold eggs and panic. Catering staff cleared plates nobody had touched. Somebody from communications had turned the department reel off, leaving the far screens dark and reflective, which only made the room look more like an aquarium after the pump failed.

Leela ended up in the hallway outside the basement lab with Omar, June, and Ben while an IT woman in a mustard cardigan photographed the lock on Mohit's office. She had a tiny bandage around one finger and kept tucking a loose braid behind her ear with the back of her hand so she would not smear the adhesive. The legal hold notices looked flimsy for what they had just done.

The lab itself still smelled the same: burnt coffee, hot dust, old couch fabric, the server closet breathing its dry mechanical heat into the room as if nothing in the world had changed. Whiteboard marker ghosts layered the walls. An abandoned ramen cup sat beside the printer. Somebody had left three clementines in a bowl to rot into leather. The place had survived too many semesters by making squalor look temporary. Today it looked mortal.

Ben leaned against the door frame, pale under the fluorescent wash. He had finally put on the volunteer polo, maybe because he needed the extra layer, maybe because once everything fell apart he wanted to resemble the harmless version of himself again.

"So what happens to the papers," he asked. "I mean the actual in-progress ones. The workshop submission, the journal revision, all of that. Because he's on all of them."

No one answered right away.

An email hit Leela's phone. Then another. She opened the first one and felt the familiar administrative nausea spread through her ribs.

All Civic Signals Lab repositories are hereby placed under preservation hold. No modifications may be made pending review.

The second was from the department office.

All external communications regarding sponsor partnerships, conference travel, recommendation requests, and submission timelines for Civic Signals affiliates should be directed to the chair until interim supervision is assigned.

It was astonishing how quickly consequence learned to write itself in bulletproof institutional prose.

"That," June said, reading over Leela's shoulder, "answers the paper question."

Omar had his own phone out. He was scrolling too fast, then backing up and reading again, the way he did when he wanted panic to behave like analysis.

"My summer funding letter names him as PI," he said. "If grants accounting freezes the account, International Students won't care that today was ethically clarifying."

June leaned against one of the stained lab couches and looked up at the ceiling. "Jesus."

"No," Omar said, with a dry, exhausted precision that was almost a joke. "Unfortunately just the university."

Ben laughed once because he was scared, then stopped because nobody joined him.

Leela moved farther into the room. The couch arm under her hand had gone shiny with years of sleeves, spilled food, anxious waiting. In the first weeks of the year the lab had still possessed the shabby cooperative feeling of a place people used hard and forgave. A rice cooker under somebody's desk. Shared takeout. Emergency chargers. Code screens left open while one person explained a proof to another with the fervor of the undercaffeinated. Mohit had poisoned that long before today. Even so, watching the institution finally name him dangerous enough for paperwork did not return the room to innocence. It turned the room into a scene.

June pushed herself off the couch. "That intro is dead."

She said it almost casually. The sentence took a second to land.

Leela looked at her. "Probably."

"Good," June said, then shook her head. "No. Not good. Just clean, finally."

Her voice had none of the brightness she used when she wanted to make a line look easy. She pulled another piece of nicotine gum out and stared at the empty foil sleeve before crumpling it. "He looked right at me and thought I'd help him tell it. That was the part that got me. The deck mattered, Baltimore mattered, but that look was worse."

Leela knew exactly which look she meant. The soft one. The intimate managerial one. The version of trust that only ran downward.

"I know," she said.

June gave a short nod as if that was enough and maybe all they could safely afford.

Ben had taken the volunteer polo back off. He was worrying one sleeve between his fingers until the cloth twisted. "Am I completely fucked," he asked.

June laughed this time, but there was no meanness in it. Only fatigue. "That's a huge question, Ben."

"I mean program-wise."

Omar looked up from his phone. "You mean career-wise, advisor-wise, and morally, but you're selecting the smallest category because it feels most answerable."

"Okay," Ben said. "Yes. That."

No one rescued him. Leela could see that he wanted rescue anyway, wanted the older people in the room to tell him he had finally done one redeeming thing and would be graded on the curve. He had been raised, she thought not without bitterness, inside systems that always had another adult farther up the staircase. Today the staircase ended in fluorescent light and a legal hold notice.

"I don't know," she said.

It was the truest thing available. It landed on him harder than blame would have.

Another message arrived, this one from Gloria's office requesting that all current advisees and affiliates remain available for interviews over the next seventy-two hours and refrain from discussing the matter with external press or sponsors. Press. The word would have seemed melodramatic yesterday. Now it sat on her screen like an overdue bill.

Ben read the same email on his phone and swore under his breath, the profanity awkward in his mouth. "My parents are going to see something somewhere and call me. They Google me. They Google all of this."

"That's not the worst thing that's about to happen," June said, but not cruelly.

Omar's phone lit again before anyone answered. `GRANTS ACCOUNTING`.

He picked up immediately. "Maya. Yes, I'm still on campus." He listened, eyes fixed on the dead whiteboard. "No, I understand the preservation hold. I'm asking whether my summer appointment can still route." A pause. "Today? If there's no interim PI signature by four, payroll won't certify the line." Another pause, shorter and worse. "And without that, International Students has nothing current to point to. Yes. I know."

He ended the call and picked up his folder in the same motion. "They froze the account. I'm going to Gloria now. If she can't get a substitute signatory before the office closes, my funding letter becomes decorative."

He was already turning toward logistics because if he did not, fear would turn him inside out. Leela respected him for it and hated that the story kept rewarding that kind of speed.

"I'll come with you to Gloria," she said.

He nodded once. "Thank you."

June rubbed a thumb over the spiral edge of the legal pad. "I've got interviews next week."

"I know," Leela said.

"I'm not saying that for sympathy."

"I know."

"I might still take the first decent offer and get the fuck out."

"That would be rational."

June snorted. "You really know how to sweet-talk a girl."

There was an old rhythm buried in that line, not restored, not safe, but briefly audible. Leela felt it like a bruise pressed lightly. She almost smiled. Almost.

The IT woman in the mustard cardigan came to the lab door. "I need everyone to understand the server closet is under preservation too," she said. "Please don't badge in there unless one of us is with you. Also, if this mini fridge belongs to students and not the lab, label it now, because facilities is asking embarrassing questions."

She said it with the embarrassed practicality of somebody who had entered the day expecting hardware tickets and now had to improvise around scandal and yogurt. June, despite herself, barked out a laugh.

"Second shelf is mine," she said. "If anybody throws out the kimchi, I'll create a separate incident."

The IT woman nodded solemnly. "Noted."

When she left, the room went quiet again except for the fans in the closet and the fluorescent hum that meant silence in Keating was never silence, only electricity pretending.

Leela sat at the edge of one of the desks because her knees had started to feel unreliable. Through the narrow basement window the day had gone gray, spring light turning thin and mean over the service road. She thought of her credit-card balance, of graduate housing, of the postponed defense that was still postponed, Mohit on leave or not. Administrative leave would not rewind March. It would not make the committee forget the version of her work he had already seeded into them. The institution had finally believed enough to stop him in public and still had not promised any of them a life after the stop.

That, she thought, was the most faithful part: not the fall itself, but the bill that arrived afterward.

June moved to stand beside her. Close enough that Leela could smell coffee and stale mint from the gum.

"Don't thank me," June said.

"I wasn't going to."

"Good."

Another beat passed.

"You can later," June said.

Leela laughed then, unexpectedly and without elegance, the sound scraping on the way out. It hurt a little. It also helped.

Omar gathered his folder, already preparing to go upstairs to Gloria before the office corridor filled with faculty advising one another how shocked to sound. Ben stayed where he was, staring at the lab as if an adult might still emerge from the server closet and explain the next semester to him. June tore the used top sheet off her legal pad and folded it smaller, once, then again, until it disappeared into her fist.

Around them the Civic Signals Lab remained stubbornly itself for a few more minutes: the dead marker smell, the old upholstery, the server heat breathing out of the closet, the ugly little ecosystem that had fed them and used them both. Then the red light on the badge reader at Mohit's office blinked from green to solid red. Even Ben stopped twisting the polo. Nobody reached for the office door again.

They had stopped him. That part was true.

The rest of it was true too: the summer funding that might vanish, the papers stranded mid-sentence, the recommendation network gone radioactive, the degree timelines already broken, the simple fact that there was no clean institution waiting behind the dirty man. The lab had finally stopped belonging to him by becoming a place that did not belong to any of them, only to the offices upstairs already translating damage into procedure.
