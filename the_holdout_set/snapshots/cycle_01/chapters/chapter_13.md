# Chapter 13

By the time Leela found the fourth-floor study room, the library had thinned to the kind of public quiet that made every ordinary sound feel indictable. Snowmelt squealed under her boots. The radiator under the window clicked and sighed. Inside the glass room June had already spread herself across the long table with the force of someone claiming a hospital chair: yellow legal pad open, laptop half charged, coat on the second seat, four folded nicotine-gum wrappers lined in a neat silver row by her wrist.

"You're late," June said without looking up.

"It is 7:04."

"That's what late means when I got here first."

Leela set down her backpack and the cardboard tray of coffees. Her right hand hurt inside the wrist brace. The cup lids had bitten damp rings into her palm. June took one, peeled back the cap, sniffed, and looked offended.

"This is decaf."

"It was the shortest line."

June finally looked at her, took in the brace, the wet hair escaping Leela's clip, whatever lived in her face after the Omar letter week, and let some sharper answer die. "Fine," she said. "We'll suffer in a dignified institutional way."

Omar arrived three minutes later with his coat buttoned to the throat and a clear bakery clamshell balanced on top of his laptop. Date bars, cut into exact rectangles, dusted with sugar that had already started to melt in the heat of the room. He set them down with an apologetic little lift of one shoulder.

"The man at Cedar Market has decided I'm no longer allowed to live on coffee," he said. "He was very severe about it."

"Good," June said. "Somebody should be."

His beard line was as careful as ever, but his jaw gave him away before he spoke another word. He sat, took his laptop out, and also, without meaning to, set the clear plastic sleeve of his I-20 packet on the table. He moved it back into his bag almost immediately, like he had exposed an organ.

For a minute they arranged their little evidence lives in silence. June's legal pad. Omar's exported emails and calendar invites. Leela's laptop, a folder of PDFs, a manila envelope she had not decided about, and the list she had made on the walk over: chronology, document index, cover memo, names of offices that might pretend not to know one another once something ugly entered the system. Through the glass, undergraduates padded past in socks and slides, carrying their ordinary panic toward exams or hookups or the vending machines downstairs. The room smelled like wet wool and reheated dust.

"Before we start," June said, flattening a page with the side of her hand, "what are we actually making. Because if this turns into three people telling war stories, Gloria's going to tuck it into a folder marked climate and call it a tragedy."

"We need a grievance packet with exhibits," Omar said. "Something that survives first contact with minimization."

"And if possible," Leela said, opening a blank document, "something that doesn't read as a personality dispute between students under stress."

June snorted. "We're students under stress."

"Yes," Leela said. "I would still prefer not to hand them that language."

That, at least, got them moving in the same direction. Leela started a chronology and put the screen where all three of them could see it. June flipped back through her legal pad, dates in the left margin, arrows and stars and greasy thumbprints from months of carrying it around in the same tote as oranges and receipts. Omar pulled up his inbox and began forwarding himself old threads with subject lines so mild they now looked psychotic. Spring planning. Quick follow-up. Notes from our chat. Mohit had always preferred violence that fit inside scheduling.

By eight-thirty the table looked less like panic and more like work. June had pages spread in a fan around her elbows. Omar had built a neat stack of printed emails and weighted the corner with his phone. Leela color-coded the chronology, though she knew June would mock her for it and also rely on it within ten minutes.

"September second," June said. "He tells me not to circulate the benchmark because Leela needs room to build an independent replication. His exact phrase is, quote, don't collapse the discovery sequence."

Leela looked up from the keyboard. "September third, he tells me June's benchmark is still too messy to trust and I should reproduce the evaluation layer myself if I want a stable dissertation chapter."

June barked one hard laugh. "Discovery sequence. Jesus Christ."

Omar scrolled. "September fifth, he writes me that pairwise stress testing will make the lab more professionally mature and that friendship can obscure technical truth."

"Friendship can obscure technical truth," June repeated. "That one should go on his grave."

Leela typed while they talked, trying to keep her fingers ahead of the shape that was emerging. Alone, any one of those lines could still be made to look like advising, eccentric perhaps, slightly manipulative if you were not paid to admire it. Together they clicked into place with a mechanical certainty that made her skin feel too small. The pattern was not that Mohit contradicted himself. It was that he did it on purpose, building each private conversation to lean against another one none of them could hear.

They found the October seminar there too once June stopped flipping pages like she was trying to start a fire and started reading them carefully. Mohit had told Omar to be "fearless about quality thresholds" two days before the talk. He had told June the week before that "some internal heat" might help her tighten the benchmark section. He had told Leela, separately, that the lab needed to stop mistaking kindness for rigor. June read the three notes in a row and then pushed the pad across the table to Leela.

"Take a picture if you want," she said.

Leela looked at her. June hated people touching her notes. She had once yanked a shopping list back from Leela with real offense because it happened to be on the reverse side of a page that also held meeting prep. Now she left the whole legal pad between them, palm flat on the cover only long enough to stop herself from snatching it back.

"I can just type from it," Leela said.

"Take the picture. My handwriting looks like a medical emergency after page twenty."

Leela did. Her phone camera hovered over June's dates, little arrows, irritated underlinings, the material proof of months June had not spent trusting memory. Up close, the paper smelled faintly of cigarette smoke from before June switched to gum and never admitted it counted as quitting. Leela cropped the image too carefully. June watched her do it and did not make a joke.

Omar pushed the bakery container toward them. "Eat something. Please. I would like to avoid assembling a grievance and a concussion in the same night."

June took a date bar, bit into it, and looked surprised. "These are good."

"Yes," Omar said. "That's why I brought them."

The room loosened after that, not into comfort exactly, but into the old lab competence that had once made the place bearable. June read dates aloud and swore at Mohit's phrasing. Omar, who normally sounded as if he had been edited by a careful journal, let irritation roughen him into speed. Leela built the packet around them, her screen filling with headings and subheadings and the ugly, almost intimate labor of turning harm into a format an institution might consent to recognize. Contradictory assignments. Misuse of unpublished work. Coercive withholding of documentation. Leverage through immigration paperwork. She hated the nouns. She also needed them.

At ten they had enough to frighten her. Not enough to win, not enough for that, but enough that a person acting in good faith could no longer call the whole year a misunderstanding. June's legal pad gave them dates. Omar's inbox gave them phrasing. The Baltimore deck gave them one clean theft, stolen figures lifted out of lab work and restaged as Ruderal's private vision. The funding-letter episode gave them leverage, documented and recent. Leela started an exhibit list and June leaned over her shoulder to read it, so close Leela could smell the mint bitterness of the gum and the soap June used when she bought whatever was cheapest in bulk. For one stretched second it felt like Baltimore in reverse, all the same proximity stripped of heat and turned toward work.

"No," June said softly, tapping the screen. "Move the deck before the visa letter. If Gloria wants to play dumb, the theft needs to show up before she gets to say this is just about Omar's paperwork."

"The deck is the cleanest artifact," Leela said.

"Exactly. Let it contaminate the rest."

Omar was watching them both with the alert stillness he got when he thought one wrong sentence could shatter a useful arrangement. "She's right," he said. "If the document begins with the visa matter, they can narrow it to process failure. If it begins with document use, then the visa letter becomes pattern rather than exception."

June pointed at him with the date bar. "See. This is why you get pastries."

He almost smiled. "I didn't know the terms of the exchange needed clarification."

Around midnight they started trying to kill the packet themselves. That was June's phrase for it. Assume disbelief. Assume Fisk exhausted and already writing the email in her head about preserving appropriate channels. Assume Mohit calm, offended, immaculate. What would survive him. What would not. The library had gone from crowded to cavernous. Somewhere beyond the study room a printer coughed and then jammed. The heating system came on full and turned the air dry enough that the legal-pad pages curled at the corners.

"This paragraph dies instantly," June said, drawing a slash through Leela's draft with the blunt pencil she kept behind her ear. "No offense."

"You're literally crossing it out."

"Because it sounds like a grant application by someone trying not to seem angry."

"I'm trying not to seem angry."

"That's the problem."

Omar took the pencil from June before she could do further damage. "The issue is not anger. The issue is proof density." He read from the screen. "'We were repeatedly placed in conflicting advisory structures that undermined research integrity and created retaliatory conditions.' That's true, but it's still summary. Give them one conflict they can't narrate around."

Leela knew the place where the packet went thin before either of them named it. September sat in the chronology like a bad tooth. Too many verbs, not enough attachments. She had written observed irregular repository duplication and then built the next paragraphs around the consequences because the source itself was missing from the file. October held. November held. January held. September did not.

June saw it a beat later. She tapped the screen with the eraser end of the pencil. "This line. 'Observed irregular repository duplication on lab infrastructure.' Observed by who."

"Me," Leela said.

"How."

Leela kept typing for one extra second, a stupid reflex, then stopped. "Server logs."

June's hand went still. Omar was already standing up with the legal pad scans, muttering something about the library copier flattening the margins into gray mud. He left the room before Leela answered the next question, and she knew later she would understand that as luck and call it strategy.

"And where are the logs," June said.

Leela looked at the manila envelope before she could stop herself.

June followed her eyes. "You've got to be fucking kidding me."

"I've got screenshots," Leela said. "Old ones. Not the raw logs."

"Since when."

The radiator hissed. Outside the glass, a student in a red beanie walked past balancing three books against his chest, fully alive inside some other life.

"September," Leela said.

June did not raise her voice. That made it worse. She took the gum out of her mouth, folded it into a napkin, and pressed the napkin flat with one thumb until the paper tore. "Before Baltimore."

"Yes."

"You had the source and you let me spend two months zooming into deck footers in a hotel room."

"I didn't have the source. I had images taken under inherited admin access I no longer controlled, and by October Research Integrity had already frozen my credentials."

"That's not the same thing as not having it."

"No," Leela said. "It isn't."

There was nothing useful to add. The shame of it was old enough now that it had lost heat and gained structure. She could feel every beam. She had found the cloned directories in September, Mohit's private Ruderal tree blooming where no private company should have existed, student repos copied under a name he had never spoken aloud to them. She had taken screenshots because she did not know what else to do, then closed the window, then told herself she needed more, then told herself she needed clean proof, then told herself another year of drift was materially impossible and a live fight with Mohit would end her before it touched him. She had not said those thoughts out loud at the time because even in her own head they sounded like excuses wearing shoes.

June leaned back in her chair and looked at her the way she looked at a broken sink, not with outrage but with immediate unwilling arithmetic. "What else."

Leela almost asked what she meant, almost made her specify it, and hated herself for the instinct before it finished forming.

"If the logs don't go in because they're messy," June said, "fine. But you keep dodging every sentence where your paper should show up. What else."

Leela rubbed the edge of the wrist brace against her thumbnail until the Velcro rasped. "If I use the logs, I need to explain why Mohit had leverage over me when I found them."

June waited.

"The conference paper," Leela said. "The robustness claim did not hold on the actual holdout split. Not cleanly. The aggregate looked good if you pooled the runs and buried the failure mode in an appendix that never happened. He said the trend was still defensible. I knew that was a sentence built to get a submission out the door. I submitted it anyway."

June shut her eyes for one second. When she opened them, there was no wit left in her face at all. "Did you know it was bad when you sent it."

"Yes."

"Did he."

"Yes."

June looked down at the exhibit list, then back at Leela. "So if we put the logs in, he says you're trying to burn him before somebody asks why your paper is dirty."

"He says I exceeded inherited admin authority, mishandled internal data, and produced a compromised result under pressure," Leela said. "And the committee hears my name first because it's on the paper."

"Your name is on the theft too."

"Not formally."

June gave a short, joyless laugh. "There it is."

Leela felt the sentence land exactly where June intended it to. Not accusation. Recognition. There it is. The line she had been walking all year and calling complexity, because complexity sounded smarter and less embarrassing than wanting two incompatible things at once: justice, and a dissertation future that still looked salvageable from the outside.

When Omar came back with the scans on a flash drive and toner dust on two fingers, June had already put the gum back in her mouth and the legal pad back under her forearm. He paused in the doorway, sensing the temperature change.

"Did the copier improve your mood," he said carefully.

"No," June said. "We have a hole in September."

Omar set the flash drive down. "Can the hole be filled."

Leela answered before June could. "Not cleanly."

Omar looked from one of them to the other, then chose the packet. It was one of the things she respected and resented in him, the way survival could make a person disciplined enough to postpone every emotional question until after the fire inspection. "Then we decide whether the current version survives," he said. "And if not, we decide what failure mode we prefer."

They kept going because there was nothing else to do. Omar rewrote his statement about the funding letter three times until it stopped sounding grateful and started sounding true. June cut whole paragraphs out of the cover memo and replaced them with one-line specifics brutal enough to breathe: Mohit used unpublished student figures in a private deck. Mohit assigned students to secretly reproduce or undermine one another's work. Mohit delayed time-sensitive immigration paperwork after direct requests. Leela reorganized the exhibits around those claims and watched the packet become sharper and somehow hollower at the same time, a body with its central organ removed so it could pass inspection.

At two-thirty Omar said, "My name stays on it."

June glanced up. "Nobody said otherwise."

"You both thought it."

Leela had. Not because she doubted him. Because she knew exactly what the university could do with a student on F-1 status who stopped being useful and started being visible.

"If my name is absent," Omar said, each clause clipped cleaner than the one before it, "they will call it hearsay dressed in concern. If my name is present, they may still do that, but at least the lie will require effort."

June chewed once, hard. "Okay."

He nodded and, after a second, added, "Also I'm tired of having the most important fact about my life appear only when it can be used against me."

That was the closest any of them came to a speech all night. It landed because he did not decorate it. He just turned back to the laptop and corrected a date in his statement from January 22 to January 23, because the email had hit at 11:48 p.m. and accuracy still mattered even after a sentence like that.

The library scanner ruined June's handwritten dates no matter what settings they tried. The yellow paper washed into a pale blur. At four-fifteen June swore at the machine loudly enough to earn a shush from the circulation desk, and they gave up on keeping the whole operation inside the library. The cold outside hit like metal. Snow along the quad had gone from white to the color of old receipts. Leela carried the backpack on both shoulders to steady the weight of the laptop, the flash drive, the envelope. Omar walked fast in silence, one hand closed around the strap of his bag. June kept one gloved hand under her armpit and the other around her phone, typing notes to herself with her thumb even while she crossed the icy path.

Keating at that hour smelled like burnt dust and old coffee. The basement lab was dark, but the sixth-floor copy room was lit, and Ben was in it with an overfull sleeve of mini powdered donuts, a department polo under an expensive winter jacket, and one sneaker up on the chair while he re-tied the lace as if the fraying tip had become the central emergency of his morning.

He startled when he saw them and then immediately overcorrected into helpfulness. "Oh. Hey. You're all up early."

"We're not up early," June said. "We're up late."

"Right. Yeah. Sure." Ben dropped his foot to the floor. Powdered sugar marked the knee of his dark pants like chalk dust. "Do you need the copier? Mohit wants sponsor packets for the breakfast thing and this machine is less cursed than the one downstairs."

Leela hated how quickly relief moved through her. "We need clean scans of the notes and one combined PDF of the packet. The library scanner kept washing the dates out."

"Totally. I can unlock scan-to-email." Ben was already reaching for his ID badge. "The margin detection on this one is better if you feed from the flatbed, not tray two. Tray two crops like crazy."

June opened her mouth, probably to say they could manage, but Omar had already set the legal pad on the counter. Time kept making pragmatists out of them against their preferences. Ben tapped in his code, bright and eager and trying much too hard to sound like a person who belonged on the staff side of a doorway.

"Send it where," he said.

"My Haviland address," Leela said. "And nobody else's if the machine gives you options."

"Sure."

His fingers moved fast over the touch screen. For a second the preview of the first page came up large and unmistakable: Graduate grievance packet regarding supervisory misconduct and misuse of student research. June's notes began on page two, followed by the chronology, the deck screenshots, the funding-letter trail. Ben's eyes flicked to the title and away. Leela saw him see it. He said nothing, only fed the next page more carefully than necessary and brushed powdered sugar off the glass with the side of his hand.

"You guys doing some kind of, I don't know, documentation thing?" he asked, too casual to be casual.

June looked at him. "That machine have a mute button."

Ben gave a thin laugh. "I was just trying to help."

"Then help by not narrating."

He flushed, genuinely young for one unguarded second. "Right. Sorry."

The scans came through crisp this time. Dates legible, arrows intact. Ben stepped back as if proximity itself might become evidence. When the machine chirped done, he glanced at the screen and frowned.

"Weird," he said. "Because I was the one logged in, it sent the packet to me too. I can delete it."

Leela's whole body wanted to grab the printer tray, the screen, the air between them. She made herself sound bored instead. "Please do."

"Yeah. Of course."

June was watching her, not Ben.

They took the scans and went down to the basement lab because no one wanted to walk back to the library with the packet this close to finished. The room was almost tender in its ugliness at that hour: fluorescent lights humming over abandoned mugs, the salvaged couch with one arm sinking lower than the other, whiteboard ghosts layered into a permanent fog of old ambitions. Leela had once loved that about the place, the evidence that people stayed here long enough to become messy. Now every surface looked like residue.

She opened the packet on the lab desktop because the bigger monitor made it easier to check pagination. June stood at her left shoulder. Omar leaned against the whiteboard, reading the cover memo out loud under his breath, catching dropped words and duplicated dates. Ben's scan confirmation hit Leela's inbox while she merged the files. A second later a text followed.

copier sent the packet to me too btw. deleting now

She typed thanks and hated how much that single word resembled trust.

Then there was only the last decision.

The manila envelope had gone soft at the corners from months in her backpack. She took it out because June was already looking at it, because pretending it did not exist had become stupid, because some part of her had carried it all this way in case proximity did what principle had not. Inside were three folded printouts and one page from her September notebook. The first screenshot showed the Ruderal directory nested inside the lab server tree, timestamped and ugly as a caught hand. The second showed the overnight clone jobs touching student repos one after another. The third was not theft but the thing attached to it, the holdout-split table from her paper, a column of bad numbers she had cut from the final version before submission because Mohit called it noise and she had needed him to be right.

June said, very quietly, "Those go in."

Omar pushed off the whiteboard. "What are those."

Leela kept her eyes on the pages. "Old admin captures. And material related to my paper."

"Would they strengthen the packet," he asked.

"Yes," June said.

Leela answered at the same time. "Not safely."

The fluorescent lights buzzed loud enough to seem personal. Somewhere in the server closet a fan rose and settled.

"If they're real evidence," Omar said, more slowly now, "I don't understand why they aren't attached."

June did not take her eyes off Leela. "Because once they're attached, this stops being a grievance about his pattern and turns into a fight about what he can throw back at her."

Omar looked at Leela then, taking in not the whole story, not the age of it, not the months, but enough. His face closed a fraction. "Is that wrong."

It should have been easier to answer him than June. Instead it was harder, because he asked the question as if there were still such a thing as a correct proportion of self-preservation.

"It is not wrong," Leela said. "It is expensive."

June made a sound that could have been a laugh if there had been any humor left in the room. "Everything is expensive."

Leela laid the first screenshot beside the keyboard. She could attach it. She knew exactly where it would go in the exhibit order, after the deck and before the funding letter, the missing hinge that would turn a plausible complaint into something harder to smother. She laid the second beside it, the clone-job timestamps. Then the paper table, its ugly omitted column. With all three on the desk, the year changed shape. No more unfortunate pattern. No more ambiguity about whether Mohit had merely drifted past ethical boundaries while overworked and visionary and badly supervised himself. With the pages there, he had done it, and she had known, and her own name was soldered to the proof in ways no careful paragraph could undo.

She saw, with horrible clarity, the packet Mohit would make in return. Her conference paper on top. Research Integrity already primed by the earlier complaint under her credentials. Committee members flipping not to the cloned directories first but to the holdout table, grateful for a version of the story that let them call everything contamination instead of choosing a side. Her stipend. The defense that had not yet officially been delayed because March had not happened yet, because futures always remained technically intact right up until the email arrived killing them.

"Leela," June said.

Leela clicked the exhibit list, added one line break too many, deleted it, and then, with the cursor still blinking, put the three pages back into the envelope.

It was a small movement. Paper on paper. Fold into fold. She felt the old creases find themselves under her fingers like muscle memory.

June did not speak for several seconds. Omar looked down at the cover memo as if it might change in his hands if he stared hard enough.

"Okay," he said finally, and the word was not agreement. It was merely the sound a person made when there were still tasks left and no authority above them was coming.

Leela attached the scanned notes, the deck screenshots, Omar's statement, the funding-letter trail, the chronology, the cover memo. She addressed the email to Gloria Fisk and the graduate studies grievance address, then copied the ombuds office because June insisted on one witness outside the department and Omar said witnesses mattered more than hope. The subject line looked flimsy no matter how she phrased it. Graduate grievance regarding supervisory misconduct and misuse of student research. She re-read it until the words flattened.

"Send it," June said.

Leela clicked.

For a moment nothing happened. Then the message vanished into Sent. The auto-response arrived fifty-three seconds later, chilled and neutral, thanking them for their submission and promising review according to university procedure. Dawn had begun somewhere outside the basement windows too high to see from here. The fluorescent lights still made everybody look sick.

Leela closed the laptop. The room went quiet except for the server hum and the cheap wall clock clicking toward six.

She slid the envelope back into the inner sleeve of her backpack.

June did not look at the confirmation email. She looked at Leela's hand folding the flap shut, making the decisive pages small enough to disappear again. The complaint now existed in three inboxes and at least two offices. It could hurt them. It could maybe start something. But June's face, when Leela finally made herself meet it, held no relief at all. It held the colder knowledge that Leela had wanted a version of justice that still left her something to defend.
