# Chapter 1

The basement lab smelled the way it always had: burnt coffee, hot dust, old upholstery, and the dry animal heat of the server closet breathing through its cracked door. For one relieved second, before she saw what had changed, Leela loved it again. The blue couch was still there with one arm lower than the other because nobody had ever fixed the internal spring. The long table was still ringed with chairs stolen over several years from conference rooms that did not miss them enough to ask questions. One whiteboard still held a ghost of June's looping handwriting under months of uncapped marker haze. The place looked like what Mohit had once promised it was going to be, which was not prestige exactly, not yet, but a corner of machine learning where people fed one another and passed around chargers and covered each other's classes and went to June's apartment after seminar to eat rice and whatever could be made with cabbage.

Then the second board came into focus.

Somebody had scrubbed it clean and left only four printed sheets taped in a neat column: fall milestones, conference deadlines, sponsor visit dates, and a calendar for one-on-one check-ins with Mohit. The shelf by the microwave, which usually held soy sauce packets, stale protein bars, tampons, and the ibuprofen bottle everybody denied owning, had been half-cleared for a row of black binders with fresh spine labels. The room had not become nicer. It had become legible to somebody outside it.

June was at her desk with her backpack open, digging through a side pocket for nicotine gum hard enough to make the zipper rasp. Her hair had the blunt unevenness of a cut she'd done herself in a bathroom mirror, and she was wearing the same paint-flecked chore coat she wore when she needed to make it through a whole day without pretending to be polished. Omar sat across from her in a pale button-down so crisp it made the basement look rude. He had rolled his sleeves once, exactly evenly, and was aligning his notebook with the edge of the desk as if order might spread by contact. At the coffee machine Ben Haber was reading the laminated instruction card June had taped up last spring after it flooded twice. He wore careful white sneakers no basement deserved and a dark jacket expensive enough that Leela could not stop seeing the price of it.

"You made it," June said. She popped the gum in, chewed once, and looked at Leela's face more closely. "You look like summer did not heal you."

"It took a crack at me," Leela said.

Ben turned too quickly, bright with first-year energy and the fear of getting the tone wrong. "Morning. I was just trying not to destroy the machine on day one."

"The machine destroys itself," June said. "You only have to stay out of its way."

Omar gave Leela a small nod. "Good morning."

He sounded as if mornings could still be good in a basement under fluorescent lights. It was one of the things about him that irritated her and steadied her at the same time.

Leela dropped her bag by her desk. Her wrist brace caught on the strap, and for an instant the old embarrassment flashed through her: thirty years old, sixth year, body already filing workplace complaints her mouth had not. On her monitor a yellow sticky note from May still clung to the bezel. `Ask Mohit about committee timing.` She had asked twice by email over the summer and gotten paragraphs back about vision, positioning, and the need to align the lab's external story after a difficult funding season. No answer had contained a date.

Mohit's office door was open. That alone made him seem generous.

"I'm going to grab him before he disappears," she said.

June leaned back in her chair. "If he says 'big picture' before minute three, you owe me coffee."

"That bet is rigged."

"Then win it."

Mohit was at his desk in shirtsleeves, blue oxford, cuffs immaculate, the kind of softness that looked accidental until you noticed how completely he controlled it. His expensive glasses were pushed halfway down his nose while he typed, and behind him the office shelves had been rearranged. The framed group photo from last year's retreat had been moved aside for a stack of white banker boxes and a new acrylic plaque from some accelerator program Leela had never heard of. He looked up and smiled with immediate warmth, as if he had been hoping she would interrupt.

"Leela. There you are." He stood, because he had learned long ago that standing to greet people made them feel met rather than managed. "How was the end of summer?"

"Short," she said. "Do you have five minutes?"

"For you, yes."

He said it like a favor and a truth.

She sat in the chair opposite him. The fabric still had a cigarette burn on one arm from whoever had owned it before the lab inherited it. Mohit lowered himself back down, hands folded loosely, listening face already in place.

"I need to lock my year," she said. "I sent the revised chapter and the methods memo. If we're treating this as my dissertation year, I need a defense timeline, committee names, the normal approvals. If we're not, I need to know that now rather than in November."

He gave a tiny sympathetic exhale, the sound of a man encountering a perfectly reasonable concern he intended to reroute. "You're not wrong."

It was not yes.

"But I think the frame has shifted over the summer in a way that makes the old sequence less helpful." He leaned back. "The center bid not landing was clarifying. Painful, but clarifying. We have to stop imagining that public-interest branding alone is going to protect the work. Reviewers are harsher. Sponsors are harsher. The field is harsher. If I send you into that environment with the same lab habits we've had, I'm not serving you."

Leela kept her face still. "I'm asking a smaller question than that."

"I know." His smile deepened, gentle enough to make interruption feel rude. "And I'm answering the larger one because it determines the smaller one."

There it was. Not `big picture`, but close enough that June would claim the coffee anyway.

"Three o'clock," he said. "Mandatory group meeting. I want everybody hearing the same strategic reset at the same time. Then you and I can talk about how your path fits inside it."

"I don't really need a strategic reset to know whether you will approve a final dissertation year."

He watched her for a beat. When he was cornered he did not get louder. He became tidier.

"I don't want to give you a false answer," he said. "You're close enough that the form of your final year matters. Close is not the same as market-ready. You understand that better than most people. Come to the meeting. Let me show you where the lab is going. Then we'll calibrate."

Calibrate. As if her life were a model drifting off distribution.

"Okay," she said, because he had made no other word available.

"Good." He brightened again at once. "And Leela?"

She looked up.

"I want you helping set the tone. Ben is new. Omar is still finding his footing. June has had a rough few months. You're the senior person in the room."

It was flattering if you did not look directly at the use it was being put to.

"Right," she said.

"We'll make this a strong year."

He meant it, probably. The problem with Mohit was that the lie and the plan so often lived in the same sentence.

When she came back out, June looked up immediately. "Well?"

"You owe me coffee," Leela said.

June barked out one laugh. "He got there before minute three?"

"He used `clarifying` instead."

"That is spiritually the same word."

Omar glanced between them. "Is there a problem?"

Leela was about to say no when the clean white calendar on the board pulled her eye again. Sponsor visit dates. Check-ins. Milestones. The black binders waiting on the shelf like a row of shut mouths.

"Not yet," she said.

The morning spread thin after that. Ben asked where they kept extra HDMI cables and thanked her too earnestly when she pointed to the mislabeled drawer. June muttered at a permissions error under her breath with the bored obscenity of long practice. Omar took one phone call in the hallway in Arabic, low and quick, then came back with his jaw set a fraction too hard. Every fifteen minutes Leela found herself checking the time as if the meeting might become smaller if she caught it early enough.

At one point she opened the dissertation chapter she had revised in July and read the first page without absorbing a word. Her own prose looked like evidence from somebody else's stalled life. The Hudson Federal card bill was due on the eighteenth. Graduate housing renewed by academic year, not by dignity. If Mohit decided to delay her again, it would not be an emotional inconvenience. It would be rent, insurance, another winter in a studio the size of a held breath, another season of telling her mother that the department's timeline was unusually complicated this year.

At two fifty-three Mohit sent a lab-wide message with no subject line. `Downstairs conference corner now, please. Bring notebooks.` Ben was already standing before the notification finished loading.

The conference corner was the far end of the lab by the dead window that looked into a cinderblock shaft. Someone had tried to civilize it years ago with a rug and two standing lamps rescued from a faculty office purge. One lamp still worked if you kicked the base. Mohit had added a tray of coffees from the bakery near the lake, the one none of them bought from unless parents were visiting, and a pastry box bleeding butter through the cardboard. The gesture was so practiced it almost worked on Leela. By reflex she was hungry.

"Please eat," Mohit said as they settled around the table. "I don't want this feeling like a punishment."

June, reaching for a croissant anyway, said, "Comforting opener."

He smiled at her as if she'd offered useful texture. Ben laughed a second too late.

Mohit remained standing. He did not use slides. That made everything feel more intimate and less appealable. He had a yellow pad in front of him and one of his expensive pens uncapped beside it, though Leela never saw him write a note he could not later use.

"I spent a lot of the summer thinking about what this lab has done well and what it has hidden from itself," he said. "We have been generous with one another. That matters to me. I mean that. But generosity can become a methodology of avoidance. We have a culture of softening our critique because we live in one another's pockets. We share food, code, panic, backup TA coverage, moving boxes, all of it. It's humane. It also means too much of our work reaches the world under-tested."

Leela hated the accuracy of `share food`. It made the next part harder to reject cleanly.

"The external environment is not getting kinder," he went on. "Reviewers are more adversarial. Industry is absorbing methods faster than academia can explain them. If we want your dissertations to survive contact with reality, we need to do our hardest stress-testing before anyone else does it to us and enjoys the blood."

Omar had his notebook open already. June had not uncrossed her arms. Ben was looking at Mohit the way first-years looked at any professor fluent enough to sound like a future.

"So this semester," Mohit said, "we're formalizing internal adversarial evaluation."

The room stayed quiet, waiting for the joke to arrive and declare itself one.

"I do not mean hostile culture," he said. "I mean disciplined red-teaming. Each of you will take a confidential brief attached to another lab member's core project. Your job will be to identify brittleness, leakage, invalid assumptions, and evaluation theater before a reviewer, competitor, or sponsor does it for us. Secrecy matters here. If you coordinate too early, you protect one another. That's human. It's also bad science."

June tilted her head. "Confidential from who."

"From the lab, except through me, until first findings are on paper."

"So confidential from the person whose work we're supposed to break."

"Stress-test," Mohit said, not correcting her so much as laundering the word. "And yes. Otherwise we drift into politeness."

Leela could hear the fluorescent hum over his voice. It made the room feel already documented.

"There are three reasons to do this," Mohit said, counting lightly on his fingers. "One: the work gets stronger. Two: each of you learns how other people's claims fail, which will sharpen your own. Three: in a constrained funding environment, I need deliverables that make this lab legible to people with choices."

There it was. Not quite a threat, not quite an explanation of the center bid that had not landed, but the weather system everything else now lived inside.

"How constrained," Omar asked.

He asked it in the courteous tone that most often meant he was frightened.

Mohit gave him a sympathetic look. "Constrained enough that I'm not interested in wasting your time on work that can't survive scrutiny."

That was not an answer either, but Omar nodded as if one had been offered.

Mohit turned the yellow pad around. He had drawn four names with arrows between them.

"High level only," he said. "You'll each get a specific brief after this."

His pen tapped once beside Leela's name. "Leela, you'll be stress-testing Omar's calibration and transfer claims on the incident-response model."

Omar's jaw moved once.

"Omar, you'll audit June's benchmark splits and baseline assumptions."

June made a short noise through her nose, not yet a laugh.

"June, you'll red-team Ben's provenance tracker and annotation reliability pipeline."

Ben sat up straighter, trying to look as if being assigned a fourth-year critic counted as a gift instead of a warning.

"Ben, you'll audit the label history and data provenance on Leela's intake corpus."

For a second Leela only heard the words `Ben` and `Leela` in that order, absurd and insulting enough to be almost funny. Then the rest of it arrived. He was giving a first-year sanctioned access to the parts of her project she had spent years learning how to explain without inviting lazy contempt.

"I'm sorry," she said, before she could make her tone nicer. "Is this on top of our existing work, or are you redefining dissertation progress through somebody else's failure modes?"

Ben looked down at the table. June looked at her with sudden alertness.

Mohit took the question like a man accepting the chance to clarify. "Those are not opposites."

"They are if the semester is finite."

"The semester is finite," he said. "Exactly. Which is why duplication, sentimentality, and under-ambitious evaluation have to end."

June chewed slowly. "Cool. Love being called sentimental by a man who bought us croissants."

Ben gave another uncertain laugh, then killed it when nobody joined him.

Mohit did not so much as glance in his direction. "June, if Omar can break your benchmark in-house, I would rather know that in October than watch someone else do it from a conference microphone in November."

The sentence landed and kept traveling. Leela saw June register that too.

"And if he can't?" June asked.

"Then your claims become much stronger."

"Lucky me."

Mohit turned back to Leela. "For you, the answer is a little more direct. You're at the stage where I need evidence not just of productivity but of defensibility. I don't think I can responsibly move your final-year approval forward until you've completed the Omar evaluation at a serious level."

He said it in the same voice another advisor might have used to recommend extra citations.

The table went still. Even Ben looked up.

Leela felt the room narrow around the cheap black chair under her. "My approval is contingent on this."

"Your approval depends on me being able to make a credible case for your dissertation's maturity," he said. "Right now, the strongest path is for you to lead with rigorous adversarial evaluation rather than one more inward-facing chapter. That's not punishment. That's strategy."

One more inward-facing chapter. As if the last year of her life were a decorative indulgence she kept trying to sneak past him.

"That's a major change," she said.

"It's an adaptation to reality."

There were versions of this conversation she could have had if she had been twenty-four, if she had parents paying her rent, if she had a different advisor already informally lined up, if she had not spent six years letting Mohit define what counted as almost finished. In none of those versions did she say what she said next.

"What does serious level mean."

June turned to look at her fully. The humiliation in that was smaller than the relief. Questions were work. Anger was expensive.

Mohit nodded once, as if pleased she had chosen adulthood. "Good. Specifics. For you it means a clean evaluation design, a workshop submission we can point to, and enough technical depth that nobody on a committee can call the work superficial. I'm not asking you to sabotage Omar. I'm asking you to show you can think like someone defending a dissertation in the current field."

Omar cleared his throat. "And if the evaluation damages the original paper."

"Then we learn something before reviewers do. Better from friends than enemies."

The word `friends` sat in the middle of the table like something dropped there by mistake.

June spoke around her gum. "And we're not supposed to compare notes, because that would what, corrupt the scientific process with basic social life?"

"I'm asking for independence until first findings," Mohit said. "After that, I expect collaboration. But not before. If you want honest pressure, you have to tolerate some temporary discomfort."

Temporary. As if he were asking them to hold a stretch.

He reached into a slim stack of folders and slid one to each of them. Heavy paper. Their names printed on labels. The folders made the whole thing feel more official than most of the university's actual paperwork.

"Please don't circulate these," he said. "These are working documents. I tailored them."

Leela opened hers despite herself. The first page listed Omar's current model components, target failure modes, suggested ablations. The second page was shorter.

`Milestone for dissertation-year approval: successful completion of adversarial evaluation brief and associated external-facing submission.`

No signature. No form number. Nothing she could appeal to except his mouth.

Around the table paper whispered under fingers. June had stopped chewing. Omar was reading with such fixed attention that it looked almost devout. Ben flipped too fast, eager enough to seem grateful, then slowed when he realized no one else was moving.

"Questions?" Mohit asked.

June looked up. "Yeah. Did you always want to run a lab like this, or is this a fun new side effect of losing money?"

Leela felt the shot of admiration before the fear.

Mohit's expression barely changed. "I want to run a lab that tells the truth about what the world will do to weak work."

"The world or you."

"If you can't tell the difference yet, this will help."

That shut the room more effectively than if he had snapped at her.

He checked his watch. "I know this is a shift. Sit with it. Send me initial scopes by Friday. Leela, stay two minutes after if you can. Everyone else, you're free."

Chairs scraped. Ben grabbed his folder and then the pastry box, hesitated, and set the box back down when he saw nobody else was taking anything. Omar slipped his notebook under his arm with the care of a man handling medical paperwork. June stood last, shoving the folder into her bag as if it might stain the table if she left it out.

Leela stayed because he had not left the possibility of not staying available either.

When the others were out of earshot, Mohit sat on the table edge instead of across from her. The pose was meant to feel less administrative.

"I know that landed hard," he said.

"You changed my year in front of everyone."

"I made the logic transparent in front of everyone. Different thing."

"Not especially."

He let that pass. "Leela, you're not a second-year. I can't keep protecting you with vague encouragement. You need a case I can defend to other people, not just to you."

Her fingers pressed into the folder so hard the paper ridge marked her skin. "I did not ask for encouragement. I asked for approval."

"And I'm telling you how to get it."

Outside the conference corner she could hear June's voice in the hallway, low and furious, then Ben's thinner answer. The ordinary rhythm would have been all of them around the table translating Mohit's euphemisms into English, stealing three extra minutes before anyone pretended to get back to work. Now even hearing the others felt slightly illicit, as if the room had been fitted with microphones while they were not looking.

"You are asking me to spend time attacking Omar's work instead of finishing my own," she said.

"I'm asking you to produce the kind of work that makes your own finishable."

"Those are not the same sentence."

"In this market, they are."

He smiled after he said it, not kindly, not unkindly either. Just as if he had placed a fact on the table and expected her professional maturity to handle it.

"Friday," he said. "I trust you to scope it well."

Trust. Another word he only seemed to use when he meant obligation.

She stood because the meeting was over only when she stood up and left.

The hallway outside the basement lab was painted the same defeated beige as every corridor in Keating. Someone had propped the fire door open with a recycling bin, and the smell of old coffee had thinned into cinderblock damp. June was by the vending machine, coat already on despite the heat, tearing another piece of nicotine gum out of its foil with her teeth. Omar stood beside her with his folder held flat against his chest. Ben was hovering near both of them and neither, one hand on the strap of his laptop bag, still trying to look like the sort of person who found all this stimulating.

The old version of the afternoon would have been automatic. They would have drifted to this exact patch of hallway and said what he really means is, what he wants is, what he's scared of is, then gone back in mildly repaired. The body remembered the movement even after the permission was gone.

June looked at Leela. "So that's psychotic."

"That's one word," Leela said.

Ben moved first, unable to stand inside silence without decorating it. "I mean, red-teaming is real, though. At least as a methodology. People do blind challenge tracks all the time."

June turned her head slowly toward him. "Thank God. I was worried we might miss the nineteen-year-old VP of Product perspective."

"I'm twenty-four," he said before he could stop himself.

"That is not the part of the sentence I was mocking."

He flushed hard enough to show his age all at once. Under the fluorescent lights he looked abruptly younger than his jacket, a kid who had overpacked for weather and status at the same time.

"I just meant," he said, faster now, "it doesn't have to be, like, interpersonal. If the point is stronger work."

Omar spoke before June could answer. "He told me not to discuss my brief."

June looked back at him. The joke dropped out of her face. "He told all of us not to circulate the folders."

"No." Omar's voice stayed even, which was how she knew he was straining to keep it that way. "He told me specifically not to discuss the assignment details with anyone in the lab. Especially the target. He said comparison would contaminate independence."

There it was. The methodology version of isolation.

Leela said, "He used almost the exact same language with me."

June gave a short, ugly laugh. "Great. So we're apparently all in a hostage situation sponsored by peer review."

Ben shifted his weight. "Maybe he just doesn't want groupthink."

June stared at him. "Ben, if you say `groupthink` in this hallway one more time, I'm going to put you through the drywall."

The threat was too specific to feel theatrical. Ben nodded as if receiving useful feedback.

Omar looked down at the edge of his folder. His thumb had started worrying the paper soft. "I don't think refusing is possible."

Leela heard the sentence for what it was and for what he had chosen not to attach to it. Funding. Visa paperwork. The thousand invisible signatures that made certain people in a university more real than others.

"No," she said. "Probably not."

June shoved the spent gum wrapper into her pocket. "I can't decide what's worse, that he thinks this is science or that he knows it isn't and doesn't care."

"Both can be true," Omar said.

It was the driest thing Leela had ever heard him say. June almost smiled.

Behind the lab door Mohit's laugh carried out briefly, warm and social. Ben's head turned toward it before he could keep himself from doing that. Mohit called his name a second later, asking if he could help move a couple of boxes from the office. Ben looked at the three of them, caught between relief and embarrassment, and then back toward the door.

"Yeah," he said. "Sure. One second."

June watched him go. "Jesus."

The word did not contain contempt by itself. Mostly fatigue.

Omar adjusted his grip on the folder. "I should get back upstairs."

Leela knew he did not work upstairs. None of them did. But she understood the lie and let him have it.

"Okay," she said.

He nodded to both of them. "If anything becomes less impossible, let me know."

"That is not how impossibility works," June said, but softly now.

Omar's mouth moved at one corner, almost a smile, and then he was gone up the stairs with the careful, upright pace he used whenever fear was trying to make him rush.

June leaned back against the cinderblock wall and shut her eyes for a second. Without the jokes her face looked younger and harsher at once.

"You all right?" Leela asked.

"No. You?"

"Not especially."

June opened her eyes. "Did he say anything extra to you because you're the dissertation emergency."

Leela considered lying and found she was too tired to build the sentence elegantly. "He said he won't move my final-year approval until I do the Omar brief at what he calls a serious level."

June's expression changed, not to pity exactly. To recognition. "Fucking hell."

"Yeah."

"And if you tell him no."

Leela looked past her through the little wired-glass pane in the lab door. Ben was inside now, stacking banker boxes while Mohit stood too close, talking with one hand in his pocket like a man mentoring rather than deploying. The black binders on the shelf by the microwave looked stupidly official. The blue couch sagged under nobody.

"Then I don't know what the year is," she said. "Which is sort of the point."

June pushed off the wall. "Come to dinner tomorrow."

It was not an invitation so much as a refusal to let the afternoon define the whole room.

"Okay," Leela said.

June nodded once and went back inside for her bag. Leela stayed in the hallway long enough to feel the building's air push weakly through the vent above her, all noise and no cooling. She had imagined, stupidly, that the first day of the semester might give her a shape again. Instead it had taken the old shape apart and relabeled the pieces.

When she returned to her desk, Mohit's folder was where she'd left it, square on the keyboard like a bill. She opened it again because not opening it would have been a form of superstition. The technical brief was specific enough to be real: calibration curves, transfer failures, error clusters across city datasets. Work she could do. Work that might even be good. That was part of the obscenity. The assignment was not nonsense. It was worse than nonsense. It was useful.

The second page looked meaner in the lab's ordinary light than it had in his hand.

`Milestone for dissertation-year approval.`

No official form would say it that nakedly. No official form needed to. Mohit's control lived in the territory just outside paperwork, where everything consequential could still be described later as mentorship, misunderstanding, a conversation about standards.

Leela sat down slowly. Her chair gave the familiar squeal at the left wheel. On June's desk a mug with a chipped cartoon shrimp stared sideways at the room. Omar had left one pen uncapped, lined up perfectly beside his notebook. Somewhere behind the server closet door a fan rattled and corrected itself. All the proof of the lab's old life was still physically present. None of it had helped.

She could have walked across campus and asked Gloria Fisk what counted as normal advisor behavior. She could have called her mother and said the year was slipping again, this time for a reason ugly enough to sound fake. She could have refused the brief on principle, which was another way of saying she could have risked paying the principle in rent and time and the slow bureaucratic death of a sixth year with no schedule.

Instead she pulled her laptop closer and opened a blank email.

`Subject: adversarial scope`

Her fingers stayed above the keys for a moment. The wrist brace pressed warm into her skin. Around her the lab kept making its old sounds, fluorescent hum and fan whine and the far-off clatter of someone dropping a spoon in the sink, as if a place could keep describing itself long after the people in it had agreed to become something else.

She typed that she understood the brief and would send a first-pass plan by Friday. She typed that Omar's calibration claims were a sensible place to start. She did not type that she needed the approval, that the card payment was due on the eighteenth, that another year of drift would mean another year of explaining her life in smaller and smaller lies. She did not type that he had built the choice so cleanly that agreeing could still pass for reason.

She hit send because refusing had no immediate grammar and because wanting to finish and agreeing to this had become, in one afternoon, the same sentence.
