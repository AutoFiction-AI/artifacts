# Chapter 11

By the second week of January, the Civic Signals Lab smelled of wet wool, hot dust, and the wholesale-store onion bagels Mohit bought whenever he wanted management to pass for care. The basement had the same fluorescent hum it had carried all fall, the same server-closet heat leaking under the metal door, the same whiteboards furred over with old marker ghosts, but the semester had changed its pressure anyway. People's coats stayed on longer. Nobody sat on the salvaged couch until the room told them what kind of meeting it was going to be.

Leela arrived with slush drying in white veins on her boots and found Mohit setting out a paper box of bagels beside a stack of napkins so thin they tore if you looked at them too hard. He had his expensive glasses off and was polishing them with the hem of a dish towel he had probably borrowed from the faculty kitchenette upstairs. He never used the basement paper cups if he could help it. He had brought a real ceramic mug down with him, dark green with the university crest, as if even coffee needed hierarchy.

June was already there, sitting backward on a chair, coat still zipped, one ankle hooked through the rungs. She had trimmed her blunt hair again over break and missed by half a centimeter on the left side, a flaw that only made it look more like June had done it on purpose. She worried a square of nicotine gum out of a pharmacy blister with her thumbnail and glanced at the bagels like they had personally lied to her.

Ben came in behind Leela carrying a cardboard tray of coffees nobody had asked him to get. Snowmelt ran off the hem of his expensive technical jacket. He set the tray down, then crouched to rub a salt line off one careful sneaker with the corner of a receipt before he took his seat, doing it quickly, as if the room might forgive vanity if it looked like hygiene.

Omar arrived last, apologizing though he was only two minutes behind Mohit's calendar invite. He wore a charcoal sweater over a collared shirt crisp enough to look ironed, and he had brought a clementine in his coat pocket. He peeled it as he sat, the rind coming off in one long bright strip that he laid beside his notebook in a neat coil. Fear, on him, usually started in the jaw. Today it had not started yet, but Leela could see where it would go.

Mohit waited until everyone had something in front of them, coffee, gum, notebook, laptop, a cheap paper plate with nothing on it, and then smiled in the managerial way that made warmth sound expense-accountable.

"Happy spring term," he said. "I know nobody believes in that phrase yet, but let's pretend for fifteen minutes."

June bit into the gum. "You brought onions this early. That's not optimism."

A small laugh moved through the room and died quickly. Mohit nodded as if he had permitted exactly the right amount of levity.

"I want to start with realities," he said. "The center bridge money we were hoping to have did not materialize. The department travel pool was tightened over break. Sponsor reimbursement is moving, but not on a timeline I can responsibly promise against. So I'm not going to sell you a fantasy about abundance."

He said abundance like it was a childish idea they had all outgrown. Leela looked at the bagel box. The paper lid had gone translucent in circles where the oil had soaked through.

Mohit rested both hands on the back of an empty chair. "This spring, the lab has money for one student travel slot. One fully supported trip. Registration, hotel, airfare, the ordinary things that suddenly become extraordinary when nobody wants to pay for them. And for students thinking seriously about dissertation timing, I can actively shepherd one priority defense path through committee season. One."

Nobody moved. The fluorescent tubes hissed overhead. In the server closet something gave a higher, thinner whine, then settled.

Ben was the first to speak. He did it too fast, tripping over his own eagerness. "When you say travel slot, do you mean only accepted full papers, or would a workshop count if it was, like, strategically useful?"

Mohit turned to him with a softer face, the one he used when rewarding juniors for saying the question he wanted in the room. "Good. Useful distinction. I mean visibility that compounds. I'm less interested in tourism than signal."

June snorted. "Jesus Christ."

Mohit glanced at her. "Do you want to contribute something more legible than that?"

"I'm just admiring the branding," June said. "One slot, one path. Sounds like a game show for people with bad health insurance."

Ben stared down into his coffee. Omar kept his eyes on the clementine peel.

Mohit did not rise to it. He rarely had to. "You can call it branding if that helps. I call it refusing to lie to you. Scarcity isn't a personal whim. It's the environment. The professional question is what you do inside it."

That was the part that landed. Not the one slot. Not even the defense path. The little sermon around them. Mohit was good at taking a structure that would have hurt anyway and polishing it until resistance looked naive.

Leela felt the wrist brace on her right hand catch against the seam of her sweater. She had fronted conference costs on her Hudson Federal card before and watched them survive longer than the conferences themselves. Hotel deposit, flight change fee, one dinner you were supposed to be reimbursed for and never quite were. The interest had its own memory. The defense path mattered even more. A date, a room, a committee that showed up prepared instead of vaguely hostile, a future in which Lakebridge rent ended on schedule instead of stretching into another year of cheap blinds and radiator insomnia. Until Mohit said one, she had let herself keep using the plural.

Omar cleared his throat. "What counts as strongest public story by your standard? Existing acceptances, or what can be submitted in the next cycle?"

He kept his voice courteous and almost formal, but Leela heard the strain in the careful transitions, each clause laid down with more control than he should have needed for an ordinary question.

"A mix," Mohit said. "Momentum matters. External legibility matters. So does lab citizenship. The people I can argue for are the people whose work looks both strong and socially stabilizing."

June laughed once, without humor. "Socially stabilizing."

Mohit kept going. "We had a messy fall. Some of that was real, some of it was noise, but committees don't reward mess. Sponsors don't reward mess. I need to be able to tell a coherent story about this group."

The word group lay in the room like an accusation nobody could quote back to him later.

Leela asked, "What exactly is a defense path?"

She hated how dry her voice sounded, as if she were asking about a software dependency instead of the next year of her life.

"It means I spend political capital instead of goodwill," Mohit said. "Pre-meetings. Committee management. Timely document movement. Making sure the person in question is not trying to fight six institutional headwinds alone."

His gaze flicked to her and away. The look lasted less than a second and carried exactly the amount of meaning he could later deny.

June said, "So the rest of us get to enjoy the informal path."

"The rest of you get honesty," Mohit said. "Which is more than many labs provide."

Leela thought of the Baltimore hotel room, of June standing over a stolen deck with their figures in it, both of them too angry to sit. Honesty. The word made her want to laugh and then choke on it.

Ben, still trying to recover usefulness, said, "Is there a timeline for deciding? Just so people can plan deliverables."

Mohit nodded approvingly. "By February. Earlier if someone gives me a reason not to wait. And before anyone says fairness, let me be clear: I'm not pitting you against each other. The institution already did that. I'm refusing to hide it under fake family language."

That nearly would have been persuasive if Leela had not seen the pleasure he took in the cleaned-up room. Scarcity might have been real. He was real too.

He turned to Omar. "Your robustness note is closest to travel-legible, if we can tighten the comparison story and get the claims into a form reviewers won't bounce for sloppiness. There is interest there. The sponsor conversations in Baltimore made that very clear."

Omar gave a small nod that was not gratitude so much as submission dressed up to pass inspection.

Mohit looked at June next. "You need recovery and visible steadiness. Not a dramatic swing. Clean benchmark work, clean language, no more avoidable spectacle."

June unwrapped a second piece of gum and put it in her mouth on top of the first. "Love the pastoral tone."

"You can sneer or you can listen," Mohit said. His voice stayed gentle, which made the line hit harder. "I would recommend listening."

Then to Leela. "For you, the question is whether spring reads as consolidation or drift. The Research Integrity noise does not help, even if it remains noise. So I need boring documents and finished prose, not more cleverness."

The room did not get quieter. It got more exact. Ben looked fixedly at his laptop. Omar's fingers paused on the edge of his notebook. June stopped chewing for one beat and then started again.

Leela said, "There hasn't been a finding."

"I didn't say there had," Mohit replied. "I'm talking about committee psychology. You know that."

That was the trap with him. He could say something punitive and make you sound foolish if you objected to the exact wording instead of the use.

He spent the next ten minutes turning the knife by calling it planning. Ben would help with administrative cleanup and literature synthesis. June would stabilize the benchmark branch and produce something sponsor-safe. Leela would get him a revised dissertation chapter outline by Monday and keep herself available for "cross-paper support," a phrase that meant labor without ownership. Omar would prioritize the robustness note. Mohit said the lab needed mature alignment. He said adulthood twice. He said legibility four times. Nobody touched the bagels until the meeting ended, and then Ben, perhaps because he was still young enough to be hungry through humiliation, tore one in half and ate it standing up.

Leela stayed seated after Mohit dismissed them. The chair under her felt one screw away from collapse. June pushed herself up first.

"One slot," she said, mostly to the room, mostly to herself. "Nothing says collaborative science like a lifeboat drill."

Omar put the clementine segments carefully into a line on the paper plate he still had not used. "He's not wrong about the budget."

June swung her coat on. "No. That's what makes it art."

She left before anyone could answer. Ben followed two minutes later with the tray of empty coffee cups, moving quickly, like he could still win the morning back by being useful in a hallway.

That left Leela and Omar for a few seconds in the stale onion air. Omar gathered the peel into his fist.

"Do you think he already knows?" he asked.

"About what?"

"Who gets it."

Leela looked at him. His beard line was as careful as ever, but his jaw had started at last, a small jump near the hinge every few seconds. "I think he wants us to act like he doesn't."

Omar let out a breath through his nose. It might have been a laugh if the room had been kinder. "Yes," he said. "That sounds right."

He carried his plate and peel to the trash, missed the bagel box on his way out, and came back for it, embarrassed by the miss in a way Leela would not have found embarrassing if she had not already been watching him so closely.

She gathered her laptop slowly. The semester had begun with a vocabulary lesson. One slot. One path. Honest. Mature. Legible. By the time she left the basement, she could already feel the phrases settling over the lab's uglier facts like thin snow over old salt.

Outside the basement, Keating's hallways had the overbright vacancy of the first days after break, bulletin boards full of event flyers nobody would attend, industrial carpet still damp at the edges where people had tracked slush in. The sixth-floor office window faced the lake, which had gone from black water to a hard gray plate ringed with dirty snow. The view looked expensive, almost serene, and no one in the room had time to enjoy it properly.

Leela spent an hour opening and closing the revised dissertation chapter file without revising anything. Every paragraph she touched turned back into Mohit's list. Boring documents. Finished prose. No more cleverness. Her own paper from September sat in her citation folder like a bruise she had decided was healed because looking at it directly would require admitting it was still there. She had let a robustness sentence survive that should have died. Not a fabricated result, not exactly, but a clean line sanded out of a dirtier truth. She had called it acceptable because the deadline was real, because everyone else's compromises arrived in professional fonts too, because the lab had already changed the meaning of ordinary wrongdoing. The self-exonerating story still worked on her some days. On others it sounded cheap.

At three-thirty, her phone buzzed with a calendar reminder she had set in November: pay credit card minimum by the eighteenth. She dismissed it without opening the balance. The amount existed whether she looked at it or not.

Across from her, Omar had started building figures.

He had moved his monitor to the edge of the desk so he could sit closer, shoulders tucked, one hand on the mouse, the other resting against his mouth. He had changed out of his sweater into a plain blue button-down sometime after lunch. It was rolled carefully at the forearms, not messily. Even under pressure he dressed as if sloppiness might become evidence. A mug of mint tea cooled beside his keyboard. The tea bag string was wrapped around the handle three times so it would not trail in his way.

For an hour he said almost nothing. Files opened and closed. A plot rendered, vanished, rendered again. He wrote two lines in his notebook, crossed them out so cleanly the paper almost tore, and wrote two more. At four-ten his phone lit up with an email preview before he flipped it face down.

`International Students Office: Spring funding verification reminder`

Leela saw only the subject line and wished she had not. Omar did not look up. He dragged a CSV into a plotting script, waited, saw the figure appear, and then said, very quietly, "Fuck."

It was such a rare word from him that it worked like a dropped beaker. Leela turned.

He looked at her for a second, embarrassed to have been heard, then back to the screen. "Sorry."

"What happened?"

"The interesting thing did not happen," he said.

On his monitor a bar chart sat in June's plotting template, clean colors, mean scores, little error lines. Omar's method was supposed to beat the benchmark on the corrupted-slice evaluation that Mohit had singled out. Instead the benchmark sat fractionally above it, ugly in the way small losses always were, too slight for tragedy, too clear for comfort.

"You lost by three tenths," Leela said.

"By averaged seed, yes."

He said averaged like it had personally wronged him. She got up and came around his desk before deciding whether that was kindness or interference.

June had built the plotting template months ago and hard-coded a provenance footer into the bottom margin after a week of mislabeled figures. Tiny gray text, almost invisible unless you were looking for it. Leela was looking for it before she consciously knew she was. At the bottom of Omar's plot, in eight-point font, was the source path.

`/benchmarks/corrupt/baseline_5seed_mean.csv`

Omar clicked back to the terminal and opened another file. New chart. Same colors. Different numbers. The benchmark dropped six points like it had fallen downstairs.

The footer changed.

`/benchmarks/corrupt/baseline_oldtok_smoke_seed2.csv`

Leela said, "That's June's smoke run."

Omar kept his eyes on the screen. "It's the only old-tokenizer baseline with the corruption logs still aligned."

"It trained for half the schedule."

"Yes."

"It was a debugging run."

"Yes."

She waited for an explanation that might restore the ordinary world. He gave her the technical version instead.

"The five-seed average is on the new tokenizer. My adapter was tuned on the old one because the full port broke the augmentation hooks in December. If I compare old tokenizer to new tokenizer, the methods section becomes a referendum on preprocessing instead of the actual result."

Leela leaned one hip against the desk. She could smell the mint tea going tannic. "If you compare against the smoke run and call it the baseline, the methods section becomes a lie."

His hand tightened on the mouse until the knuckles blanched. "I didn't say I would call it the baseline."

"You said nothing yet."

"Exactly."

For a moment neither of them spoke. Down the row, somebody in another cubicle laughed at a video and then immediately lowered the volume. A heating pipe knocked once behind the wall.

Omar opened the figure caption draft. The current line read:

`Comparison against reproduced benchmark on corruption slice.`

He highlighted reproduced and stared at it without deleting.

Leela said, "You can still send Mohit the wash."

He gave a short, incredulous breath. "The wash."

"What would you call it?"

"I would call it accurate."

He looked at her then, finally, all the polish gone for three naked seconds. "And then what? He just held a meeting about one slot and one path. You heard him as well as I did."

"I did."

"If this becomes a wash, it dies. If it dies, I become the person who needed special handling for a year and still couldn't produce a clean public result. That does not stay in the paper column for me. It leaks into funding, timing, every conversation where somebody decides whether delay is a small inconvenience or an actual problem."

He stopped. Started again with more control. "I'm not saying that makes this noble. I'm saying you know exactly how the lab translates softness."

The email subject line still glowed faintly through his phone screen where it lay face down. Spring funding verification reminder. Neutral language. Administrative voice. The kind of sentence that could cost a person the right to remain ordinary.

Leela thought of Mohit's look when he said timely document movement. Thought of her own September paper, the sentence she had let live because the model had looked good in every way except the one that would have ruined the abstract. Thought of the word reproduced sitting under Omar's cursor like a dare.

"You could rerun the full matched baseline," she said, because it was the clean answer and because she needed to hear whether he would take it.

Omar laughed without amusement. "On what time, on what compute, and with whose goodwill when the queue is already a week deep? By the time it finishes, the story is stale. He knows that. I know that. You know that."

That was also true. The lab had enough machines for ambition and not enough for innocence.

He deleted reproduced at last. The blank space in the caption looked worse than the word had.

Leela said nothing. She could still say smoke run. She could still say do not submit this. She could still send June a screenshot and force the matter into daylight. She imagined each consequence in order. Omar losing the first public chance Mohit had dangled all semester. Mohit calling her obstructive and unstable, the senior student who mistook methodological purity for politics. June maybe backing her, maybe not, and even if she did, what then. Leela would not get a defense date by being principled on someone else's bar chart. The sentence in her own paper sat there too, waiting to be remembered by the wrong person at the right time.

Omar saved the new figure into a folder named `jan_review_clean`.

The word clean made something cold pass through her, not in her body, in the room, like a draft from a cracked window in a building that had none.

He said, more quietly, "If you want to tell him not to use it, tell him."

Not please. Not help me. The cruelty of the moment was that he offered her the power honestly, because both of them knew what using it would mean.

Leela looked back at her own desk, at the dissertation chapter file still open to a paragraph that had not improved all day. She thought of her mother's transfer from November, already dissolved into rent, utilities, minimums. Thought of the stupid travel slot and the less stupid defense path. Thought of Mohit's talent for making every moral choice arrive already indexed to somebody else's institutional vulnerability.

"I've got my own work," she said.

It was not an answer to the question he had asked. It was worse. It let the question stand and refused to carry it.

Omar's face changed by almost nothing. He nodded once. "Of course."

She went back to her desk. Behind her, he typed. The keys sounded faster now. After a minute he opened the methods file and began filling the blank where reproduced had been.

June came in around six with a paper sack of fries going soft in grease and cold air. She tossed half the sack onto Leela's desk without asking and stood at Omar's shoulder long enough to clock that he was still on the robustness note.

"Still courting reviewers?" she said.

"Something like that," Omar replied.

She looked at the figure on his screen, then at the bag of fries, deciding which deserved her energy. "Don't use the default blue on the bars. It looks like a bank ad."

Omar changed the color. June took two fries, winced at the temperature, and went downstairs to run a benchmark job before Leela could decide whether relief counted as cowardice.

By eight-thirty the office had thinned to the people who could not afford to leave. Snow along the window ledge reflected parking-lot light back into the glass. Ben came up once to ask if anybody wanted vending-machine pretzels, too anxious to eat his own offer. Omar said no. Leela said nothing. Ben hovered long enough to notice the charged quiet, then retreated with his jacket crackling around him.

At ten, Omar sent the draft to Mohit.

Leela saw the subject line come across the lab thread two minutes later.

`Updated comparison story for morning review`

No emoji, no apology, no qualifier. Just story. She stared at the word until it stopped looking like English.

The morning review happened back in the basement because Mohit liked the lab more when everyone was forced into the same air. Snowmelt had dried into dirty crescents on the concrete by the door. Somebody had wiped the conference table badly enough that it smelled faintly of lemon cleaner over the usual coffee and fan heat. The bagel box was gone. In its place sat a plastic tub of mini muffins from a grocery store bakery, each one in a paper cup the color of old bruises.

Mohit stood at the far end of the table with his ceramic mug and an expression of brisk gratitude, as if the lab had rallied in exactly the way he had hoped. Ben had already connected the monitor and was clicking too quickly through windows, apologizing every time the wrong one appeared. His hair was still damp from a shower. He had either woken up early to perform competence or had not slept at all.

June came in last with a knit cap pulled low and a strip of nicotine gum tucked into the sleeve of her coat. She took one muffin, peeled the paper off it in perfect clean strips, and did not eat it.

"Let's keep this tight," Mohit said. "I want to lock the narrative before the week fragments."

Leela sat halfway down the table, close enough to the monitor to read the small things, far enough from Mohit to avoid having to watch his face continuously. Omar plugged in his laptop. His shirt today was white. Too white for January. He had shaved his beard line even closer than usual. When he clicked to the figure, the room's attention sharpened around it the way people pretended numbers deserved.

The chart looked excellent. Not beautiful. Better. It looked clean.

Omar spoke without rushing. "This is the corruption-slice comparison after aligning preprocessing with the adapter configuration. Under that matched setup, the intervention remains ahead across all reported categories, with the largest gain in the high-noise bucket."

June's chewing stopped. Leela watched her eyes move, not across the bars, but to the bottom edge of the slide.

There it was. June's tiny gray provenance footer, the habit of a woman who trusted tools more than people.

`/benchmarks/corrupt/baseline_oldtok_smoke_seed2.csv`

June said, "Why is the smoke run in the footer?"

Ben's hands froze over the keyboard. Omar looked at the slide as if he had not expected it to confess on him.

"It's the only old-tokenizer baseline with aligned corruption logging," he said. "The release mean is on the December preprocessing stack."

June turned to him. "It's also the half-trained debug run from when the memory leak was chewing the A6000s."

"Yes."

"And you're calling that the matched comparison."

"It's matched on tokenizer and logging."

"It's not matched on training schedule."

Mohit lifted one hand, not interrupting yet, just placing process on the table. "Let's separate substantive disagreement from nomenclature."

June gave him a flat look. "That's a nice trick. No."

Omar said, more tightly now, "I can specify the training distinction in the methods."

June tapped the untouched muffin paper against the table. "You can specify it in neon if you want. It's still the weak run."

The old room would have let the sentence stand there and forced somebody to decide whether truth was more important than comfort. The new room waited for hierarchy. Leela could feel it. Ben was staring at her screen as if somebody senior might generate the correct moral vocabulary if he stayed still enough. Mohit turned his head the slightest amount toward her, an invitation shaped exactly like a request for expertise.

"Leela," he said, "technically speaking, is there a clean way to scope this comparison without overclaiming it?"

He had handed her a door and called it neutral.

June looked at her then, not pleading, not warning, just looking. That was worse. Omar did not look at her at all. His attention was fixed on a point just to the right of the monitor, the way people stared when direct gaze might make them shatter their own composure.

In Leela's mouth, the only honest sentence tasted useless. That is a debug run and you know it. The next honest sentence was worse. I have seen this before and kept going. She thought of her own paper. Thought of the line Mohit could pull out later if he needed another story about slippage, about judgment, about senior students who wanted sympathy for compromises they only objected to when somebody else made them. Thought of Omar's phone lighting with that soft administrative reminder. Thought of what June's vindication would purchase, which might have been satisfaction and not much else.

She heard herself say, "If the caption says matched old-tokenizer baseline and the methods state that the available aligned run used a shortened training schedule, then the comparison is scoped. Not ideal, but scoped."

The room changed around the sentence. She felt it happen. June's face did not move much, but the angle of her shoulders shifted, the kind of small adjustment people made when a weight they had not wanted to carry got handed somewhere else and still did not disappear. Ben started typing again immediately, relieved by the existence of a procedural verb. Scoped. Mohit nodded as if clarity had emerged organically from good-faith discussion.

"Good," he said. "That is the right level of precision."

June barked out one humorless laugh. "Precision."

Mohit ignored it. "Then let us be precise. We're not claiming a universal benchmark win. We're claiming that under the matched preprocessing configuration, the intervention outperforms the available aligned comparator on the corruption slice. That is narrower, cleaner, and much harder for a reviewer to swat away as sloppiness."

He said cleaner without irony. Ben, taking dictation from power, repeated it under his breath while editing the slide deck.

Omar sat very still. "I can add a sentence on the training schedule and archive the exact run metadata."

"Do that," Mohit said. "And make sure the repo naming is less chatty before submission. Nobody needs internal folklore in a public path."

There was the laugh the room might have had in another life. Nobody took it. The file name remained there on the slide, smoke run and all, until Ben updated the export.

June tore the muffin in half and finally ate a bite. "If you're going to use it," she said to no one and everyone, "at least stop calling the thing a reproduced benchmark. That's bullshit."

Ben said, "I can change it to matched baseline."

"Old-tokenizer matched baseline," Leela said.

Her own voice startled her. Not because it sounded guilty. Because it sounded practiced.

Ben nodded too hard. "Right. Old-tokenizer matched baseline."

June looked at Leela for one second longer than the room required, then leaned forward, put two fingers on Omar's laptop, and moved the cursor to the y-axis label.

"Also your font is too small," she said. "If we're doing this, at least don't make it ugly."

That was how the agreement happened. Not with consent. With labor.

Ben fixed the caption. June increased the axis font and darkened the error bars because she could not bear a sloppy figure even when she hated the story it told. Omar opened the methods file and added the sentence about shortened training schedule in language careful enough to sound transparent and not careful enough to undo the effect. Mohit refined the abstract line so the claim narrowed without losing lift. Leela watched the document populate with euphemism and exactitude, the two oldest academic substances, and then opened the Overleaf draft when Ben could not find the right place for the caption update.

She typed:

`Comparison against an old-tokenizer matched baseline on the corruption slice; the available aligned baseline run used a shortened training schedule, so results should be interpreted as configuration-specific rather than global benchmark replacement.`

It was accurate enough to survive expert scrutiny and inaccurate enough to do the job. A sentence built the way institutional shelter was built: just enough truth in the beams that the whole thing could stand.

"Yeah," Ben said, reading over her shoulder. "That reads a lot better."

June said nothing. Omar said, "Thank you," so quietly that it might have been intended for the screen.

Mohit took his mug in both hands and looked around the table with the composed satisfaction of a man who mistook other people's trapped choices for mentorship working. "This," he said, "is what maturity looks like. Not purity. Not melodrama. Constraint management."

June peeled another strip of paper off the muffin cup until it came away in ragged pieces. "You should embroider it on a pillow."

Mohit smiled. "If it gets the paper out the door."

He moved on to other tasks after that, but the meeting had already decided the chapter of the semester that mattered. Travel remained one slot. The defense path remained one path. Nothing had become fairer. The only thing that had changed was that the lab now had a new small public lie it had made together, with enough fingerprints on it that no one could point cleanly from outside the circle.

When the review broke, Ben stayed behind to push the updated figures to the shared folder. He renamed the directory `submission_clean_v2` and then, perhaps sensing what the word clean had started to sound like in the room, changed it to `submission_rev`. June took her coat off the back of the chair and left without her muffin. Omar lingered just long enough to close every window on his laptop before he stood, as if open tabs could testify. Mohit carried his ceramic mug upstairs, where the real cups lived.

Leela remained at the table with the updated draft on her screen. The basement sounded normal again. Fans, fluorescent buzz, somebody in the far room laughing too loudly at nothing. She put her hands flat on the laminate and felt the tacky residue the lemon cleaner had left. Across the table, June's half-peeled muffin paper sat like evidence from a very small crime.

June came back for her gum wrapper. She saw Leela still there and stopped.

For a second Leela thought she might say it. Any version of it. That was a smoke run. You know it. Don't make me do this with you. Instead June picked the wrapper up, rolled it into a hard silver bead between finger and thumb, and said, "Your clause is doing a lot of lifting."

Leela looked at the sentence on the screen. "That was the point."

"Yeah." June tucked the bead into her pocket. "I know."

She left. No door slam, no clean accusation, no absolution. Just the small mechanical thud of the basement door and the hum rising to fill the space she had taken with her.

Leela saved the file, synced the folder, and closed the laptop. In the shared drive the paper sat under its revised name with the new caption locked in place, available to all of them, owned by all of them, deniable by none of them in any way that would matter. Nobody had said the word dishonest. They had used matched, scoped, clean, precise, mature. By noon those words belonged to the result, and because they belonged to the result, they belonged to the lab.
