# Chapter 5

The basement seminar room already smelled wrong for October: hot projector dust, grocery-store fruit that had gone mealy in its plastic tray, and coffee cooked down to a dark medicinal syrup on the warmer plate. The Civic Signals Lab called this lunch. When faculty were coming, Mohit upgraded the humiliation budget. There were real plates today, thin white ones borrowed from somewhere upstairs, and a pink box from the bakery near the lake with cardamom knots nobody in the lab ever bought for themselves.

Leela stood in the doorway for a second longer than she needed to, balancing her laptop against her hip and watching June test the clicker at the podium. June had cut her hair again since September, blunt and uneven in the back where she'd clearly done it in her bathroom mirror, and she was already working through a pack of nicotine gum hard enough to show in her jaw. She had on gray work pants, black boots still dusted at the welt with sidewalk salt left over from last winter, and the faded navy overshirt she wore when she wanted to look like a person who fixed things instead of defended them.

June clicked forward, back, forward again. "If this remote dies in front of outsiders," she said without looking up, "I'm setting Keating on fire."

Her tone was light enough for anybody passing in the hall. Leela heard the steel under it anyway.

"I brought my laptop too," Leela said. "We can mirror from mine if the adapter gets weird."

"Great. Love redundancy. Love a field where we can build multimillion-dollar models but not reliable HDMI."

Leela set her bag against the side wall near the extra folding chairs. The room had no windows, only a strip of dusty transom glass over the hall door that admitted a weak institutional brightness. Someone had wiped the table and left damp arcs behind. The whiteboard still carried three months of ghost equations and a half-erased shopping list from some lab dinner long enough ago to feel archaeological: cilantro, onions, sparkling water, batteries.

June was running on speed and contempt, which for her counted as composure.

"Did Mohit say who was coming?" Leela asked.

"Two faculty from stats, one from policy, a postdoc from Data Futures, three first-years who heard there'd be food, and whatever bored souls drift in if the smell gets under the door." June finally looked at her. "Also Omar sent me an email at one fourteen asking if my thread IDs were stable across scrape versions, which isn't a thing a man asks at one fourteen unless God has shown him a knife."

The line should have made Leela smile. It almost did. Instead she looked toward the back of the room where Omar had put his messenger bag on a chair and was lining up a printed copy of June's slides with the straight, careful movements he used when he was trying not to show that his hands wanted to do something else. He was neatly dressed as ever, navy button-down, dark trousers, beard line exact enough to look freshly repaired. One cuff was damp where his coffee had sloshed over the lid seam. He kept smoothing the wet crescent with his thumb, trying to erase the spill by friction alone.

He didn't look at June. He didn't look at Leela either.

"What did you tell him?" Leela asked.

"That if he wanted stable IDs he could pray for them like the rest of us." June clicked to her title slide, frowned, adjusted one line break, clicked back. "He sent 'Thanks' with a period. That's never good."

Leela had been having versions of the same feeling since she woke up: not fear exactly, because fear had edges, but a broad low pressure spreading under the day. Mohit had sent three messages since eight. June had sent none since the night before, which felt worse. Need a strong room. Faculty may join early. Keep this as a working seminar. He had never once in six years used the phrase working seminar when he meant anything kind.

"You can still tell him no questions till the end," Leela said.

June laughed once through her nose. "Sure. I'll tell him no, and then he'll tell me yes in a more professional font."

The door opened behind them and Ben came in sideways, shoulder first, because he was carrying both the short HDMI cable and the long one with the grim ceremony of somebody returning from a rescue mission. His puffer jacket was still on despite the basement heat, expensive enough to shine a little at the seams. He had a sleeve of paper cups tucked under his chin and the pink bakery box balanced on top of them. A crescent of dry skin showed where he'd worried one cuticle bloody.

"I got backup cables too," he said. "And napkins, because the department kitchen only had the weird compostable forks that snap if you look at them."

"Our infrastructure emperor," June said.

Ben grinned, relieved by the joke before he'd fully earned it. "I also grabbed the extension cord from 312 because theirs is literally industrial grade, so if anything fails now it isn't on me."

"Noted," Leela said.

He set the bakery box down with too much ceremony, centered it, then nudged it again until the label faced outward. The little act of hospitality made Leela feel worse. Ben always looked happiest when he got to assist in a scene that mattered. He mistook proximity for innocence.

Mohit arrived a minute later carrying his leather folder and a stainless travel mug he never left anywhere long enough for someone else to touch. His sleeves were rolled to identical heights below the elbow. He wore the expensive glasses that made him look, from a distance, like a man whose taste had simply improved with age rather than a man who had started making money somewhere he had not mentioned. He took in the room in one sweep: food table, projector, faculty chairs, students. Then he smiled the smile that let everyone choose their own explanation for why he was smiling.

"Excellent," he said. "This looks good."

June clicked once more through her opening slide and said, "I'd like to keep questions to the end if we can. I can move faster that way."

Mohit's smile held. "Let's see how the room develops. We have a fuller audience than usual, and I want to make sure the discussion is useful."

"It'll be useful at the end too."

"Of course." He set down the folder, not the mug. "I just don't want us performing the usual artificial distinction between presentation and feedback when the whole point is to pressure-test. Omar has done some adjacent analysis that may be helpful to surface in real time."

There it was, almost polite enough to miss if you had not spent years watching him rename force as process.

June stilled with one hand on the clicker. "Adjacent how?"

Mohit looked at Omar for half a second and then back to June. "Related failure modes in benchmark construction. It may sharpen the conversation."

June's face did a small hard thing and then smoothed over it. "Great. Love sharpening."

The first faculty arrived while nobody answered that. A statistics professor Leela knew only as Professor Adler came in with a bike helmet clipped to her tote and one reflective ankle strap still fastened over her slacks; she'd clearly biked in too fast to finish becoming office-shaped. Behind her was a policy professor who always smelled faintly of clove cigarettes though Leela had never seen him smoke, and a postdoc from another lab with a baby sticker on her water bottle and the raw red eyes of somebody losing an argument with sleep. They greeted Mohit. They nodded at June. They took plates and did what invited faculty always did in basement rooms: accepted food first, so later they could tell themselves they had made the event feel collegial.

By noon every chair was taken except the one by the humming A/V rack that nobody wanted because the fan blasted warm dust at face height. Two first-years leaned against the back wall with cookies in napkins. Ben had claimed the side table nearest Mohit and opened his laptop at an angle that looked like attentiveness from every direction. Omar sat in the second row, not beside Leela, not beside June's empty backpack in the front, but exactly far enough away from both to imply accident.

Leela took a seat on the aisle where June could see her. She told herself the seat meant solidarity. It also gave Mohit a clean line to her if he decided to bring her in, which she knew he might, which she should have taken as a reason to sit in the back and did not.

Mohit made the introduction himself. He used the full lab title, the grant language, the line about civic applications. He praised June's rigor, her systems intelligence, the unusual ambition of her benchmark suite. By the end of thirty seconds of praise Leela could feel the room already being arranged. Mohit only became lavish in public when the lavishness could later be converted into plausible innocence.

"June has been building an evaluation benchmark for policy-evasion detection in local civic forums," he said. "The problem, as many of you know, is that models often look robust until users become even slightly inventive. So today you'll hear about a benchmark designed to capture that drift under realistic linguistic pressure."

He stepped aside. "June."

June stood, took one sip from her paper cup, and put it down too hard. "Hi. Thanks for coming to our basement while the weather is still pretending not to hate us."

A small, grateful laugh moved through the room.

"Short version," she said, clicking to her first content slide. "A moderation model can score beautifully on a polished benchmark and still eat shit the second a real person decides to misspell on purpose. My project is a benchmark for that gap. Local Facebook groups, school-board forums, municipal chat boards, places where people talk ugly and sloppy and like they know exactly how much the platform can and can't catch."

Leela had seen the slides twice already, once at June's apartment in fragments over takeout containers, once alone as a PDF June had sent after midnight with the subject line if you have ten minutes please tell me slide 9 doesn't make me look illiterate. In the room they played differently. June was good at this. Not warm, exactly, but alive in a way the rest of them rarely were once a projector turned on. She moved with the clicker like she was cutting wire. She did not inflate the technical language or apologize for it. When she said thread-level annotations, she meant it. When she said civic euphemism, she had examples from real moderation failures that made the faculty shift forward in their chairs despite themselves.

She took them through the data collection pipeline, the annotation scheme, the taxonomy of evasive language. She showed screenshots with usernames blacked out and little red boxes around phrases that had slipped standard moderation. school boad. clean-up operation. just asking questions about vote math. She had built the examples well enough that even the policy professor looked briefly embarrassed by how easy the games were.

"The benchmark matters," June said, "because people don't announce they're gaming your model in the nice clean syntax your training set prefers. They talk like their cousin's in the thread, and they steal each other's phrasing, and they half-joke their way around policy language. If your system can't survive that, it doesn't matter how elegant the ROC curve looks in a paper."

Leela watched the room lean in and felt, against her will, a flare of pride that made everything worse. June should have been allowed the ordinary vanity of being good at her own seminar. It was not much to ask from academia, which was why it had become impossible.

June clicked to a results table and began walking through the core finding: every standard model degraded under paraphrase and local euphemism, but the degradation was sharpest exactly where deployment rhetoric was most confident. The benchmark did not only lower scores. It reordered the leaderboard. Models that bragged about safety turned out to be brittle as sugar glass.

She was three slides from her limitations section when Mohit lifted two fingers from his mug.

"Can we pause here?" he asked. The question was pure theater. He did not wait for permission. "I think this is the right moment to widen the lens slightly. Omar, do you want to share what you found when you stress-tested the split assumptions?"

No one moved. The postdoc with the baby-sticker bottle stopped halfway through unscrewing the cap. Professor Adler's reflective ankle strap flashed once under the table as she crossed and uncrossed her feet. Two of the first-years lowered their cookies without seeming to know they were doing it.

June stayed facing the screen for a beat too long. When she turned back, her expression was still almost neutral. "I'd rather finish the main result first."

"It is the main result," Mohit said, smiling just enough to make the sentence sound generous. "Or at least one important framing question around it."

Papers shifted. Somebody's compostable fork snapped against a plate near the back. The postdoc finally recapped her bottle without drinking. Ben sat very still in a posture that managed to look eager and frightened at once.

Omar had already taken out his printout. He did not rustle the pages. He held them with the same care he used at the grocery store, the bruised-peach care of a man who handled damaged things gently even when it changed nothing.

"Sure," he said.

His voice was even. That made it crueler, though Leela knew cruelty was not the whole truth of it.

"I reran the benchmark using thread-level grouping and near-duplicate hashing across scrape versions," he said. "The issue isn't the premise. The issue is that the current split lets the model see too much of the same social fabric on both sides."

June crossed one arm over her waist. "Define too much."

Omar looked at the page, then up. "Posts from the same neighborhood thread appear in train and test. In several cases, posts from the same user do as well under variant usernames. When I grouped by source thread and removed character-level near duplicates, the headline robustness result contracted substantially."

"Substantially" was a courthouse word in his mouth. Leela felt it land around the room.

June said, "Numbers."

Omar turned the printout so the nearest row could see. "Your best condition falls from an eleven-point improvement over the baseline to under two. The gap is inside the confidence interval once the thread holdout is clean. More importantly, the benchmark may be measuring recognition of recurring local idiom and author signature rather than generalization to unseen evasive forms."

He paused. He was trying, Leela realized, to keep his language from sounding like a knife. The attempt only highlighted where the blade already was.

"For example," he said, "the model isn't necessarily learning civic policy evasion in the abstract. It may simply be learning that one account in Nassau County always writes school board as skool boad with the same missing letters. That's a real social pattern. It's not the same claim as robust transfer."

Somebody at the back gave a low involuntary whistle and then pretended it had been a cough.

June's laugh this time was one dry exhale. "Okay. Several things. One, yes, people in local groups repeat themselves. That's why they're local groups and not weather systems. Two, the benchmark is supposed to preserve community texture. If you sterilize it into isolated single posts, you break the object."

"I agree," Omar said. "That is why I grouped by thread, not by post. The community texture stays. The leakage changes."

"Leakage is your word."

"No," Mohit said softly from his chair. "I think it's a fair methodological word."

There it was: not only allowed, but ratified.

June looked at Mohit then, properly looked at him, and something went bare in her face before she got hold of it.

"Great," she said. "Then let's use the fair methodological word. The thread-held-out reruns were already in progress. They aren't in the deck because the cluster has been backed up for weeks."

Leela felt the sentence hit her like a thrown object. June did not look at her when she said it. She did not need to.

Mohit spread one hand. "And that's useful context. At the same time, if the current headline depends on an uncleared split assumption, then I think the room deserves to understand that now rather than later."

The whole trick sat inside deserved. The room became the injured party; June became the one who owed it an explanation. Public embarrassment passed for a responsible release schedule.

Professor Adler cleared her throat. "Was the thread-level grouping planned from the start, or is that a response to this critique?"

June answered before Omar could. "Planned. I've got notes from August on it. The random split was a first pass to get error surfaces and annotation sanity."

"But the slides show the random split as the primary result," Adler said.

"Because I don't, in fact, control the laws of time."

That got a laugh from one of the first-years, quick and guilty. Nobody else joined.

Omar leaned forward, elbows on knees now, the pages hanging between his hands. "June, I'm not saying the work fails. I'm saying the central claim needs to narrow. If you present this as robust transfer to unseen euphemism, it overstates what the current experiment shows."

June snapped her gum once and took it out of her mouth, folding it into a napkin with two sharp fingers. The little domestic motion in the middle of the bloodletting did something awful to Leela. It made June look less like a speaker and more like a worker being asked to clean up while she was still being hit.

"You could've sent me that in an email," June said.

Omar's jaw tightened. Fear showed there first on him, exactly where the continuity of his face started to break. "I did ask about the thread IDs."

"At one in the morning, like a man leaving a bomb under a cake."

"June," Mohit said, still soft. "Let's not dramatize. This is what an internal seminar is for."

Ben typed something with intense concentration, then stopped typing when he seemed to realize his screen could be seen from the side.

Leela could hear the server closet through the wall, a constant fan-whine rising and falling like breath through teeth. She knew, all at once and with a clarity so complete it felt stupid that she had not known it earlier, exactly how Mohit had built the room. He had told Omar his adversarial work was anonymized and ordinary. He had told June this was her seminar. He had told the faculty they were coming to see strong work under honest pressure. He had told Leela, by omission, that if she sat in the second row and behaved, she might make it through the hour without being used.

He had not needed to lie perfectly. He only needed each person to arrive with a different script.

June clicked back to the results table, reclaiming the slide the only way she had left: by putting her hand on it. "Fine. Here's the context the room deserves, then. The benchmark still shows that local euphemism and thread familiarity break standard moderation models. The cleaner split hurts the pretty number. It doesn't reverse the failure mode. If you want a benchmark that ignores community carryover entirely, that's a different benchmark, and maybe a cleaner one for some papers, but it's not this one."

She was good even now. Too good, maybe; the room could use her composure against her. The fact that she was still capable of a sharp answer let everybody keep pretending the terms were fair.

Mohit tipped his head toward Leela.

"Leela, you've been closest to the infrastructure side and to the reproducibility bottlenecks this month," he said. "How would you characterize Omar's concern? Is this a limitation to flag, or does it materially alter the claim?"

He might as well have stood up and put his hand between her shoulder blades.

Every bad option arrived at once. If she backed June cleanly, Mohit could later say she was defending a result she knew was unstable. If she backed Omar, she helped nail June to the wall with work June had not been given time to finish. If she tried to split the difference, the split itself would be the wound, because a room like this always heard nuance in the direction of power.

June turned to look at her. Not pleading. That would have been easier. It was the opposite of pleading. It was the look you gave somebody whose intelligence you had already counted on.

Leela became intensely aware of the wrist brace under her sleeve, the edge of the Velcro rough against the thin skin near her thumb. She could feel the heat from the A/V fan across the aisle on one cheek. A drop of coffee had dried tacky on the side of her paper cup and glued itself to two fingers.

She knew what she could say. She could say June had been rerunning the clean split already. She could say compute allocation had been a mess and the room was treating an intermediate version like a final claim. She could say the critique was valid and the ambush was not. She could say any one of those things.

Instead she heard herself choosing the sentence that sounded most defensible from a future committee room.

"I think," she said, and hated how careful she sounded, "if the thread-level overlap is doing what Omar says, then the transfer claim probably needs to be narrower right now. I don't think it kills the benchmark, but it isn't a footnote either."

Silence.

Not a dramatic silence. Worse. The ordinary, administrative silence of people hearing a question settle.

June looked back at the screen. The side of her face had gone flat in a way that made her look older. Not wiser. Just more expensive to damage.

"Great," she said after a second. "That's useful. Anybody else want a turn with the knife before I keep going?"

Nobody spoke.

Mohit folded his hands. "Let's keep the tone productive."

June gave him a smile so brief it barely counted as visible. "Oh, absolutely."

The policy professor raised two fingers, cookie still in hand. "If the cleaner split is the one you'd trust in deployment, what survives of the claim now?"

June kept one hand on the podium. "The part where the models still fail on local euphemism. What gets smaller is the transfer claim. I stop saying robust transfer to unseen evasive language and say the benchmark is stronger for within-community failure under a clean thread holdout."

"And the baseline?" Professor Adler asked, already writing.

"It looks less embarrassing once you stop letting it peek at the same PTA mothers twice."

One of the first-years laughed before he could stop himself. Nobody else joined him. Professor Adler's pen kept moving.

"Would you submit it that way," she asked, "with the headline narrowed?"

"I'd submit the narrower claim with the reruns attached," June said. "I'd prefer not to workshop the distinction over pastries, but here we are."

Omar leaned forward a fraction. "I think that would be a stronger paper."

June nodded once without looking at him. "Thank you for the live developmental edit."

The policy professor said, "So the annotation scheme still holds, but the generalization claim has to be fenced."

"Yes," June said. Her voice had gone lower each time she'd answered, until what remained was a service-worker briskness so stripped down it sounded almost polite. "The label distribution doesn't collapse. The benchmark doesn't collapse. The bragging rights collapse."

That got a quick, guilty sound from the back that might have been a laugh and might have been somebody choking on coffee. Leela wanted to stand up and throw something at all of them. They were grateful to June for making the violence manageable. A funny victim reduced paperwork.

Mohit looked at the clock and said, "Why don't we take ninety seconds while June pulls up the appendix with the split notes. Then she can finish the architecture section and we can return at the end if needed."

It sounded gracious. It also sounded like a man granting a bathroom break.

June set the clicker down and unplugged her laptop with movements so precise they had gone past calm into something else. "Sure," she said. "Ninety whole seconds. Luxury."

She stepped out into the hallway carrying the laptop against her chest. The hall outside the seminar room was colder and smelled of floor cleaner and old radiator metal. Somebody had left a rolling cart of recycling bins near the vending machine. One fluorescent tube above the copy room door buzzed louder than the others, then dimmed, then came back.

Leela followed before she'd decided what she meant to say.

June had already put the laptop on the wide windowsill beside the stairwell door, though the window only faced another interior hall. She was tapping through folders too hard, missing once, swearing under her breath.

"June," Leela said.

"Don't."

Her voice was quiet now. That was worse than if she'd shouted. June only got this quiet when the joke budget was gone.

Leela stopped two feet away. "He's making it bigger than it is."

June laughed once, a sound with no amusement in it at all. "You helped."

"I wasn't trying to."

"I know. That's the amazing part."

From the room behind them came the soft surge of people standing, the paper-plate noise of second cookies being taken under cover of discussion. Mohit's voice floated through the door, genial, already reframing. Wonderful energy in the room. Exactly the kind of honest exchange we want.

Leela said, "The issue is real. I couldn't pretend it wasn't."

June turned then. Up close, she looked less angry than Leela had expected and more injured, which was harder to meet. There was a faint wet mark on the front of her overshirt where the paper cup had touched it. Her left hand still held the wad of napkin with the dead piece of gum inside.

"You had one sentence," June said. "Not a speech. One sentence. 'She's already rerunning the clean split.' That's all. Then it's a seminar and not an execution."

Leela opened her mouth and found nothing in it she could stand behind.

June shook her head, not dramatically, almost tired. "I know what the flaw is. I know Omar's not making it up. I know the slide should've been cleaner. What I didn't know, till just now, was that you'd sit there and help him make me look dishonest because that version was safer for you."

"That's not fair."

June's eyes went wide for the first time. Not in outrage. In disbelief. "No? What's the fair version, Leela? That you froze in exactly the direction that protects you most? That's not unfair. That's basically a campus tradition."

The sentence should have hit like sarcasm. It landed like a report.

Leela could feel the old impulse rising, the one that always made her start adding clauses until maybe one of them contained a shelter. "I was trying to say the benchmark still holds, just more narrowly. I thought if I overstated the defense he'd push harder, and then it would get worse, and I didn't know if the room would believe me if I said the reruns were underway because-"

"Because you had the machines."

That one she did not deny.

June looked down at the laptop, clicked into the appendix folder, found the file she wanted. When she spoke again her voice had flattened almost completely.

"I asked for two nights on Hades," she said. "Not your kidney. Two nights. You left me on read and then you told the room the flaw wasn't a footnote."

The vending machine compressor kicked on beside them with a shudder. Somebody laughed inside the seminar room, too loud, the laugh of a person performing that the event remained normal.

Leela said, "I'm sorry."

June tucked the napkin into her pocket. "That's nice. It would have been nicer two minutes ago."

Mohit opened the seminar-room door halfway and leaned out with his mug still in hand, wearing the mild face of a man checking on late catering.

"You good?" he asked June. "No rush, but let's land the rest while we have the faculty."

June looked at him long enough that Leela thought, absurdly, maybe she would throw the laptop at his face. Instead she smiled with every part of her mouth that wasn't feeling.

"Absolutely," she said. "Wouldn't want to waste the faculty."

Mohit nodded once, satisfied by the word and not the tone, and disappeared back into the room.

June picked up the laptop. For a second she held it low against her stomach the way somebody might hold an ice pack to a bruise they didn't want to show. Then she straightened.

Leela said her name again, softly this time, because she had not earned anything louder.

June did not look at her. "Watch and learn," she said.

Then she went back in.

The room had resettled itself while she was out. People were back in their chairs with the obscene efficiency of academics who believed sitting down again restored innocence. Someone had opened the bakery box all the way. Half the cardamom knots were gone. Professor Adler had her pen ready. Ben had angled his laptop farther from the aisle.

June plugged in, pulled up the appendix, and stood at the podium under the fluorescent panels that flattened everyone's skin to the color of old paper. Leela stayed by the door for a beat before forcing herself back to her seat. The chair scraped louder than it should have. Nobody turned.

June put both hands on either side of the podium, not gripping, just placing them there like measurements.

"Okay," she said.

The word came out lower than before, stripped clean.

"Appendix first, since we're apparently doing surgery before triage. These are the thread-grouping notes from August. This is the author-linking mess. This is where the cleaner split changes the score, and this is where it doesn't. After that I'll finish the architecture and the deployment cases, because those still exist."

She clicked to the appendix and brought up the dated notes, the hashed user clusters, the ugly yellow cells where the held-out numbers sank. Nobody interrupted now. They watched her narrow her own claim in public. Professor Adler wrote without looking up. The policy professor had turned his plate upside down on his folder so the grease wouldn't get on the paper. Omar sat bent over his printout, staring at it hard enough to suggest the sheet itself might clear him.

"When the split is clean," June said, touching the laser dot to the table, "the improvement is modest and local. Fine. Then the honest claim is local. The benchmark still tells you something useful. It just doesn't get to swagger."

A chair leg screeched at the back. One of the first-years slipped out with half a cardamom knot wrapped in a napkin, trying for stealth and failing. Ben had started typing again, no longer pretending not to.

June clicked back to the architecture slide. "Model stack is the same. Thread context. Author-link heuristics. Error slices by euphemism family. None of that vanishes because the cleaner split takes a bat to the pretty number."

Halfway through the next sentence her voice caught. She cleared it and kept going. On the second catch, the postdoc with the baby-sticker bottle stood, crossed the aisle, and set the bottle beside June's paper cup without a word.

The whole room saw it.

June stopped for exactly one beat, nodded once without looking up, unscrewed the stranger's bottle with her free hand, and drank. Then she braced her left hand on the lectern and kept explaining the architecture while Professor Adler wrote down the smaller claim as June's headline all along.
