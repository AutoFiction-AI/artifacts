# Chapter 2

By the second flight of June's stairs, the beer cans in Leela's tote were knocking together hard enough to bruise her leg through her jeans. The hallway held the usual Alder Street mixture of old oil, damp plaster, and whatever somebody downstairs had decided could count as incense. Outside, the immigrant strip was still loud in the late-summer heat: buses gasping at the curb, somebody dragging metal shutters halfway down, bass leaking from a car that had been parked under the no-standing sign for most of the year. June had texted an hour earlier: `beer if possible. something acidic if you're feeling rich.` Leela had bought six cheap lagers, two limes, and a bunch of cilantro that already smelled overcommitted.

Tuesday dinner had begun two years earlier because everybody kept ending seminar hungry and Mohit had once made a whole performance out of community before he got bored of paying for dumplings. June's apartment took over after that. It was smaller than the lab but warmer in all the ways that mattered. People sat on the floor. Somebody always chopped something. If the evening went well, the room let them be stupid for an hour without penalty.

Leela wanted the room to do that again. She wanted its folding table and dented rice cooker and the absurd confidence of June's little kitchen to tell her Mohit's new brief had not, in fact, changed the species of their lives overnight.

June opened the door with her shoulder because both hands were occupied: one with a wooden spoon, one with a packet of nicotine gum she'd bitten open with her teeth. Her blunt black hair was clipped back with a binder clip she must have stolen from the lab, and she had on an old diner T-shirt with a cartoon coffee cup on the chest and grease marks that no wash cycle had fully defeated. Heat rolled out behind her carrying soy, garlic, onion, and the red pepper sting of something cooked past politeness.

"You're late," she said.

"I'm six minutes late."

"Exactly. Shoes off."

Leela kicked off her sneakers into the pile by the door and squeezed past the folding table, already unfolded and already in everybody's way. The apartment was the same cramped second-floor geometry it always was: one front room pretending to be living room and dining room together, one bedroom half blocked by a curtain instead of a door, a kitchen so narrow the oven and the opposite counter nearly touched knees. A box fan rattled in the window more for morale than airflow. June had wedged cookbooks under one leg of the table, and the fridge wore its usual collage of takeout menus, expired coupons, and one postcard of Lakebridge in winter so ugly it had become permanent.

Ben was already there, perched at the edge of the counter as if the apartment were a place he had not yet earned the right to lean on. His expensive navy jacket was folded very neatly over the back of June's desk chair, and his careful white sneakers sat by the door heel to heel, cleaner than anything else in the room. He had brought a bottle of orange wine and a tub of marinated olives from the expensive grocery up the hill, the kind of contribution that showed both effort and a childhood in which stores like that had not been a dare.

"I also brought bread," he said quickly, as if she might dock him points. "But June said the olives were, quote, rent marbles."

June pointed her spoon at the stove. "Because they are. Put the beer in the sink. The fridge is full of condiments pretending to be groceries."

Leela dropped the cans into a bath of cold tap water and melting ice. On the stove, chicken thighs and potatoes were collapsing together in a Dutch oven stained brick red with pepper paste. A pan of cabbage sweated down beside it with enough black pepper to make the air feel textured. The rice cooker on the floor gave off small patient puffs, like a thing refusing panic on principle.

"I brought limes," Leela said.

"Jesus. Rockefeller." June took them anyway. "Cut one. Cucumber salad needs help."

Leela found the cucumbers in the sink and the only sharp knife in the apartment. The familiar work steadied her more than the room itself had. June cooked the way she talked: with brisk contempt for unnecessary elegance. Smash, salt, vinegar, sesame oil, done. Ben had already set out mismatched bowls and was pretending not to notice that June rearranged them after he stepped back.

"Where's Omar?" Leela asked.

"Texted that the bakery line was obscene," June said. "Which is what happens when you insist on buying dessert on a Tuesday in a neighborhood with six bakeries and no self-respect."

Ben laughed too loudly, then caught himself and reached for the opener. He held the wine bottle at the angle of somebody performing competence for an audience that might or might not be hostile. Leela saw, not for the first time, how young he really was when nobody rewarded him fast enough. Twenty-four in a room built on thirty-year-old damage.

June slapped a bowl onto the table. "Ground rules. Nobody says 'adversarial robustness' before the second beer."

"What about 'deliverable'?" Ben asked.

"Immediate ejection."

"Cross-functional alignment?"

"Now you're just flirting."

Leela laughed. It came a beat late but still landed, and for a minute the room found its old rhythm. Ben tried the wine and said it tasted like cider somebody had resented. June told a story about the downstairs tenant stealing her laundry detergent one capful at a time, which she claimed was a worse crime than grand theft because it required patience. Leela described the departmental seminar speaker from that afternoon, a political scientist from Cornell who had said "trust stack" six times in fifty minutes and clicked through slides like he was apologizing to them for not having gone to business school. June did an imitation so precise it was almost mean. Ben nearly choked on a laugh. When Omar finally arrived, pink from the stairs and still neat in a blue button-down that looked ironed because he probably ironed it, he came in holding a white bakery box tied with red string and apologized for the lateness before he'd even shut the door.

"I bought plum pastries," he said. "This was apparently a municipal emergency."

"You came with dessert. You're forgiven," June said. "Shoes."

Omar set the bakery box down with the care of a person placing evidence on a table. Then he took off his loafers and lined them beside the others with a quick little adjustment until both toes matched the wall exactly. He smelled faintly of starch and street heat. When he passed Leela the stack of bowls, his cuffs were rolled to identical heights, not a millimeter off. Even exhausted, he looked as if he had decided on his own behalf that sloppiness was a luxury for citizens.

They ate shoulder to shoulder because the table was too small to permit any other social arrangement. June at the head because it was her apartment and because nobody challenged her there. Leela on the side with the bad table leg. Ben tucked between the fan and the window, his knee bumping the radiator cover whenever he forgot where he was. Omar opposite Leela, cutting his chicken carefully before mixing it into the rice.

For the first ten minutes they held the line. The food helped. June's chicken was spicy enough to bully speech into honesty and then, perversely, keep everyone quiet while they chewed. Sweat gathered at Leela's hairline. The cucumbers were cold and sharp. Outside, somebody yelled in Spanish and got an answer from half a block away. The fan chopped their voices into smaller pieces and sent them back around the table.

Ben talked about orientation week, how one of the new robotics students had already called the engineering complex "brutalist but kind of iconic" and gotten nearly booed. Omar described the undergraduates in his recitation section with the exhausted respect of a man who had already learned two names for every one of his own students: the official one and the one on the roster. June said the only correct thing to teach first-years about academia was where to cry without being found. Leela told them the vending machine on Keating three had started dispensing two peanut-butter crackers for the price of one, then ruined the joke by adding that it was probably a calibration error. June said, "See, this is why you need us. Any normal person would stop at free crackers."

Nobody had run out of things to say. Ben kept worrying his fork through the potatoes, June kept tossing jokes into the middle and yanking them back by the handle, and Omar kept drinking before he finished a thought. Mohit sat between them anyway, upholstered into every skipped clause. Even the jokes swerved around him.

June stood to get more rice, used the movement to reset the mood, and failed by half an inch. "All right," she said. "We're twenty-three minutes in. That's basically Olympic level. Who's breaking first?"

Ben looked up too fast. "Breaking what?"

"The fake normal," June said. "It's doing its best."

"I thought we weren't doing work talk."

"We're not. We're doing gossip, which is the only form of peer review left to the people."

Omar gave a small smile into his glass. Leela watched his jaw tighten and release before he drank. He had said almost nothing about the meeting in the hallway after it, only that Mohit had separately told him not to discuss certain details. At the time that had felt ominous enough. Tonight it felt like a door somebody had locked from the inside.

Ben set his fork down. "I don't know if it helps, but I think he was trying to make roles clearer."

June leaned back. "Whose roles."

"Everybody's. I mean, not like in a punitive way." He winced at his own word choice. "He just said the lab had gotten kind of... diffuse. That people were doing too much invisible labor and it was making authorship blurry."

Leela heard Mohit's syntax in the sentence, thinned out and made younger in Ben's mouth. Invisible labor. Clear roles. Authorship blurry. Mohit had a way of offering a grievance to the exact person most likely to feel flattered by it. To Ben, a first-year standing at the edge of everybody else's history, he had offered clarity as a gift.

"Did he," June said.

"He said this could be good for me, actually. Because I get exposure across projects instead of being stuck in the first-year corner forever. Like I can see how the senior work is structured."

"Exposure," June said. "Terrific. Very useful after death."

Ben smiled because he understood he was supposed to, then stopped smiling because June had not made it easy enough.

Omar wiped his fingers with a paper napkin folded twice before use. "That's interesting," he said. "Because he told me almost the opposite."

Leela's thumb stuck to the sweating beer can. The fan kept knocking a spoon against the serving bowl. June went so still that Leela set her bottle onto the old conference-badge coaster one careful inch at a time, listening to the cardboard catch and release.

"Opposite how?" she asked.

Omar looked at her, then at June, then at the chicken as if it might issue procedural guidance. "He said the problem with the lab was blur itself. That we'd become too sentimental about boundaries. If the work mattered, overlap shouldn't frighten anyone."

"That sounds like him," June said. Her tone made it impossible to tell whether she meant last week or last year.

Ben frowned. "But that still sounds like the same pitch from a different angle."

"A sentence only first-years say," June muttered.

Leela could have left it there. She felt the option in her hand like a bead of sweat sliding around the beer can. Instead she heard herself say, "He told me this was part of proving I was ready for a final year. That if I handled it maturely, it would make my timeline easier to formalize."

There. A partial truth. She had left out the ugliest part, that her approval to keep existing in the program had been hung on Omar's model like a coat on a nail. But it was enough to make June's eyes sharpen and Ben forget his fork.

June's eyes cut to her and stayed there a second too long. "Maturely," she said. "God, of course he did."

Ben looked startled, then almost guilty, as if Leela's sixth year had only now arrived in his imagination as a real calendar. Omar's face did something smaller and worse. It grew more careful.

"So for Ben it's exposure," June said, counting off on greasy fingers. "For Leela it's maturity theater. For Omar it's apparently anti-sentimental overlap. Great. Everybody gets their own little horoscope."

"It wasn't a horoscope," Ben said, then seemed to hear himself. "Sorry. That sounded stupid."

"No, keep going," June said. "I'm always interested in how my professional death looks from freshman orientation."

"I didn't mean that." He pushed his bowl away an inch, appetite shrinking in public. "I just mean maybe he uses different language with different people. Faculty do that."

"Sure," June said. "Usually to lie."

Leela could feel herself beginning to overexplain, the impulse arriving with the old panic that if she didn't sort the terms cleanly enough the emotional version would win by default. "Different language can mean targeted language," she said. "He tells each of us the version that'll go down easiest. I hate it. I'm saying I don't yet know whether this is ordinary faculty manipulation or something worse, and the difference matters."

June looked at her as if weighing whether to rescue or wound. "You hear yourself, right?"

Leela did. She hated it before the sentence even finished leaving her. She heard herself replacing the word lie with targeted language and the difference matters, stacking qualifiers until the real point disappeared. She stabbed a potato with more force than it needed.

Omar cleared his throat. June's fork stopped halfway to her mouth. "There's one more thing," he said. "I wasn't planning to mention it because I may have misunderstood. But since we're doing horoscopes."

June sat back down.

Omar's fingers worried the edge of his napkin into a tighter square. "He said the new robustness line would create room for one person to lead the first paper if they could demonstrate serious range. Not just implementation. Judgment. He said the person who identified a real failure mode cleanly would not be treated as support labor. They would own the correction."

Nobody moved.

Leela heard the word own as if it had dropped onto the table and cracked. One person. The first paper. Own the correction. Mohit had wrapped a future with only one chair in it in the language of a task.

June spoke first. "One person where."

"Lead author," Omar said. "Possibly the talk as well, if it moved quickly enough. He made it sound..." He stopped, and for a moment the carefulness broke. "He made it sound like the sort of thing that changes how people see you."

Ben stared at him. "Like an internal paper?"

"There's no such thing as an internal paper when your advisor is shopping it to everyone he has ever met," June said.

Omar's mouth tightened. "I understand that."

"Do you?" June asked, not loudly.

The spoon settled against the serving bowl with one last thin click. Leela felt the bad table leg stop jittering under her wrist and understood, a second late, that nobody was moving. Omar had not meant to show the card whole. June had not meant to react before deciding how. Ben was trying to shrink without physically moving. The chicken on Leela's plate had gone cold in the sauce.

She saw the structure all at once and not at all. If Omar had been promised leadership for finding a weakness, then weakness was not a technical fact waiting in the data. Weakness was now a door. Somebody got through; somebody became the wall.

"Did he say whose failure mode?" Leela asked.

"No."

"Then whose work are you supposed to own the correction of?" June said.

"He didn't phrase it that way."

"That's because the way I phrased it's ugly."

Ben jumped in too quickly. "Maybe he just meant there'd be one natural point person. Like for coordination. That doesn't automatically mean somebody loses."

June turned to him. "Ben, I need you to hear the sentence you just said and ask yourself whether you've ever seen a lab in this building coordinate anything without blood on the floor."

He opened his mouth, shut it, and reached for his beer instead. His ears had gone red. Leela had the brief, unwelcome impression of him at fourteen, trying to talk his way back into a lunch table.

Omar rubbed at a streak of sauce on the edge of his plate with his thumb. "I'm not saying yes to anything," he said. "I'm saying he said it."

"Those aren't separate things anymore," June said.

The cruelty of it was that June was right and wrong at once. Leela could feel both truths scraping against each other. Omar had not made the offer. He had still heard it. He had carried it here in his mouth like a stone and now set it on the table between the rice bowl and the salt.

June stood up abruptly and started stacking plates that were not yet empty. "Dessert," she said. "Because apparently we deserve plum pastries before the republic collapses."

The line was funny enough that everybody let it be funny. That was the last generosity the room managed. Omar opened the bakery box. Ben asked whether the plums were local, a question so stupid with nerves that June actually laughed for real. Leela said the limes had been criminally priced and June called that the most important economic indicator of the quarter. Omar said the bakery was selling cardamom knots now and they were an offense against both cardamom and bread. They all played along because dinner required an ending and none of them knew how else to get to one.

Ben left first, insisting on taking the trash out as if volunteer labor might rebalance the evening. At the door he looked around with a hopeful, battered expression and said, "Next week I'll bring normal olives."

"Bring cheaper wine," June said. "This one tastes like a candle confessed."

He laughed, relieved to have been permitted a path out, and was gone in a rush of night air and stair noise.

Omar stayed long enough to help carry plates to the sink. He did it with his sleeves rolled higher now and with the concentrated dignity of a man refusing to seem either ashamed or defensive. At the door he turned back once, bakery box in hand.

"For what it's worth," he said, to no one person exactly, "I may have overstated it. He speaks in futures. Sometimes he means only atmosphere."

"That's not better," June said.

Omar accepted that. "No," he said. "It isn't." Then he left too.

With both of them gone, the table showed its gaps all at once. Ben's chair sat crooked by the window, Omar's napkin square remained beside the salt, and the fan kept chopping air nobody needed. The stove ticked as it cooled. Grease hardened on the plates in streaks the color of dried rust.

June picked up the sponge. Leela took the dish towel without asking. They had done this often enough that the motions did not require coordination. Wash, rinse, dry, stack. The sink was too small for the pot, so June attacked it in circles while Leela stood hip to hip with her at the counter, catching whatever could be saved from slipping back into the basin.

For a while they said nothing. The quiet had weight to it, but not the clean kind. It felt like a storage problem.

Leela dried a bowl and said, carefully, "Did he tell you anything else?"

June did not look up. "Mohit? Besides turning Tuesday dinner into a hostage video?"

"June."

"Yeah. He told me things." She rinsed, set the pot down harder than necessary, and bit into another piece of nicotine gum. "He always tells me things."

Leela waited. Outside, a siren moved up Alder and away again. The towel in her hands had gone damp and useless.

June exhaled through her nose. "After the meeting he stopped me in the hall and did the soft voice. The one he uses when he wants you to feel selected instead of cornered."

Leela knew the voice exactly. So did everybody in that lab, probably. It changed almost nothing in the sentence and everything in the balance of power.

"He said," June went on, scrubbing at the Dutch oven as if the enamel had personally betrayed her, "'I don't want you blindsided later if someone is sitting on a benchmark weakness and hoping you won't catch it before external review.'"

Leela set the bowl down so she would not drop it.

June laughed once, without joy. "That was the phrasing. 'Sitting on.' As if the data were a fucking tack somebody could hide under a cushion."

"What did you say?"

"What do you think I said? I said, 'Interesting, thanks,' because he's a faculty member and I enjoy housing." She finally looked at Leela. Her face had flattened into the expression it wore when fear was trying to pass as annoyance. "Then I spent the rest of the walk home doing inventory in my head. Who has my benchmark scripts, who touched the preprocessing, who I'd forgotten to cc on what, whether I left anything sloppy enough for somebody to turn into a morality play."

Leela could feel the damp hem of her T-shirt sticking to the small of her back. June kept grinding the sponge over one patch of enamel until it squealed. Mohit had planted a social hallucination and left June to live inside it.

"He told Ben the lab needed clearer roles," Leela said.

"Of course he did."

"And Omar that overlap was maturity, basically. Or professionalism. Something adjacent."

"And you?"

Leela folded the towel once, then again. There were only so many ways to delay a plain sentence before the delay became its own answer. "That if I wanted him to formalize my last year, I should show I could do hard things without making them interpersonal."

June barked a laugh. "So that's your version. Don't make them interpersonal. Incredible."

"I know."

"Do you? Because from where I'm standing it sounds like he told you to keep everything at frames and degrees until nobody has to say who's getting cut."

The line landed because it was aimed well. Leela felt it in the wet ceramic under her fingers. She had, after all, nearly done exactly that at the table. Degrees. Frames. Ordinary faculty manipulation. The sort of sentence people built tenure cases out of while somebody else bled quietly under it.

"I'm aware," she said. "It sounded bad."

"It sounded like you."

That hurt more because June said it without relish. No joke on top, no cushioning profanity. Just a clean placement of the blade.

Leela set the towel down. "What do you want me to say?"

June turned off the tap. Water ticked from the faucet into the quiet. "I don't know yet. That's sort of the problem."

They stood there facing the sink like people waiting for an x-ray to develop. Leela could smell the vinegar from the cucumber bowl, the detergent June bought in bulk because the laundromat upcharged for soap, the faint sweet burn of the gum in June's mouth. She wanted to ask the direct question. Do you think he's trying to turn us on each other? The sentence rose, found every possible cost attached to it, and would not cross.

June broke first, but only by a fraction. "If you hear anything about my benchmark," she said, eyes still on the rack of drying plates, "I need you to tell me before he does."

June kept slotting plates into the rack as she said it, one after another, without looking over. Each plate hit the metal tines with a neat little click. That ordinary rhythm made the request land harder.

Leela thought of Omar's careful jaw at the table, of Ben borrowing Mohit's diction because he did not yet own his own, of the way Mohit had made every offer sound private and therefore flattering. She thought of her own year hanging on a signature. She thought of how quickly the room had frozen around the phrase one person.

"Okay," she said.

June nodded once, then went to the counter and started spooning leftovers into reused yogurt tubs. Halfway through, she wiped her hand on the dish towel, dug a black Sharpie out of the junk drawer, and wrote directly on the lids: JUNE on one, LEELA on the other. The marker squeaked against the plastic.

When Leela finally left, June handed her the tub with her name on it and said, "Text me when you get home." It was what she always said after dinner. Her thumb pressed once across the lid before she let go.

On the stairs, the tub sweated in Leela's palm. Her name sat in June's black block letters across the lid, catching the stairwell light each time she turned. By the time Alder Street opened below, the ink had started to feather at the edges, but the name was still easy to read. So was June's request.
