# Chapter 16

She still had not looked at the phone when the washer lurched into its spin cycle. The laundromat on Alder was hotter than her apartment and louder than the committee room had been, which made it easier. Heat rolled off the dryer banks in a soapy, metallic breath and turned the windows into sweating mirrors. Every pair of boots on the floor carried a white salt tide at the edges. Leela sat on an orange plastic chair with her knees touching the black laundry bag and watched her sheets slap the glass as if the machine were trying to shake an answer out of them.

She had brought the good blouse from the meeting too, because it still smelled faintly of the faculty conference room: burnt coffee, radiator dust, the sweet chemical wipe somebody had used on the table before the ambush. Mohit's packet was folded in the tote with the detergent, the blood on the words further review dried into a gray feathered bruise. She had worn the blouse because it still fit under the blazer she bought for Baltimore and because somewhere in her body a childish part of her believed a committee could be managed by collars. Now the collar points knocked uselessly against the side of the washer. Her defense had not been denied. It had been "postponed indefinitely pending further revision and review," which was worse in the particular way academic language liked to be worse. Denial had a date inside it. Indefinitely was a hallway with fluorescent lighting.

She unlocked her phone to check the time and saw the Hudson Federal reminder at the top of the screen again: minimum payment due on the eighteenth. Under it sat Mohit's email from 7:12 that morning, subject line "Next Steps." He had written three short paragraphs in the careful managerial tone he used when he wanted cruelty to look like structure. He was sorry the meeting had become difficult. He remained committed to helping her identify a constructive path. He thought it would be best if they spoke one on one before she let anxiety distort the opportunities still available to her. She had read the message in bed while the radiator clanged hard enough to make the spoon in the mug on her nightstand tremble. She had not replied. She had dragged the laundry out instead, as if separating darks from lights qualified as a governance model.

The option of leaving had acquired its own practical vocabulary by breakfast. It involved storage bins, sublet groups, and the question of whether the graduate-housing office would let her out before summer if she furnished the room and found a woman willing to take over the lease. Graduate housing took its money at the start of every month whether or not a future still existed to justify it. Leaving also meant deciding whether she could put the books in Meera's garage without Meera looking too directly at the number of them, and whether she could say on the phone to her mother that the program had shifted and she was rethinking timelines, as if six years were a haircut that had grown out badly. The shame in that version was domestic, not spectacular. It lived in luggage, in family WhatsApp replies, and in the fact that Anita had sent her twelve hundred dollars at Diwali and called it nothing, just keep it, just until the next stipend.

The other option sat in Mohit's email. She could ask for the remediation plan, accept that the paper from September would now live forever as proof of her compromised judgment, rerun experiments on access she no longer controlled, apologize in rooms where every apology became data, and perhaps, if she kept her head down long enough, receive a smaller future as a reward for professional maturity, in the form of a later defense, a weaker letter, or a pity introduction to somebody else's postdoc. It was not even impossible. That was the insulting part. The committee had not thrown her off campus. It had left the door open exactly wide enough for her to crawl back through it.

When the dryer buzzed, June texted. "Interview done. If one more man in a quarter zip says mission alignment I might bite him."

A minute later another message came in. "They want another round. Remote this time. Thank fuck."

Leela looked at the spinning socks and wrote, "That sounds promising."

June answered fast. "Promising is not the word. But it pays actual money."

Then, after a pause long enough to imagine June walking through an airport concourse or across some glass lobby full of upholstered optimism, she added, "If you're serious about getting out, I can send recruiter names. These places hire unfinished PhDs all the time."

There was nothing cruel in it. That made it worse. June was not withholding tenderness as punishment. She was already moving in the direction of salary bands, vesting cliffs, offices with windows, and she was decent enough to hold the door open behind her. The sentence did not ask how Leela was. It assumed the relevant question had become where else she could land. Leela typed, "Committee pushed the defense. No date." She deleted it, retyped it, and sent it.

June's reply took longer. "Fuck. Did they put it in writing?"

"Not yet."

"Get the memo before you talk to Mohit. Forward me anything they send."

Then June sent one more message. "And yes, I meant the recruiter thing."

Leela held the phone in both hands until the screen dimmed. She had spent the last three weeks telling herself that once June was done being furious, or done being scared, or done taking whatever Mohit's meeting had offered her, they would return to the part where the two of them could say the true sentence out loud and decide what to do with it. But June's texts came from another weather now. They were helpful, sharp, and already budgeting for departure. Waiting for her to come back emotionally was not a plan. It was a superstition with a charging cable.

She folded everything while it was still hot enough to sting her fingers. The wrist brace had gone through the mesh bag and come out shaped like a small defeated exoskeleton. Her socks steamed faintly in the cold air that leaked every time the front door opened. When she stepped back outside, the tote against her thigh radiated warmth through her coat for half a block and then surrendered. Alder Street was half slush, half grit. The halal grocer had stacked clementine boxes in a tower by the door and covered them with a blue tarp that snapped in the wind. A nail salon window advertised student discounts in letters missing three vowels. Above the strip, campus rose in gray stone and self-esteem.

She cut behind the library toward Keating because there was nowhere else to go with a Sunday and a postponed life. The quad was almost empty. A grounds truck idled by a snowbank, and somewhere a metal flag halyard knocked against a pole with the patience of torture. Without undergraduates moving through it, the campus looked less prestigious and more recently expensive. All the glass had the expression of a place expecting witnesses.

On the sixth floor the shared office smelled of wet wool and printer heat. One radiator hissed under the lake-facing window. Her cubicle still held the stupid archaeology of a nearly finished degree: reviewer comments clipped together with a binder clip shaped like a cat, a dead succulent in a Haviland mug, three conference badges, the defense checklist she had made in January when March had still sounded like a time rather than an injury. She sat down and opened the document called REVISION PLAN.

It contained seven headings, all reasonable in isolation, and they told her to rerun the robustness experiment with a corrected baseline, expand the ethics subsection, clarify the provenance of repository access, and meet with her advisor to define authorship boundaries. The list was her own. She had typed it the night before the committee because she believed, with the embarrassing persistence of an obedient student, that if she arrived with work already broken into subproblems the adults in the room might treat her like a person capable of solving them. Now the headings read like chores assigned to the woman who had lost the case against herself.

She tried to turn them into an email to Gloria Fisk. Dear Professor Fisk, thank you for convening the committee. I would appreciate clarity on the specific milestones required for reconsideration of my defense timeline. She got that far and stopped. Every noun in the sentence was somebody else's noun, especially reconsideration, timeline, and milestones. The university loved a word that could describe a life and a project plan with the same sterile usefulness. If Gloria answered, it would be with process. If Mohit answered, it would be with a meeting. If she followed either path, the full price of staying would be silence about everything that had made the committee version of her possible.

She opened the hidden folder instead. It sat inside three other boring names on her hard drive because hiding had once felt like prudence. Inside were the September logs from hades, exported before Research Integrity cut her access; screenshots from the Ruderal deck in Baltimore; drafts of June's grievance timeline and scanned pages from the yellow legal pad, June's blocky handwriting getting angrier as the semester went on; Ben's forwarded email chain; Omar's funding letter versions; and her own conference paper, the clean draft and the dirty one, along with the rerun results that had refused to reproduce the claim Mohit later used to paint her as ethically suspect. The folder did not flatter anyone. That was partly why she had never given it to anybody in full.

If she exposed the archive as it actually existed, she was not the heroine in it. She was the person who saw the cloned repositories in September and decided not to blow up her life yet. She was the person who padded a result when the deadline narrowed and June needed the same machines. She was the person who ghostwrote part of Omar's funding letter because the clean procedure would have left him out of status. She could tell morally satisfying versions of each choice. She had. The archive disliked those versions. It kept the timestamps.

Outside the window, the lake looked like a sheet of unpolished aluminum. Students crossed the quad below in twos and threes, their scarves blown flat behind them. Leela imagined the three futures available to her with the same exhausted precision she had once applied to debugging. In one, she disappeared without giving the institution anything more to weaponize. In one, she bargained. In one, she turned the whole mess over and let the broken parts of her own record come with it. The third future was the only one that did not require her to keep pretending the first two were forms of control.

At three, Mohit sent a second email. "I know this is painful," it began, which was how he sounded when he wanted to name pain as a managerial condition rather than a fact somebody had produced. She closed the laptop without answering, put the hard drive in her coat pocket, and walked out before she could draft something professionally suicidal like go fuck yourself or professionally useful like thank you, I'd welcome the chance to discuss. The elevator shuddered down six floors. In the lobby, the heat hit her face and then the outside cold erased it.

The walk to North Lake took twenty minutes and felt punitive by the last five. Wind came unobstructed across the frozen water and found every gap in her coat. Salt cracked under her boots on the boards of the footbridge. The ice below had gone from the first damp gray of winter to something thicker and opaque, with old snow blown into ridges like folded paper. A red warning sign was cable-tied to the railing, and the plastic had split through the word THIN so that it looked like a stammer.

She stood halfway across, hands deep in her pockets around the hard drive and the folded printout of Mohit's email, and let the wind force tears out of her eyes. Crying was too organized a word for it. The cold just made the face honest. From there Keating was visible through bare trees, a slab of lit windows at the edge of campus, and beyond it the black-glass administration building where every room managed to sound already transcribed. The university looked coherent from a distance. That was one of its talents.

In model evaluation, a holdout set was the data you kept back so the system could not memorize the answer key and call the trick generalization. She had taught that definition to undergraduates three semesters running. This year nobody had kept anything back. Mohit had trained on their trust, their money problems, June's temper, Omar's visa clock, Ben's hunger to be chosen, Leela's belief that if she stayed reasonable long enough reason would be rewarded. The institution had trained on the rest. If the archive stayed only on her laptop, it would end up like everything else at Haviland: something she could be talked into trimming until it barely accused anyone.

Her mother had left a missed call and a voicemail consisting of two questions and one instruction: had she eaten lunch, and did she know yet about the cousin's engagement weekend in April; also, wear the real boots, not the city ones. Leela listened to it once and put the phone away. The love in Anita's voice had the dense practical weight of cooked rice. It did not tell her what to do with Mohit. It only made leaving feel like admitting that all the waiting at home had been for nothing.

By the time she turned back toward town her face hurt, the kind of clean pain that at least belonged to weather. She did not feel brave. She felt used up. The anger sat low and steady, no longer dramatic enough to mistake for courage. Mohit had already made her compromised. The committee had already entered that compromise into the record. The only remaining question was who would get custody of the full version.

Her apartment was overheated for once. The radiator hammered in bursts like somebody testing pipes with a wrench. She set the tote of laundry on the mattress, opened the laptop on the desk, and worked through the folder without music. Each file demanded a small act of nonvanity. She kept the logs. She kept the rerun notebook that proved the September result was dirty. She kept the draft complaint with her own hedges still visible in tracked changes. She kept the messages where June asked for the full server evidence and Leela said not yet. She kept the upload metadata on Omar's letter. She kept the deck versions with the stolen figures and the cached screenshots in case the PDFs later disappeared. She made a chronology in plain language because there was no point in handing a lawyer a cathedral of filenames and calling it truth.

When everything fit, she compressed the directory, encrypted it, checked the checksum, and stared at the blinking cursor where the archive still needed a name. complaint_final was a joke. misconduct_bundle sounded like something Mohit would send. evidence made her think of courtroom television and clean victims. She typed `holdout_set`, watched the underscore appear like a hinge, and pressed return. The name sat there flat and technical, which was exactly what she needed.

The graduate workers union had circulated counsel's contact information twice that winter, once after the travel-slot fiasco and again when stipends hit late in January. Leela found the email thread, half hidden under campus announcements, and wrote a version of the truth short enough to be sent: I'm a PhD student facing advisor retaliation and research misuse. Documentation is available if helpful. Is someone available today? The reply came eleven minutes later from Marisol Vega. "I'm here till six-thirty. If you want to bring materials in person, do that. Don't send the files over university email."

Marisol's office was over the pharmacy on Alder, up one flight of narrow stairs that smelled of old mop water and cumin from the takeout place next door. A corkboard outside the door held strike photos, rent-rights flyers, and a faded drawing of a tabby cat in a hard hat. Leela arrived with her backpack damp at the shoulders from blown snow and stood for a second with her hand on the knob, aware that handing over the drive would turn the day from contemplation into record.

Marisol Vega was younger than Leela had expected, maybe early forties, with blunt bangs she had clearly cut herself and men's snow boots patched at one toe with a neat strip of silver duct tape. A paper cup of mint tea sat untouched beside her keyboard until a skin formed over it. She moved it out of the way with mild embarrassment only after Leela noticed, as if cold tea were a recurring personal defeat. "Come in," she said. "Shut the door all the way. It sticks."

The office had one real lamp, one filing cabinet, and enough paper for three kinds of trouble. Marisol waited until Leela sat down before pulling a legal pad toward herself. "Start wherever the timeline actually starts," she said. "Not where the university started paying attention."

Leela did, but not elegantly. She started with the server logs in September because that was the first moment the year offered her a knife and she put it back in the drawer. She moved through the adversarial pairings, the conference paper, Baltimore, the grievance, Ben forwarding it, the committee. Twice she found herself drifting into explanatory jargon and had to stop because Marisol's face stayed politely blank whenever the vocabulary got too lab-specific. So she translated. He stole figures. He made scarcity formal. He used my dirty result when it was useful to him. I helped keep Omar enrolled by faking part of a letter. I sat on the logs because I thought I could survive this if I timed it right.

Marisol did not look shocked. She did not look impressed either. She asked dates, file names, who had originals, who knew what, whether Mohit had ever put any of his contradictory instructions in writing, whether June and Omar were willing to corroborate, whether Research Integrity already possessed anything that could later be compared. Her questions had the plain brutality of competence. By the time Leela finished, the story sounded less like a confession and more like a chain of custody.

"Does the archive have the parts that make you look bad?" Marisol asked.

"Yes."

"All of them?"

Leela almost said enough of them. "I think so."

Marisol shook her head once. "I need a less academic answer than that."

Leela felt something mean rise in her throat and burn there. "Yes. The parts where I waited. The fake-clean paper. Omar's letter. The parts where I thought I could manage him."

"Good," Marisol said. "If it's everything, it's useful. If it's almost everything, Haviland will spend six months finding the seam and pull the whole thing apart."

Leela took the drive out of her pocket and set it on the desk. It was small enough to look unserious, black plastic with a scratch along one edge. "I don't know what this gets me," she said.

Marisol glanced at the drive but did not pick it up yet. "It might not get you anything good."

"I noticed."

"It might get you retaliation, or a settlement designed to buy silence, or an investigation so narrow it insults you on purpose. The university's favorite move is to admit a process problem and deny a power problem."

Leela laughed once, ugly and brief. "That sounds right."

"But if what you want is a clean exoneration, this isn't that office," Marisol said. "If what you want is for the documents to survive you losing your nerve, or losing your laptop, or getting talked into revision number twelve, I can help with that."

"Do June and Omar know you're bringing me the full file?" she asked a moment later.

"Not yet."

Marisol tapped the drive with one fingernail and pushed it an inch back across the desk. "Then we slow down. I'm not taking blind custody of a sealed archive that can blow back on two other grad workers before either of them hears it from you."

Leela looked at the drive. "I'm not asking their permission."

"I didn't say permission." Marisol pulled the legal pad closer. "I said notice, and I said chain of custody. Open it. We make a manifest in plain English. Then you tell whichever one you're most tempted to protect by editing."

Something in Leela's chest tightened hard enough to feel medicinal. She plugged the drive into Marisol's desktop and typed the passphrase herself. `holdout_set` mounted on the screen. The folder tree opened down the screen: `server_logs_sept`, `ruderal_deck_baltimore`, `june_timeline_scans`, `omar_letter_versions`, `paper_dirty`, `paper_clean`, `complaint_drafts`, `text_exports`. Her hand moved to the mouse before she had decided anything. She clicked once on `june_timeline_scans` and then on `omar_letter_versions`, feeling the old stupid reflex rear up again, the wish to hand over truth with the teeth filed down.

Marisol watched the cursor stop. "If those are the folders you're suddenly ashamed of," she said, "they're probably the ones that keep the rest from looking curated."

Leela opened a message to June because Omar's name carried too much paperwork inside it and June had already spent the day talking like someone halfway out the door. She typed, "I'm with union counsel. I'm handing over the full archive tonight. Your notebook scans are in it. So are the texts where you asked for the server evidence and I said not yet. I'm telling you before the copy starts."

June replied before Leela could regret sending it. "Is it actually the full thing or the tasteful grad-student version of the full thing?"

Leela typed, "All of it."

Another bubble came back almost at once. "Then keep the ugly parts. Keep my pages. Keep your not yet. Keep the fucking logs. If you clean me up now, he'll own the language again."

The shame that rose in Leela's throat was hot and almost clarifying. She took her hand off the mouse and wrote on Marisol's pad: June's handwritten timeline pages and related text messages documenting Leela's withholding of server evidence. Beneath that she wrote: Ghostwritten funding-letter drafts and upload metadata tied to Omar's visa paperwork. Her handwriting looked pinched and schoolgirl neat.

"Good," Marisol said. "Now I can tell what I'm preserving."

She started the copy only after the list was complete. A progress bar opened, then stalled at twelve percent long enough for Leela to feel her pulse in the bad wrist before the numbers began moving again. Marisol watched the transfer window, then the checksum utility, then the legal pad, as if mistrust were simply another office supply. "Once this is copied," she said, "I'll put one encrypted copy on union storage and one on an offline drive. If you decide next week that this was a terrible idea, you can still decide that. What you won't be able to do is pretend it never existed."

"What do you do with it?" Leela asked.

"Right now? Preserve it. Build the index. Figure out what corroborates what. See which parts matter only if June says yes, which parts matter only if Omar does, and which parts stand on timestamps alone. Then we wait for a setting where they can't reduce it to mentoring style and unfortunate misunderstandings."

"A public setting."

"Usually," Marisol said. "Power behaves better when it has an audience."

The progress bar reached the end. Marisol verified the checksum once on screen and once on paper, writing the string down in tight block print that made every letter look like it could testify. Leela watched her do it and felt something settle, not relief exactly, more the end of a useless appeal. She had spent months trying to preserve a version of herself that might still deserve uncomplicated sympathy if the facts ever became public. That version did not exist. It had not existed the night she hid the logs, or the deadline week she let the false result stand, or the hour she doctored Omar's letter because the clean procedure was a polite machine for immigration disaster. Wanting a clean alibi had been one more way of staying loyal to the institution's taste in victims.

Marisol labeled a folder HOLDOUT SET / L.K. / MARCH 2026 and slid it into a drawer. "Leela," she said, looking up for the first time in several minutes, "they can already call you compromised. He already did. The committee already stamped it. The difference is whether compromised means documented and contextualized or whether it means whatever version he tells first."

When Leela stood, the room tilted from hunger. Marisol pointed to a basket of granola bars without looking up from her notes. "Take one. Counsel's not your mother, but counsel can still tell when somebody hasn't eaten."

Leela took one because refusing would have felt theatrical. It was peanut butter and stale at the edges. By the time she got downstairs the snow had started again, not dramatic flakes, just the dry mean kind that moved sideways under the streetlights. Her backpack was lighter by almost nothing. Upstairs, Marisol had the checksum on paper, the manifest in plain English, and the copied drive already in a labeled drawer.

Whatever story Haviland later told, `holdout_set` was no longer just a folder Leela could open, rename, or thin out at two in the morning.
