# Chapter 6

By the time Leela left Keating, the seminar had been over for nearly two hours, but the room still seemed to exist under her skin. She could still see June at the screen with one hand braced on the lectern, doing that impossible thing of continuing to speak while her authority leaked out into the fluorescent air. Leela had tried texting once from the sixth-floor bathroom, something cowardly and small, `You home?`, and June had not answered. She did not blame her. She kept seeing the point where Mohit turned in his chair and said, almost pleasantly, Leela, do you want to weigh in here, as if he were offering her the floor instead of asking her to finish the job.

The engineering building was on night mode now, lobby lights reduced, glass doors black with reflected hallways. Outside, the cold had sharpened since late afternoon. Not winter yet, not the knife of it, but the first weather that made your teeth feel overexposed. Omar was standing beside the loading-dock ramp with his backpack on one shoulder and a flattened paper coffee cup in his hand. He looked as neat as he had looked at noon, button-down still tucked, beard line still exact, which only made the day feel more deranged. Leela thought briefly, stupidly, about turning back into the building and taking the long way out through the library.

Instead he said, "Have you eaten?"

She laughed once through her nose. "Is that a trick question?"

"I don't know. You looked like you were going to walk home and have coffee for dinner out of spite."

It was annoying that he was right. She had seven dollars in her checking account until Friday, half a sleeve of saltines in her studio, and enough anger to mistake that for a plan. He crushed the paper cup smaller, then dropped it into the trash beside the loading dock.

"The buffet under the tax office stays open till eleven," he said. "If we get there now, they won't hate us until we're already sitting down."

She should have said no. She should have gone home and sat with what she had done to June, or failed to do, which was the same thing from the only vantage point that mattered. Instead she said, "Fine," because she was hungry enough that the word came out before principle could organize itself.

They walked downhill through the strip between campus and Alder Street, past the beauty supply place with mannequins fading in the window, past the halal butcher hosing pink water toward the curb, past a laundromat that always looked overlit enough to expose sin. The town below Haviland had its own hours, its own smells, its own patience with graduate students in bad shoes. Keating, with its sealed corridors and server heat, always made the rest of Lakebridge feel invented until Leela came back into it and heard men stacking fruit crates in Spanish behind the grocery or saw a delivery driver smoking beneath a neon OPEN sign that had lost the O. Omar kept his hands in his coat pockets and walked quickly, not talking. Once he reached up and touched the side of his jaw, checking, maybe, whether his face was still under control.

The buffet was still open in the technical sense. Half the overhead bulbs had been turned off, which made the steam trays glow harder. At the register a woman in a gray fleece vest was painting her left thumbnail dark green while a dubbed soap opera ran above her head on mute. Without looking up she said, "Nine-fifty-five. Kitchen closes at ten. You can stay after, but no refills after ten-fifteen."

"We're scholars, not animals," Omar said.

The woman finally glanced at him, took in the campus badges clipped to both their bags, and snorted. "That's not what I said."

Leela paid cash because the handwritten card minimum sign made the decision for her. The bills left her hand with the humiliating lightness of money that already had another use. Then they were inside the smell of fryer oil and cardamom and bleach, carrying plates still warm from the stack.

The food was bad in several distinct ways and exactly what she wanted. Paneer gone rubbery at the edges. Green beans cooked past apology. Tandoori chicken lacquered red enough to stain a napkin. A tray of lo mein in the middle of the Indian food for reasons nobody here respected enough to explain. She took too much on the first pass because appetite had arrived late and angry. When she sat down, Omar was arranging his plate with the unconscious fussiness of someone who hated having sauce touch anything before he had chosen it. He had somehow built a first round that looked sensible, lentils, rice, cucumber salad, one piece of fish, as if he were auditioning for the role of man who had not just detonated a colleague in public. Then, as soon as he saw Leela looking, he said, "This is the respectable plate. The second plate is where the moral collapse happens."

Against her will, she smiled.

"Good," he said. "I was worried I'd have to do all the work."

It was the first thing all day that had felt remotely like relief, not because it was funny enough to matter, but because it let her chew for a minute without also having to perform righteous clarity. Oil and salt hit her bloodstream so fast she could have cried from it. Her body had apparently made its own administrative decision and classified the seminar as something to survive after calories.

For a while they only ate. Spoons touched china. The soda fountain hissed and reset itself with nobody using it. Somewhere near the kitchen a child was whining in Spanish while an adult voice kept answering with the flat, invulnerable patience of someone who had done fourteen hours on their feet. Leela kept seeing June's face anyway, and what stayed with her was the recovery: June forcing her voice lower and flatter and talking through the bruise of it, which in June always meant the jokes were over and the damage would be itemized later, in private.

Leela put down her fork. "Did he tell you to do that?"

Omar did not pretend not to understand. "Yes."

She had wanted him to lie first. A lie she could kick open. This made her angrier.

"That's it?"

"No," he said. "That's just the shortest truthful answer."

She waited.

He folded his paper napkin once, then again, into a thinner rectangle. "He told me there was an internal robustness review attached to one of the benchmark papers. He said the paper would be anonymized to keep it clean. He said whoever it was had agreed to the process in advance, because otherwise it would become personal and useless."

"And you believed that?"

"Enough to proceed."

"That's not the same sentence."

"No." He took a drink of water. "It's still the one I had."

Leela leaned back against the booth. The vinyl gave under her shoulders with a sticky sigh. "Anonymized. Jesus Christ."

"I know how it sounds now."

"No, I don't think you do."

That landed. He let it land. Outside the plate-glass window, a bus shuddered past and splashed old rainwater against the curb. Omar looked at the table, not at her.

"He said the point was to simulate conference conditions," he said. "He used the phrase 'adversarial pressure test' at least three times, so I assume he believed repetition would turn it into ethics."

"He made you say it in seminar?"

"He said it needed to happen in the room, yes."

"Why?"

Omar gave a short, humorless laugh. "Because in private June could argue back and in public she had to keep being June."

The sentence was ugly enough that Leela stared at him. Its precision made it worse; falseness would have been easier to swat away. Omar saw that and rubbed the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger, as if he could erase the phrasing after it was already in the air.

"I'm not trying to sound insightful," he said. "He all but said it. He said she needed to learn how the field would treat that claim if she took it outside. He said it'd be kinder for the correction to come from us."

"Us."

"Yes. That was the word."

She thought of Mohit's face in meetings when he said our students, our work, our standards, each pronoun padded and soft and impossible to prove harmful. She stabbed a piece of chicken and found she had no interest in swallowing it now.

"When did you know it was her?" she asked.

He was quiet long enough that the answer arrived before he gave it.

"The night before," he said. "He sent the slide deck after midnight. He said not to circulate it and not to overprepare because spontaneity mattered."

"So you knew?"

"Yes."

"And you still did it?"

"Yes."

She wanted the pleasure of calling him a coward and could not quite have it, because the word would have slid too easily over the place where her own silence sat. Instead she said, "Why?"

Omar pushed rice into a neat mound with the side of his fork, then stopped when he saw himself doing it. "Because by that point refusing wouldn't have made me innocent. It would've made me difficult. There's a difference in this department."

"That's not an answer either."

"It's closer." He looked up. "He's been telling me for weeks that I need to be more visible, less careful, that the theory work alone is not enough if I want anyone to think I can lead a room. He said there might be a workshop paper out of the robustness audits. He said he could maybe put my name forward for the winter visit at Meridian if I showed I was useful beyond my narrow piece. He said many things. None of them were promises in the legal sense. All of them were promises in the only sense that matters when someone controls your funding."

Meridian. Leela knew the name. Fancy private lab, absurd salaries, the kind of visit that got repeated in bios for years after. She had not realized Mohit was dangling that kind of bait already, though of course he was. He had learned early that his best manipulations were the ones ambitious people could help narrate for him.

"So you sold her out for a maybe?"

"Yes," Omar said, and for the first time there was the faintest edge in his voice, not anger exactly, something more exhausted and therefore less manageable. "And I'd appreciate it if you wouldn't talk as if I haven't been doing the arithmetic myself."

That shut her up for a second. He ate two bites in quick succession, less from hunger than from the need to have his mouth occupied.

When he spoke again, he was quieter. "Also, if I'm confessing properly, I was angry with her."

Leela blinked. "With June?"

"Not in some grand tragic way. In the ordinary petty way people in labs hate each other for three days at a time and then call it weather. She's got a habit of talking about theory as if it's where results go to die. She doesn't mean it as cruelly as it sounds, but she says it anyway. He knew that. He used it."

That, too, felt true enough to offend. June did talk that way when she was tired, with the brisk contempt of someone who had spent a week fixing infrastructure and had no patience left for elegance. Omar had the kind of dignity that made mockery slide off until, eventually, it didn't.

"So there was that," he said. "It doesn't justify anything; it just made the lie easier to step into."

Leela looked down at her plate. She had made a small wreck of it without noticing, sauces dragged together, one torn naan corner hardened where it had cooled. This was the part nobody ever wrote into faculty statements about collegiality, the fact that exploitation recruited through whatever was already bruised. Nobody needed to invent a wound if a lesser one was available.

"He asked me to weigh in," she said finally. "Today. In the room. Like I was some neutral fucking authority."

Omar's expression changed, not softer, but less defended. "I know."

"I could've said it needed to wait. I could've said the benchmark issue wasn't settled. I could've said almost anything except what I said."

"Which was almost nothing."

"Yes."

"That's not the same as nothing."

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Make me feel better because you're embarrassed."

He held up one hand, surrender or correction. "Fair."

The woman at the register called that the kitchen was closing. Omar rose for his second plate with a grimness that made the whole act funny again, and when he came back he was carrying exactly what he had promised: the shameful buffet truth. Two pakoras gone soft, lo mein, an alarming square of pizza from the children's tray, and a scoop of gulab jamun shining in syrup beside everything else like a bad decision he'd decided to commit to fully.

"That can't all be on the same plate," Leela said.

"I think you'll find that it can."

"This is why empires collapse."

"Empires collapse for many reasons," he said. "Tonight one of them is sugar syrup on institutional pizza."

She laughed again, actually this time, and hated him a little for making it possible.

But the relief only opened enough space for June to come back sharper. Leela imagined June in her apartment, shoes kicked under the radiator, nicotine gum chewed flat to bitterness, replaying the room with the precision she usually reserved for code regression. She had wanted Leela to do one small, visible thing in public. Leela had not done it. Sitting here with Omar, building an account of how Mohit had engineered the blow, did not alter the fact that June had paid the cost alone in the room. On impulse Leela pulled out her phone and typed, `I'm with Omar. Can I come by later?` She hated the sentence for how quickly it tried to sound useful. She sent it anyway. Nothing came back.

"Do you think he planned all of it," she asked, setting the phone facedown, "or is this just the ordinary version? Regular academic bad faith. The kind everybody explains away because tenure exists and feelings don't."

Omar chewed, swallowed. "I think 'planned' gives him too much theater. He doesn't need a lair. He needs to know which person to corner in public and which one will call it rigor afterward."

"That still sounds like planning."

"Fine," he said. "Then yes. But it's the kind that gets rewarded."

That was the most useful thing anyone had said to her since lunch. She felt it click into place with a depressing neatness. The point did not absolve Omar; it pinned his choice back inside a structure where choice still counted. Mohit had not hypnotized anyone. He had only made every refusal expensive and every compromise narratable.

They stayed until the register woman began stacking chairs onto the empty tables in the far section with blunt, warning noise. Leela was full in the graceless way buffet fullness always was, her stomach hot and overcommitted, her brain briefly slowed by grease. Outside, the cold hit the sweat at the back of her neck and turned it mean. The storefronts on the block had mostly gone dark. A tax-refund banner snapped against a chain-link fence. Somewhere farther down Alder, a couple were fighting in low furious voices while a car idled with music shaking faintly through its doors.

They started uphill toward campus. Without the table between them the conversation felt riskier, as if whatever either of them said next would have to survive in thinner air. Omar zipped his coat all the way up and then tugged the zipper tab twice, a little checking gesture Leela had seen him do before major presentations, as if fastening something could count as preparation.

"There's one other thing," she said.

He looked over, wary at once. "That's not an encouraging opening."

"No."

Her hands were cold enough that she tucked them under her arms. She had not planned to tell him anything tonight. She had planned, if she was honest, to let him carry the whole moral burden of the seminar for at least a week, maybe longer, because anger was simpler than inventory. But now he had given her something she could use, and the cheapness of taking it without returning anything showed too clearly.

"Earlier this semester," she said, "Mohit asked me to look into what he called a repo breach. He said somebody might have been copying work off the lab server."

Omar slowed. "And?"

"And it wasn't clean." She felt herself immediately start to sand the sentence down and hated that, too. "The logs showed mirrors being made. Whole directories. Not random pulls, not somebody poking around where they shouldn't. It looked like top-level access."

He stopped walking. They were under the dead white spill of a parking-lot lamp outside the closed pharmacy, bright enough for her to see the hard line that formed along his mouth when fear showed up before the rest of his face had decided what to do with it.

"Top-level as in you?"

"Inherited admin. Still my credentials on paper. Yes."

"Top-level as in Mohit?"

She looked at the pavement. Rain had dried in dirty crescents around old leaves. "Somebody who did not need to steal a password," she said.

That was enough. It was also a lie by reduction. She did not say Ruderal. She did not say private folder. She did not say that she had seen Mohit's own account move student repositories into a place no student had been told existed, then gone home and decided not to become the kind of person who reported it. The name of the folder sat in her head with terrible clarity anyway, eight letters and a little underscore of corporate ambition. Saying only this much made her feel both clever and dirty.

Omar stared at her for a long second. The cold moved around them in clean knives.

"When," he said.

"September."

His laugh came out short and disbelieving. "September."

"Yes."

"And you're telling me now?"

"Yes."

"Why now?"

There it was. She could have said because tonight made the pattern undeniable, because his confession demanded one, because I need you useful. All of them were true. None of them were the whole thing.

"Because if he's moving work around at that level," she said, "then today wasn't just him being an asshole in seminar. It was operational. And because if I tell you everything the first time I say any of it, you'd be an idiot to trust me and I'd be an idiot to trust you."

He let that sit. His breath showed white between them.

"That's almost honest," he said.

"We're having an almost honest conversation."

"Apparently."

They resumed walking, slower now. The campus lights above the hill looked expensive and remote, a brochure version of knowledge waiting to resume in the morning. Omar shoved his hands deeper into his pockets.

"He told me something else," he said after a while. "About why the seminar had to happen that way."

Leela waited.

"He said if I kept acting grateful all the time, people would mistake that for weakness. Those weren't his exact words, but close enough. He said international students get trapped performing reliability. He said I needed to show that I could make a hard call in a room and survive being disliked for it. He made it sound almost protective."

Leela felt a pulse of disgust so specific it was nearly admiration. That was Mohit's favorite register, the one where exploitation arrived dressed as preparation for a cruel world that only he was honest enough to name.

"He says versions of that to all of us," she said. "He picks the sentence you've almost already told yourself."

Omar nodded once. "Yes."

They reached the footpath behind the library where the wind shifted and came cleaner off the lake. Fallen leaves had pasted themselves against the fence in dark wet fans. Leela's stomach was beginning to resent the buffet, but the solid weight of it still felt preferable to the airy sickness from earlier. Beside her Omar walked with his head bent slightly into the cold, shoulders tight, not elegant now, simply tired.

"So what are we saying," he asked. "That he's strategic or that he's merely like every advisor with enough money and not enough supervision?"

"Those aren't different categories."

"No," he said. "But one is something June can name and one is just weather."

"June doesn't need a vocabulary lesson. She needs to know what he said to you."

He turned to her. "Tonight?"

"Why not tonight?"

"Because I already stood up in front of her and did it once," he said. "Showing up at her door with an explanation of my own behavior isn't confession. It's vanity."

"It's not vanity if it's true."

"Are you about to tell her everything you know?"

The answer moved through her before she could dress it up. "No."

"Then don't ask me to arrive with my partial truth while you keep yours in your pocket."

That shut her up. He was right, but that was not even the part that stung. He had found the exact shape of the wrongness faster than she had.

Her phone vibrated in her coat. She pulled it out with fingers gone clumsy from cold.

June's reply sat on the screen in a single hard block: `Not with him. Don't call tonight.`

Omar watched her face and then looked away. "She answered?"

"Yes."

"And?"

"She doesn't want either of us near her."

He nodded once. "Fair."

They walked another few steps with the message still burning against her palm.

"Maybe the useful question isn't which one," she said. "Maybe it's whether he keeps each of us in a separate room long enough to make us accept the smaller version."

Omar looked at her. "Smaller version."

"The one where you're not being used, just corrected. The one where I'm not covering for him, just being practical. The one where June didn't get set up, she just had a rough seminar. The one where tonight's answer is that she's overreacting instead of that we earned this."

He was quiet for several steps. Then he said, "That does sound like him."

She almost said the folder name then. Almost. It sat at the back of her tongue like a pill she might still decide not to swallow. But if she gave him that, everything changed at once. He would want proof. He would want dates. He would want to know why she had hidden it. He would be right to want all of it. Tonight she did not have the stomach for a full moral accounting, not with grease cooling in her blood and June's refusal still warm on her screen and the week ahead not even pretending to be survivable.

So she said, "If he asks you to review my work again, you tell me first."

Omar answered immediately. "Yes."

"And if he asks me anything about your models, your benchmarks, your paper, I tell you."

"Even if it makes you look bad."

"Especially then."

He considered her in profile for a second, as if testing the sentence for hidden conditions. "All right," he said. "And if he asks about tonight."

"We got food because we were hungry."

"Which is true."

"Yes."

"And we complained about him like graduate students since the beginning of time."

"Also true."

He gave the faintest smile. "You see. We're finally building a rigorous method."

By the time they reached the edge of campus, Keating was visible again above the trees, all lit rectangles and sealed doors, the building that had taught them to call theft collaboration if the server cluster stayed on. June's text had gone dark with the rest of the screen, but Leela could still feel it there. Under it she could feel, almost physically, the two larger omissions still walking between them: the part of Omar's ambition he had not fully named, the part of her evidence she had still kept for herself. Neither omission cancelled the other. Neither balanced anything. But the conversation had produced a narrow strip of ground that had not existed at dinner, a place to stand without Mohit in the middle of it translating everyone's motives for them.

At the crosswalk Omar stopped and waited for the signal even though no cars were coming. He had the tired, overcorrect habit of someone who obeyed empty streets on principle. When the light changed, he said, "I'm not asking you to forgive me."

"Good."

"I'm asking you not to let him narrate me to you without my participation."

That was such an academic way to put a plea that she would have laughed if June's message had not still felt like a shut door in her hand.

"Same," she said.

He nodded. Then they crossed.

Whatever this was, June's answer had already stripped the word trust out of it. It was leaner and meaner, built out of hunger, shared usefulness, and two edited confessions offered in the cold. June had paid for the lesson in public and, when Leela finally reached toward her, refused to make herself available as the cleanup crew for it. Still, when Leela headed toward graduate housing and Omar kept on toward the international student apartments, they were no longer carrying only Mohit's version of the day. They had made a smaller truth between them, damaged and usable, and it was nowhere near enough. It did nothing to shrink the September logs still sitting behind everything else, admin-level and ugly and hers on paper. For the moment, it was all they had.
