# Chapter 12

Upstairs, the paragraph Leela had failed to fix the day before was still open on her screen when Omar came off the elevator in a rougher version of himself than usual. He usually entered rooms as if he had already considered the possibility that he was imposing and decided, for everyone else's ease, not to perform the apology aloud. That morning the sixth-floor graduate office got no such performance. He stepped out with his scarf knotted wrong, one side hanging nearly to his knee, and a clear freezer bag under his arm because the navy accordion folder he used for official documents had split along the bottom seam. Inside the bag, a passport photocopy was bent around a stack of I-20s, and a packet of cardamom biscuits had burst somewhere along the walk over, leaving pale crumbs in the corners like grit.

Leela looked up from a paragraph she had been revising for forty minutes without improving. The radiator by the window had been knocking all morning in a patient, judicial way. Omar crossed to her cubicle too fast, then stopped too hard, as if his body had overshot the version of calm he meant to present.

"Do you have a minute," he said. Then, because even that was too imprecise for what was happening, "No. I need you to look at something now."

He set the bag on the desk. His hands were steady for one second and then not at all. Leela saw the tremor in the right one before he tucked it under his left wrist.

"What happened?"

"My funding letter isn't signed." He took out a stapled checklist printed from International Student Services. Every line had been highlighted in alternating yellow and blue. "The packet has to be complete by four-thirty or they treat the extension as late. They told me that three times in three slightly different sentences, which I assume means they enjoy it."

Leela read the checklist. It was the kind of administrative page universities produced by the thousands, all beige wording and false gentleness, but the requirements were brutally plain when you looked at them long enough. Updated I-20 request. Proof of current funding. Faculty letter on departmental letterhead stating stipend amount, appointment dates, and fee coverage. Advisor signature. Upload before close of business for same-cycle processing. At the bottom, in bold, a warning about delays affecting work authorization and status documentation.

"What's the actual date?" she said.

Omar pulled another page from the bag, this one an email thread printed so many times the toner had started to ghost at the edges. "Today is the last day they will accept the packet without kicking it into late review. They can still review late submissions, but then there is a gap on record and more forms and a conversation I would prefer not to have with the federal government for the rest of my life."

He said federal government with the kind of brittle accuracy that told her he had already spent the morning imagining every possible outcome and ranking them. His jaw kept shifting sideways, a tiny violent movement under the careful beard line. Omar was usually dressed as if a day might suddenly become important in public. Today the collar points of his shirt were uneven. One was folded under.

"When did you send it to Mohit?"

"Monday." He took out the draft letter. "I sent him a version. He changed two words, told me not to say `guaranteed` because nothing's guaranteed, which was charming, and asked me to resend. I resent it. Then he wanted to discuss `broader spring alignment` before signing. Yesterday he was unavailable. Today he's apparently in external meetings until the collapse of empire."

He handed her his phone. Mohit's last email sat on the screen, timestamped 8:07 a.m.

Before I sign anything, I'd like clarity on your judgment and on whether we're aligned about spring priorities. I'm tied up with external meetings until later this afternoon. Let's find a moment once the temperature in the room is lower.

It was exactly Mohit's voice, soft enough to sound embarrassed on behalf of conflict, clean enough that anyone outside the lab would read it as basic managerial caution. Leela felt the click of recognition in her chest. He was not forgetting. He was pricing.

"What room?" she said.

Omar gave a short laugh that had no amusement in it. "I assume the room where I become teachable."

The office around them went on making its ordinary sounds. Somebody at the far end was opening a yogurt with the violence of a person already angry at email. The copy machine beeped twice and then gave up on whatever page it had been trying to swallow. Through the long window the lake was a sheet of dirty pewter and the sky had the packed, low look of more snow.

Leela read the checklist again, slower. The letter needed exact dates, January 15, 2026 through August 31, 2026. It needed the annualized funding figure. It needed tuition and fee remission stated explicitly. It needed a faculty signature. One page, maybe two if someone got decorative, and all of Omar's legal safety for the next stretch of his life had been narrowed to that stupid page and the hours left on the clock.

"Did you call ISSS?" she asked.

"At nine. At ten fifteen. At ten forty-two." He was speaking faster now, losing the commas between thoughts. "The woman I got the second time was kind enough to explain, again, that they cannot issue the updated document until the funding proof is complete, and if it is incomplete after today they have to annotate the file. She said annotate the file in the tone people use for a relative who has started drinking at lunch. I asked whether that meant I was out of status. She said she could not advise me on that question until she saw the file. So now apparently my life is Schrödinger's paperwork."

He put both palms on the desk, leaned over the highlighted sheet, and closed his eyes once. When he opened them, the polish was gone. His intelligence and irony were still there, but the screen he usually kept between fear and other people had dropped.

"I know he's doing this on purpose," he said. "I know because if he wanted a meeting, he could have had a meeting. He wants me waiting. He wants me to hear every hour as him."

Leela looked at the email timestamp again: 8:07. He had offered 3:30, parked the signature just beyond reach, and turned the whole day into something Omar had to count in half-hours.

"Come on," she said, already standing. "We're going to Gloria."

Omar blinked. "She can't sign it."

"No. But she can tell us exactly what he thinks he can get away with."

They walked across the quad with Omar carrying the freezer bag tucked under his coat against the wind. The snow at the curb had gone gray and ridged from plows. Leela's right hand hurt inside the wrist brace she had started wearing again after too many weeks of clenching at keyboards and carrying tension there as if the joint itself could solve anything. Omar kept checking his phone every dozen steps, not because a miracle was likely but because waiting without checking would have felt like negligence.

Baines Hall looked as if it had been designed by people who believed glass made authority less cruel. Black window bands and neutral carpet carried the permanent artificial-citrus smell of places where decisions were documented before they were made. Gloria Fisk's assistant waved them in after one look at Omar's face.

Gloria stood when they entered. She had the same immaculate silver hair and practical suit she always did, but when she opened the paper folder in front of her the top sheet was not a memo. It was the day's crossword half-done in blue pen, half the clues filled with the kind of clipped certainty of somebody who solved them between meetings and resented interruptions only on principle. She slid it under a budget spreadsheet before speaking.

"Sit," she said. "Tell me what the exact document is."

Omar sat but did not relax into the chair. He took the checklist from the bag, smoothed it on her desk, and went through the requirement line by line with the meticulousness of a man reciting instructions for his own rescue. He named the funding dates, the annualized stipend amount, the fee remission language, and the upload deadline. He did not say please until the end, and then only once.

Gloria read without moving her mouth. "And Professor Saran has the draft letter already?"

"Since Monday," Omar said.

"With the figures?"

"Yes."

"Has he indicated there is any actual funding uncertainty?"

Omar's jaw shifted again. "Not in any way that would survive quotation."

Leela handed over the phone with Mohit's email. Gloria read that too. Her face did not change much, but she set the phone down more carefully than she had picked it up.

"I can remind him that this is time-sensitive," she said.

"I do not need a reminder," Omar said. The formality snapped through his voice like wire. Then he corrected himself, more controlled and somehow more alarming. "I need the signed document filed before the office closes."

Gloria folded her hands. "I understand the urgency."

Leela almost laughed. The sentence landed with the weightlessness of departmental empathy, which was to say none. Omar did not laugh. He was too far gone for that kind of campus comedy.

"Then sign it yourself," Leela said.

Gloria looked at her, not offended, just tired. "I cannot certify lab funding that is under a faculty member's grant authority unless the department is formally reassigning that support, and I cannot do that in an hour and a half. That requires accounts, a revised appointment, and several people none of us want to meet in this context."

"So he gets to do this."

"He gets to control the account he's the principal investigator on," Gloria said. "That's not the same statement, however much it feels like it right now."

"It's exactly the same statement right now," Leela said.

Gloria did not rise to it. That was one of her talents. She absorbed accusation and returned administration.

"If you want this handled as a complaint," she said, looking at Omar now, "we can discuss that. But a complaint does not produce a same-day letter. It produces a file, and files move at the speed files move. I'm trying to keep this in the category where something practical can still happen."

Omar's fingers dug into his knee hard enough to blanch. Gloria kept her voice level, as if the damage might stay smaller if she could keep it inside process nouns and factual subject lines.

Omar sat very still for a second, then leaned forward. "What practical thing?"

Gloria turned the checklist toward herself and tapped the relevant lines with the blue pen. "The office does not need a long narrative. They need dates, gross stipend, fee coverage, and confirmation of good standing. If you have an older approved version, the format can be reused. The international office does not care about style. They care about whether the required fields exist and whether the faculty signature is there."

Leela watched the sentence land. Gloria had not given them a solution. She had given them the shape of the lock.

"Do you have an older version?" Gloria asked.

"Of course I do," Omar said. It came out almost offended. "I've got every version. I've got the rejection email from my first visa appointment in Cairo because I needed to remember what exact lie they wanted the second time."

That was the first piece of himself he had offered all morning that had nothing to do with task flow. Leela saw Gloria hear it too. Something softer crossed the older woman's face and then got covered over at once.

"All right," Gloria said. "Send Professor Saran the request again. Copy me. Keep the subject line factual. No accusations. No speculation about motive. If he responds, he responds. If he doesn't, I will note that I asked."

"And if he still doesn't sign?" Leela said.

Gloria capped the pen. "Then we will know more than we know now."

"That's a disgusting sentence."

"It's an accurate sentence." Gloria's own voice had shortened now, the courtesy stripped back to structure. "Do not copy the dean's office. Do not copy International Student Services on an accusation unless you are prepared for the practical result, which is delay. If there is a path to solving this before five, it is narrower than you want and quieter than you deserve."

The folder was still open on her desk. Leela could see a square of crossword grid peeking out from under the budget sheet, one clue unanswered. It was not a window or a scrap of moral clarity, just a little private puzzle interrupted by other people's institutional emergencies.

Gloria reached for the phone. "Forward me the checklist. I will call him."

They left with less than they had come for and more than Gloria would have admitted she had given. In the hallway Omar stopped so abruptly Leela nearly walked into him. His phone had vibrated.

Mohit had answered the email he had just resent with Gloria copied.

I see you've expanded the audience on this. That was unnecessary. I'm still in external meetings. We can speak at 3:30 about the letter and, more importantly, about the broader issue of judgment.

Omar stared at the screen. Then he barked out a laugh so dry it made two undergraduates glance over from the water fountain.

"The broader issue," he said. "Of course. God forbid the narrow issue of whether I remain legally legible."

Leela read the message over his shoulder. Omar's mouth went flat and he stopped walking. Mohit had finally put enough on the screen to make the leverage visible: no signature before 3:30, and no relief before Omar sat through whatever apology, promise, or submission Mohit had decided to collect.

"Do you have the old letter on you?" she said.

Omar looked at her. "Yes."

"Show me."

They went back across the quad more quickly. Snow had started again, not in flakes exactly but in hard white grains that clicked against coat fabric. Omar dug through the freezer bag while they walked and produced a prior funding letter in a plastic sleeve. He had labeled the top with a strip of masking tape in neat block letters: SPRING 2025 - APPROVED. Of course he had. Omar's version of panic still archived itself.

In the sixth-floor office, Leela spread the old letter, the current draft, and Gloria's checklist across the narrow desk surface. The old one had exactly what Gloria said it would: departmental letterhead, one paragraph of reassurance, the dollar figure, the tuition line, Mohit's signature in dark blue script above his typed title. It was amazing how small a weapon could look once formatted correctly.

"This is forgery," Omar said.

"Yes."

"I am aware you know that. I am saying it because one of us should."

Leela kept looking at the page. "Did he ask you for anything specific at three-thirty?"

"Not yet."

"He will."

Omar rubbed a hand over his face. "I know."

He sank into the chair across from her desk and went suddenly, visibly cold. The office heat was unreliable and the walk back had dampened his trouser cuffs, but the chill in him had plainly moved past weather. He took the packet of broken biscuits out of the freezer bag, stared at it as if he had never seen it before, then set it down unopened.

"I hate that this works," he said. "I hate that all of this works. The letter is one page. The whole architecture is one page. That is the joke."

Leela heard herself say, "Ben uploads half his forms."

Omar looked up sharply. "No."

"He has access to Mohit's office and probably the faculty side of the portal."

"No."

"Then what? You go to the three-thirty meeting, he takes you apart for twenty minutes, and if he feels satisfied maybe he signs before four-thirty and maybe he doesn't."

"That's still less insane than involving Ben."

"It's less degrading," Leela said. "I'm not sure it's less insane."

She already knew where her mind had gone because it had gone there with obscene speed. Ben ran errands for Mohit. Ben formatted slide decks and room requests and whatever else could be disguised as mentorship when passed downward. Ben wanted proximity more than dignity and authorship more than sleep. The lab had spent months learning each other's vulnerabilities as if that were just another research method. This one was simply easier to name.

Omar understood what she was thinking and hated it on sight.

"Absolutely not," he said again, but there was less force in it now because the clock on her computer said 1:47 and time had started eating principle by the minute.

"Do you want the letter filed?"

He looked at the highlighted checklist. At the old signature. At the current draft with Mohit's comments still embedded.

"I want not to need any of this," he said.

"That wasn't one of the options this morning."

Down the hall somebody started laughing too loudly at something on speakerphone, the bright dead sound of people not yet in trouble. Leela picked up the current draft. Mohit had altered almost nothing. He had changed `will receive` to `is expected to receive`, moved the amount down a line, and added one sentence about satisfactory academic progress, as if the money were a reward for character rather than payroll.

She knew this voice. She had spent years helping him refine it in smaller ways, drafting bullet points for recommendation letters, smoothing awkward phrasing in grant materials, listening while he rehearsed compassion into language precise enough to travel. She knew where he placed warmth and where he hid the knife.

"Come on," she said.

Ben was in the basement lab, predictably close to both Mohit's office and the printer that jammed every other day. A mushroom-shaped humidifier puffed beside his monitor because winter heat made his nose bleed, a fact he had once explained to the room in gruesome and unnecessary detail. Today the little plastic cap was glowing green. Ben was collating flyers for some sponsor visit, lining the stacks so precisely they looked ironed.

He straightened when he saw them. "Hey. What's up?"

Leela did not waste time easing into it. "We need Mohit's faculty portal access."

Ben gave a startled laugh. "What?"

"Omar's funding letter hasn't been signed. It has to be uploaded by four-thirty."

The laugh died. His eyes flicked to Omar, then back to Leela. "Okay, but if Mohit hasn't signed it, then I can't just, like, do a fake admin move. That's insane."

"You do admin moves for him all the time."

"Not fake ones."

Omar said, very calm now in the way he became when panic had burned through and left only clean edges, "He's withholding the signature to force a private meeting. The document itself is substantively complete. I need it filed today."

Ben shifted his weight. "I mean, I get that it's bad. I'm not saying it's not bad. I'm saying if he sees I touched the portal, that's directly traceable to me."

Ben's gaze flicked to Mohit's office door and then back to Leela. He was not arguing about ethics anymore. He was calculating how directly the click could be traced to him.

Leela tried one more version of decency. "He put you there because he trusts you. Use that for something that isn't pathetic."

Ben flushed. "That's not fair."

"No," Omar said. "It isn't."

Leela had the useless thought that the two of them might join on that, on fairness or injury or any of the old exhausted nouns people used before somebody had to actually do something. Then Ben said, "I've got qualifying review in six weeks."

Leela almost snapped that first-years barely had qualifying review so much as they had a dressed-up check-in, but Ben kept talking, faster now that he had started.

"Mohit keeps saying I need something visible by then," he said. "Not errands. Not being helpful. A real paper line, or summer support becomes a conversation."

The sentence told her what she needed. Ben was not asking whether this was wrong. He was asking what wrong could buy.

"And if this ever gets audited, I'm not just the kid who clicked one box today," he went on. "He had me push a visiting-scholar intake from his office last week and certify two reimbursement forms in December from his login. He said that's normal. If this goes bad, it goes bad like a pattern."

Omar's face changed at that, not surprise exactly. It looked like recognition with one more layer taken off it.

Leela said, "Then stop pretending this is the first clean thing you're about to dirty."

Ben gave a small, wounded laugh. "That's not much of a sales pitch."

"The workshop extension of my robustness paper isn't hypothetical," she said. "I've already got a live draft. You help me now, and you go on it as third author. Not acknowledgments. Author. The reproducibility appendix and evaluation harness go under your name, and I send you the draft tonight."

Omar turned to her so fast his chair scraped the floor. "Leela."

She did not look at him. Ben's whole face changed, not into trust, exactly, but into the bright greedy attention Mohit could summon from him in seconds. It made him look younger and worse.

"Third out of how many?" he said.

"Three."

Ben kept staring. "And you don't use the other part of this against me."

Leela let the silence sit long enough for him to hear what she was choosing not to say. "Help now, and today stays about Omar. Keep stalling, and I stop making that distinction for you."

Ben swallowed. The humidifier puffed once between them like a cartoon cloud. "Could you put the paper part in writing?"

Ben wet his lips and kept looking at her, waiting for the promise in a form he could cash. Leela felt something cold and efficient settle into place inside her, something she recognized too late because it had been standing next to Mohit all year.

"I'll email you tonight," she said. "You help now."

Ben looked at Omar, maybe hoping for absolution from the person in actual danger. Omar's mouth tightened.

"Do it or don't," he said. "I'm done helping other people feel moral while my life is administrative theater."

That did it. Ben exhaled through his nose, nodded once, and stood.

"His office computer is probably still logged in," he said. "He had me upload a visiting scholar form from there last week. If the session timed out, I can't get through two-factor. If it didn't, maybe. And if anyone asks, I was never in here."

"Maybe is enough," Leela said.

Mohit's office was unlocked because of course it was; people like him believed openness was proof of innocence. The room smelled faintly of cedar and printer toner. His desk was immaculate except for a pile of donor packets clipped with black binder clips and a bowl of the expensive wrapped chocolates he never ate himself. Ben woke the iMac. Haviland's blue login banner flashed, hung long enough to stop all three of them cold, then disappeared into an already-open browser session.

"Still live," Ben said, with equal parts relief and horror.

Leela moved beside him. "Find the draft."

Ben opened the shared drive. There were folders nested inside folders with names that tried and failed to look orderly. `travel_forms`. `donor_followups`. `intl_students`. `misc_admin_old`. Inside `intl_students` Omar's paperwork sat in a little midden of other people's dependencies: `omar_support_spring2025_signed.pdf`, `omar_support_draft_jan2026_v2.docx`, `chen_ds2019_update.pdf`, `fee_remission boilerplate.docx`, `scan from printer 14.pdf`.

"Jesus," Ben said softly.

"Open the draft."

The Word document appeared, already half-written in Mohit's voice because Omar had learned, as all of them had, to ghost the sentence rhythms faculty found easiest to sign. Leela read fast. The end date was still August 15 from some older payroll template, and the health-insurance sentence had vanished entirely. She fixed the dates and put the fee remission back in full. She kept the parts Gloria had effectively approved: amount, appointment period, good standing. She stripped out the little hesitations Mohit had added only to remind Omar who controlled certainty. Then she changed `I am pleased to confirm` to `This is to confirm` because she knew that cadence would matter. It was less generous, more official, and closer to him.

"Annualized amount?" she said.

"Thirty-six thousand," Omar answered immediately. "Tuition and mandatory fees covered. Health insurance through the graduate appointment."

He was standing behind her now, leaning so close she could feel the chill still coming off his coat sleeve. His fear had narrowed him into usefulness. He was reading each line as if bad phrasing itself could get him deported.

Ben hovered by the door. "If he checks version history, this is bad."

"If he checks version history," Leela said, "we'll already have gotten what we need."

"That isn't reassuring."

"It wasn't meant to be."

She left the cursor at the blank signature line. It looked accusatory even on Mohit's screen.

Ben said, "There has to be a scan."

No tidy `signature_assets` folder waited for them, and no helpful PNG hid under a vanity filename. Ben searched `sign`, then `mohit`, then `blue` and got donor headshots, a conference logo, and nothing they could use. Finally he opened the old signed letter. Mohit's signature sat in dark blue above the typed title, flattened into the PDF like part of the page. The room was very quiet except for the fan in the iMac and the muted hiss of the hallway heater.

"Can you pull it off that?" Omar said.

"Maybe."

The first screenshot took half the closing paragraph with it. The second turned to mush when Ben resized it. Then the office phone rang, one abrupt bureaucratic blast that made all three of them stop breathing until it clicked over to voicemail. Ben swore under his breath. Leela took the mouse, zoomed the old PDF to four hundred percent, cropped until only the blue loops remained with a strip of white around them, and pasted it into the draft. On letterhead the rough edges almost disappeared. That was as clean as they were going to get.

Mohit's name dropped onto the page in dark blue script, smooth and practiced, the same stroke Leela had seen on recommendation letters and reimbursement approvals and a dozen other small permissions that added up to adult life in the university. On this page it looked obscene, not because it was false exactly, but because it was true in the wrong direction. They all knew he would have signed eventually if the afternoon had gone the way he wanted.

Leela adjusted the placement by two millimeters. Her hand did not shake until after.

She exported the file as a PDF.

"Open the portal," she said.

The Haviland International Student Services site was not waiting helpfully in a tab. Ben found the faculty dashboard buried under donor calendars and a half-finished recommendation letter. Omar's name sat under Pending Actions with a red status marker beside Funding Verification. Everything else was green. Passport copy received. Enrollment confirmed. Current address verified. Financial documentation pending. A life arranged into check boxes by people who would never have to carry it.

Ben dragged the PDF into the upload field. The site flashed an orange banner: REQUEST TYPE MISMATCH.

"What now?" he said.

Omar leaned in so hard his knuckles hit the desk. Ben had left the request menu on `Initial Sponsorship`, whatever form Mohit had last touched.

"No," Omar said. "Continuing appointment. Spring only. If you send it to initial review, it goes to the wrong queue."

Ben changed the dropdown and uploaded again. The progress bar crawled. Twenty-two percent. Fifty-one. Eighty-nine. Then it stalled.

"Don't do this," Omar said to the screen, very softly, as if soothing an animal.

The progress bar finished. A new field appeared: Certify that the attached document accurately reflects committed support for the period listed.

Ben took his hand off the mouse. "That's the part that's directly on me."

Leela turned to him. "Your ID is already all over his admin trail. This one at least buys you something. Third author. The appendix is yours. Click the box."

He looked sick. The green humidifier glow had followed him only in her mind; here under Mohit's office lights he was just a twenty-four-year-old in an expensive jacket trying to decide what sort of cowardice had the better resale value.

"Third author?" he said.

Leela heard Omar make a sound behind her, not quite disbelief anymore. More like the disgust you gave a street trick you could not afford not to use.

"Yes," she said.

"And not, like, acknowledgments."

"Author."

Ben clicked the box.

Then he clicked Submit.

The page stalled long enough for Leela to hear her own breathing and Omar's and the heater ticking in the wall. Then it reloaded and the red marker beside Funding Verification turned green.

Submitted by faculty recommender at 4:23 p.m.

Omar let out a breath so hard he had to sit down on the visitor chair by the bookcase. He did not lower himself so much as fold without warning, as if his knees had revised themselves without consulting him. He pressed the heel of his hand to one eye and laughed once more, this time thin with shock.

"That might be the ugliest document I've ever loved," he said.

His phone buzzed. Then Leela's. The student-side system had sent an automatic receipt to Omar and a duplicate notification to the faculty email on file. She pictured Mohit somewhere across campus in a donor meeting, feeling his pocket vibrate and knowing before he looked exactly what had happened.

Ben was still standing by the desk. "Could you send the email now?" he asked. "Just so it's concrete."

Leela turned and stared at him. He flushed again, but he did not back down. Of course he didn't. He had learned from the same teacher she had.

"You really want this timestamped in the next thirty seconds," she said.

"I want to know I'm not the only idiot in the room."

That, at least, was honest.

She sat at Mohit's desk because there was nowhere else to do it and opened her own email on the edge of the iMac screen. Her wrist brace brushed the keyboard. The office smelled more strongly of cedar now, or maybe she had stopped noticing everything else. She typed Ben's address. June came to mind immediately, along with the paper that already had too much rot in it and the ease with which authorship had turned into a currency loose enough to tip with.

Then she wrote:

Ben,

Per our conversation, when I prepare the workshop extension of the robustness paper this spring, I will include you as third author and credit you for the reproducibility appendix and evaluation harness updates. I will send the current draft tonight.

Leela

It was explicit, disgusting, and entirely legible. Ben leaned in just enough to verify the line. Omar said nothing. The green status marker stayed green.

She hit send.

In the outbox, the message to Ben sat directly above the automatic receipt confirming that Omar Elmasry's updated funding materials had moved from INCOMPLETE to SUBMITTED. On the screen the two emails looked as if they had been generated by the same office. They had solved the four-thirty problem. The edge of Leela's wrist brace had pressed a red groove into her skin, and what they had actually started would take the rest of the week.
