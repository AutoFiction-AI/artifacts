# Chapter 6

The flip phone had spent Sunday afternoon vibrating across the kitchen counter like an angry insect, bumping the sugar bowl a quarter inch at a time. By the third voicemail Nate understood that Sunday dinner was not a suggestion. Renata Altieri's messages had escalated in under two hours from checking in to prosecution. Nate had missed the last two Sundays because nobody had informed him that being Dominic meant reporting to a small colonial somewhere across Bridgeport to eat until you were forgiven for whatever you had done that week. Renata had apparently decided the grace period was over.

The first message asked where he was. The second wanted to know if Kassos Development had started charging overtime for neglecting your mother. The third informed him that if she had to keep the pork in the oven one more hour it would turn to rope and he could choke on it. Nate listened to all three while standing in Dominic's kitchen with his keys in one hand and no idea what version of son he was supposed to bring.

He drove with the air conditioning on high and still arrived with his shirt sticking between his shoulder blades. The August heat sat low over the city and made everything feel touched already, the steering wheel tacky, the seatbelt damp where it crossed his neck. Dominic's Honda Civic rattled over patched streets and past blocks of triple-deckers, corner stores, chain-link fences with plastic grocery bags caught in them like surrender flags. Nate had built Altieri in his head for years without ever admitting he was building him. The house in those private renderings had always been big and quiet, something with stone lions and a foyer that smelled like money and floor polish, the kind of place where boys learned early that other people's losses were just paperwork. He turned onto Renata's street and found a row of small colonials with narrow driveways, short lawns, and cheap white siding gone a little dull under the sun.

The Altieri house had a bent screen door, a concrete birdbath with no bird in it, and two tomato plants in buckets by the front steps. One of the tomato cages had been fixed with what looked like a coat hanger. There was a statue of the Virgin Mary half-hidden in the hostas, blue paint peeling from the mantle. A box fan sat in the front window doing its useless best. Nate killed the engine and stayed there a second too long, looking at the house and feeling stupidly cheated, like he had spent years preparing for the wrong movie.

The front door opened before he reached it.

Renata came out through the screen like she had been watching for his car from the window. She was short and solid and fast, gray hair sprayed into shape, flowered apron over a black dress, one forearm dusted with flour clear up to the elbow. A pencil was tucked into the back of her hair. She caught his face in both hands so hard his teeth clicked.

"Look at you. Jesus, Dominic, stand still. Why am I seeing bones here? Turn your head. No, the other way. You lost weight."

"I'm okay, Ma."

"You're not okay. You got shadows under your eyes. What are they doing to you over there, those people? You don't call, you don't come by, you look like somebody forgot to feed you. Come in. Wipe your feet. Not there, there. I just washed."

She kissed one cheek, then the other, then shoved him through the doorway with a flat hand between his shoulder blades. Nate barely had time to pull the screen shut behind him before the house hit him all at once: garlic and pork fat and baking bread, a cooked-tomato sweetness in the air under the sharper espresso smell, the rattle of the box fan in the front room, a game show on low volume from the television, a pot lid clicking against steam somewhere behind Renata's shoulder. The kitchen was visible straight down the hall and was already crowded with heat.

Every flat surface between the door and the kitchen held a framed face. School pictures. Communion pictures. Wedding pictures. Dominic and Tommy in Little League uniforms. Dominic in what looked like a high school graduation gown, expression caught between pride and apology. Tommy at maybe ten with a missing front tooth and a fish hanging from one fist, grinning like the fish had personally insulted him. Renata younger and darker-haired in front of a bakery counter, holding up a sheet cake big enough to bury a child under frosting. A photo of Dominic and Sarah at somebody else's wedding, Sarah laughing at something outside the frame while Dominic looked at her instead of the camera. Nate took all of it in with the desperate concentration of a man trying to build a family tree from wall decor before the test started.

"Where's Sarah?" Renata called over her shoulder.

"She had school stuff."

"On a Sunday."

"Yeah. Papers."

"Those people never stop. Fine. Fine. Next week bring her. Put your keys there, not on the good table, are you crazy? There. Sit down. No, don't sit there, I need that chair. Sit there."

The house was not large enough for her voice and apparently never had been. It pushed ahead of her into every room. Nate obeyed the pointing. The kitchen table was too big for the kitchen, a heavy oval that had to be squeezed past sideways if you wanted to get from the stove to the back door. Three mismatched chairs fit around it cleanly and the fourth had one leg wrapped at the base in gray duct tape. On the counter sat a bakery order pad, a cordless phone, a bowl of peaches beginning to freckle, and two envelopes under a magnet clip, one from the electric company and one from Saint Margaret's Church. A dish towel hung off the oven handle. Another one had been thrown over the back of a chair. Renata picked both up, rejected both, and wiped her hands on her apron instead.

"Take that cover off the bread," she said. "No, the foil. What are you, half asleep?"

Nate peeled foil back from a loaf the size of a brick. Steam breathed out. The crust crackled.

"You eat lunch?"

"Yeah."

"What'd you eat?"

He had, in fact, eaten lunch. He just could not remember what Dominic would have admitted to eating under maternal review. "A sandwich."

"What sandwich?"

"Turkey."

Renata gave him a look that suggested turkey was not a lunch but a symptom. She turned back to the stove and lifted the lid from a wide enamel pot. Tomato sauce popped. Thick ribbons of pork fat gleamed at the surface. She dipped a spoon, blew on it once, and shoved it at him without turning around.

"Taste."

He tasted. Garlic, tomato, wine, salt, something deeper and sweeter from the meat that had been in there all day coming apart in its own patience. The spoon burned the tip of his tongue.

"Good," he said.

"Good," Renata repeated. "I know it's good. Needs salt?"

Nate had no idea. "Maybe a little?"

She frowned at him, then pinched some in with her fingers. "See? That's why I ask. Your palate's not dead yet."

There was nowhere in the house to stand without learning something. The refrigerator was plastered with snapshots and business cards and a school fundraiser flyer for a spaghetti supper at Saint Margaret's. The wall by the kitchen phone held a paper calendar with bakery pickups written in blue ink and several birthdays circled hard enough to dent the paper. Dominic's was marked with a star and a question mark beside it, as if Renata had been planning something complicated. The drawer pulls had been replaced one at a time over the years with whatever fit. The linoleum had a hairline crack near the sink that someone had tried to stop from spreading with clear tape. It was a house of repairs, not upgrades. Nate kept noticing the same thing over and over in different forms: somebody here fixed things instead of replacing them.

"Get the plates," Renata said.

He froze. "Which plates?"

"The good dinner plates. Jesus Christ, Dominic."

Before he could fail, he was already opening the second cabinet to the left of the sink, not the first, not the third. Inside sat a stack of heavy cream-colored plates with a green border.

He stopped with one plate halfway out, skin prickling under his shirt. It happened again like that, not memory exactly, more like stepping into grooves Dominic had worn so deep the house still expected him to follow them.

Renata either didn't notice or didn't care. "Four," she said. "Not those little ones, the big ones. Tommy'll pretend he's not hungry and then eat like a wolf."

"Tommy's coming?"

She snorted without looking up from the pot. "Of course Tommy's coming. Late, but yes. He called an hour ago with traffic, which means nothing, because traffic from where? From the moon? Put the forks down, points up. No, that's knives. You know what, move."

She shouldered him gently out of the way and redid half the place settings in six seconds. Nate watched and tried to arrange his face into the expression of a son accustomed to this level of corrective management. It helped that Dominic, he was learning, had apparently grown up inside a system where questions were less useful than standing still until someone told you the correct thing.

Renata kept talking. About the bakery. About a woman who wanted seeded rolls without seeds. About Mrs. Caruso's grandson who had tattooed a snake on his neck and expected people not to form opinions. About a cousin's wedding in September where the flowers were going to be all wrong because the bride had no taste and too much confidence. Nate nodded when it seemed safe. When she asked direct questions he answered as little as possible and, when possible, redirected.

"How's the bakery?" he asked, because that was his oldest trick as himself and apparently still the only one he trusted.

"Busy. Humid. The proofing room's a nightmare in this weather. Frankie burned six trays of pignoli yesterday because he thinks a cigarette break is part of the baking process. I should fire him. I won't. His mother'll kill me. Take that salad out of the fridge."

He took out the salad.

"Bigger bowl. Why would I mean that bowl? What are we, animals?"

It turned out the easiest part of being Dominic in this house was that Renata left almost no dead air for him to fill. Sarah noticed pauses. Victor used them. Renata bulldozed straight across them carrying food. If he nodded, carried things, and said "yeah" in the right places, the machinery of the afternoon kept moving.

Still, the pressure was different from anywhere else. At the office he was faking competence. At home with Sarah he was faking intimacy. Here he was faking history. Every object around him had known Dominic longer than Nate had been alive in his own body. The dent in the baseboard by the back door, the school pictures, the star on the calendar, the cabinet his hand had opened without permission. The whole house was a witness box.

Renata shoved a glass of red wine at him. "Drink. Not too fast. Sit for one minute before Tommy gets here and ruins the peace."

Nate sat. The kitchen chair complained under him. From here he could see into the front room where a crocheted throw had slipped half off the couch and a stack of bakery boxes sat by the doorway waiting to be returned or reused. A small shelf held more photographs, one of Dominic and Tommy in suits at what looked like a confirmation, both of them red-faced and miserable, Renata between them smiling like she had won something. Nate had spent years reducing Dominic Altieri to a signature block and a photograph on a company website. The afghan sliding off the couch and the bakery boxes by the door kept getting in the way of that.

The doorbell rang.

Renata closed her eyes for half a second. "There. The prince."

* * *

Tommy came in with a white pastry box tied in red string and enough energy for three people. He was thinner than Dominic in a way that made Dominic's medium build read suddenly as steadiness rather than softness. Same dark hair, same family nose, but everything on Tommy moved faster, eyebrows, hands, mouth. His blue dress shirt had lost the day's argument with the heat and his tie hung loose at his throat. There was a healing scrape near his jaw and a cheap gold pinky ring that he kept spinning with his thumb between gestures, quick enough that it almost disappeared. He smelled like mint gum over beer.

"Before anybody says anything," he announced, lifting the box like evidence, "I brought dessert."

Renata stared at the box. "From where."

"A bakery."

"From where, Thomas."

"A place on Madison."

"So not a bakery."

"It's a bakery. They sell baked goods. That's in the charter."

"Throw it out."

"Ma."

"Throw it out."

"They're cannoli, not anthrax."

"From a place on Madison." She put a hand to her chest. "You walk into my house with enemy pastry."

Tommy kissed her cheek before she could slap the box out of his hands. "You look beautiful. Younger every week."

"You smell like beer."

"Beer is bread, technically. I'm supporting the family business."

"You're impossible."

"And yet beloved."

He grinned over her shoulder at Nate and came in for a hug Nate had no chance of avoiding. Tommy slapped his back twice, hard. Up close he looked more worn than the performance wanted to admit. The scrape on his jaw was fresh. His eyes were bright in the oversharpened way of someone running on nerves and charm.

"Dom. Look at this guy. You still alive?"

"Apparently."

"Strong answer. Low energy, but strong."

Renata took the cannoli box from him as if it might leak poison onto the counter. Tommy watched her set it down and then looked back at Nate with a small jerk of the chin toward the hallway.

"Hey, gimme a hand for a sec?"

The line was casual enough that Renata didn't even turn. Or maybe she did hear something in it and chose not to stop it. She was already banging cabinet doors and complaining to the air about rival ricotta. Nate followed Tommy out through the hall and onto the small back porch, where the evening heat still sat against the siding. Tommy pulled the screen shut behind them. The metal latch clicked. His grin stayed in place for another second, then loosened.

"So," he said. "Lemme just say upfront, I know, and I hate it, and if I had literally any better play, I'd be doing that instead."

Nate leaned against the rail. The wood was sticky with old humidity and bad paint. "What happened?"

"A little run of bad luck."

"That's not a thing that happened."

"Okay, no, fine, a sequence. A chain of events. A whole parade of dumb decisions, but with context, Dom. Important context."

"Tommy."

"I need fifteen hundred."

Nate looked at him. Tommy held up both hands like he could stop the number from sounding like itself.

"I know. I know what it sounds like. It sounds like fifteen hundred dollars. That's because it's fifteen hundred dollars. But it's not forever. It's bridge money. It's temporary. I'm between outcomes. I can square it next month."

"For what."

"Come on."

"For what?"

Tommy exhaled through his nose and looked past Nate into the yard, where a grill cover flapped once in the little breeze and settled again. "Cards, mostly. Some football. I got cute in a game behind Vinnie's and then I chased it with preseason like an asshole."

"It's August."

"Yeah, I know what month it is. Paulie Gallo doesn't care what month it is. He wants fifteen by midnight because it's his cousin's money and I already burned Wednesday, Saturday, and today. The scrape on my jaw? That was him keeping the conversation lively. He said if I show up light again, he takes the car and starts with fingers."

Fifteen hundred was bad. Tommy getting to it that smoothly was worse. No long preamble. No real embarrassment until after the number. He had walked this route before; the porch boards probably remembered his shoes.

"Fifteen hundred," Nate said.

"Yeah."

"Jesus."

"Not helping. Also, honestly, low estimate if I keep standing here talking to you instead of solving it."

"He knows where Ma lives?"

Tommy's ring spun once around his finger and stopped. For the first time since he had come through the door, his voice dropped instead of rising. "Yeah. I got picked up here once after dinner a couple months ago. I told him to wait at the curb and he came to the porch anyway. So now he knows."

That wasn't the whole story, probably not even most of it, but it was the part Tommy couldn't joke into haze. Nate thought of the scrape on Tommy's jaw, the gum over beer, the way Renata had not asked traffic from where as a real question because maybe she was tired of answers.

"I can't do fifteen," Nate said.

Tommy blinked, recalculating in real time. "Okay. All right. What can you do?"

Nate should have known Dominic's finances by then. He knew where the extra bath towels were, what brand of coffee Sarah bought, how long the drive to Kassos usually took if traffic on I-95 behaved. Money was fuzzier. Bills had been paid, checks had been written, and the account balance lived in fragments: an ATM receipt in the cup holder, a checkbook register in the glove compartment, numbers glimpsed and not retained because the rest of Dominic's life kept producing more urgent fires.

"Come here," he said.

They went down the steps to the driveway. The Civic had held the day's heat inside it. When Nate opened the driver's door, stale hot air pushed out smelling of vinyl, paper, and old coffee. He flipped open the glove compartment and came up with the checkbook from under the insurance card on the first try. Tommy hovered outside the door, bouncing once on the balls of his feet.

Nate flipped through the register. Rent. Gas. Stop & Shop. Cable. A debit card purchase he couldn't decode from the abbreviation. The balance, in Dominic's handwriting, sat a little above six hundred. Six-forty-something. One more line and the page would start with a four. There might be more in savings. There were credit cards in the wallet. But it was Sunday, 2005, and Nate still felt like online banking was a stunt somebody else could pull. Whatever money existed in theory was not the same as money he knew how to reach without setting off another part of Dominic's life.

"I can do six," he said.

"Six hundred?"

"That's what I said."

"I asked for fifteen."

"And I'm telling you six."

Tommy ran a hand over his mouth, thinking. For a second Nate expected a pitch, a harder sell, another loop around the story. Instead Tommy nodded once, fast.

"Okay. Okay, fine. Six is good. Six covers enough that Paulie doesn't come looking here tonight. Six buys me till Tuesday."

Nate braced the checkbook on the steering wheel and started writing. The paper had gone soft in the heat. His forearm stuck to the vinyl. Six hundred and 00/100 took up more room on the line than it should have. The pen bit hard enough to emboss the next blank check underneath, dragged once through the last name, and then Dominic Altieri sat there anyway, neat and practiced.

Tommy didn't snatch at it. He waited one second, too careful for a man asking his brother to burn grocery money, then took the check by the corners. The ink was still wet enough to leave a blue smear on the side of his thumb when he folded it.

"You sure?"

"No."

"Fair."

"This is it, Tommy."

"Yeah. I know. I know."

Tommy tucked the wallet away and then, reflexively, the grin came back online, just enough to function. "You're a prince, you know that? Ugly prince, but still. Ma's right, by the way. You look like shit."

"Good to know."

"I'm serious. You sick?"

The question landed closer to truth than Tommy knew. Nate defaulted to the oldest safe move he had. "How bad is this really?"

Tommy's eyes slid away. He looked out toward the street where somebody's sprinkler ticked over dry grass. "Bad enough that if I don't put cash in his hand tonight, next stop is this house. He already knows where Ma lives. That's what the six does."

"That means it's not handled."

"It means he stays off her porch till Tuesday. Right now that's handled. Eat dinner before she comes out here and baptizes us both in gravy."

He clapped Nate on the shoulder and headed back inside before Nate could stop him. The performance had resumed, but not perfectly. Nate stayed by the open car door with the checkbook on his knee. On the stub, TOMMY - 600.00 pushed the balance down into the forties. From the porch Tommy called, "Tuesday," like that was the same thing as solved. Nate slid the checkbook back under the insurance card and followed him in.

At the table Renata had already served the first course and was pretending not to notice the extra five minutes. Tommy slid into his chair and crossed himself. Renata and Nate did the same. Nate was a half-beat late and hoped that in a family this noisy, religion had enough latency to cover him.

"Eat," Renata said.

"I'm sitting down," Tommy said.

"You can sit and eat. You're not in a hospital."

"Yet," Tommy said, and shoved bread at Nate. "Take that before she says you're wasting away again."

"He is wasting away," Renata said. "Look at his face."

"My face is fine," Nate said.

"Your face is corporate now."

"That's not a medical condition."

"It should be."

The food arrived in waves. Braciole cut thick enough to need a real knife. A bowl of rigatoni slick with sauce. Roasted potatoes with rosemary. Salad dressed more aggressively than the salad had consented to. Bread warm enough to fog the butter dish. Renata served as if portion size were a moral argument and she intended to win. Nate tried to say that was enough after the second spoonful of pasta and learned the phrase had no force in this house. Renata heard it the way weather heard complaint.

"Why aren't you eating?" she asked him after building a plate heavy enough to anchor a tent.

"I'm eating."

"You peck. You're pecking."

"He's pacing himself," Tommy said around a mouthful of bread. "Strategic."

"Nobody asked you."

"You should, though. I got instincts."

"Your instincts belong in jail."

Tommy laughed and reached for more potatoes. The laugh came easy. Too easy. Nate could already see the way Tommy's desperation hid inside motion. He interrupted because if he stopped interrupting the silence might say something worse.

For long stretches Nate barely needed to speak. Renata narrated the bakery's entire week, stopping only to accuse Tommy of not listening, at which point Tommy defended himself by repeating the last three nouns she had used in the wrong order. Tommy answered with stories from somewhere near the truth about a guy who had tried to pay a bookmaker with concert tickets, a waitress in Milford who could throw a customer out without spilling coffee, a car that had died on the Merritt because somebody had mistaken the gas gauge for a suggestion. Renata corrected the facts of stories she could not possibly have witnessed. Tommy objected to the corrections on principle. Nate ate and took inventory.

By the second helping he knew Renata saved empty butter tubs for leftovers and kept them nested under the sink in perfect stacks. Tommy hated olives and picked them from the salad one by one, lining them up on the bread plate without seeming aware he was doing it. Dominic had apparently once tried to make espresso for Christmas breakfast and produced something Renata still referred to, three years later, as "that black poison." A cousin named Gina was marrying a man nobody trusted because he said "broil" like "broy-ul." Nate took it all in the way someone learned the rules of a game by coming in late and trying not to look lost.

"You gonna tell Ma where you were last night?" Nate asked Tommy once, before he could stop himself.

Tommy pointed his fork at him. "This from you, Mr. No-Show?"

"I had work."

"Yeah, and I had civic obligations."

Renata made a sound deep in her throat that might have been a prayer or a threat. "If your civic obligations have lipstick on them, I don't wanna hear it."

"Why's it always lipstick? Why can't my bad decisions be dignified?"

"Because I know you."

"That's profiling."

"Eat."

Tommy ate.

Nate kept waiting for the trapdoor under the meal, the question only Dominic could answer, the family anecdote that would expose him in one bright humiliating flash. A few came close. Old vacations. Old fights. Old disasters retold with the lazy confidence of people who assumed everybody at the table had been there. Nate smiled when Tommy smiled, laughed a little late, said "Jesus, yeah," and let the others do most of the remembering around him. The strange part was that it worked better here than anywhere else. In a room where everybody cut everybody off and half the stories started in the middle, incomplete knowledge passed for participation.

But every time he looked up there was the electric bill under the clip, the chair leg knocking his shin, Tommy's cheap tie gone limp in the heat.

Renata turned on him midway through the second helping of pasta. "So. When're you marrying Sarah."

He nearly dropped his fork.

Tommy barked a laugh. "There it is."

"What?" Renata said. "It's a normal question. They're living in sin already, what's one more step."

"Ma," Nate said.

"What ma. You love her?"

"Sure."

"Sure," Tommy repeated. "Strong start."

"Shut up."

"I mean, if that's the proposal, maybe workshop it."

Renata stabbed the air with her spoon. "I'm serious. She's a good girl. Smart. Too thin, but that's not her fault, that's American culture. She puts up with both of you, which qualifies her for sainthood. So."

Nate put his fork down because holding it suddenly seemed too complicated. "We're good."

"That's not what I asked."

"We're fine."

"You hear this?" Tommy said to no one. "The man sounds like a hostage video."

"At least he has a job," Renata shot back.

"Oh, here we go."

"Here we go what. You want me to lie? You want me to say you're a provider?"

"I provide excitement."

"Ulcers, maybe."

Tommy spread his hands and looked at Nate as if asking for a fair tribunal. Nate gave him nothing because there was nothing safe to give. He could not very well explain that every marriage question in the room felt like somebody had put jumper cables on the basic fact of what he had done. Sarah's face flashed for a second, not in any romantic movie way. Just the practical fact of her in the apartment, barefoot in the kitchen, asking if he wanted more rice. The woman in Renata's sentence was the same woman Nate had been lying to in bed for weeks. The room felt smaller.

"We're okay," he said finally, because vague emotional language had saved him before.

Renata watched him for a second longer than he liked. Then, true to form, she redirected straight into the physical.

"Take more potatoes."

"I don't need more potatoes."

"You need everything. Take."

She spooned them on his plate before he could refuse. The conversation moved on, because in this house nothing stayed on one burner long unless it was sauce.

After dinner, Tommy insisted the cannoli from Madison were "actually decent" and Renata insisted they tasted like sugared drywall and then ate half of one while listing its flaws. Nate cleared plates because standing still felt suspicious. At the sink Renata washed, Nate dried, Tommy leaned in the doorway with espresso and contributed no labor whatsoever.

"You know what's criminal?" Tommy said. "Nobody respects dessert anymore. Everybody wants low-carb. Everybody wants fruit. Fruit's breakfast pretending it's not."

"Then eat fruit and cry," Renata said.

"That's not a dessert philosophy."

"Your whole life isn't a philosophy."

"That one hurt."

"Good."

Nate stacked plates in the right cabinet without having to think about it. By the third one, the ease of it was starting to get on his nerves. Renata handed him another without looking. Tommy kept talking, circling stories, landing nowhere, restarting. At one point he went quiet in the middle of stirring sugar into his espresso, staring down into the cup as if he could read the next few days there. Then he caught Nate noticing and snapped back up.

"You want the last one?" Tommy asked, lifting a cannoli.

"Nah."

"See? Wasting away."

"Take it home," Renata said. "And don't leave the box in the car this time, you had ants for a week."

"That happened one time."

"One time too many."

"Noted."

He said it lightly, but he tucked the box under his arm with more care than the joke warranted. When he kissed Renata goodbye, it was quick and real. He kissed Nate's cheek too, unexpectedly, the way a brother might if the family had always done affection at speed so nobody had to announce it.

"Thanks," he said, so quietly it almost got lost under the running water.

Then he was gone down the front walk, engine starting a moment later, tires crunching over the loose gravel at the curb.

When Tommy left, the house lost one of its frequencies. The television in the other room sounded farther away. The box fan came back into the room. Renata moved slower as she packed leftovers into butter tubs and old bakery containers, pressing lids down with both thumbs until each one snapped shut.

"Take this," she said. "And this. Sarah'll eat that tomorrow. No, not that one, that's for me. This one. Here."

Nate stood by the door holding two warm containers against his chest. The front hall smelled less like dinner now and more like coffee and dish soap. Up close the family photos repeated the same faces in different bad lighting: Tommy in a clip-on tie, Dominic with sunburn across his nose, Renata in church clothes, everybody squinting at somebody else's camera. The frames nearest the kitchen had gone cloudy at the corners from years of steam.

Renata wiped her hands on a towel and came over to him. For the first time all evening she wasn't talking while moving toward the next task. She put a hand on his arm, then lifted the other to his cheek again, gentler now than at the door. Her thumb rested once against the stubble there, almost absentminded.

"You sat still tonight," she said. "Usually you're halfway out the door before the coffee."

He looked at her.

There was no accusation in it. No forensic precision the way Sarah sometimes watched him now, assembling a case from drawer choices and silences. Renata sounded relieved.

Sarah had said softer. Renata had come at the same thing sideways.

"Yeah?" he said.

"Yeah." Renata shrugged, like the thing was obvious and not worth dressing up. "You dried dishes. Usually I turn around and you're gone while the coffee cups are still in the sink. Tonight I had to tell you to go home. It's nice. That's all. Drive careful."

She kissed his cheek again and opened the door behind him before he could answer. Outside, the evening had cooled by maybe one degree. The screen door banged shut and then opened again because Renata had one more thing to say.

"Call your mother once in a while."

"I will."

"You better."

Then it shut for real.

Nate stood on the walkway with the containers warming his forearms through the thin plastic. Through the front window he could see Renata moving back toward the kitchen, already tidying, already doing something with her hands because there was always something to do. The box fan kept turning. Somewhere down the block a dog barked and somebody laughed too loud. He got into the Civic and set the leftovers on the passenger seat, where they tipped and then settled.

His shirt smelled like pork and coffee and dish soap.

The checkbook was still on the floor by the passenger seat where he'd dropped it, register open just enough to show the fresh stub: TOMMY, 600.00, the balance now starting with a four.

He backed out of the driveway and drove home with Renata's leftovers knocking softly against the passenger door. Tomorrow that same hand had to go back to Kassos, stay late, and touch the Eastbridge Plaza file again.
