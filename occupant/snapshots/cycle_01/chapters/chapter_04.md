# Chapter 4

Nate spent the first six workdays of Dominic Altieri's life calling in sick from a flip phone he still used like it might explode.

By Thursday Ray had stopped sounding sympathetic. By Friday he had stopped pretending this was funny. Monday morning, parked outside Kassos Development with Dominic's key ring sweating in his hand, Nate listened to Ray's last voicemail again because there was nothing else to do except go inside.

Come in or don't, Ray had said. But if you don't, don't make me keep lying for you.

The office occupied the second floor of a brick warehouse that had once held machine parts or textiles or whatever else old American buildings held before someone stripped the guts out and called the result loft-style. A rusted loading door still sat on the side of the building like an old scar. Somebody at Kassos had leaned into the history just enough to make it decorative. The lobby had exposed brick, black-and-white photos of tugboats on the harbor, and a potted ficus that had gone gray at the tips from office air and neglect. Up on the second floor the effect gave out. Carpet tile, fluorescent panels, beige desktop towers under desks, the hot dust smell of computers that had been on since dawn.

He sat in the Civic for another minute with the engine off, listening to the metal tick as it cooled. The July heat pressed through the windshield and turned the car into a damp box. Dominic's shirt stuck to his back. Sarah had watched him knot the tie that morning, then stepped in and redid it without comment, fingers quick and practiced at his throat. She had been in a green skirt and a sleeveless blouse, grading essays standing up at the kitchen counter while toast cooled beside her.

"You'll be fine," she'd said, not looking up from a paper. "You're just going to work. People do it every day."

He had almost laughed at that. He had almost said, I'm not scared of work, I'm scared of being murdered by Microsoft Outlook and a zoning board. Instead he'd said, "Yeah," because that was what Dominic said lately when he was trying not to sound like himself or sound like Nate or sound like somebody who had killed a man and then gotten stage fright.

He got out of the car.

The elevator had a hand-lettered OUT OF ORDER sign taped over the call button, so he took the stairs behind a man carrying a roll of blueprints and an Egg McMuffin. The man nodded at him with the blank office intimacy of somebody who knew a face and not much else.

"Dom. Back from the dead."

"Looks that way," Nate said.

The office floor opened up all at once: a large rectangle broken into islands of desks, metal filing cabinets, a conference room with cloudy glass, Victor's corner office beyond it. Phones rang in different keys. A fax machine screamed somewhere near the copier. Every desk had paper on it. Not a tasteful stack. Paper in leaning towers, paper rubber-banded into tubes, paper pinned with red tabs, paper clipped to legal pads, paper spreading out from keyboards like spill patterns. Nate's first irrational thought was that the entire company could have been solved by a decent search function.

"Jesus Christ."

Ray Dunlap came around the corner of a filing cabinet holding a yellow folder and a coffee mug with a chipped UConn logo. He was tall in the way some men stayed athletic long after the sport had left them, broad in the shoulders, softening through the middle, tie already loosened at eight-thirty. He shook two peppermints out of a little metal tin into his palm and chewed both at once.

"You look like shit," Ray said. "Good. Means everybody'll buy it."

"Thanks."

"Don't thank me. You owe me."

Ray said it flatly, not mean, just true. Then he clapped Nate once on the shoulder and steered him through the maze.

"Vic's got you at ten. He was going to do it at nine, but I told him you were probably still leaking from somewhere." Ray glanced sideways at him. "You remember Janice in reception? No? Fine. She took three calls from your mother and one from Sarah last week. Mrs. Kline wants the Easton package redone. Brennan's still screaming about wetlands at Seaview. Dreher's office wants the variance packet hand-delivered because apparently fax machines are beneath democracy. And if anybody asks, you were in bed with some kind of stomach flu that made you hallucinate but not enough to go to the hospital."

Nate nodded like those were all words from a language he spoke. "Right."

Ray stopped at a desk near the windows. "And this is yours, in case the fever took that too."

Dominic's desk was larger than Nate expected and neater than the rest of the room, which somehow made it worse. A beige monitor sat square in the center with Outlook open behind the screen saver. To one side: a black phone with six glowing buttons, a stack of pink phone-message slips, a brass letter opener shaped like a miniature sword. To the other: two tidy trays labeled IN and OUT, both full. A framed photo of Sarah on a windy beach with her hair in her mouth and one eye shut against the sun. A legal pad in Dominic's blocky handwriting. Three manila folders clipped together beneath a Post-it that said CALL AL RE: DELIVERY ACCESS. The familiarity of the arrangement, the fact that somebody had lived here in this exact geometry, made Nate's skin crawl harder than the mirror ever had.

Ray was still talking.

"I moved the Milford stuff to later in the week because you were gone and because I like myself. Easton has to go first. The Kline woman is already threatening to hire her nephew instead, and I don't want to spend my Tuesday listening to Vic explain why we lost a strip center in Easton to a kid with a fax machine and a hotmail account."

"Yeah."

"Don't yeah me. Are you actually back, or are you gonna stare at that phone like it's a snake and make me do your job all day?"

Nate looked at the phone. It had more lit buttons than a small aircraft. "I'm back."

Ray watched him for a second too long. "All right."

He put the yellow folder on the desk and flipped it open.

"Easton. Mrs. Kline hates the parking count. Wants more landscaping. Wants the loading lane shifted because her dry cleaner tenant thinks the trucks are going to hit customers. We already told her the lane can't move because of physics. You just need to clean up the packet for tonight and make sure the revised site plan gets copied full-size. Full-size. Not legal, not letter. Full-size."

Nate looked at the site plan as if brute force might make it legible. Boxes, arrows, hatch marks, a key full of symbols that meant nothing to him.

"Got it."

Ray kept his finger on the paper. "What does full-size mean?"

Nate looked up.

Ray's face didn't change. Peppermint, tired eyes, nothing dramatic. Just a man deciding whether the person in front of him was joking.

Nate said, "Eleven by seventeen?"

"No." Ray slid the plan toward himself, then back. "Twenty-four by thirty-six. Plotter room. Through there." He jerked his chin toward a side room Nate hadn't noticed. "Dom."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"I said I know."

That came out sharper than Nate meant it to. Ray leaned back a little, folder still open between them. Nate could feel the mistake sitting there. Dominic, from everything Nate had pieced together so far, did not snap at people before nine in the morning. Dominic definitely did not snap at Ray, who had apparently spent a week covering his ass.

Nate looked away first. "Sorry. I just still feel like crap."

Ray let the silence sit for a beat. Then he shut the folder.

"Fine. Then work slow and don't improvise." He tapped the legal pad. "Write things down if you have to."

"I'm writing things down."

"Good."

Ray walked off, then came back three steps later and dropped the peppermint tin on the desk.

"Keep those. Your breath still smells like death."

Nate watched him go. The tin said ALTOIDS on the lid, dented at one corner. It was the first gift he'd gotten in 2005 that wasn't also a trap.

He sat down.

The chair rolled more easily than he expected and sent him into the desk hard enough to rattle the phone. He caught himself with Dominic's hands on Dominic's blotter while two women at the next bank of desks discussed school supplies over the partition. Nobody looked over. Offices, he realized, were full of private humiliations too minor to interrupt anybody else's.

He picked up the legal pad. Dominic's handwriting from before the transfer occupied the top third of the page.

Call surveyor re: wetlands flags

Dreher packet by Thurs

Tommy - Friday? do not forget

The last line sat there in the same blunt block letters as the rest. Tommy. Friday. Nate had no idea what Friday had meant. He also did not love that the dead man had been reminding himself to call his brother while Nate had been using the brother's body to fake stomach cramps on a flip phone.

He turned to a fresh page and started making himself a dictionary.

Wetlands flags
Variance packet
Full-size plot
Hand-deliver to Dreher
Do not die

The computer itself was easy. That part almost pissed him off. Windows XP was still Windows, Outlook was still Outlook, and Excel had the same dead-eyed grid it would have twenty years later. The problem was that every file it opened assumed a baseline of knowledge Nate did not possess. He could navigate menus. He could not tell the difference between a site assessment and a survey, or whether a setback was a physical object or a legal requirement, or why everyone in this office said tenant estoppel like it was a phrase a child should know before third grade.

Twice before ten the phone rang and he let it go to voicemail because he couldn't bring himself to pick it up. On the third ring Ray leaned over the partition, looked at him, looked at the phone, and kept looking.

Nate picked up.

"Kassos Development, Dominic speaking."

The line went silent for half a second as he processed the fact that he'd managed the greeting without choking on it.

A man launched into a complaint about drainage at Seaview and something called a retaining wall, his voice rough with old cigarettes, backup alarms beeping somewhere behind him like he was calling from the middle of his own aggravation. Nate made a series of noises that sounded, to his own ears, like a malfunctioning chatbot designed to imitate concern.

"Right," he said. "No, I hear you. Let me just pull that up."

He clicked blindly through folders while the man got angrier.

Ray appeared at his elbow and flattened a hand on the desk. "Brennan?" he mouthed.

Nate nodded once.

Ray held out his hand. Nate gave him the receiver with such speed it was almost graceful.

"Tom, hey. Yeah, he's just getting caught up. No, I know. I know. Put a bullet in me, right? Listen, I talked to Russo Friday. The wall's getting looked at this week. No, not 'looked at looked at.' Actually looked at."

Ray walked away with the phone cord stretched behind him, already solving the problem in a tone halfway between apology and annoyance. Nate stared after him, then at the desk, then at the framed photo of Sarah.

She was laughing in it. Whoever had taken the picture had caught her mid-protest, head turned, hair blown across her mouth. It was the kind of photo a person kept at work because he liked the exact way a specific woman looked when the day was giving her trouble. Not because he needed a generic reminder that he had a girlfriend. Dominic had picked that picture. Dominic had set it on this desk. Dominic had cared about things between calls about retaining walls and parking counts and Tommy on Friday.

At nine-fifty Ray came back, put the receiver down, and said, "Don't answer Brennan again unless you want him to move in with you."

"Noted."

Ray reached past Nate, hit a button on the phone, and changed one of the line settings without explaining it. "That'll dump him to voicemail unless it's an emergency."

"Thanks."

"Mm-hm."

He should have left it there. Instead he said, "You really don't remember where the plotter room is?"

Nate kept his eyes on the legal pad. "I had a fever."

"A fever that erased architecture."

"I said I had a fever."

Ray stood with one hand on the partition. Somewhere in the office somebody laughed too loudly at something not funny enough. The copier started up. Ray scratched his jaw, mint on his breath.

"All right," he said finally. "Then here's your update. You were out for a week. People covered. Vic's covering now because he likes you. Don't make me look stupid for telling him you're worth covering."

He said it quietly, which made it worse. Nate looked up. Ray's expression was not suspicion exactly. It was the look of a man trying to decide how much weirdness friendship was supposed to absorb.

"I'm not trying to," Nate said.

"Good."

At ten Janice from reception called to say Victor was ready for him.

Nate stood up so fast the chair shot backward into the filing cabinet.

"Smooth," Ray said.

"Thanks."

"Take the Easton folder. And don't promise anything."

Nate picked up the wrong folder.

Ray took it out of his hand, replaced it with the yellow one, and for the first time all morning actually smiled.

"Jesus, man."

* * *

Victor Kassos's office had windows on two sides and enough air-conditioning to qualify as a separate climate. The door stood open. Victor was inside with his back half-turned, sleeves rolled neatly to the forearms despite the suit, talking into a speakerphone in a voice so warm it almost qualified as physical contact.

"No, no, we're not stepping away from the number, Howard, we're just making sure the number reflects the universe as it currently exists. That's all. Right. Right. Give my best to Elaine."

He clicked the phone off and turned.

He was not large, which somehow made the force of him more irritating. Silver-gray hair, deep tan, good suit that fit like it had opinions. Fifty-five, Nate knew from the character sheet in his own head, but the kind of fifty-five with a private trainer and a doctor who returned calls immediately. He smiled as if Nate's arrival improved the day in a measurable way.

"Morning, Dom."

Before Nate could decide whether Dominic hugged bosses, Victor came around the desk, took him by the upper arm, and thumped him once between the shoulder blades.

"You look terrible," Victor said. "Excellent. Means you were actually sick and not off in Atlantic City with your brother."

"No Atlantic City."

"Good. Because if you were going to humiliate me, I'd at least want a postcard."

Victor gestured him into one of the leather chairs and slid a glass bowl across the desk. Wrapped chocolates, gold foil. Nate took one because not taking one felt like its own kind of answer.

"Sarah still alive?" Victor said.

"Yeah."

"And your mother? She called here and informed Janice I was working you to death. Which, fair. But I like to think I'd hear about it before the family launched a class action."

Nate made what he hoped passed for a smile. Victor took his own seat, linked his hands over a legal pad, and looked at him with an attention that did not feel casual even under the grin.

"All right," Victor said. "Let's keep this short. Ray says Easton's almost ready. Seaview is, as usual, a nest of babies. Milford can wait till Wednesday. What I need from you this week is simple. Tenant communications on Eastbridge. Dreher's packet hand-delivered. And try not to die again because the office gossip was getting creative."

Eastbridge.

The word landed without context for a split second. Then the strip mall flashed in his head whole and hot: the end unit, the faded L on LAZAR'S, Mort behind the counter with a box of screws under one arm.

Nate said, "Right."

Victor's gaze stayed on him. "You with me?"

"Yeah. Sorry. Still not all the way back."

"So don't be all the way back. Just be useful."

He said it kindly. That was the thing. Victor did almost everything kindly. Even his impatience arrived dressed as advice.

He flipped open a folder on his desk. "Walk me through Easton."

Nate put the yellow folder in his lap and opened it. The site plan might as well have been a schematic for an organ transplant.

"Mrs. Kline wants the loading lane moved," he said carefully.

"Mm-hm."

"And more landscaping."

"Yes."

"We told her that, uh, that wasn't really possible because of physics."

Victor leaned back in his chair.

Nate heard himself and wanted to put his own head through the window.

Victor's mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "Did Ray say physics?"

"He may have."

"Ray says physics when he means truck radius."

"Right."

"So say truck radius."

"Truck radius."

"Beautiful. Now tell me why the lane isn't moving."

Nate stared at the page. A little box on the corner said SHEET C-4. If he threw himself hard enough into the floor maybe everyone would agree this had been a medical event and let him go home.

"Because..." He looked at the arrows, the boxes, the hatch lines. "Because the site's constrained."

Victor watched him.

"By the building footprint," Nate added.

Victor kept watching him.

Nate said, "And the... parking count."

Victor nodded once. "Better. And because if we move the lane we lose two spaces, and then Kline starts crying about retail turnover, and then we all shoot ourselves in the parking lot." He tapped the paper with one finger. "You're allowed to use plain English, Dom. You always get worse when you think you're supposed to sound like a lawyer."

Always. Nate filed that away. Dominic tried on formality when pressured. Good to know. Too late, but good to know.

Victor closed the folder. "Fine. Easton's fine. Dreher wants to feel important, so let him feel important. If you have to wait outside his office for half an hour while his girl files her nails, wait. Smile. Bring the packet back signed." He slid another folder toward Nate. "Eastbridge. I want you back on tenant calls this week."

Nate didn't touch the folder.

"Is there a problem?" Victor asked.

"No."

"Good."

Victor settled farther into his chair, hands folded over his stomach now, and the air in the room shifted by a degree. He didn't get colder. He got stiller.

"Ray says you've been off your game since before you got sick."

There it was. Not accusation. Not even concern, exactly. More like somebody laying a clean napkin beside the plate before the knife came out.

Nate said, "I've just had a lot going on."

Victor nodded as if this were not only acceptable but expected. "Everybody does."

He let that sit. Then he smiled again and reached for his phone.

"Here's what I care about. If you're in trouble, tell me before the trouble becomes mine. If you're tired, take a day. If Sarah's on your ass, buy flowers. If Tommy's on your ass, stop answering the phone. But don't vanish for a week and leave Ray to sandbag for you. That's not fair to him, and it's not how we work here. Understood?"

"Understood."

"Good." Victor picked up the receiver, then paused. "And eat something. You look like a divorcé."

Nate almost said, I am a divorcé. Instead he unwrapped the chocolate, put it in his mouth because his hands needed a job, and stood.

"Thanks, Vic."

Victor waved him off. "Go. And tell Ray I stole you from him because I like him irritable. It keeps the blood moving."

Outside the office, Nate shut the door carefully behind him and stood in the conference-room corridor with chocolate turning to paste in his mouth.

Ray was waiting at his desk with a stack of rolled plans under one arm.

"Well?"

"I'm alive."

"That's a low bar."

"It's the one I had."

Ray snorted once. "Did he yell?"

"No."

"Worse, then."

Nate looked at him.

Ray shifted the plans to his other arm. "If Vic ever starts yelling, that means he already gave up on you. Come on."

"Where?"

"Plotter room. Since you were apparently raised by wolves."

The plotter room turned out to be a narrow side chamber with a machine the size of a hospital bed and three curling site plans draped over a metal rack. It smelled like hot ink and old dust. Ray walked him through it one-handed, talking fast, not quite disguising the fact that he should not have had to.

"This tray for bond, this one for vellum. If the rollers slip, don't yank, you'll tear the sheet and then Janice'll come in here and kill both of us. Preview here. Scale here. And if you print in color, print in color for a reason, not because the buttons are pretty."

Nate nodded.

"Show me."

Nate put the Easton file in backward. The machine made a noise like an offended moose and stopped.

Ray closed his eyes. "Beautiful. Great start."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Apparently not."

Ray fixed the feed, hit the right sequence of buttons, and got the plan rolling. As it printed he leaned against the wall and crossed his arms.

"All right," he said. "Seriously. What's going on with you?"

Nate kept his eyes on the plan emerging inch by inch. "I was sick."

"Yeah, and before that?"

"I don't know."

"Dom."

Ray didn't say his name like a question. He said it like a knock on a locked door.

Nate looked down at Dominic's hands, at the faint scar across the left knuckle, the one detail he had memorized from the bathroom mirror because it kept proving this body had belonged to a person who'd once punched something and healed crooked.

"I've just been off," he said. "That's it."

Ray let out air through his nose. "Fine. Be off quietly, then."

He took the finished plan, checked the margins, and handed it back.

"And if you forget how to use the copier too, at least do me the favor of pretending it's a joke."

* * *

The rest of the week developed its own ugly rhythm.

Nate survived by making himself smaller. He took notes on everything. He let calls go to voicemail, then listened twice before deciding whether a response could be faked. He learned where people kept forms. He learned that a man named Russo was a surveyor, that Dreher liked to make people wait, that Janice in reception wore white sneakers under her desk and kept a battery-powered fan pointed at her knees because the air-conditioning never reached the lobby. He learned that two women in accounting hated each other politely, that Ray ate peanut-butter crackers for lunch every day but told everybody he was cutting carbs, and that Victor passed through the office twice in the afternoon not because he needed anything but because he liked to be seen moving through his own kingdom.

On Tuesday Nate nearly hand-delivered the wrong packet to the wrong office and got saved by Janice, who took one look at the folder tab and said, "Honey, unless Councilman Dreher started approving coastal permits from city hall, turn around."

On Wednesday he spent forty minutes trying to understand a spreadsheet Dominic clearly found effortless. The columns tracked tenant square footage, notice periods, lease expirations, contact status. Nate could read the numbers. He could not understand how those numbers connected to anything happening in the world. A person ran a diner for sixteen years and somewhere else a spreadsheet turned that into a date, a checkbox, a phone call.

On Thursday he called a contractor by the wrong name and the contractor laughed in his ear and said, "Who is this?" Nate hung up, called back two minutes later, and said the line had dropped.

He was bad at this. Not in the lovable montage way where incompetence made a man plucky. He was bad in the humiliating adult way where other people had to cover for you while pretending they weren't doing it. Ray slid corrected terms across his desk without comment. Janice re-routed calls. Victor, when he appeared, kept everything in the register of mild paternal amusement that somehow made Nate feel younger and shittier than open contempt would have.

By Friday afternoon the office had gone slack with the promise of weekend. Somebody had brought in supermarket cookies. The phones rang less often. Sun burned white through the warehouse windows and made everybody's desk look overheated. Ray was in and out, preparing for a site visit in Milford first thing Monday, tossing folders into a leather briefcase that had seen better decades.

"Before you leave," Ray said, passing Nate's desk without stopping, "take a look at Eastbridge and flag anything weird in the tenant sheet. Vic wants status clean before Monday."

Nate looked up. "Anything weird."

"You know. Calls not returned, people lawyered up, anybody threatening to chain themselves to a radiator. The usual."

Ray kept walking, then turned back three steps later.

"And don't overthink it. It's just a status pass."

Just a status pass.

Nate waited until Ray disappeared into the conference room before he touched the folder.

It was thicker than he expected. A full accordion file, green, tabbed with white stickers in Dominic's handwriting. EASTBRIDGE PLAZA. Somebody had underlined PLAZA twice. The folder left a clean pale rectangle on the desk blotter where it must have been sitting all week or longer, like an object on an altar that had finally been moved.

He pulled it onto his lap and opened the first section.

Site plans. Elevations. A survey of the lot with arrows and measurements that meant little until they didn't. Even stripped of emotion, even flattened into black lines and labels, the place was instantly recognizable. The rectangle of parking. The end unit larger than the others. Delivery access at the rear. Utility easement along the side. The shape of the hardware store, rendered by a firm in New Haven or Stamford or hell, maybe right here in Bridgeport, by somebody who had never stood inside and smelled the sawdust.

He turned the page.

Tenant sheet.

The office hum seemed to change pitch. Or maybe his hearing did.

Top of the list: NAIL DESIGN BY KIM. Then PARK CLEANERS, GAIL'S DINER, AL'S BARBER SHOP, CESAR'S MARKET. Familiar in a different order, flattened into units and contacts and square-foot numbers and notice dates.

Then:

MORTON LAZAR  
LAZAR'S HARDWARE  
UNIT 7

There it was. Not Dad. Not Mort. Not the man behind the counter with the screws in his hand. Morton Lazar, in office type, as if that had ever been his real name. Under it: tenant since 1989. Contacted 6/14. Follow-up pending.

Nate read the line three times. The words did not become more symbolic under pressure. If anything they got meaner. Contacted 6/14. Follow-up pending. The kind of notation a person made between cups of coffee. The kind of notation that could destroy a life without once raising its voice.

He looked around the office as if someone might have changed shape while he wasn't paying attention.

Nothing had. Janice's laugh floated up faintly from reception. Somebody at the far end of the room was arguing with a copier and losing. The break-room coffee maker clicked as it finished another pot. Every ordinary office noise stayed ordinary.

He turned to the next section.

Timeline.

A project schedule ran across the page in colored bars: notice period, vacancy coordination, utility disconnect, demolition mobilization, site clearance, environmental review, preliminary grading. Late December had a thick block of color over it with DEMO WINDOW stamped across the bar in all caps. Holiday week. Not a single day, not on this sheet, but enough. Late December. While everyone was full of ham and wrapping paper and pretending the year had gone somewhere respectable, a crew would show up with equipment and eat the plaza alive.

The folder had more. Contact logs. Internal memos. Draft letters with tabs clipped to the corners. Nate could see them waiting there, the whole bureaucratic digestive tract that turned a place into a cleared site. His hands went cold despite the heat.

He made himself read.

Park Cleaners had requested relocation assistance. Gail's had asked about compensation for equipment removal. Al's Barbershop had written NO FORWARDING ADDRESS on a form so hard the pen had torn the paper. Lazar's Hardware had a neat line next to it in Dominic's hand: owner requests meeting after business hours, wife present.

Wife present.

Linda in the back room with the books, maybe wiping her hands on a rag before coming out. Mort standing straight because he always stood straighter with strangers. Dominic sitting in one of the mismatched chairs from behind the counter or maybe not sitting at all, because who sat down to tell somebody their store was being erased.

Nate shut the folder.

He kept his hand on it.

The leatherette surface of the desk had begun to split at one corner. Somebody had taped over a crack in the phone cord. A coffee ring stained one of Dominic's legal pads. There was a paper clip bent into a loose S near the keyboard, the kind of absent-minded damage a living person made while listening to a bad call. He stared at these things because they were manageable. Desk things. Office things. But they would not stay neutral. Every object here had joined the conspiracy by simple proximity. This chair had held Dominic while he made notes about Mort. This phone had carried the call. This blunt office light had shone on the folder while the work got done.

He opened the file again.

MORTON LAZAR  
LAZAR'S HARDWARE  
UNIT 7

He touched the line with one finger, then snatched his hand back as if the toner might come off on him.

It did. A faint gray smudge on the pad of his thumb.

Not much. Just printer dirt. But for one stupid second it felt like contamination, proof that the office had marked him in some visible way. He rubbed his thumb against the side of his hand. The gray spread.

"You still here?"

Nate jerked so hard the folder half-closed.

Ray stood at the edge of the desk with his briefcase and car keys. He had changed into a short-sleeved polo for the drive home, tie gone, collar damp with sweat.

"Yeah," Nate said.

Ray glanced at the open folder. "Status pass taking your whole afternoon?"

"I got distracted."

"By tenant spreadsheets." Ray nodded as if this were a credible species of emergency. "Hell of a life."

He set his briefcase down long enough to fish a folded antacid packet out of his pocket, tapped two tablets into his palm, and chewed them dry.

"You good to handle that?" he asked.

Nate looked at him. "Handle what."

"Eastbridge."

The word sat between them now with all its regular office innocence. Ray did not know. He only knew Dominic had been weird and sick and slower than usual and maybe upset about something he wouldn't say. He did not know Nate had spent half an hour in a parked car two days earlier watching his father sell deck screws through a plate-glass window. He did not know Ray himself had just handed a murder weapon back to the guy who'd built it.

"Yeah," Nate said.

Ray watched him for a moment.

"All right." He picked up the briefcase again. "Don't stay all night. If you melt into the carpet, Janice'll make me explain it Monday."

"I won't."

"And eat something."

That, apparently, was a company value.

Ray started away, then turned at the conference room and came back one last time, expression not suspicious exactly, not soft either. Human, which was worse.

"For what it's worth," he said, "Vic likes you. More than most people around here. Don't make him regret it."

Then he was gone.

The office emptied by degrees after that. Accounting left first, then Janice at the desk downstairs, then somebody from drafting who whistled tunelessly all the way to the elevator before remembering the elevator was broken and swearing at the stairwell. Nate stayed where he was, the Eastbridge file open again, and read just enough to make himself sicker.

He found an updated contact list with home numbers. He found contractor estimates clipped under a tab. He found a page of demolition prep notes that mentioned utility shutoff sequencing and fencing. He did not keep going long enough to find whatever waited behind the later tabs. He knew there would be more. There was always more. The miracle of bureaucracy was not that it destroyed people. The miracle was how much paperwork it took to make the destruction feel administrative.

Outside the warehouse windows, the parking lot reflected the late sun in a white glaze. Nate could see his own faint outline in the glass when he leaned back: Dominic's shoulders in Dominic's office chair, Dominic's tie loosened now, Dominic's face a pale ghost over the reflected rows of parked cars.

He had come here expecting something cleaner. Villainy, maybe. A lair. Men in suits cackling over blueprints. Some version of evil with better production design.

Instead it was this.

Peppermints in a dented tin. Somebody's chipped college mug. Antacids chewed dry on the way to the parking lot. A boss who gave you chocolate and told you to buy flowers if your girlfriend was mad. A project folder underlining PLAZA twice in black marker. A note beside his father's name: follow-up pending.

He sat very still and let the desk press into his forearms.

Somewhere below him a door slammed. The sound came up through the floorboards and into the chair. He looked again at the tenant sheet, at the flat office font, at Morton's formal name printed above Unit 7 like the whole of a man could be reduced to occupancy and notice status.

This was Dominic's desk. Dominic's phone. Dominic's handwriting on the tabs. Nate had killed the man and taken his chair, and now he was sitting in the exact workstation where the paperwork for Lazar's Hardware had been stacked, clipped, moved from IN to OUT.

The toner smudge was still on his thumb.
