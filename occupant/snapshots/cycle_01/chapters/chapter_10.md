# Chapter 10

The call came to Dominic's desk phone a little after three, while Nate was pretending to understand a stack of contractor revisions for a retail pad in Milford. The fluorescent lights in the Kassos office made everything look tired before it was tired. Carpet tile, humming desktops, coffee that tasted like the inside of a wet cardboard box. Ray was at the copier feeding in a packet one page at a time because the machine kept chewing the corners.

Nate almost let the phone ring out. Then he saw Ray glance over and picked it up.

"Dom speaking."

"Jesus Christ, there you are." The voice was male, fast, cheerful in a way that felt practiced. "It's Marco. Marco DeSantis. I had to get your number from your mother because apparently you vanished from the surface of the earth."

Marco meant nothing to him for half a second, then everything at once. Real life. One more person Dominic had accumulated before Nate arrived and forgot to leave instructions for.

Nate said, "Hey."

"Hey?" Marco laughed. "That's what I get? I'm in Bridgeport for one night and I get hey?"

There was a pause in which Nate could either confess ignorance to a stranger with access to Dominic's mother or agree to see him. He could feel the office around him, the slow machinery of attention. Ray lifting pages from the copier. Someone in the break room laughing too hard at something Victor had probably said.

"Sorry," Nate said. "Long day."

"Yeah? Mine too. My mother had cataract surgery this morning, I'm running on hospital coffee, and if I go back to the Marriott and watch cable by myself I'm going to put my head through the dresser. Meet me for a drink."

Nate looked down at the marked-up plans in front of him as if there might be a better answer written in the margins. "Tonight?"

"That is usually when drinks happen, yeah."

"Right. Sure."

"Seagull on Fairfield. Seven-thirty. You still know where Fairfield Avenue is or did real estate finally melt the useful parts of your brain?"

"I can find it."

"Beautiful. See you then."

The line clicked dead.

Nate kept the receiver to his ear a second longer, listening to nothing. When he set it down, Ray was already looking at him.

"Friend of yours?" Ray said.

"College."

"You looked thrilled."

Nate forced something shaped like a smile. "Just surprised."

Ray nodded and went back to the copier, which gave a long choking sound and spit one page halfway out like it was coughing up evidence.

For the next four hours Nate did less work than usual, which was already an accomplishment. He went through Dominic's Outlook contacts under the cover of looking for a zoning memo. There were two Marcos. One was a drywall subcontractor in Danbury. The other was a personal address, `mdesantis74@aol.com`, with exactly three emails in the inbox and one in Sent. A forwarded joke about the Red Sox from 2004. A reply from Dominic that said `still an asshole, call me sometime.` An email from March about Marco's mother's eye surgery and how she was scared of going under. Dominic had written back, `Tell her it's routine. She'll be flirting with the nurse by lunch.`

That gave Nate almost nothing, but nothing with edges. DeSantis. Mother. Eye surgery. Old enough together for insults to count as affection. The rest was blank space.

By six thirty he had sweat through the undershirt under Dominic's button-down even though October had finally cooled the city down and the windows by the stairwell leaked a thin draft. He drove to the bar with the road atlas still on the passenger seat out of habit, though by now he knew the main routes. Dusk had dropped early. Wet leaves pasted themselves to the curb. Headlights shone off black pavement and the Honda's heater smelled faintly like dust every time he turned the fan up.

The Seagull was the kind of bar that had probably been called a tavern by people who liked themselves. Low ceiling, mirrored back wall, Christmas lights stapled over the liquor shelves and left up all year. A baseball game ran soundless above the taps. The place smelled like fryer oil, beer, and the damp wool of coats steaming off by the door.

Marco was already there in a booth, one arm slung over the cracked vinyl backrest like he'd rented the place for the night. He was Dominic's age or close to it, thick through the shoulders, dark hair receding at the temples, tie loosened and tucked into his shirt between buttons so it wouldn't fall in his drink. He had a white hospital visitor sticker still on the cuff of his suit jacket and a packet of cinnamon gum on the table beside his beer. He saw Nate and stood up with his whole face.

"There he is."

Nate had a split second to decide whether these men hugged. Marco decided for him, coming in one-armed and hard on the shoulder, a slap that said years. Dominic's body answered the contact without panic. Nate's did not. By the time they sat, Marco was already talking.

"You look good, man. Little tired, but who isn't. Sit down. I ordered you a bourbon, then I remembered you used to get weird about bourbon, so I drank it. You can have a beer like an adult."

"Beer is good."

"See? Growth."

Marco signaled the bartender with two fingers. Nate watched him closely. People told you what version of themselves they believed in by what they repeated. Marco repeated stories before he even got the second sip down.

"My mother asked about you, by the way. Said if I saw you I had to tell you she still makes that lemon cake you inhaled at graduation."

"How is she?"

"Good. Better than good, actually. Walking around this morning in that backless hospital gown like she's on a cruise ship. She flirted with the anesthesiologist. My father was thrilled."

"Sounds right."

Marco barked a laugh and pointed at him with the neck of his bottle. "See, there you are."

Nate nodded like there was a there there to return to.

The bartender dropped off a beer. Nate took a swallow immediately so he would not have to speak while Marco looked at him.

"I had this whole drive up from White Plains," Marco said. "Traffic the whole damn way, and all I could think about was that road trip to Montreal when your muffler came loose outside Binghamton. You remember that? The sound of that thing scraping the road for like forty miles while you kept saying it was probably fine."

Nate smiled because Marco was smiling.

"Probably fine" felt safe enough to belong to many men. He said, "That sounds like me."

"It was absolutely you. Jessica was in the back seat crying because she thought the car was on fire."

He gave the name Jessica the same way Sarah gave names from Dominic's life: not as information but as a fact already loaded into the room. Nate set his beer down carefully.

"How'd she end up on that trip anyway?" he said.

It was the sort of question Dominic might ask if he wanted the story retold for fun, and Nate hated how proud he felt when it worked.

Marco rolled his eyes. "Because you were in love with her, obviously."

"Was I."

"For like six weeks. Maybe eight. Which, in college time, is a common-law marriage." He grinned. "You don't remember? Jesus. You wrote her that note after Professor Heller's midterm. The one on the back of the blue book. She read it out loud in the Myrtle kitchen."

Myrtle. Heller. Blue book. Jessica. Nate laid them down in his head like cards he might need later.

"That sounds humiliating," he said.

"It was fantastic. For me. For you it was a disaster. Then she hooked up with that guy from the crew team and you spent the whole ride to Montreal pretending you were over it while chain-smoking clove cigarettes out the window like some asshole in a movie."

Nate laughed, because Marco was laughing and because if he didn't make noise soon the space would harden around what he wasn't saying. The laugh came a beat late. Marco either didn't notice or was too deep in the story to care.

"You still talk to any of those people?" Nate asked.

"From college? Christ, no. Not unless you count Christmas cards as intimacy. Brendan, sometimes. He's in Jersey now, selling insurance and collecting children. Jessica's in Chicago, I think. Somebody told me she married a surgeon. Good for her. Good luck to him."

He drank. Chewed a piece of gum. Pushed the empty wrapper into a tight square with his thumbnail and tucked it under his bottle label as if he'd been doing that since birth.

"How's Sarah?" he said.

The question landed more cleanly than the others because Nate at least knew which life this belonged to. "She's good. Busy. School."

"Teaching, right. History?"

"Yeah."

"I always liked her. Too smart for you, obviously, but that never stopped anybody." Marco leaned back, studying him with friendly narrow eyes. "How long is it now, what, four years?"

Nate took another swallow of beer to buy time. Sarah's and Dominic's apartment had too many photographs and too little chronology. "Around there."

"Good for you, man." Marco said it without irony. "You needed somebody who didn't think every bad idea was a challenge."

"Thanks, I think."

"You know what I mean. Junior year, if somebody had told me out of the two of us you'd be the one living with a teacher and I would be the one eating vending-machine pretzels in a Marriott because my mother wanted me to stay close to the hospital, I would've said you were out of your mind."

Nate said, "Still time to turn it around."

"No, this is the turned-around version. You should've seen me in '02."

He said it lightly, then waved it away before it could turn into a confession. Another human thing Dominic probably knew and Nate didn't. The whole night was built out of things like that: exposed rebar sticking out of a foundation Nate was trying to walk across without looking down.

Marco kept talking. Nate kept surviving.

The Myrtle house was apparently a rental with six boys and one bathroom and mushrooms growing in the basement. Professor Heller taught macroeconomics and wore the same brown corduroy jacket every day until somebody on campus started a rumor he slept in it. Brendan once got so drunk at a St. Patrick's Day party he climbed onto the roof and announced he was dropping out to become a lobsterman. Jessica was either a brunette or a catastrophe or both. Dominic had thrown up outside a diner in New Haven after some exam and then tried to flirt with the waitress while holding his own stomach.

Nate learned to listen for the shape of his required response. When Marco slapped the table, it meant agreement. When he winced laughing, it meant shame remembered fondly. When he shook his head and smiled into his bottle, it meant somebody had grown older in a way both men were supposed to respect. Nate mirrored all of it. It felt like doing tech support for an operating system with half the menus missing.

Every now and then he got too close to the edge.

"Remember that time we went down to Atlantic City with Phil Russo's fake IDs?" Marco said.

Nate made the mistake of saying, "That was senior year, right?"

Marco looked at him. Not sharply. Just long enough for Nate to hear the compressor kick on behind the bar.

"Sophomore," Marco said. Then he laughed. "See? You're getting old. Thank God. I was starting to think you were going to die at twenty-one on purpose."

Nate let out air through his nose. "Yeah. Sophomore."

Marco squinted at him again, then shrugged and reached for the peanuts.

By the second beer Nate had accumulated a decent list in his head: Myrtle, Heller, Jessica, Brendan, Atlantic City, Phil Russo, clove cigarettes, Montreal, mother lemon cake. It was still nothing like memory. It was just indexing. He could feel each item slide around as soon as the next one arrived.

"How's work?" Marco asked.

Nate said what he always said lately. "Busy."

"That means awful."

"Pretty much."

"Still with Kassos?"

"Yeah."

Marco made a face that suggested he knew just enough about Kassos Development to disapprove on principle without being able to name a permit. "Money's money, I guess."

"Something like that."

"You always said you'd do it for a couple years and then go out on your own."

Nate kept his expression steady. Dominic had said that. Dominic had wanted things. Dominic had explained those wants to other people over beers in rooms like this, and now Nate was sitting in the residue getting graded on consistency.

"Plans change," he said.

"They do." Marco rubbed the visitor sticker on his cuff with one thumb, peeling it halfway off and pressing it back down again. "My mother asked if you still played ball Sundays."

"Not lately."

"Yeah, me neither. Knees. Back. Life." He smiled at Nate, softer this time. "You seem different, man. Calmer."

There it was again. Good different. Softer. Calmer. As if replacing a man with his killer had improved his temperament.

Nate looked down at the wet ring his bottle had left on the table. "Maybe I'm tired."

"Maybe. Or maybe Sarah domesticated you." Marco lifted his beer. "Either way, congratulations."

They stayed another forty minutes after that, talking mostly about nothing. Traffic. Weddings. Which people from school had gotten fat in a depressing way and which in a prosperous one. Marco complained about his hotel mattress and asked if Bridgeport still had the pizza place near the train station where Dominic once ate six slices and then swore he could drive. Nate got through it with the same method he'd used all night: offer something small and bland, then redirect.

When they stood to leave, Marco hugged him again.

"Don't disappear for another year," he said. "And call your mother. She said you missed Sunday dinner twice."

"I know."

"No, you don't know. I heard the voicemail she left me by accident. The woman sounded ready to personally drive over and drag you out by the ear."

"That sounds right too."

Marco laughed, slapped his arm, and headed for the door with his jacket over one shoulder. He turned back once to point at Nate through the glass.

"Tell Sarah I said hi."

"I will."

Outside, the air had gone cold enough to make the beer sit sharp in Nate's stomach. He got into the Honda and shut the door and stayed there with both hands on the wheel. The windshield had misted at the edges. People came and went past the bar in little bursts of laughter and coat sleeves and smoke.

He had survived. That was the problem. Surviving made the next one necessary.

He drove home with the radio off, repeating names at red lights like he was afraid they would leak out of his ears before he got inside.

***

Saturday afternoon Sarah sat at the kitchen table with forty-one quizzes, a mug gone cold beside her, and a yellow highlighter tucked behind one ear. She had changed into old gray sweatpants after school and put her hair up with a black clip that looked like it could break a finger. The apartment smelled like onions because Nate had started them too early and now they were somewhere between translucent and scorched.

"Do you want me to just order something?" Sarah called from the table without looking up.

"No, I've got it."

"That is not the voice of a man who has got it."

"It's onions. I can beat onions."

"Historically, maybe not."

He smiled despite himself and stirred the pan. The oil hissed up his wrist. Outside the kitchen window the trees along the lot had started turning, a few yellow leaves caught in the chain-link fence around the dumpsters. October made the apartment feel smaller because it drove everyone indoors. Summer at least had open windows and noise from the street. Fall was just the radiator pinging and the television muttering in the living room and whatever two people couldn't help bringing home with them.

Sarah flipped a page, marked something, flipped another. "How was Marco?"

Nate kept his back to her a second too long. "Fine."

"Fine good or fine awkward?"

"Both."

"That sounds like Marco."

He scraped onions across the pan though they didn't need scraping. "You know him that well?"

"I've met him like six times. He's loud and he thinks every story needs a reenactment." She looked up then, just enough to smile. "Did he tell the Montreal car story again?"

Nate reached for the salt because reaching for something looked more natural than freezing in place. "He told a lot of stories."

"Yeah, that sounds right."

She went back to the quizzes. Nate added garlic, then tomatoes, then the sausage he'd sliced badly because Dominic's body held knives differently than his had. He had met Marco once, maybe six times, maybe twenty. Sarah had her own archive of Dominic in there, nested and organized and instantly searchable. Everybody did. He was the only one working from corrupted files.

For a while they talked about school and groceries and whether the landlord was ever going to fix the hallway light. Ordinary, flat lines that carried him farther than anything important did.

"I had three kids today put the same wrong date for the Missouri Compromise," Sarah said.

"What'd they put?"

"Eighteen fifty-six."

"That's not even close."

"I know. One of them looked at me like I was being elitist about it."

"Maybe he was very committed to eighteen fifty-six."

"Maybe he should've taught the class, then."

He drained pasta. She stacked finished quizzes into one pile and unfinished quizzes into another, edges tapped against the table until they lined up exactly. When she got precise like that, it meant she was tired or angry or both. Nate had learned that much. He had not learned enough.

They ate at the table because the couch had turned into paper territory. Sarah asked him to pass the parmesan. He asked if she wanted more wine. She said no, then yes, a little. He poured too much and she gave him a look that wasn't about the wine.

"Jen's trying to book a weekend on the Cape before it gets too cold," she said after a while.

"Yeah?"

"With her boyfriend. She asked me where we stayed last summer and I couldn't remember the name." She twirled pasta, chewing as she thought. "What was that restaurant in Wellfleet? The little place with the raw bar. I was going to tell her to avoid it."

The fork in Nate's hand clicked against the plate.

He said, "Which one?"

Sarah looked up.

"In Wellfleet," she said.

"No, I know. I just mean which..." He trailed off because there wasn't a second half of the sentence that made sense.

She waited.

Nate tried, "The one by the water?"

"They were all by the water."

"Right."

He took a bite so he would not have to keep producing language. The sausage tasted mostly like fennel and panic.

Sarah set her fork down. "The yellow place. Tiny menu. The rain was coming sideways off the parking lot."

Nate said, "I don't know."

"You picked it."

"I'm blanking."

Now she was very still. Not theatrical. Not wounded. Just still in the way a teacher went still when a student had said something impossible and she wanted to make sure she'd heard the exact shape of it.

"Okay," she said. "The next night, then. Not the clam shack. The other place. You sent back the oysters because they tasted wrong and then you talked about it all the way to Truro."

Nate stared at his plate.

He could hear the refrigerator motor, the faint scrape of somebody moving furniture upstairs, a siren far enough away to be part of the weather. He had spent four hours in a bar pretending shared history with a man who had last seen Dominic in pieces. This was worse. This was one woman at their own table, asking about a weekend that had happened in the body he was wearing.

"I just don't remember the name," he said.

Sarah kept looking at him.

"Do you remember the trip?" she asked.

It was not a trap if he answered carefully. It was not a trap if he lied well. It was not a trap if he had not already failed.

"Some of it."

"Some of it."

"It was a year ago."

"It was last August."

He rubbed his thumb over the handle of his fork until the cheap metal warmed under his skin. "I've had a lot on my mind lately."

Sarah's face changed by degrees, the warmth pulling back but not disappearing. "I'm aware of that."

"I'm serious."

"I know you're serious." She said it softly, which was worse than if she'd snapped. "I'm just trying to figure out what that means now."

Nate looked at her and then away. On the table between them sat the parmesan can with the green lid half off, a wet ring from her wineglass, one of her quizzes with `see me after class` written in the margin in blunt, slanted script. He wanted to say something that was true and usable. There was almost nothing in the overlap.

"I'm off," he said.

"Yes."

"I know."

"Do you?" she asked, and then shook her head before he could answer. "Forget it. Jen can stay in a motel."

She picked up her fork again and finished eating. Not angrily. Not quickly. Just with her attention elsewhere.

Nate forced down a few more bites because not eating would turn this into a scene and he could not survive scenes right now. The sauce had gone oily. One onion strip had burned black and curled into itself like a dead worm.

When they were done, Sarah carried her plate to the sink and rinsed it. "I'm going to finish those quizzes on the couch," she said.

"You want help with anything?"

"No."

She said it the way she said no to students who had confused apology with a revision policy.

In the living room she sat with her legs folded under her and the stack on her lap. Nate washed dishes slowly, giving her time to look up, laugh, let it pass. She did not. Once, while he was drying the pan, he turned and found her watching him from over the top of a paper. Not suspicious exactly. Measuring.

Later, in bed, she put lotion on her hands and wrists before turning off the lamp. He knew the smell now, the clean powdery one that mixed with her shampoo on the pillow. She lay on her side facing away from him.

"Did Marco's mom do okay?" she asked into the dark.

The question was gentle. It made his chest feel full of ground glass.

"Yeah," he said. "She's okay."

"Good."

That was all. But she had asked about Marco's mother, not because she cared especially about Marco's mother, but because she was checking whether what he'd said the night before had roots. He lay awake listening to the radiator tick and the intermittent traffic on the avenue below until her breathing deepened into sleep. Then he kept lying there, because even moving felt like it might count as an answer.

The client call came Monday at 10:17, while Ray was leaning over Nate's desk with a set of revised site drawings and explaining, for the second time, why the drainage notes had to go out before noon if they wanted anything from engineering by Wednesday.

"I'm not saying it makes sense," Ray said. "I'm saying if Pete in engineering doesn't get it before lunch he acts like you've personally robbed his house."

Nate nodded like that was actionable knowledge.

Ray straightened up. "You with me?"

"Yeah."

The phone rang. Ray tapped the stack against the desk to square it and turned away. Nate picked up.

"Dominic Altieri."

"Dom, Stu Halpern. Tell me you have good news because my lawyer's standing in my office acting like we're buying Chernobyl."

The voice was nasal and impatient, with a dry little cough under the words and a car blinker clicking somewhere in the background even though he said he was in his office. Nate's stomach dropped so fast it felt physical, a clean downward pull.

He knew the name in the empty way he knew all work names lately. Familiar as wallpaper. Unusable as fact.

"Stu," Nate said.

"Yeah, Stu. The access easement on Fairfield Commons. Your guy sends over the revised language Friday, now Feldman says if the anchor tenant gets first refusal on the south lot we're dead in the water on financing. We had this sorted in April. I don't have time to re-litigate it because somebody got cute with a draft."

Nate looked blindly around Dominic's desk as if the answer might be labeled in red. There were three open files, a yellow pad full of Dominic's handwriting that still looked like somebody else's pulse, two coffee rings, and the Eastbridge folder sitting in the right-hand stack like a live thing.

"Right," Nate said. "Let me just pull up the latest version."

"Why are you pulling it up now?" Halpern said. "You told me Friday it was clean."

Ray had stopped at his own desk two rows over. Nate could feel him there without looking.

"I just want to make sure we're talking about the same language."

"We're talking about the language you sent me, Dom."

The blinker kept clicking. Maybe the man was in a car after all. Maybe he had lied automatically because people did that all day long. Nate clung to the tiny irrelevance because the actual subject of the call was a hole with no bottom.

"Of course," he said. "I'm just..."

What. What was he just.

"You sound strange," Halpern said.

"No, I'm sorry. It's just noisy in here."

"Then close the door."

There was no door. Dominic's desk was in the open bullpen with everyone else's, close enough that Nate could hear Ray's stapler and somebody in accounting unwrapping hard candy.

"Right. Listen, let me call Feldman directly and sort out what he thinks he's seeing."

"No, you listen." Halpern's voice sharpened. "I don't want you sorting out what he thinks he's seeing. I want to know what changed between April and Friday. Did the anchor tenant push for this? Did Kassos push for this? Because if somebody moved the goalposts after we shook hands, I need to know before I walk into a lender meeting looking like an asshole."

Nate stared at the file folders. The words anchor tenant and first refusal and lender meeting were all English. Together they formed weather.

"I should have that in front of me before I answer," he said.

There was a beat of silence on the line. Then Halpern gave a short disbelieving laugh.

"You should have it in front of you now. This is your deal."

Nate's mouth had gone dry. He could hear himself about to say something catastrophic, something like remind me or which parcel or I thought Ray handled that one. He swallowed it so hard his teeth clicked.

"Give me twenty minutes," he said. "I'll call you back."

"Ten."

"Ten."

Halpern hung up.

Nate kept holding the phone. He had the peculiar sensation of being both too hot and cold in the fingertips, as if his body had split the panic into departments and assigned them contradictory instructions.

When he set the receiver down, Ray was standing in his doorway. Not in the doorway of an office. In the empty rectangle of carpet that led between desks. He had the drainage drawings tucked under one arm and a coffee cup in the other hand. His face was blank in the way people got when they were trying not to make your embarrassment bigger by naming all of it at once.

"You didn't know who that was," Ray said.

Nate opened his mouth.

Ray shook his head. "Don't give me some bullshit about the line being bad. You didn't know who that was."

People around them kept working. A keyboard clacked. Victor's door opened and closed somewhere down the hall. The office did not stop for humiliation; that was one of the things Nate hated most about offices.

"I blanked," he said.

"On Stu Halpern." Ray set the coffee down on Nate's desk without asking. "You've been dealing with Fairfield Commons since spring."

"I know."

"Do you?"

The question was not rhetorical. Ray looked tired, more worried than angry. He had the build of a man who'd once gone to the gym on purpose and now mostly lifted file boxes. There was a damp dark patch at the collar of his shirt from the walk in from the parking lot. He looked like a coworker who did not want to become a witness.

Nate said, "I've been off lately."

"No kidding."

"I'm dealing with some stuff."

Ray looked at him for a long second. "Personal stuff?"

Nate nodded.

Ray glanced over his shoulder toward Victor's office, then back at Nate. When he lowered his voice it got flatter, not kinder. "Then deal with it enough to know who's on the phone. Halpern goes to Vic because you sound lost, this gets bigger than a weird Monday."

"I know."

"Stop saying that if you don't."

Nate shut up.

Ray exhaled through his nose. "I can call him and tell him engineering kicked something back, buy you an hour. That's it. After that you're actually going to have to read the file and sound like yourself."

Yourself. The word landed and kept landing.

"Okay," Nate said.

Ray picked up his coffee again. "And Dom?"

"Yeah."

"If you're sick, be sick. If you're having some kind of breakdown, take a week. I don't care. But this half-assed drifting around? I can't cover that forever."

He walked away before Nate could answer. Not storming off. Just going back to his desk, because the drainage notes still had to go out before noon and the world had the bad taste to keep being specific.

Nate sat very still until the feeling came back into his hands.

Then he spent fifty-five minutes doing the thing he should have been able to do in the first place. He found the Fairfield Commons folder. Found Halpern's correspondence. Found the April draft, the Friday revision, the memo from legal, the highlighted note from some paralegal about anchor-tenant language. He called Halpern back, said three cautious competent things in a row, and got off the phone without dying. Halpern stayed irritated but within normal-business irritated, which in that office counted as rescued.

No one congratulated Nate. No one should have.

At lunch he left the building and walked two blocks to a pharmacy that sold school supplies in one cramped aisle near the back. Halloween candy had taken over the front endcaps. A rack of knit gloves stood by the window even though it was barely cold. Nate bought a green spiral notebook, two packs of blue pens, and a cheap plastic folder with pockets on the inside. The cashier, a woman with a bandage around one thumb and a silver cross pinned to her vest, rang him up without looking at his face for more than a second.

"Receipt in the bag?" she asked.

"Yeah."

He carried the bag back to the office like contraband.

That night Sarah had parent conferences and did not get home until after eight. Nate ate a sandwich over the sink, listened to the apartment stay empty around him, and waited for the hour to get late enough that putting paper on the table would not feel insane. When she came in she looked wrung out and teacher-polite, kissed his cheek absentmindedly, and went straight to the shower. By the time she fell asleep, he had the notebook out.

He sat at the kitchen table in boxer shorts and Dominic's old college T-shirt, the one with cracked lettering across the chest from a school Nate had never attended. The overhead light was too bright. The page looked almost blue under it.

He wrote on the cover first.

DOM

Then, after staring at it, he crossed that out so hard the pen tore the cardboard.

He opened to the first page.

Family.

Renata - bakery, Sunday dinners, will call if missed. Grabs face. Notices changes, calls them good.

Tommy - gambling, asks for money like asking for salt. May show up late. Charming when he needs something, quiet when scared.

Friends.

Marco DeSantis - college. Myrtle house. Montreal road trip. Jessica Brill? clove cigarettes. Prof Heller. Mother eye surgery / lemon cake / call Ma.

Brendan - insurance? Jersey? bachelor weekend maybe missed.

He stopped, tapped the pen against the page, then turned to a fresh sheet.

Sarah.

History teacher. Summer school in July. Parent conferences Oct. Coworker Jen. Cape trip Aug 2004: rain / Wellfleet raw bar / Truro drive / oysters bad / yellow place / he picked it / he talked about it after.

likes:

He stared at the word until it became stupid. Likes what. Tea? Books? Sex on Tuesdays? The side of the bed by the window? He crossed it out.

He started again.

What she knows:

Providence trip.

Concert tickets.

Fight about Renata.

Cape trip.

How he used to tell stories.

What he remembers without trying.

That line sat there longer than the others. He looked toward the bedroom, where she was asleep ten feet away behind a half-closed door. The shower had left a damp towel slung over the bathroom rack. Her work bag sat by the couch with folders sticking out of it. On top was one of her quiz piles held together with a binder clip. An ordinary life. The kind that did not expect to be cataloged by an intruder at midnight.

He turned another page.

Work.

Stu Halpern - Fairfield Commons. Access easement / anchor tenant / lender. Needed answer immediately. Ray covered once.

Ray - knows too much already. Short leash. Ask before calls if unsure.

Victor - don't improvise. Never.

Eastbridge - file right stack / demo 12/28 / Mort.

He pressed the pen so hard on Mort that the name embossed through three sheets.

Another page.

Habits.

Calls Ma? Sundays or Tues?

ball Sundays? no lately.

drinks beer not bourbon? ask indirectly.

college stories repeat: Atlantic City sophomore not senior.

He flipped back and forth, adding arrows, question marks, fragments too thin to deserve full lines. The notebook filled with the kind of information a normal person carried in muscle and embarrassment and affection. What brand of coffee Renata bought. Which clients Dominic could joke with. Which drawer held batteries. Sarah's friend from Providence whose name might have started with L. Tommy's debts. Marco's lemon-cake mother. Ray's limit.

At some point the whole thing tipped from practical to grotesque. He was not making reminders. He was building a user manual for a dead man's life, section by section, with his girlfriend under one tab and his boss under another and his own father jammed crooked in the middle of work notes because there was nowhere clean to put him.

He got up, went to the sink, drank water straight from the tap, came back, kept writing.

The pages made a dry insect sound every time he turned them. The spiral bit into the side of his hand. Once he thought he heard Sarah moving and slid the notebook under a grocery circular on instinct, heart punching against his ribs, but the apartment went quiet again.

Near one in the morning he made an index on the inside cover.

Family - 1
Friends - 3
Sarah - 5
Work - 7
Places / trips - 10
Things to ask without sounding insane - 12

He almost laughed at that last one. Almost.

On page ten he wrote CAPE COD across the top and tried to build a weekend out of scraps.

Rain.

Wellfleet.

Yellow restaurant.

Raw bar.

Truro.

Why did Dom hate oysters there?

Did he always hate oysters or only there?

What else happened that weekend?

Under that he wrote:

How many more of these are there?

He sat with the pen in his hand and listened to the refrigerator hum and the faint traffic on Fairfield Avenue and Sarah breathing through the cracked bedroom door. He had spent months telling himself the hard part was staying undiscovered until he could figure out what to do about the strip mall, Victor, all of it. That had been a comforting lie, one of the last ones available. The hard part was this. The stupid, endless density of another person's life. The millions of tiny remembered things that made a man legible to the people who loved him.

He looked down at the notebook. It was already twelve pages deep and full of holes.

By the time he put the cap back on the pen, he understood something he had been trying not to understand since July. He was not learning Dominic fast enough to become him. He was only getting better at measuring the distance.
