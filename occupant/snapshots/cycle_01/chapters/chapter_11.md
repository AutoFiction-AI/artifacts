# Chapter 11

Nate went into the office on Saturday because the office made more sense than the apartment.

At the apartment there was Sarah in one of his T-shirts, meaning Dominic's T-shirt, hair twisted up with a pencil while she stood over the sink rinsing a coffee mug and asking, not suspicious exactly, just careful now, whether he was really going in for half a day. At the office there were fluorescent lights, carpet tile, desktop hum, and problems that at least pretended to have folders.

He badged through the glass door a little after nine. The converted warehouse always felt temporary when nobody else was in it, like the whole company had moved in on a Friday and might be gone by Monday if the landlord changed his mind. Motion sensors woke sections of the ceiling one bank at a time. Gray cubicle walls, fake ficus by reception, the conference room with its long veneer table and whiteboard furred over with half-erased marker. Victor's corner office was dark. Ray's desk was empty except for a coffee ring and a Sports Illustrated with a folded page marking something important to him. The break-room coffee had been sitting since Friday and had reduced itself to a tar-black inch in the bottom of the glass pot. Nate poured some anyway.

He had come in telling himself this was triage. Thursday's client call had gone bad in a new and embarrassing way, not because he'd said the wrong thing but because he had not known who was on the line. Ray had seen his face when the name came up, that blank panic, and Ray had said what Nate had already known: You didn't know who that was. The spiral notebook was no longer optional after that. It sat open beside the keyboard now, blue cover bent back, pages divided into crude categories in Nate's blocky handwriting.

Clients.

Friends.

Family.

Things Dom apparently liked.

Things I already fucked up.

The whole project had become analog because 2005 refused to be searched the way he wanted it searched. There was no cloud history of Dominic Altieri's life waiting to be scraped. There were drawers, folders, a flip phone full of numbers without context, paper calendars, and this desktop computer that felt both ancient and intimate. He had spent the last week building himself a manual for being someone else. He hated the notebook. He needed it.

The computer clicked awake under his hand. Windows XP came up blue and cheerful and insulting. Outlook opened automatically because Dominic, unlike Nate, had apparently been the sort of person who wanted work waiting for him the second the machine finished loading. The inbox was already a swamp. Ray had sent two messages late Friday, one with a contractor estimate attached, one with a subject line that just said `call me monday`. Somebody in accounting wanted backup documentation for a reimbursement. A vendor was asking if the revised concrete schedule had been approved. Nate ignored all of it.

He was here for sent mail. Sent mail was safer. Sent mail was archaeology. He did not have to answer anybody in it.

On the left side of the screen the folder tree sat in its little hierarchy, plus signs and inbox counts, everything nested and contained. `Mailbox - Dominic Altieri.` `Inbox.` `Drafts.` `Sent Items.` `Deleted Items.` He clicked `Sent Items` and got a few seconds of white screen while the machine thought about whether to obey him. Then the list filled in.

There were hundreds of messages. Maybe more than a thousand. Dominic had been competent enough, conscientious enough, or trapped enough to leave a trail for everything. Site photos, invoice follow-ups, schedule confirmations, replies to lawyers, replies to contractors, replies to people Nate had never heard of and would probably have to pretend to know on Monday. Dominic wrote clean emails. Not fancy. Not corporate robot either. Full sentences. Specific asks. A thank-you at the end more often than Nate would have expected. He spelled people's names right. He answered questions the first time. Half the folder was ordinary office sediment, proof that a life could disappear and still keep producing small evidence of itself.

Nate took a swallow of the break-room coffee and made a face at the burnt taste. He flipped to a fresh page in the notebook and wrote `Sent Items` at the top like this was a plan and not panic with headings.

The original reason he'd come in was to find the client from Thursday, to work backward through Dominic's outgoing messages until the name attached to a face. But Outlook was bad at being searched in 2005 terms and Nate was bad at using 2005 search tools. He clicked around for a minute, found a `Find` field that seemed to search only whatever folder view he was already in, typed part of the company name as he remembered hearing it, got nothing, deleted it, swore softly, then sorted by subject, then by recipient, then back by date when both of those somehow made things worse. The little gray scroll bar on the right was microscopic. March through October compressed into a sliver.

He dragged the scroll thumb up too far, landed in late May, then nudged downward line by line with the mouse wheel.

`RE: Harbor Point revised grading plan`

`FW: Dreher fundraiser table`

`Eastbridge Plaza tenant notice draft`

He stopped.

The line sat there in the middle of everything else, same font, same narrow column, same beige Outlook screen. There was no red flag, no hidden archive, no password-protected confession. It was just another sent email in the work account of a man who handled projects for a development firm.

He clicked it.

The reading pane opened at the bottom. Subject: `Eastbridge Plaza tenant notice draft`. Date: June 8, 2005, 8:41 AM. From: Dominic Altieri. To: Victor Kassos.

The body was one line long.

"I understand. I'll handle the notifications."

That was it. There was no sign-off, no `Thanks, Vic`, no attempt to sound enthusiastic. There was just the sentence and the timestamp.

Nate stared at it long enough for the screen to dim a shade and then brighten again when he bumped the mouse. He felt the chair under him in a new way, the cheap cylinder giving a little as his weight shifted, the edge of the plastic arm digging into the soft inside of Dominic's forearm. `I'll handle the notifications.` As in the letters. As in Morton Lazar, Unit 7, Eastbridge Plaza. As in the paper Nate had held in August like it was a murder weapon with a signature at the bottom.

He moved up the thread.

Victor's message above it had come in three minutes earlier.

`Dom -`

`We've covered this. Notices go out today. Please handle.`

`V`

That was all. Victor's style stripped down to its studs. There was no greeting unless the name counted as one, and there was no explanation because explanation implied appeal. The message had been sent from Victor's desktop, not his BlackBerry, and Nate hated noticing that because it meant Victor had been at his desk too, maybe ten feet from where Nate sat now, deciding the final version of somebody else's ruin before lunch.

Nate scrolled higher.

The first message in the chain had been from March, subject line longer, more hopeful than the ones below it. `Eastbridge Plaza - alternatives before notice`. Dominic had written almost four months before Nate arrived in this body, before the botched pitch in Victor's office, before Gail's turkey clubs and Mort's hand across the diner counter, before Sarah started looking at him as if she was trying to line his face up with somebody she remembered.

Dominic's email was not a plea. That was the first thing. Nate had expected pleading or moral language or something naive and easy to dismiss. Instead it was work. Dominic had laid out three options in plain paragraphs. Preserve the end units through the first phase of demolition. Delay tenant notices sixty days to allow relocation, lease assignment, or inventory liquidation. Set aside a modest moving allowance for small businesses whose buildouts could not be replicated cheaply.

Then came the names.

`Morton Lazar, Lazar's Hardware, Unit 7, has sixteen years on site and inventory that will not clear in thirty days without a liquidation loss. Gail Ruiz's diner does morning business that will not survive a long closure. Mrs. Park's equipment is fixed into the current space in a way that makes relocation more expensive than the current notice window assumes.`

Nate read Morton's name once, then again, because the first time it had not fully entered his head as language. It was Morton Lazar, not Lazar's Hardware, not tenant #7, not end unit. It was his father, whole name, typed by Dominic Altieri in black letters on a gray screen on March 17 at 6:12 in the evening.

Nate had spent most of his adult life thinking of Dominic as a signature at the bottom of the notice. Dominic, in Nate's mythology, had not known who Mort was. Why would he? Guys in offices did not know the people they erased. That had been part of the whole point, the machine, the folder on Nate's laptop, the ugly steady certainty that if he could reach one man, one specific man, and remove him, the original sin of the whole thing would stand up and show its face.

Dominic knew Mort's name.

He knew how long the store had been there.

He knew what kind of loss a thirty-day clearance sale would force.

Nate kept reading.

Dominic had attached two files, both named in the flat professional style of someone who expected to need them later: `Eastbridge_alt_footprint.xls` and `tenant_relocation_notes.doc`. In the body of the email he summarized the numbers, small and practical. Preserving the end units through phase one would require revised fencing and utility routing but would not materially affect the delivery date for the main pad. A relocation stipend would cost less than even one contested commercial holdover. He was not arguing from justice. He was arguing in Victor's language: schedule, cost, risk. He had done the work.

Victor's reply, sent seventeen minutes later from his BlackBerry, was four lines.

`No.`

`The approved footprint assumes full clearance. Council signed off on this version. Investors are aligned on this version.`

`Do not reopen alternatives with the tenants.`

Under it was the little device footer, automatic and stupid and period-specific: `Sent from my BlackBerry Wireless Handheld`.

Nate looked at that footer longer than he should have. Some rich middle-aged guy somewhere, probably in a restaurant or the back of a town car, killing a month's worth of work with one thumb and a piece of plastic clipped to his belt. Dominic had attached spreadsheets. Victor had typed `No.` in a moving car.

He scrolled to April.

There was another thread, same subject line with `RE:` stacked on top of itself, Outlook compressing weeks into a handful of indented messages. Dominic again. He had not taken the first no for final. Nate's stomach moved unpleasantly at that. It would have been easier if Dominic had sent one decent email, gotten swatted, and folded. One brave gesture. One conscience scene. The kind of thing Nate could dismiss as a guy wanting to feel good about himself before doing the same thing anyway.

That was not what this was.

The April email was tighter, less explanatory, already written by somebody who knew the answer was probably no and wrote it anyway. Dominic proposed a reduced demolition sequence that kept Units 6 and 7 standing through winter. He pointed out that Lazar's Hardware had built shelving and specialty bins to the space over sixteen years and could not just roll that inventory into a van and reopen somewhere else on a weeknight. He noted that Gail Ruiz opened before dawn and did most of her business before ten, the kind of traffic a temporary closure killed for good because breakfast habits were brittle and people changed routes fast. He even mentioned the optics, carefully, like a man trying not to sound like he was using that word.

`If we are set on redevelopment, we can still avoid being the company that empties out a working strip in one shot when there are cleaner options.`

Nate leaned closer to the screen. Dominic had not written like Nate. That should not have surprised him, but it did. There was no vagueness in it, no hiding behind feeling. Dominic did not say this feels wrong. He said what could be changed, what it would cost, why it mattered, where the schedule flex was real and where it was imagined. Nate had gone into Victor's office in September with a mouthful of half-understood zoning words and panic-sweat in Dominic's collar. Dominic, months before, had made the same case without embarrassing himself once.

Victor's reply came the next morning at 5:58.

`We are not revising scope.`

`Demo contractor is already booked and capital is committed. Keep Eastbridge moving.`

There was no anger in it and no philosophy, nothing a person could grab and fight. There was only the language of doors closing in offices Nate would never see: contractor, capital, scope. Those were words with no blood in them doing bloody work.

By the time Nate got to the May messages his coffee had gone cold in the paper cup. He drank it anyway, because getting up felt too noticeable even though there was nobody there to notice him.

The May thread was the worst of them. Dominic had been worn down enough by then that the email started with `I know I've raised this more than once.` Nate actually shut his eyes for a second when he read that line. There it was, the office version of a flinch, a man announcing in writing that he understood the power arrangement before he made one last try against it.

He still tried.

He wrote that the current notice period all but guaranteed forced liquidation for the small tenants. He wrote that Morton Lazar in Unit 7 had merchandise built around long-tail inventory, specialty fasteners and hardware stock that had value only if the store had time to move it deliberately instead of dumping it cheap. He wrote that the diner, the cleaner, the barbershop, all of them, were part of the occupancy case that had kept the plaza stable in the first place, and that wiping them out in one move was the sort of thing that traveled farther than people expected. He recommended either a relocation package or a phased vacate date. He wrote like a person still trying to keep the job and keep the people. Nate could feel the shape of that compromise in every sentence.

Victor answered eleven minutes later.

`No extension.`

`Notice is June 8.`

`Please handle.`

Then came Dominic's final line.

"I understand. I'll handle the notifications."

Nate sat back and the chair rolled an inch on the carpet before catching. The office was so quiet he could hear the buzz in one of the fluorescent tubes over the conference room and the tiny hard mechanical tick inside Dominic's desktop tower. Outside the warehouse windows the October light was thin and whitish, the kind of fall morning that looked cold even when you were inside. Somewhere in the building ductwork something kicked on and a ribbon of air moved across the back of his neck.

He put his hand over his mouth, not because he thought he was going to throw up but because it seemed like the face might come apart otherwise.

This could not be right. Not because the emails were unclear. They were clear enough to hurt on the first pass. But because if they were right, then the whole structure of his life changed shape all at once and there was nothing underneath it except the act itself.

He scrolled back to the top and read the chain again.

The second time through, he stopped noticing the facts first and noticed the tone. Dominic had not been performing conscience for the record. There were no extra recipients copied in to witness his decency, no legal department, no private note to himself, no hedge language in case Victor ever pulled the messages in some review months later. He was not laying down a paper trail to save his ass. He was trying to move Victor. Then he was trying again. Then he was trying once more in a smaller, tighter way because the bigger way had failed.

On the second read Nate noticed other things too. Dominic always used Mort's full name, not just Lazar. He always wrote `Gail Ruiz`, never `the diner lady` or `tenant 3`. In one email he corrected the draft notice language from `personal property disposal` to `inventory removal` and wrote in the margin comments that the first phrase was likely to inflame people unnecessarily. In another he flagged that Mrs. Park's equipment had been financed and might carry removal penalties. That detail had nothing to do with Nate's father. Dominic had gone past Mort. He had looked at the whole strip and seen people in it.

Nate had not.

That landed harder than he expected. His whole righteous story, all those years, had been built around love for his father and hate for the man on the letter. But hate flattened everything outside its beam. He had never bothered to imagine the diner, the nail salon, the cleaner, the barber, not really, not until he started eating lunch there and hearing chairs scrape and mugs knock together and people say each other's names across the counter. Even his grief had been self-centered. It had been Mort, the store, March 2006. The rest had stayed background. Dominic, the guy Nate had killed for it, had seen more of the place from a file and a site visit than Nate had seen from nineteen years of anger.

He opened the top drawer of the file cabinet beside the desk and pulled the Eastbridge folder out with more force than he meant to. The metal drawer made a loud clean sound in the empty office, a report that seemed to hang there after it was over. He set the folder on the desk, fingers clumsy on the tab, and found the copy of the notice letter after less than a minute because he knew exactly where it was. He had read it enough.

`Morton Lazar`

`Lazar's Hardware`

`Unit 7, Eastbridge Plaza`

The formal language was the same as before. The date was the same. The bullshit courtesy about redevelopment and tenancy termination was the same. The signature at the bottom was the same too, in blue ink: Dominic Altieri, Project Coordinator.

In August this letter had felt like proof. Dominic had signed it. Dominic had sent it. Dominic had done the thing Nate needed him to have done, cleanly and personally, so the murder could keep making sense.

Now the letter sat on the desk beside the email chain and changed meaning without changing one word.

`I'll handle the notifications.`

This was what that meant. It did not mean agreement or enthusiasm or secret villainy finally dropping its mask. It meant Victor had said no for the last time and Dominic had stayed in the chair and done the part of the job that landed on his desk because the investors were aligned and the contractor was booked and the council had approved and his mother still needed Sunday dinners attended and Tommy still needed bailing out and Sarah still needed rent paid and ordinary life did not stop requiring money because your boss had made you do something rotten.

Nate looked at the signature until it blurred, and then at his own left hand, Dominic's left hand, broad and scarred across the knuckle, resting on the letter like it belonged there because of course it did. This hand had signed the notice. This hand had typed Morton's name into emails trying to stop the notice. This hand had touched Sarah's face in the dark while Nate stood behind the eyes and took credit for being softer than Dominic had been. This hand had passed six hundred dollars to Tommy. This hand had shaken Mort's in the diner.

He had been living inside the evidence for months and still had not understood what he was holding.

He thought about the machine in 2025, the copper coil and the modified magnetron and the AI's patient little paragraphs telling him where to solder what. He thought about his apartment, the half-broken blinds, the folder on his laptop labeled `ALTIERI`, every clipping and record and screenshot shoved into one place until a person became a target file. He had fed Dominic's name into the machine the way somebody keyed an address into a GPS. The difference was the machine had not taken him to the address. It had made him the address. Nate had called that justice because he needed some word for it that let him keep moving.

There was no word for it now that made it smaller than what it was.

He had not just killed the wrong man. Even that came out too neat, like an error in a report that could be corrected with a new line item. Wrong man implied there had been a right one waiting somewhere at the end of the sentence. The uglier thing was that he had killed a man who had already made the arguments Nate believed only he would ever think to make. Nate's big act of resistance in Victor's office, all his sweating half-understood sabotage, had been months late and amateur besides. Dominic had gone first. Dominic had gone better. Victor had shut him down and kept building.

Nate put the letter back in the folder and then took it out again, because putting it back felt too much like erasing the connection he had finally been forced to see. The paper had a soft office smoothness to it, handled but not often, and the edge nicked the pad of his thumb. He welcomed the tiny sting. It gave the moment a location in the desk, the paper, his thumb, the buzzing light.

He read the emails a third time, though by then it was really the second time that mattered, the second time being the pass where belief failed and the words did not.

`Morton Lazar, Lazar's Hardware, Unit 7`

`inventory that will not clear in thirty days without a liquidation loss`

`cleaner options`

`No extension.`

`Please handle.`

`I understand. I'll handle the notifications.`

The sentence looked different every time. On the first read it had been bureaucratic. On the second it was defeat. On the third it looked almost intimate in its plainness, the kind of sentence a person wrote when he was too tired to decorate the truth. It did not say I agree, or sounds good, or absolutely. It just said I understand. I hear the shape of the wall. I'll do the part of this you are making mine.

Nate imagined Dominic in this chair in June, maybe tie loosened, maybe jacket off on the hook, maybe rubbing at the bridge of his nose before he typed the line. He hated himself a little for the speculation. He had not earned tenderness toward Dominic. Tenderness was cheap after the fact. Still, the picture came. Not because Nate wanted it to, but because he had been wearing Dominic's body long enough now to know some of its habits. The way the shoulders tightened first. The way the left thumb rubbed across the scarred knuckle when he was thinking. The way this chair always sank a fraction under full weight. Dominic had been here. Dominic had tried. Dominic had lost. Nate had arrived a month later and called the corpse a villain.

There were tears in his eyes before he understood what was happening, not dramatic ones, not a breakdown, just enough to thicken the screen and make the letters fuzz at the edges. He wiped them hard with the heel of his palm, angry at the sentimentality of it even as he did it. Crying in a cubicle on a Saturday over a man he'd murdered was not useful. Nothing about this was useful.

He pulled the spiral notebook closer and tried to write what he had learned because writing things down was what the notebook was for. His pen hovered over the page.

March-June: Dom pushed back on Eastbridge.

He stopped. The sentence sat there in blue ink, thin and stupid. Pushed back. Like Dominic had objected to a meeting time. Like Mort's store, Gail's diner, Mrs. Park's equipment, sixteen years of shelves and screws and rent checks and morning coffee, all of that could fit into a phrase from a project update. Nate put the pen down so hard it bounced once on the notebook coil.

He crossed the sentence out until the paper nearly tore.

Then he closed the notebook.

For a while he just sat there with the emails open and the file beside the keyboard, not moving except to breathe. He listened to the building, the vent, the light, a truck down on the street backing up with the intermittent electronic beep that every truck in America seemed to use. Somewhere beyond the warehouse district, beyond the office park roads and the Saturday errands and the normal little lives of the city, Mort was alive in his house or his store or at the diner having coffee. Sarah was alive in the apartment Nate had stolen. Victor was alive too, probably on a golf course or at a fundraiser or somewhere equally upholstered, the sort of place from which a man could flatten a strip mall with six lines of email and never once say the word destroy.

Nate had built his adult life around a story because the story held the pieces in place. Mort had died because Dominic Altieri killed the store. Nate had killed Dominic Altieri because some acts deserved an answer. The story was ugly but load-bearing. Without it the weight did not go away. It just dropped lower, onto the thing itself. A 34-year-old man in 2025 had aimed a machine at a 31-year-old stranger in 2005 and erased him. Then he had gone on wearing the stranger's face while taking the stranger's job, the stranger's family, the stranger's bed, and telling himself there had at least been a reason.

There had been a letter.

There had been a signature.

There had also been this. The months of trying. The names. The spreadsheets. The stupid hope of a person who thought if he explained the damage clearly enough, somebody above him might decide not to do it.

Nate turned back to the monitor and read the March message one last time, slowly enough to hear the cadence of the sentences in his head without pretending it was Dominic's voice exactly. It was just a man's written voice, careful and competent, tired by the end but not cynical yet.

He had typed Morton's name because he was trying to keep Mort from getting erased by the process.

Nate sat very still in Dominic's chair, his hand on Dominic's mouse, Dominic's inbox at his back and Dominic's sent mail open in front of him, and understood with a flat, nauseating clarity that would not improve no matter how many times he read it again:

He had killed a man who was trying to help.
