# Chapter 12

By the time Nate got back to the apartment it was fully dark, the kind of late-October dark that felt a little premature, like the day had shut down before anybody had finished with it. The Civic ticked as it cooled. He sat with both hands on the wheel and watched the yellow squares of other people's windows across the lot. Someone on the third floor had a blue TV glow stuttering through the blinds. Somewhere nearby a dog barked twice and stopped.

Morton Lazar, hardware store, sixteen-year tenant.

The sentence had lived in his head for three days now. Dominic had written it in March, months before Nate arrived to kill him. He had typed Mort's name into an email to Victor because he was trying to keep the store from getting flattened. Trying and failing, but trying. Nate kept rereading the outbox at work like maybe one more pass would reveal the trick, the lie, the line that made the murder make sense again. It never did. The words just sat there in Outlook, plain as drywall. Morton's name. Unit 7. Community impact. Relocation assistance.

He took the grocery bag off the passenger seat and went upstairs.

Sarah was at the kitchen table with a stack of quizzes spread in front of her and a yellow legal pad full of names and numbers. She still had her school ID clipped to her cardigan. She always forgot to take it off until after dinner, like part of her stayed in teacher mode until somebody physically unhooked her from it. Her hair was down, not tied back the way she wore it at school, and there was a crease on one cheek from where she'd probably put her face down on her desk for ten minutes between the last bell and the faculty meeting she had mentioned that morning.

The apartment smelled like onions and the damp wool of her coat drying over the back of a chair.

"Hey," she said without looking up.

"Hey."

"Did you get bread?"

He held up the paper bag.

"Good." She made a mark on one of the quizzes, paused, then looked up at him. "One of my sophomores wrote that the Missouri Compromise happened in 1864 because Lincoln needed a win. A win. Like it was a baseball game."

Nate put the bread on the counter. "Maybe he was thinking of something else."

"He was thinking of absolutely nothing. That's what was happening." She capped her pen with her teeth, uncapped it again, and pointed at the stack. "I had three answers today that referred to slavery as a 'trade disagreement.' Public education is going great."

He managed a sound that could pass for a laugh.

Sarah watched him for a second longer than the joke required. "Long day?"

"Yeah. Kind of."

"Victor?"

"Work."

"That's what Victor is."

"Right."

She went back to grading. "There's soup on the stove. I started it before I sat down. Can you cut the bread?"

He washed his hands and reached for the knife block. Dominic's hands did everything with a little more confidence than his own ever had, even small kitchen stuff. Broader fingers. Better grip. The scar across the left knuckle pale under the overhead light. He cut the loaf and tried not to think about Dominic typing his father's name with those same hands. He failed.

The flip phone on the counter started ringing, the bright stupid polyphonic song he had never figured out how to change. Sarah glanced at the little outside screen before he could turn it over.

"It's your mom."

He dried his hand on the dish towel and looked at the phone. `MA` in blue-white block letters.

It kept singing.

"You gonna get that?" Sarah asked.

"In a second."

He let it ring once more, twice. Then it stopped.

Sarah put down her pen. "It's Tuesday."

He looked at her. "So?"

"So it's Tuesday." She said it like she was reminding him of garbage day. "You used to call her on the drive home every Tuesday."

"I was at work."

"You were home by six ten."

He checked the soup because it gave him somewhere to put his face. Lentils, carrots, onion, a little sausage. Sarah cooked like she taught, not fancy, just organized and competent and slightly insulted by the existence of people who couldn't follow directions. Steam hit his face. "I can call her back."

"You can." She was quiet. "You just don't."

He ladled soup into bowls. The apartment was small enough that silence had nowhere to go. It sat right there between the stove and the table and the couch, taking up space like another person.

Sarah picked up her pen again but didn't write anything. "Can you grab the peeler?"

"Yeah."

"By the sink."

He opened the top drawer on the left side. Measuring spoons, chip clips, a takeout menu folded into quarters, a packet of soy sauce gone stiff.

"Other one," Sarah said.

He opened the second drawer. Forks, knives, spoons.

Sarah looked at him.

He closed it and opened the shallow drawer under the oven mitts, which held dish towels and a flashlight with dead batteries.

"Dom."

Not loud. Not angry yet. Just his name, but it hit him the same way it always did, with that instant wrongness in his chest, like he'd been tagged by a label printer and the sticker didn't belong on him. He turned too fast.

The peeler was hanging on a hook inside the cabinet door under the sink. Sarah got up, crossed the kitchen in three steps, took it down herself, and peeled the carrots over the trash with quick flat strokes.

Nate stood there with his hands empty.

"Sorry," he said.

"No, you're not." She kept peeling. Orange ribbons dropped into the can. "You're embarrassed, maybe."

"I said I was sorry."

"You've lived in this apartment for over a year."

"I forgot where the peeler was."

"No." She set the carrot down and finally looked at him. "That's not what happened."

He could feel the shape of the argument before it arrived, like weather pressure. "What are you talking about?"

"You guessed." She wiped the blade of the peeler with her thumb and set it next to the cutting board. "You guessed where it would be."

"Because I forgot."

"No, because you do that now. With everything."

He laughed once, bad decision, dead on arrival. "It's a peeler."

"It's not a peeler."

The radiator in the bedroom knocked twice. Outside somebody's muffler coughed in the lot. Sarah leaned a hip against the counter and crossed her arms, and the shift in her face was so slight he almost missed it. Not more emotional. Less. The temperature went down.

"Then what is it?" he said.

"Do you want the short version or the long one?"

"Sarah."

"No, I mean it. Because I've got both."

He put the bread knife down carefully. "I don't know why we're doing this."

"Because you opened three wrong drawers and then looked at me like I was the one being weird."

"I didn't look at you like anything."

"You did." She took a breath. "And because it's not the drawer. I know it isn't the drawer. You know it isn't the drawer. I'm tired, and I had a meeting that should've been an email, and one of my students called World War One 'the first Iraq war,' and I came home wanting soup and maybe a normal evening. But I'm not doing another normal evening where I pretend I don't notice what's right in front of me."

He said nothing.

Sarah nodded once, like she'd expected that. "Fine. Long version."

She went back to the table, but only to move the stack of quizzes out of the way. It was the same motion she made when clearing a desk before a conference with a parent. Space for evidence.

"You don't know where things are anymore," she said. "Not just the peeler. The batteries. The extra sheets. The good can opener. You stand in the kitchen and think about cabinets before you open them. You ask me where we keep coffee filters every other week."

"People forget stuff."

"Not this much."

"I've had a lot on my mind."

"I know. That's what you say every time." She folded her hands. "You stopped calling your mother on Tuesdays. You used to do it every week on the drive home, and then you'd come in and tell me what Tommy broke or borrowed or lied about. That stopped in July."

July. The word landed hard enough that he almost flinched again.

"You stopped leaving notes." Her voice stayed level. "Not romantic notes. I'm not talking about movie shit. You left dumb little reminders on the coffee maker. Buy detergent. Pick up my blazer. Tell Tommy absolutely not. I still have one on the fridge from June because it made me laugh and I never took it down. That stopped."

He stared at the tabletop. There was a faint burn ring near the edge from before he got here, maybe before Dominic and Sarah had even moved in together.

"You don't finish stories the same way," Sarah said.

"What does that even mean?"

"It means I'll mention something and you wait. Like you're waiting for the rest of it. Or you nod too fast because you think you've gotten enough context to get through the scene." She shook her head, not dramatic, just baffled by him. "Marco came through town and you got away with it because Marco likes hearing himself talk. But I watched you. I watched you do math with your face for two hours."

So she had seen that too. Of course she had.

"The Cape trip," she said. "That was not a one-time bad memory. I let that go because people blank on things. But it wasn't a blank. It was like you were hearing about it for the first time and trying to reverse-engineer the correct reaction."

"I was tired."

She looked at him.

"OK," he said. "Yeah. I didn't remember one dinner."

"It wasn't one dinner."

"What do you want me to say?"

"Something true would be a nice change."

He laughed again, and this one sounded ugly even to him. "Jesus."

"Don't." It came out sharper, and then she lowered her voice again. "Do not do that thing where you act like I'm overreacting because you're uncomfortable."

He leaned back against the counter and crossed his arms because she had crossed hers first and apparently he was reduced now to bad copying. "I'm not doing anything."

"That's exactly the problem."

She got up and came into the kitchen again, not close enough to touch him, just close enough that he couldn't pretend this was a conversation happening in another room.

"Sometimes I say your name and you look scared for half a second." She tapped two fingers against the counter between them. "Not guilty. Not distracted. Scared. Like I caught you doing something, and all I did was say 'Dom' from the bathroom."

He opened his mouth.

"And don't tell me I'm imagining that either, because I'm not." Her eyes were dry. That made it worse. "Sometimes I kiss you and you go still first. Not all the time. Enough. Sometimes I tell you a story about my day and you ask me a question I already answered because you were somewhere else. Sometimes you look at me like you're trying very hard to be exactly the right amount of affectionate."

His face felt hot. He could hear the burner under the soup, low and steady.

"You want specifics?" Sarah said. "You used to grab the back of my neck when you passed behind my chair. Every time. It was annoying. I told you it was annoying. You kept doing it because you said my neck was there and your hand was there and it would be weird not to. You haven't done that once in months."

Nate looked away.

"You used to argue with me about Tommy in exactly the same order every time," she said. "First you'd say he was your brother. Then you'd say this was the last time. Then you'd say that like it meant anything. Now you just go quiet and look sick."

His throat worked. Nothing useful came out.

"You used to know when I was pretending not to be upset," Sarah said. "That was maybe the thing you were best at. Now I can go around this apartment like a storm cloud for an hour and you don't see it unless I tell you directly."

"That's not fair."

"No?" She tilted her head. "Then tell me what's fair."

"I've been trying."

"At what?"

"At all of this."

"What is 'all of this'?"

He spread his hands. The apartment. Work. Life. Time travel murder. Fraud. The stolen body. The dead man in the outbox. None of it had names he could safely use.

"Everything," he said finally, which was pathetic the second it left his mouth.

Sarah blinked once, slowly. "Do you hear yourself?"

"I'm going through something."

There it was. The true useless sentence. He hated himself before she even answered.

"I know," she said. "Everybody's going through something. That's not a passport. You don't get to stamp that on the counter and walk past the rest."

"It's not that simple."

"Then make it less simple. Use more words."

He rubbed a hand over his face. "Work is bad. Victor's on me all the time. Tommy's a mess. I don't sleep great. I'm trying to keep up."

"All right." Sarah nodded again. "That covers work. It covers your family. It doesn't cover why you look at me sometimes like you need a second to remember what I'm doing here."

"That's not what happens."

"It is."

"No, it isn't."

"Then what is it?" She waited. "Because from where I'm standing, I live with a man who knows things he should not know and doesn't know things he should. I live with a man who is sweeter than he used to be in some ways, which would be nice if it didn't feel like it came from somewhere I can't see. I live with a man who keeps acting like he wants credit for suffering when I can't even get a straight answer about where the hell he went."

The last part landed harder because it was accurate. He had gone somewhere. Twenty years and one body away.

"I'm here," he said, and even to him it sounded weak.

Sarah looked at him with open disbelief. "Are you?"

Neither of them moved.

The soup started bubbling too hard. Nate reached past her to turn down the burner and she stepped back automatically, making space for him without thinking. The old choreography of sharing a tiny kitchen. That hurt more than if she had flinched away.

"I'm not the same person I was," he said.

The sentence came out before he could stop it. Too close to the bone, and useless anyway.

Sarah stared at him. "I know."

He looked up.

"That isn't news," she said. "That's the entire issue."

He let out a breath through his nose. "People change."

"Yes." She gave him the answer the way she probably gave test instructions. Plain, patient, furious. "People change. They do not wake up one day and stop knowing a year of their own life. They do not forget how their own kitchen works. They do not start reacting to their own name like it's a prank."

Nate leaned both hands on the counter and stared at the laminate. It had a fake stone pattern that fooled nobody. He could feel Sarah waiting for something more. Something real. The room seemed full of possible sentences, all of them catastrophic.

I killed your boyfriend.

I came here because I thought he destroyed my father.

It was me in the bed in July and August and all the nights after.

He couldn't even hold the first two in his head long enough to look at her.

"Is there somebody else?" Sarah asked.

He looked up so fast his neck twinged. "No."

That at least was simple.

She believed him. He could tell by the tiny shift in her face, the way one possibility left the board. It did not help.

"Then what?" she said. Not louder. Lower. "If it's not another woman, if it's not drugs, if it's not some head injury you're too embarrassed to tell me about, then what is it?"

The fact that she had to reach for head injury before the truth was almost funny. If he had told her he was hiding a tumor, maybe the relationship would have survived.

"I don't know how to explain it," he said.

"Try."

"You wouldn't believe me."

Sarah actually laughed then, a short exhausted sound. "That's not for you to decide."

"It matters if it's true."

"You saying it matters if it's true isn't doing great things for my blood pressure right now."

He pushed away from the counter. There was too much body in the room all of a sudden, his own borrowed body an obstacle to thinking. He walked to the living room, turned, came back. Sarah watched him pace once, which was humiliating because Dominic probably had not done a lot of apartment pacing.

"I've been trying to figure some things out," he said.

"About us?"

"About everything."

"What does that mean?"

"It means I..." He stopped. Started again. "I haven't been handling things well."

"No."

"I know."

"Do you?" Sarah asked. "Because from where I'm sitting, you think that if you stay calm enough and say the vaguest possible thing, eventually I'll get tired and let it go."

He didn't answer.

"That's what you've been doing, by the way." Her voice stayed maddeningly even. "And maybe it works at work. Maybe your boss likes hearing 'I'm on it' and 'it's been a long week' and all the other bullshit non-answers men say to each other instead of having a real conversation. It doesn't work here."

"I know."

"Do not say that unless you actually mean it."

"What do you want from me?"

Sarah looked genuinely startled by that. Not theatrically. She had not expected him to ask.

"I want to know who I'm living with," she said.

He stopped moving.

The silence after that was so clean it almost hummed.

Sarah's gaze dropped to the table for a second, to the quizzes she'd abandoned. When she looked back up, there was more hurt in her face than anger now, and he found that harder to stand.

"I know this sounds crazy," she said. "I know how it sounds. I'm not saying you've been replaced by a pod person or whatever." She rubbed the bridge of her nose. "I'm saying the person I've been with since July is not... he's not where you used to be. Maybe that's depression. Maybe it's something else. Maybe you're lying to me about one specific thing and the lie is eating the rest of you. I don't know. But I know I'm not stupid, and I know I'm not making it up because it's a rainy Tuesday and I need a hobby."

Rainy. He hadn't even registered the weather past the dark. Now he could hear it, a light dry tapping at the bedroom window.

"You're not stupid," he said.

"No, I'm not."

"I didn't say you were."

"No, you just act like I'm the problem whenever I notice something inconvenient."

He started to say that's not fair again and stopped because she would be right about that too.

Sarah pulled out one of the kitchen chairs and sat down, suddenly looking tired in a way she hadn't a minute ago. The teacher voice was still there, but the effort under it showed. She tucked one foot under the other calf, a posture so familiar to him now that it tightened something in his chest. On the chair beside her was her tote bag, unzipped, with a clementine rolling loose at the bottom against a stack of photocopied primary sources.

"I'm gonna make this very easy," she said.

He hated the formal tone because it meant she had gone past the part where she wanted comfort. "OK."

"No." She raised a hand. "Don't say OK like you're agreeing to a dinner reservation. Listen."

He shut up.

"I'm done pretending this is stress," she said. "Stress is forgetting to buy milk. Stress is snapping at me because Victor's an asshole. Stress isn't months of looking at your own life like it's somebody else's coat."

He winced before he could stop himself.

Sarah saw that and kept going.

"If there is something happening to you that you can say out loud, say it. If there is something you're hiding, stop hiding it. If what you need is help, real help, then we can deal with that. But I'm not going to keep living like this, where I have to decide every day whether to trust my own eyes."

"Sarah..."

"No. I'm talking."

He nodded.

"I don't need the whole polished explanation tonight. I don't need you to suddenly become great at feelings. I need one honest thing that is actually about this house, this relationship, me and you. Not Victor. Not Tommy. Not some huge weather system of unnamed misery." She held his gaze. "If you can't give me that, then things aren't fine between us. And I need you to stop acting like they're fine."

His mouth was dry. "I never said they were fine."

"You crawl into bed like they're fine."

That shut him up completely.

Sarah looked away first, toward the dark window over the sink. "Yeah," she said, almost to herself. "Exactly."

He felt the sentence in his body like a physical strike. Crawl into bed. The crude accuracy of it. The fact that she still did not know the half of it.

"I'm sorry," he said again, and this time he meant it in the only way available to him: vast, useless, too late.

Sarah's expression didn't change. "I know."

"I don't know what to say."

"That's also part of the problem."

"I'm trying."

"Maybe. But you're trying in a way that leaves all the work to me." She stood up. "I don't want to have this conversation at the kitchen counter with lentils on the stove, but apparently that's what we're doing because if I wait for you to bring anything to me on your own, I'll be eighty."

He almost smiled despite himself. Not because it was funny. Because it was so specifically her, irritated even in pain, reaching for the exact ordinary exaggeration instead of some big speech. The smile died instantly when he saw her notice it.

"Don't," she said.

"I wasn't..."

"You were. And I need you not to do that either. Don't make me feel like I'm being dramatic so you can stay comfortable."

"I'm not comfortable."

"Good."

He shut his mouth again.

Sarah turned off the burner, carried the pot to the back of the stove, and covered it. Dinner reduced to a future problem. Then she walked into the bedroom. Nate followed two steps behind and stopped at the doorway while she opened the hall closet and took out the spare blanket.

"What are you doing?" he asked, though the answer was obvious.

"I'm sleeping on the couch."

"I'll take the couch."

"No."

"Sarah."

"No." Still quiet. Still worse than yelling. She tucked the blanket under her arm and reached for a pillow. "You can keep the bed."

"I'd rather take the couch."

"This isn't about furniture."

He stood in the doorway with one hand on the frame. Her side of the bed still had the book she'd been reading face down on the nightstand, spine bent in a way he had once, stupidly, been irritated by. Now the sight of it made him feel sick.

"Please don't do this," he said.

She turned toward him then, really turned, pillow in one hand, blanket in the other. "That sentence only works if there's an alternative you're offering me."

He had none.

"Right," she said.

He could have told her then. He knew that later, lying awake, because he kept locating the exact three-second space where it had still technically been possible. Not likely. Not survivable. But possible. She was standing right there in the bedroom they shared, waiting with a patience so thin it was nearly translucent. He could have said my name is Nate. He could have said I came here for the wrong man and killed the right one. He could have said every decent thing you've noticed in me belongs to somebody who had no right to use them.

Instead he said, "I don't want you out there."

Sarah let out a breath through her nose. "Then give me a reason to stay in here."

He looked at her and had nothing that wasn't a weapon.

After a second she nodded, once, exactly like she had at the counter. Decision made, evidence entered, discussion over. She walked past him with the pillow and blanket, the scent of her shampoo following her into the living room.

He stood in the bedroom doorway while she spread the blanket on the couch with quick efficient snaps. She took off her glasses and set them on the coffee table on top of one of the ungraded quizzes. Then she lay down facing the back cushions, fully clothed except for her shoes.

"Sarah."

"Please leave me alone tonight."

He stayed there one second too long.

Without turning around, she said, "And don't call your mother because you think it'll fix something. Call her if you want to talk to her."

He almost said I know. He caught himself.

He went back into the bedroom and shut the door because she had not asked him to, but it felt implied.

* * *

The bed was too big with only one body in it.

That was not even true, technically. It had always been a normal queen bed in a normal apartment bedroom. The room had not changed size because Sarah was on the couch. But absence had scale. It took measurements. It turned the dent in her pillow into a statement and the folded-back corner of her side of the comforter into evidence.

Nate sat on the edge of the mattress and listened to the apartment settle.

Radiator.

Traffic on the wet street.

One of the upstairs neighbors running water through bad pipes.

The couch springs in the other room complaining as Sarah adjusted herself once and then went still.

He put his face in his hands.

The easiest version of the story would have been a nervous breakdown. He could feel himself reaching for it automatically even now, the way a tongue found a sore tooth. Work pressure. Sleep problems. He had been dissociating, maybe. He could learn that word in a way 2005 would accept. He could say Tommy had gotten worse. He could say the Eastbridge project was getting to him, which was even true. He could give her a piece of real misery and let it stand in for the whole structure.

He was already building the lie while sitting in the aftermath of being accused of lying. That seemed worth noting. Not because it would stop him. Just because the system diagnostics should be honest at least somewhere.

He lay back without getting under the covers. The ceiling had a hairline crack running from the light fixture toward the corner. He had looked at that crack after sex more than once, Sarah asleep against his shoulder, and told himself he was staying because there was nowhere else to go. That had been true. It had also been convenient.

He tried the confession in his head again.

My name is Nate Lazar.

Too abrupt. Sounded like an AA meeting.

I'm not Dominic.

Not even wrong, exactly. Biologically, he was Dominic. Fingerprints, face, the whole cursed hardware stack.

I came here from 2025 in a machine I built with AI instructions and a pile of parts from Amazon because I thought Dominic Altieri destroyed my father's life, and instead of sending me back physically it projected my consciousness into his body and erased him.

That was closer. Also not a sentence you said to a woman already sleeping on the couch because you couldn't find the vegetable peeler.

He rolled onto his side and stared at Sarah's pillow. There were three dark hairs caught in the cotton. One of them curved across the fabric like a question mark. The intimacy of noticing that now, after what had happened, made him feel disgusting.

He tried again, smaller.

I killed someone.

True. Still useless.

I killed your boyfriend.

True. More useful. Catastrophic.

I've been sleeping with you under false pretenses since July.

There it was. The sentence around which all the others orbited. Not time travel. Not identity. Not even murder, if he was being completely honest about what he was most unwilling to say out loud in a room with Sarah Chen on the other side of the wall. That was the sentence that made his mouth refuse to work.

Because if he said it, he would have to watch her understand every other thing retroactively. The first time in the kitchen. The bed. Her hand on his chest while she fell asleep. The nights she had turned toward him half awake and kissed Dominic's shoulder with her eyes still closed and found Nate there instead, Nate who had hated the man she thought she was touching and then stopped hating him too late to matter.

It wasn't only that she would think he was insane. She would. She should. It was that if, for even one second, she believed him, then the room would have to hold the correct shape of what he'd done. There would be no more stress, no more distance, no more vague male damage hanging in the air like mildew. There would be the fact itself, sitting between them with its ugly name on it.

Fraud was too clean a word for a bed.

He put his forearm over his eyes. Dominic's body was good at holding tension in silence. Different shoulders than his own had been. Different back. He wondered if Dominic had ever lain here after a fight with Sarah and known exactly what to say in the morning to repair it. He wondered if that knowledge lived anywhere except in people Nate kept hurting.

From the living room came the soft rustle of paper. Sarah wasn't asleep after all. He stayed very still.

The rustle came again, then the tiny click of her glasses being picked up, then set back down. Maybe she'd tried to read. Maybe she'd just needed something to do with her hands. He pictured her on the couch under the spare blanket, knees bent because the couch wasn't long enough, one arm folded under the pillow. She hated sleeping in the living room when she had a cold because the radiator out there never worked right. Last winter Dominic had brought the space heater in from the bedroom closet and put it near the coffee table. Nate knew that because the heater was still in the closet and because Sarah had mentioned it once in August, smiling about how he always overdid the extension cords.

You don't know where things are anymore.

No, he thought. Not anymore. Not at all. He knew where the heater was now only because he had been told.

He sat up.

For a second he thought he might actually do it, walk into the living room and tell her the whole thing before cowardice had time to put its shoes back on. He put his feet on the floor. The carpet was cold through his socks. He stood, went to the bedroom door, and opened it two inches.

The living room lamp was still on. Sarah was a shape under the blanket, turned away, one shoulder making a small ridge. Her tote bag sat on the floor beside the couch, one paper halfway out like a tongue. The TV was off. The apartment looked staged for a product nobody would buy: failed domestic life, one-bedroom model.

He stepped into the hallway.

The floorboard near the bathroom creaked. Sarah did not turn over, but he saw her shoulders tighten.

"What?" she said.

Her voice was tired, not inviting. Not curious.

He had come out here to tell the truth and immediately found himself trying to assess whether the timing was right for it, which answered that.

"Nothing," he said.

The word hung there, pathetic and familiar.

Sarah was silent for a beat. Then: "Please don't do this if it's another nothing."

He stood in the dark hall with one hand on the wall. The paint had a rough spot where somebody had patched an old nail hole badly.

This was the second opening tonight, maybe the last one he would get that looked anything like grace. He could still take it. He could say there is no version of this that makes me less dangerous. He could say you have been right every single day. He could say Dominic Altieri tried to save my father and I erased him anyway.

Instead he said, "I'm sorry."

Sarah made a sound that might have been a laugh and might have been a breath catching in the wrong shape. "I know," she said. "That isn't enough."

"I know."

"Then go to bed."

He stood there another second, long enough to understand what she had given him in that sentence. Not forgiveness. Not even proximity. A simple instruction because she was tired and because somebody had to end the interaction in a functional language.

He went back into the bedroom.

This time he got under the covers because not doing so had begun to feel theatrical, and he was in no position to accuse anyone else of performance. The sheets were cool on Sarah's side. He kept to his own half out of instinct and because crossing the middle seemed obscene.

He thought about the two other lives he had access to and none of the lives he wanted. Fourteen-year-old Nate somewhere in Bridgeport probably doing algebra homework badly or watching TV with his shoes on. Dominic Altieri dead in July because an embittered stranger from the future had decided a name on paperwork was enough to justify deletion. Sarah on the couch ten feet away because Nate had decided, over and over, that not confessing was a temporary problem instead of the substance of his life.

The rain eased up. A car went by on the street outside with its bass turned high enough to rattle the blinds for a second. Then quiet again.

He could tell her tomorrow, he thought. Not the whole thing. Something. Enough to keep her from leaving. The thought arrived fully formed and businesslike, and he recognized in it the exact same mechanism that had gotten him through every bad conversation at work since July. Offer a manageable truth. Buy time. Patch the visible failure. He hated the familiarity of it.

Still, he held onto it because the alternative was to get up right now and say words that would end the only relationship in his stolen life that felt like anything but punishment. It was not noble that he couldn't do it. It was not strategic either. It was fear, plain and middle-sized and complete.

Out in the living room the couch springs gave once, then again, as Sarah shifted. He pictured the cushion seam pressing into her hip. He pictured her awake, eyes open, staring at the dark the way he was. There was a version of this night in which he went to her and made it worse in the honest direction. There was another version in which he stayed here and let morning do what mornings did, which was dull the edges and make survival look like a task list.

He stayed where he was.

After a while that stopped being hesitation and became a decision.
