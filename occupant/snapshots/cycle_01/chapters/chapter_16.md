# Chapter 16

The conference room windows looked out on the loading dock, where a gray skin of old ice had survived three days of weak sun by hiding in the strip of shadow thrown by the warehouse wall. Nate stood at the end of the table with a legal pad he did not need and watched Ray flatten the Eastbridge site plan with both palms. The paper had been opened and rolled and opened again so many times the corners no longer obeyed. Somebody had drawn a thick orange perimeter around the whole footprint months ago. This morning someone else had added block letters in blue marker across the center: DEMO 12/28. There were twelve days left.

Not the barbershop. Not Gail's counter. Not Unit 7. Just demo, as if the whole strip mall had already become one thing.

Ray sat down first. He had a dry split at the edge of his thumb from the weather and kept catching it against the spiral on his notebook. He swore under his breath once, put the thumb in his mouth without thinking, then went back to flipping through a packet of permits. He looked tired in the stale fluorescent way everybody in the office looked tired by December, necks disappearing into quarter-zips, faces roughened by radiator heat and the dark at five o'clock.

Victor came in last with his coat over one arm and a clementine in the other hand. He'd peeled it cleanly, one orange ribbon dangling from his fingers. The room picked up the smell before he sat down.

"All right," he said. "Let's get this out of the way."

He did not sound grim. He sounded mildly inconvenienced, the way he sounded discussing snow removal or a delayed appraisal. That was part of what made him good at this. He could move a whole building toward destruction in the same tone another man used to confirm a lunch reservation.

The speakerphone in the middle of the table gave a burst of static and then a man's voice said, "You got me?"

"We got you, Sal," Victor said. "Go ahead."

Sal from Pugliese Demo was all throat and no face, a smoker's rasp coming through cheap office plastic. He ran down his side of it. Fencing delivered the day before. Excavator and loader on site by seven. Dumpster placement along the south edge so traffic could still move through the far end of the lot until the city shut the lane. Utility disconnect confirmed for the twenty-seventh. One last asbestos bag pickup from the boiler wrap behind the old nail salon.

Nate knew what the words meant now. That was another small ugliness he had not asked for but had acquired anyway. In July the whole language of Dominic's work had bounced off him like a foreign operating system. Now each item landed cleanly. The machine was not abstract anymore. It was plastic mesh fence zip-tied to posts. It was a utility crew pulling meters. It was a loader bucket nosing through cinder block where Gail had poured coffee for twenty years.

Ray said, "Police detail's set for seven to eleven. After that it's just the crew and site watch."

"Good," Victor said.

He slid a stapled packet toward Nate. Yellow tabs had been stuck to three places along the right edge. Nate looked at his own typed name on the cover sheet without touching it.

FINAL SITE CLOSEOUT
EASTBRIDGE PLAZA
Project Coordinator: Dominic Altieri

"I want the vacancy certifications in there, site photos, utility confirmation, and the release for the contractor," Victor said. "Nothing exotic. Just make it complete. Dreher's office gets jumpy when there are loose ends."

There was a brief silence in which the heater clicked in the wall and Sal coughed into whatever room he was sitting in.

Nate said, "Lazar's still has stock on the floor."

Victor looked at him. Not sharply. Not yet. Just looked.

"They're clearing it," Victor said.

"I know, but they're not finished."

"By Wednesday they will be."

Ray shifted in his chair, eyes on the permit packet, not on Nate.

Nate kept his hand on the legal pad. The cardboard cover had started to soften at the corners from the damp in his palm. "If they aren't?"

Victor separated a section of clementine with his thumbnail. He chewed before he answered. "Then we send laborers in and finish it. We are not moving the date."

Sal's voice crackled. "Weather looks good, too. Cold's better than rain."

Victor pressed the speakerphone button once with his forefinger to mute it and looked back at Nate.

"Dom," he said, and now the warmth was there, the version of concern he used when he wanted obedience to feel like guidance. "We're done having this conversation."

It was almost the same tone he might have used to tell a nephew not to park on the lawn. Nate heard what sat underneath it anyway. Victor had already given him the education, the warning, the cleaner language for the trap. Investors. Councilman. Contract chain. Everyone already fed. There was nothing left now but timing and paper.

"I'm not trying to have it again," Nate said.

"Good."

Victor unmuted the phone.

Sal started talking about the order of demolition, starting with the center units and working out so the unsupported spans did not behave unpredictably. Ray asked a question about debris haul-off. Victor wrote a note in the margin of the site plan in compact, neat capitals. Nate sat there and listened to them discuss the sequence in which Eastbridge Plaza would stop being a place.

The strip mall came apart in the room before any machine touched it. First the utility line. Then the fencing. Then the police detail. Then the laborers. Then the bucket through the facade. Gail's diner existed for eight more business days and already had a dumpster allocation.

When the meeting ended, Ray gathered his permits and stood.

"You want me to chase the city photos?" he asked Nate.

"No, I got it."

Ray nodded. He hesitated like he might say something else, then didn't. He rubbed at the cracked thumb again and left.

Victor stayed seated, coat still over the back of his chair. He was pulling the white threads off the clementine pith and laying them in a neat little pile beside his coffee cup.

"This is the part people always think is dramatic," he said.

Nate did not answer.

"It never is. That's why it works."

He looked up then, not smiling, not unkind.

"I know you don't like this project. I know that. But there is a difference between disliking a thing and putting yourself in front of it. You understand me?"

Nate could have lied. He could have nodded and done the easy version of Dominic, the agreeable one, the one he had gotten better at by repetition and fear. Instead he said, "Yeah."

Victor leaned back. "Finish the packet. Get it to me Monday. Then go home for Christmas and eat too much. That's my advice."

He said it like a man doing Nate a favor. Like the problem on the table was how much turkey they would all be pressured to consume by their families.

Nate picked up the packet and left before he said something stupid enough to matter.

At his desk he set the paperwork down on top of the Eastbridge file and stared at the yellow tabs for a full minute without sitting. He could hear the office moving around him. Copier lid slapping shut. A laugh from accounting. Ray on the phone saying, "No, if they want the revised survey they'll have it after lunch, not before." Every sound ordinary. Every sound insulting.

The cover sheet sat on top, clean and official, and under it were the pieces of the thing: photo checklist, contractor release, utility notice, site closeout summary, and the signature page flagged in yellow. The signature line was not on top. You had to work toward it. That was funny in a way he hated. Even now the system wanted him to feel like there had been steps, like the end was an outcome of diligence rather than the point of the whole exercise.

He slid the packet into his drawer, shut it, and went to Gail's for lunch because he did not know where else to put his body for the next hour.

The bell over the diner's door gave its tired metal cough when he came in. Heat and fryer grease hit him at once, the smell of coffee boiled past dignity, onions from the flat-top, bleach from a recent mop. Gail was behind the counter rolling silverware into paper napkins. She had one sneaker unlaced and kept flexing the foot inside it between wraps like her arch hurt. A little fake pine garland drooped across the pie case, one corner already falling off the tape.

"You want the usual?" she asked without looking up.

"Yeah."

"We're out of rye."

"White's fine."

"Soup's better today."

"I know."

She snorted. "Then have the soup."

He took a stool anyway. The counter had been worn satin-smooth where thousands of forearms had rested. Through the front windows he could see the strip mall lot in winter light, the slush pushed to the edges in black ridges. Two doors down, Lazar's Hardware had both front lights on even though the afternoon was bright.

Gail set coffee in front of him before he asked. "You look like hell."

"Thanks."

"No problem."

She moved away to shout an order to the cook, came back, took a sip from the mug she kept hidden under the register, and jerked her chin toward the windows.

"They're packing over there," she said.

Nate followed her glance without moving his head much.

At first all he saw was the hardware store's hand-painted sign and the poster for snow shovels in the window. Then Mort crossed the front of the store carrying a box with both arms wrapped around it. Not a moving box. A produce carton, banana logo on the side, the cardboard bowed in the middle from weight. He moved carefully, not because he was old, not yet, but because the box was full of metal and you learned the weight of metal by respecting it.

Linda came into view a second later behind the glass, a tape gun in one hand, black marker in the other. She set the tape gun on the counter, tucked the marker behind her ear, and used both hands to flatten the top of another carton before sealing it. Then she stood still for a moment, talking to Mort through the open aisle. Nate could not hear her through the diner glass, but he knew that posture: the practical argument, not angry exactly, just efficient and tired.

Gail wiped the counter with a towel that had given up on being clean around ten that morning.

"Mort brought over six coffee cans," she said. "You know, the big ones from Restaurant Depot? Wanted to know if I had room in back for them because he doesn't want to throw out the loose fasteners yet. Says if they dump those in boxes they'll end up at the bottom with all the saw blades and nobody'll ever sort them again."

She gave a dry laugh that carried no amusement in it.

"Linda told him he was out of his mind and took two anyway."

Nate wrapped both hands around the coffee mug though it was too hot. The ceramic pushed heat into the pads of his fingers until it hurt.

"They've been at it all week?" he asked.

"Since Monday, on and off. Customers come in and Mort stops to help them like it's a regular day. Guy came in for weather stripping twenty minutes ago and Mort walked him over there, explained the whole thing, measured his damn window from memory with his fingers in the air. Linda's boxing up drill bits behind him like the place isn't getting knocked down in less than two weeks."

She set his sandwich down and looked at him properly for the first time.

"You ever notice how people keep doing the small parts?" she said. "That gets me. Not the big stuff. The small stuff. The creamers refilled. The floor mopped. Him helping some idiot with weather stripping."

Nate looked back out at the store. Mort had disappeared into the back. Linda was in the front now, peeling yellowed sale stickers off a shelf edge one by one and dropping them into a little pile in her palm. An act with no future in it at all.

"I hate that they picked right before Christmas," Gail said. "I know it's business, I know how these bastards talk, end of year, tax this, calendar that. Still. Shitty week to do it."

She reached under the counter and came up with a folded newspaper section and a pen. A crossword half-finished in blue ink. She frowned at one clue, wrote two letters, crossed one out hard enough to tear the paper, then folded it shut again.

"Mort keeps saying they'll sell what they can out of the garage," she said. "Like that's the same thing. Like you can take sixteen years and move it into a garage."

Nate took a bite of the sandwich because not eating would have made her look at him again. Turkey, too much mayo, white bread already damp in the middle. He chewed and swallowed and tasted almost nothing.

"You all right, hon?" Gail asked.

He nodded because speaking felt dangerous in a way it had not ten seconds earlier.

She let him have the nod. That was another thing about her. She was nosy, but not infinitely. There was a point where she stopped pushing because she had other people to feed and only so many hours in a shift.

"Anyway," she said. "If you want pie later, order it now. I got one coconut cream left and some bastard at table three is pretending he doesn't."

Nate made himself look at the menu, at the pie list written in black marker on a laminated card that had a crack through the word blueberry. Outside, Linda carried two smaller boxes to the front and set them by the door with her knees because her hands were full. Then Mort came back, took one look at how she had stacked them, and bent to rearrange them so the heavier one sat on bottom.

He did it automatically. Not sentiment. Not symbol. A man who knew how weight behaved.

That was the turn of the knife, maybe. Not the box itself. The correction.

Nate paid cash and left half the sandwich on the plate.

When he stepped outside the cold hit the wet place where the coffee had splashed on his hand, and for a second the air felt sharp enough to cut there. He stood by his car with the keys in his fist and looked once more toward the hardware store. Mort had opened the door to drag one of the boxes inside the threshold. Linda held it steady with her foot.

Neither of them looked up.

* * *

That night Sarah had papers spread across the coffee table in three slanting stacks, each stack squared carefully against the edge before she moved on to the next. She graded with a green mechanical pencil when she was tired of red, a tiny act of rebellion against the whole profession, and every few minutes she tapped the eraser twice against her lower lip while she read.

Nate stood in the kitchen with the refrigerator door open longer than he needed to and looked at leftovers he was not going to eat.

"There's lentil soup," Sarah said from the couch.

"Okay."

"You don't have to say okay like that."

He shut the fridge. "I meant okay."

She glanced up at him, not suspicious now exactly, just practiced. Living with him had taught her to assess tone the way some people checked weather.

"Fine," she said. "Then have soup or don't. I'm done refereeing."

It was not a fight. That was part of the problem. They had settled into this narrow, almost peaceful lane after the bigger blowup in October and Tommy's appearance at the door a few weeks ago. The lane was worse in some ways because it could pass for ordinary if you did not look directly at it. Sarah told him practical things. He responded. They slept in the same bed again more often than not. Some nights her foot touched his calf under the blanket and some nights it didn't. He had started measuring the state of the relationship by unintentional contact, which was pathetic enough that he did not let himself say it in full even inside his own head.

He heated the soup, carried it to the small table by the window, and did not eat much of it. Sarah turned pages. The radiator clanked. A car alarm went off down on the street, yelped three times, then stopped.

On the counter by the phone sat Dominic's wallet, where he'd dropped it that morning. Nate brought it to the table and opened it while Sarah was still reading. License. Two credit cards, the plastic softened at the corners from actual use. Insurance card. Gas receipt. ATM slip. He did not need to read the balance again. He knew it already from the day Tommy had stood in the kitchen bleeding lightly into a dish towel and saying four thousand like the number might turn smaller if he kept repeating it. Zero in checking. Zero in savings. Salary as a live wire he could not afford to let anybody cut.

If he refused to sign, Victor would not argue for long. Nate understood that now too. Victor had a way of making punishment feel administrative. He would call it a lapse in judgment or a temporary leave or maybe just a poor fit. He would be sorry it had come to this. He would tell Nate to take a week, think about what he wanted, maybe circle back in January if there was a role. Then the local phones would ring once or twice and stop ringing. Dominic Altieri, who had pushed back on a major project and then balked at closeout, would become complicated in exactly the way employers hated.

And then what.

Then rent on an apartment Nate had no legal or moral claim to. Car payment. Gas. Groceries. Tommy maybe showing up again because Tommy always did. Sarah asking what had happened, why he had suddenly blown up his career over a strip mall she knew he had no reason, supposedly, to care about. There was no explanation that preserved anything. Refusal was not a noble exit. It was just another collapse, and not even a useful one. The building would still come down. Somebody else's pen would do the job.

He put the cards back into the wallet and looked over at Sarah. She had taken off her glasses and was pinching the bridge of her nose with her eyes closed.

"You done?" he asked.

"In a minute."

"You want tea?"

She opened one eye. "Are you actually making tea or are you doing that thing where you ask and then disappear into your own head halfway through boiling the water?"

"I can make tea."

"Then sure."

He filled the kettle. While the burner ticked alive, she said, "Tommy called earlier."

He turned.

"I didn't answer," she said. "He didn't leave a message."

"Okay."

"That's all."

She put her glasses back on. The conversation ended there because she ended it there. Sarah's evasion, when she chose it, was always practical. The tea whistled. He made two mugs and brought hers over. She said thanks without looking up from the last paper.

He took his mug into the bedroom and sat on the edge of the bed in the half-dark. Dominic's side of the closet door didn't shut right unless you lifted it a little. Behind it, pushed against the wall, was the level he had bought from Mort in November. He took it down and laid it across his knees.

The bubble floated in its little glass tube, searching for center while his hands moved. He remembered, all at once and without permission, being eight and kneeling on a plywood subfloor in socks while Mort explained what level actually meant. Not straight. Not good. Not even. True, Mort had said, and then laughed because Nate had made a face at the word. True enough for a shelf, anyway.

He set the level aside so hard the wood knocked against the bedframe.

There were only three directions the thing went in, and every time he ran them he ended up in the same place.

He could refuse.

He could sign.

He could warn Mort.

The third one was the easiest to pretend at because it had the best moral branding. Warn your father. Sounds good. Sounds active. Sounds like the kind of thing a person in Nate's position ought to do if he wanted to go on believing there was still a difference between him and the men he hated.

Saturday afternoon he drove to Eastbridge Plaza to test it and parked three rows back from the hardware store in a space crusted with salt. He left the engine running because the cold came in through the Civic's doors no matter what the heater did. The lot was half empty. Gail's diner had a handwritten sign in the window advertising gift certificates nobody was going to buy in enough numbers to matter.

Through Lazar's front glass he could see Mort and Linda moving through the store in winter coats now, the heat apparently turned low to save money or because the place already felt like not-theirs. Mort was at the wall of fasteners with an apron over his coat, taking down the little metal drawers one at a time. He'd slide a drawer out, check the label, put it into a box lined with newspaper, then pause to move something else so the weight sat right. Linda wrote on the side of each box in thick block letters. HINGES. COPPER FITTINGS. ASSORTED SCREWS, MACHINE. She used the full words, not abbreviations. She had always been the one who wanted the label to say exactly what the thing was.

Once, Mort stopped and held up a packet of finish nails to show her something. Even at a distance Nate could tell from the shape of the exchange that it was not about the nails. It was about whether they were worth boxing, worth pricing again, worth carrying into whatever came after this. Linda answered without taking the packet. Mort looked at it a second longer, then set it in the box anyway.

Warn them about what.

That the date was real? They were wearing coats indoors and packing the shelves into produce cartons. That knowledge was already in their wrists, in Linda's marker hand, in the way Mort bent to protect the bottom of a box before he protected his own back.

That Victor would not move? Mort already knew the answer was no. He did not know Victor's name in the intimate, professional way Nate knew it now, but he knew no when it came from a letterhead and the city and a contractor and a date. Working people knew when a thing had gone out of reach. They had to. Rich people got to confuse delay with possibility because delay cost them less.

Nate sat in the car until the windshield filmed over again from his own breath. At one point a customer came out of the diner carrying a bag and looked straight at him, then away, because a man sitting in a running car in winter is not that unusual in a strip mall parking lot. That was how things hid. You could tuck almost anything inside the kinds of misery people already recognized.

He did not go into the store.

He drove to the office the next day because the apartment made the options feel too domestic, too mixed up with Sarah's tea mug in the sink and the smell of her shampoo on the pillow and the level in the closet. The warehouse was almost empty on Sunday. Only the front office lights were on, left that way by somebody who expected to come back in an hour and never did. The place smelled like toner and cold dust.

He sat at Dominic's desk, woke the computer, and opened Outlook.

The Sent Items folder was still there, still mundane. Client updates, contractor follow-ups, lunch plans, one note to Sarah about picking up dry cleaning, and underneath them the chain from spring. Dominic arguing as far as he could. Victor closing the door as often as necessary. Morton Lazar named plainly in one of Dominic's messages, not as a symbol, not as some future wound in Nate's life, just as a tenant worth trying to preserve.

Nate read the last line again.

I can take care of the notifications.

The first time he had seen it, in October, it had hollowed him out. This time it did something slower and worse. It sat there in normal office font, no flourish, no coded plea for absolution, no secret message from the dead man whose life Nate had taken over. Dominic had not been noble in the line. He had not even been especially eloquent. He had lost, and then he had answered like an employee.

Nate had spent nineteen years building Dominic into the kind of man who enjoyed sending letters like that. Then five months dismantling the lie, piece by piece, until all that remained was a quieter and more dangerous truth: Dominic had known it was wrong and had still done it because wrong was attached to his paycheck and his boss and his family and the apartment key in his pocket and the version of himself other people relied on. Nate had always treated that as cowardice from a safe distance. It had been very easy. Distance made every moral problem feel binary.

Up close it was a nest of obligations and appetites and ordinary fear.

He saw them then not as abstractions but as inventory.

Sarah in the other room saying there's soup if you want it and meaning several other things under that.

Tommy at the kitchen table with his lip split, promising on his life in a voice that already knew his life was not good collateral.

Renata stuffing foil trays into Nate's hands on Sundays because feeding people was how she bullied the world into staying alive.

Victor's dry patience and the narrowness of Bridgeport business and the fact that Dominic's whole adult life, the entire borrowed scaffolding Nate now stood inside, had been built in the shadow of men like him.

Mort and Linda lifting boxes because the date on paper had entered their bodies weeks ago.

And underneath all of it, Nate himself, smaller than he wanted to be. Not a martyr. Not a man prepared to throw away salary and shelter and access for a refusal that would change nothing. He could dress it up as strategy if he wanted. Keep the job. Stay close. Maybe help after. Maybe do something for Mort in January, February, March, when the real danger would come. Some of that was even true. But the ugliest part was true too. He wanted to keep living in the apartment. He wanted hot water. He wanted not to become unemployed in a decade where he had no other life to fall back into. He wanted Sarah to keep asking whether he wanted tea, even in that wary, exhausted voice.

He wanted the life he had stolen to go on feeding him.

That was the understanding he had been avoiding, maybe because it implicated him more deeply than the murder had. The murder had been one catastrophic act committed by a version of himself he could still, on bad days, talk about as if it had belonged to rage or confusion or the machine. This was simpler. This was a desk, a file, and the choice to continue being the man on the paperwork because the alternative was too expensive.

By Monday morning the packet was on his chair when he came in, updated and ready. Somebody from admin had clipped the city photos to the back and added a sticky note in looping handwriting: `Need your sig + Vic's. Thanks!`

Ray was already at his desk, eating peanuts from the vending machine cup by cup like he was making them last on principle.

"You in yesterday?" Ray asked without looking over.

"For a little."

"Jesus. You need a hobby."

"I had one. It sucked."

Ray laughed through his nose. "Fair."

He rolled his chair back far enough to glance over the partition. There were dark half-moons under his eyes, and he needed a haircut.

"Vic wants Eastbridge before lunch," he said. "Then supposedly we're all human beings for a week."

"Supposedly."

"You doing anything?"

Nate looked at the packet on his desk.

"Family stuff," he said.

"Yeah. Same."

Ray disappeared back behind the partition. A phone rang somewhere. Someone in the front office was talking too loudly about a ham. The copy machine started up again. Nothing in the room acknowledged what the folder on Nate's desk contained. It was one project among others. One more thing to clear before Christmas.

He opened it.

The site photos were first. Faded storefronts, salt-streaked curb, chain-link sections stacked by the south edge waiting to be installed. The hardware store sign in one photo, still up. Gail's front windows in another, a neon COFFEE sign glowing red even in daylight. Utility disconnect notice. Contractor release. Closeout summary.

At the end, the signature page.

There was a line for Victor and a line for Dominic. Dominic first.

Nate took the pen from his mug. Cheap blue office pen, chewed cap, one side of the clip cracked. He set the tip on the line and waited for some external interruption, some mercy so stupid and procedural it would let him postpone the thing without choosing. Fire alarm. Power outage. Victor called into a meeting. The pen ran dry. Nothing.

He signed.

Not slowly. That was another ugliness. Dominic's signature had become easier over the months, the hand learning what the hand already knew. The D came down in a hard vertical stroke, then out, then the fast compressed middle letters, then the last name with the high crossing slash through the t. There it was. Dominic Altieri, clean and practiced. The body did not hesitate.

He dated it, capped the pen, and sat there looking at the blue ink sink into the paper fibers.

That was all.

No wave of nausea. No courtroom in his head. No cinematic sense of a door closing forever. The heat ticked in the ducts. Ray sneezed twice. Somebody laughed down the hall. Nate turned the page, checked the tabs one more time because the packet needed to be complete, clipped it together, and carried it to Victor's inbox outside the office door.

On Victor's credenza sat a plate with three clementine peels curled on it like shavings. Nate put the folder in the tray marked OUT and stood there a second too long.

He had been in Dominic's body since July. He had been sleeping in Dominic's bed, taking Dominic's paychecks, answering to Dominic's name. None of that had done it as completely as this did. This was the point of contact. This was where the resemblance stopped being costume and became choice.

Given the same walls, the same money, the same boss, the same people to feed and protect and lie to, he had arrived at the same line and done the same thing.

He went back to his desk and opened a different file because there was still an hour before lunch and the day was still going.
