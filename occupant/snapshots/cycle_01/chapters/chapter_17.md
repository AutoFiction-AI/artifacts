# Chapter 17

Nate got to Eastbridge Plaza a little before seven, early enough that the police cruiser had its light bar off and the orange barrels looked provisional, as if somebody might still come collect them and apologize for the misunderstanding. The strip mall sat behind a fresh run of chain-link panels and plastic mesh, all of it silver and fluorescent against the gray morning. The windows had been emptied. Paper darkened the insides. A loose strand of Christmas tinsel skated along the curb in the wind and snagged on one of the sawhorses.

No one from Kassos had told him to be there. There was nothing for him to sign, nothing for him to inspect. Demolition belonged to other men now, men in insulated coveralls and hard hats with coffee cooling in their trucks. His part had been two weeks earlier in Victor's conference room with a legal pad under his hand. The pen had moved. That was all. He had kept his job. The rest of it only needed machines.

He parked across the street because there was nowhere else he could stand. Not with the crew, not with the people who'd lost the place. The Honda's heater took too long to matter. He shut it off after a minute because the fan made the windshield smell like hot dust and old pennies, and he didn't want one more machine in his ears.

The stores looked smaller with the signs still up and the insides gone. Gail's Diner had its red script over the awning, but the windows below it were blanked out with butcher paper. The barber pole outside Al's shop had been unplugged and stood there dead and striped. Lazar's Hardware still had the hand-painted sign on the end unit, the L a little washed out, exactly the way it had looked the first day Nate drove here with the road atlas open on the passenger seat and the impossible fact of his father alive on the other side of the glass. Back then he had sat in the car for forty minutes and told himself there was time. Five months. Then four. Then weeks. Then paperwork. Then a date circled on calendars that did not care what he wanted.

A flatbed rolled up first with a compact excavator chained down on the back, then a larger one behind it, the kind with a hydraulic thumb bolted to the arm so it could grab and peel instead of only smash. A water truck idled at the curb. One of the workers hopped down with a thermos in one hand and a clipboard in the other, his knit cap pulled so low it bent his ears. He looked about twenty and had a blue ink stain across the side of his wrist like he'd lost an argument with a pen that morning. He talked to the cop, laughed at something, then went back to unhooking chains.

Nate stayed in the car until the windows started fogging. Then he got out and stood with his hands in his coat pockets on the opposite sidewalk, his breath showing. Cold came off the pavement even harder than it came out of the sky. It had rained two days earlier and frozen at the edges of the lot. Mud in the potholes wore a skin of white. Every sound carried: diesel idle, chain drag, a metal ramp clanging down onto asphalt, someone coughing deep enough to sound structural.

He had not told Sarah where he was going. He had said he needed to stop by the office, which was true in the way most of his life was true now: not false enough to argue with, not true enough to survive contact. She was still in bed when he left, one foot out from under the blanket, hair over her face, winter break from school turning her alarm irrelevant for the week. He had stood in the doorway longer than he should have, looking at her and thinking there were whole categories of damage that did not announce themselves at the moment they were done. Some of them asked what time he'd be back. Some of them mumbled okay and rolled over.

The first of the former tenants arrived at seven-twenty, not in a cluster but singly, the way people showed up for wakes if they were not sure how much grief they were entitled to. Mrs. Park came from the side street in a navy wool coat with a strip of dry-cleaning tape still stuck near the hem, the white paper wrinkled but never peeled off. She carried her handbag in both hands like she was afraid somebody might make her check it. Al came next in a tan overcoat shiny at the cuffs, his barbershop keys still hanging on the shoelace he wore around his neck because he'd once lost them in a snowbank and never trusted pockets again. Cesar crossed from the bus stop with his Mets cap pulled low and a paper cup of the Dominican coffee he stocked for his wife, the dark roast smell reaching the street before he did.

They gathered by the barricade without touching it. Nobody said good morning. Gail arrived last, moving fast the way she always moved, even in the cold, even with nowhere to get to. She had her hair pinned up crooked with two metal combs and had come in the same white diner shoes she wore behind the counter, the backs mashed flat from stepping into them without using her hands. She carried a stainless steel coffee carafe under one arm and the pink order pad from the diner shoved into her coat pocket, the cardboard corner bent soft from years of handling. For a second Nate thought she'd forgotten she did not need it anymore. Then he understood she had brought it because she did not know what else to bring to the death of a place where she'd spent half her waking life.

They stood together and watched men they didn't know set ramps and back machines into position. A city truck went past, slowed, and kept going. The cop moved one barrel six inches to the left with the care of a man performing his own small portion of the ritual.

"You'd think they'd wait till after New Year's," Cesar said.

Al shrugged into his collar. "Why."

"Because people got Christmas," Cesar said. "Kids. Stuff."

Gail unscrewed the carafe and poured coffee into the cap. Steam came up between her face and the dark storefronts. "You think that matters to those assholes."

"Not to them," Cesar said. "I mean normal people."

Mrs. Park said, "My son said maybe weather delay."

"Your son optimistic again?" Gail asked.

"He got that from his father."

Nobody laughed. Mrs. Park didn't look like she'd expected them to.

Nate knew all their voices now. Not intimately. Not the way he knew Sarah's or the way, against his will, he'd learned the shapes Renata and Tommy made in a room. But enough. Enough to know that Gail's voice lost some of its speed when she was tired, enough to know that Al answered most questions as if he had already been arguing with them alone in his shop for ten minutes, enough to know that Mrs. Park always mentioned her son when she was trying to make a situation more manageable by putting another person in it. He had eaten turkey clubs at Gail's counter and listened to these people complain about delivery schedules, about parking, about the nail salon's busted hot-water heater, about the way teenagers bought one soda from Cesar and loitered in front of everybody else's windows. He had built his attachment the stupidest possible way, through lunch.

A pickup with the demolition company's name on the door pulled in beside the excavator. Nate recognized the last name. Not the brother-in-law's first name, which Victor had mentioned once in that flat, explanatory tone he used when he wanted Nate to understand that corruption had already hardened into weather, but the family name. Dreher. It was stenciled in blue block letters on white metal: `DREHER CONTRACTING`. Under it, smaller: `SITE PREP / DEMO / REMOVAL`.

There it was. Not hidden, not whispered, not slid under a table. Just painted on a truck parked in front of his father's store.

Gail looked up and saw him across the street.

It took him half a second to realize she was looking at him and not through him. Her face changed in the smallest possible way, not surprise exactly, more a recalculation of the morning's geometry. She said something to Al, handed him the coffee carafe, and crossed at the gap between the barrels.

Nate's first thought was not what she might ask. It was what his face looked like to her right now. Dominic's nose going red in the cold. Dominic's mouth. Dominic's body standing on the wrong side of the street while men unpacked the machinery that would take the place apart. It had been one thing to shake Mort's hand wearing this face, to sit at Gail's counter and be nobody in particular. It was another to bring Dominic's body to the demolition as witness. That changed the face. Maybe only for him. Maybe not.

Gail stopped a few feet away. Up close he could see she'd put lipstick on out of habit and then worried it off her lower lip.

"You hear anything?" she asked.

He shook his head.

"Nothing at all."

"Nothing useful," he said. His voice came out flat from the cold and from how carefully he was keeping it level. "I'm sorry."

She looked past him to his car, then to the machinery, then back at him. "You with them today?"

"No."

"Office?"

"No. I just came."

Gail absorbed that. She had a face built for impatience, quick and readable, but right now it went still in a way he'd only seen once before, when a kid in the diner dropped a glass and cut his hand badly and she turned from waitress to triage nurse without showing anybody the jump in her pulse.

"Okay," she said.

He waited for her to ask the next question, the one that would make him either lie outright or tell a truth useless enough to count as lying anyway. She did not ask it. She tucked both hands into her coat pockets, glanced at the strip mall again, and said, "Mort and Linda are on their way."

The sentence landed in him like a board dropped flat.

"Yeah," he said.

She nodded once. "All right."

Then she went back across the street, took the coffee carafe from Al, and poured for everybody else before she poured for herself.

By eight, there were maybe a dozen people if you counted the ones who'd come only to stand at the edge for a minute and then drifted farther down the sidewalk. A woman from the nail salon Nate only knew as Tran had shown up with a cigarette she never lit and a puffy silver coat that made her look, from a distance, almost festive. Two guys Nate had seen in Gail's diner but never spoken to stood with their hands in their pockets and kept talking about the Giants game as if persistence alone could make it possible not to be here. The conversation moved in the shallow grooves of real speech, obvious things said because silence was worse.

"They start at eight?" one of them asked.

"Looks like."

"Thought it'd be nine."

"Why would it be nine."

"I don't know. Feels like a nine thing."

The cop checked his watch. One of the workers climbed into the excavator cab. The engine turned over thickly, coughed, caught. Its first full growl rolled through the cold air and into Nate's chest with a pressure he felt in Dominic's sternum, not his own. Dominic's body knew construction noise. It met the sound without the flinch Nate expected from himself, and that unnerved him more than the sound did.

Linda's car came five minutes later.

Nate recognized it before he saw them because Mort still drove as if every trip were partly a delivery run, slow into turns, careful over potholes, braking early. The car eased against the curb on the far side of the crowd. Linda got out first and came around to the passenger side before Mort had even opened his door. He did not need help walking. That was not what her hand on his arm was for.

He had worn the good coat.

It was dark wool, brushed clean and pulled out for church and funerals and weddings and apparently for this, though this was none of those and all of them. The collar sat awkwardly against his neck the way clothing did when a man spent most of his life in canvas and denim and only put on wool when the day had already gone bad. Nate could see the reading glasses on the chain tucked under the lapel, the outline of them pressing against the fabric. There was probably still a carpenter's pencil in one pocket. Mort forgot things in coats for months at a time. Linda once found a spark plug in the freezer and nobody could explain how it had gotten there.

Linda kept one hand through Mort's elbow as they crossed the sidewalk toward the others. She was as small and brisk as Nate remembered, her short hair flattened under a knitted hat, her store keys looped around the blue plastic coil bracelet she wore when she had too much to carry and no free pocket. The keys hung against her glove with every step. She was crying already, not hard, not dramatically, just steadily enough that she kept taking sharp breaths through her nose in the cold and wiping under her eyes with the back of her wrist like she was annoyed at her own face for doing this in public.

The crowd made space for them. Gail put a hand on Linda's shoulder. Al touched Mort's upper arm and then took his hand away right after, because there was not really anything to do with a man's arm under those circumstances.

From across the street Nate could not hear the first few words. He saw them, though. Linda's mouth pinched and moved quickly. Gail said something short back. Mort nodded once to whoever was speaking and took his place at the barricade.

He stood with his hands in his coat pockets and his jaw set so hard it changed the lower half of his face.

That was the posture. Nate knew it before he could name it. It was the stance Mort used when the furnace went out on the coldest day of the year and there was no money to replace it. When a supplier had shorted an order and then insisted they hadn't. When fourteen-year-old Nate knocked over an entire shelving unit in the garage trying to impress nobody. Mort got quieter, put his hands away, and kept the rest of himself locked down so the practical part could finish the job. If his hands were free, he would use them. He would sort. Measure. Pick something up and fix it. Here there was nothing to pick up yet and nothing to fix, so he kept them buried.

Nate stepped farther back into the shadow of the bare sycamore at the curb, as if that would help. Mort was not looking at him. Mort was looking at the storefronts with the concentration of a man trying to remember a room he built himself before somebody else rearranged it beyond repair.

The foreman, a thick-necked guy in an orange jacket with reflective strips gone grimy at the edges, walked the line with the cop and pointed at the fence, the hydrant, the overhead wires. He talked with one gloved finger, not dramatic, just competent. Men nodded. The water truck pulled closer. A hose came off the side and uncoiled over the sidewalk, laying black and rubbery through the slush.

Nobody in the crowd raised a voice. Nate had expected, if not a fight, then at least some sharp phrase thrown toward the machinery, something human and inadequate hitting metal. Instead everybody watched the way people watched surgeons through glass when there was nothing to be done but see whether the body opened on the table was one you loved.

A worker cut the power line to the barber pole and dropped it into the cab of a pickup. Another peeled away caution tape from the side door of the diner and wound it around his arm because it would tangle in the tracks otherwise. They were not cruel. That made it worse. Cruelty would at least have been a choice. This was efficiency. The men were doing a day's work in good faith, and the day's work was to erase the physical record of other people's days.

The excavator went into the nail salon first.

Its bucket punched through glass with a crack too sharp for how heavy the machine was. Then the hydraulic thumb closed, gripping aluminum frame and mullion together, and the whole front of the unit came away in a shriek of metal. The water truck sprayed a fan of mist that turned immediately to cold grit on the asphalt. Pink insulation showed, then a waiting-room chair tipped sideways and disappeared under falling block. Someone near Gail said, "Jesus," softly, as if in church, and the machine kept moving.

Nate had seen demolition before in clips online, sped up, narrated by men who loved heavy equipment and talked about reach and torque and cycle times. This had none of that stupid distance. Every bite took a room with it. A counter. A sink. A mirror bolted to a wall. Fluorescent tubes burst in brief white flashes under the falling ceiling. The sound was not one sound but a sequence: impact, metal complaint, the patter of smaller debris, the hiss of water, the diesel rev as the arm lifted and reset.

Tran from the nail salon smoked her cigarette down to the filter without seeming to notice she'd finally lit it. Mrs. Park crossed her arms tighter under her coat. Cesar kept taking the lid off his coffee and putting it back on as if maybe it had become the wrong drink while he wasn't looking.

The barber shop went next. Al watched the old chrome chair roll halfway out of the blown-open front and topple sideways into plaster dust. "Son of a bitch," he said, but with no real force behind it. More surprised than angry, as if he'd expected the chair to know better after all those years.

He took the keys off his neck and held them in his fist for a while. Nate noticed that, noticed everything, the close-third curse of being trapped inside his own witness. A lesser day might have blurred. This one indexed itself. The dead barber pole against the pickup tire. Mrs. Park's dry-cleaning tape fluttering at her hem. Gail's order pad working loose from her pocket every time she shifted her weight and her pushing it back in without looking.

Mort did not move except to plant his feet a little wider when the excavator hit the block between units and the vibration carried through the ground.

Linda said something to him. Nate couldn't hear it. Mort answered without turning his head.

Then the machine took the front of the diner.

The awning came away first, bent down the middle like cardboard, and the red script sign held for one extra second after the bolts underneath had already failed. It dangled stupidly, one side lower than the other, `GAIL'S` still readable and `DINER` tipped toward the pavement. Nate had seen that sign from the parking lot, from the diner stool, from Mort's side of the counter when they drank bad coffee and talked about nothing. He had seen it in summer heat and October rain and under the yellow parking-lot lights that made the whole plaza look flatter after dark. Now one remaining fastener snapped and the sign came down in a flat metal slap against the curb, bounced once, and lay there faceup in the spray.

Gail made a sound Nate would not have heard if he hadn't already been listening for every small break in every human face on the block. Not a cry. Not a curse. More like the involuntary noise somebody made when cold water hit a bad tooth.

She handed the carafe to nobody. It dropped onto the curb and rolled once without opening. Then she turned and walked back toward her car.

Al said, "Gail."

She shook her head without looking at him.

"Nope," she said. "I'm done."

And kept walking.

No one tried to stop her. Why would they. The sign had fallen. That had been her line.

The carafe stayed on the curb, a little puff of steam still escaping where the cap hadn't sealed right.

The diner collapsed inward faster than the first two units. Booth vinyl flashed red in the dust and disappeared. Nate saw a section of counter he knew by touch, the spot where the laminate had bubbled from an old coffee-burn and Gail always set the pie plate two inches to the left of it. It broke into three pieces under the excavator track. He had the nauseating certainty that if the machine had paused and somebody had asked him which fragment had once held the sugar dispenser and which had held the napkin caddy, he could have answered.

Linda started crying harder when the diner went. Not because it was Lazar's Hardware. Because it was next. Because this was how close next was.

Mort took one hand out of his pocket long enough to touch the inside of her elbow. The movement was small and automatic, not showy comfort, just contact. Then his hand went back into the coat.

"It's all right," he said, and did not sound like he believed it in any direction.

The excavator repositioned. Workers dragged twisted awning frame out of the way with chains. A section of cinderblock wall leaned outward, hesitated, then came down into itself in a booming collapse that kicked dust across the street hard enough to salt the hood of Nate's car.

Now the end unit.

Lazar's Hardware sat larger than the others and deeper. It took longer to open. The machine worried at the front corner first, cracking block and peeling back metal frame. When the face of the store split, Nate could see farther in than he ever had as a customer or as a child. The sight was obscene in a specifically architectural way. A building was not meant to show its cross-section to strangers. Pegboard. Stud bays. The fluorescent run over aisle three. A slice of back room with cardboard boxes stacked to shoulder height. Shelving tilted, held for a second by stubborn bolts, then came spilling forward.

Not spilling. Pouring.

Specialty fasteners came down in metal streams from busted bins, a granular silver rain into plaster dust. Bolts, washers, anchors, screws. Inventory with specific names becoming weight and noise. Nate saw the wall Mort loved most disappear by category. Machine screws. Carriage bolts. Toggle wings. The blue-and-white bin labels flashed and vanished. Somewhere in there were the left-hand threaded screws Mort had once explained to him with the patient pride of a man showing a kid that the world contained more particularity than he had guessed. Those were the parts other stores stopped bothering with. Mort kept them anyway, because the whole point of a place like Lazar's was that a man could walk in needing one impossible little thing and walk out with it in a paper bag.

Linda covered her mouth with both gloved hands. Her keys clacked against her bracelet.

Mort kept looking.

He did not blink much. He did not wipe his face. His jaw had gone beyond set into something structural, the way a beam held under more load than it had been rated for because no one had yet told it it was allowed to fail. Nate watched his father watch the machine tear open the place where sixteen years of rent, inventory, arguments, coffee, bookkeeping, deliveries, and ordinary Tuesdays had occurred, and there was nothing dramatic in Mort's face at all. That was the worst part. If he'd shouted, the morning would have acquired shape. If he'd cried, somebody could have stood beside him inside the recognizable weather of it. Instead he stood with his hands in his pockets and watched his life be translated into debris.

At some point Linda followed his gaze across the street and saw Nate.

Nate knew the moment because her face altered around the crying, not less grief but a new question laid over it. She leaned toward Mort and said something. Mort looked, finally, across the road.

Their eyes met.

For one blind second Nate thought Mort knew. Not the impossible part. Not time travel or overwritten consciousness or anything so grand and stupid. Just knew enough. Knew this face was somehow part of it. Knew the stranger called Dom had promised to look into things and had failed and had come anyway because failure did not cancel witness.

Mort gave him the smallest nod.

Not forgiveness. Not accusation. Not gratitude either, exactly. Only recognition. You're here. That's you there. I see you.

Nate could not nod back. His neck had gone rigid. He stood under the sycamore and took the weight of that tiny public acknowledgement in Dominic Altieri's face while Dominic Altieri's signature rode in a file drawer across town and in the tendons of the hand inside his pocket.

The hardware sign came down almost gently compared to everything else.

The machine got under the awning, lifted, and the old wood sign tore from two brackets at once. It dropped face-first into the debris with a muffled crack rather than a crash, swallowed by dust before Nate saw where it landed. Linda made a broken sound and stepped in closer to Mort. He didn't put an arm around her. He stood where he was, hands still pocketed, as if if he moved one inch now he would never stop moving.

"Those bins'll be everywhere," he said after a while, so quietly Nate almost lost it under the engine noise.

That was what he had to say. Not this is my store. Not sixteen years. Not bastards. The bins. The inventory. The practical world surviving one last second in language even while it was being ground down.

The machine worked the remaining walls from the corners, methodical and unhurried. Roof trusses folded. Ductwork gleamed suddenly in the open air. A bent rack of shovels slid out of the rubble and vanished under a track. Nate saw the register counter tilt and go. He saw the back-room door, the one Linda used with her shoulder when her arms were full, break in half at the hinge side. He saw a carton marked `PAINT THINNER` burst and darken a patch of dust into mud. He saw, absurdly intact, a single broom fall bristle-first and stand that way for a second before the next section of roof crushed it flat.

He knew this day already. Not in detail, maybe not even in memory. More the way a body knew an old break when weather moved in. This was the hinge the rest of his life had swung on. After this came the garage. After this came months of his father moving slower and talking less and standing in rooms as if waiting for instructions no one had given him. After this came the version of Nate who told the story with one name missing from it and built a whole architecture of blame in the gap. He had come back to the hinge and found the same door.

There had been a part of him, stupid and persistent as mold, that believed proximity itself might matter. That if he got close enough to the machinery of the past he might find the loose bolt, the hidden menu, the one patch note history had somehow missed. He had killed a man over that belief. He stood now on the sidewalk in the dead man's coat with his dead man's face and watched the store come down on schedule, and the belief finally gave up. Not in fire. Not with revelation. It simply had nothing left to stand on.

By the time the excavator was pushing the last broken block into rough piles, half the crowd had gone. Tran tossed her cigarette into the gutter and walked to the bus stop without looking back. Mrs. Park stood through the flattening of what had been her dry cleaner and then left with both arms folded so tight she looked self-contained, almost formal. Cesar stayed longer, until a worker loaded twisted refrigeration pieces onto a truck from what had been his bodega, then said, "All right," to no one Nate could see and headed down the block with his empty coffee cup still in hand.

Al lingered with Mort and Linda. The three of them looked at the lot the way people looked at a stripped room after the movers had gone, except this stripping had teeth marks and dust suppression and a police detail.

Eventually Linda touched Mort's sleeve and he nodded. He turned from the barricade with the slowness of a man making his body obey something his mind had not signed off on. Before he got into the car he looked once more across the street.

Nate was still there.

Mort did not nod again. He only looked, then lowered himself into the passenger seat. Linda drove. Their car eased away so carefully it seemed impossible that the street under it was the same one where excavators had spent the morning eating concrete.

Nate stayed through the cleanup.

That was a different sound. Smaller metal against larger metal. Backup beepers. Chains cinching. Workers talking about lunch in ordinary voices while they raked what remained of other people's square footage into manageable heaps. The cop left first. Then the water truck. The orange barrels stayed. One of the men smoked beside the flattened sign from the diner and checked his watch every twenty seconds like he was late for daycare pickup.

By noon the lot did not look freshly destroyed so much as anciently ruined. That was the speed of it. A building went from familiar to wreck to archaeology in four hours.

The front fence had a loose panel near where the hardware store used to be. Nobody was watching that closely now. The workers were on the far end loading scrap. Nate crossed the street before he could think through whether he should.

Mud sucked at his shoes where water and gypsum dust had made a paste. The air inside the fence smelled wrong for December: wet concrete, broken insulation, electrical dust, the faint medicinal sweetness of spilled solvent. Pieces of the strip mall remained recognizable only if you had loved them or spent enough dull afternoons inside them to build a map. A section of diner menu board with `TURKEY CLUB` still readable under gray slurry. A barber-shop mirror cracked into a spiderweb that reflected nothing whole. Blue plastic bins from the hardware store split open along their sides, screws and anchors glittering in the muck like fish scales.

He went to where the end unit had been by the instinctive geometry of a person who had once been small enough for this place to determine the scale of his world. Here was where the counter should have been. Here the aisle with paint supplies. Here the wall of fasteners. He stepped over a twisted shelf upright and almost slipped on a scatter of washers.

That was when he saw the screw.

It lay half buried in gray paste beside a splintered piece of pegboard, no larger than the last joint of his thumb, dull with dust except where the threads caught light. He would have missed it if he had not been looking at the ground the way Mort had taught him to look at floors in old houses and work sites: not for beauty, not for evidence, just for the one thing that would bite you if you forgot it was there.

Nate crouched and picked it up.

Left-hand thread. You could tell as soon as you wiped it with your glove. The spiral ran the wrong way, stubborn and specific. Mort had shown him that once when he was a kid, laughing a little at how offended Nate had been by the idea that screws could be backwards on purpose. Some machinery used them so vibration wouldn't walk them loose. Most stores quit carrying them because almost nobody needed them until the exact day they did, and then they'd drive all over town and wind up at Lazar's because Mort had decided years earlier that being the place with the odd part was half the job.

Nate held the screw in his gloved palm and looked at the lot.

It was not much. Less than not much. A cheap piece of inventory that probably cost cents and had survived only because it was too small to interest a machine. But it had been in the store that morning. Mort had ordered it, unpacked it, shelved it, probably sold three from the bin and meant to reorder next month. Now it sat warm from Nate's hand, the threads catching lint from his glove.

He put it in his coat pocket.

Then he stood there a minute longer in the ruin of the place that had organized his life long after it stopped physically existing, and when one of the workers yelled, "Hey, sir, you can't be in there," he raised a hand without looking back and stepped out through the loose fence panel as if he'd only taken a wrong path by accident.

* * *

By one-thirty the lot was quiet enough to hear the wind move trash against the fence.

The machines were still there, but idling now, waiting for whatever came after this. Surveyors. More trucks. New stakes in the ground. The noise had dropped out of the block so suddenly Nate's ears kept trying to supply it, phantom clanks, phantom diesel. Without the strip mall in place the street had a bad haircut look to it, too much scalp showing. You could see farther than you were supposed to. Behind where the stores had been there was only scrub grass, frozen mud, a line of sagging back fences, and beyond that the rear walls of houses that had never been meant to front a commercial street.

He sat in the Honda with the screw in his pocket and both hands on the steering wheel until his knuckles hurt.

There was dust in the seams of his coat. Dust on the dashboard. Dust dry in the corner of one eye that he had to rub away with the heel of his hand. He looked in the rearview mirror once and saw Dominic looking back at him with a smear of gray at the temple and a face that seemed, for the first time since July, fully earned. Not deserved. Earned. This was the face that had signed. The face that had stood across the street and been nodded at by the man it had helped destroy.

He started the car.

Driving away made less sense than staying had. The road remained the road; lights changed; a mail truck blocked one lane near the pharmacy; a woman in earmuffs carried two shopping bags and waited for the crosswalk signal. But the city had shifted a fraction of an inch around the missing building. Nate felt it in the route more than he saw it. At the corner where he usually caught the first glimpse of the faded hardware sign if he glanced right, there was no sign now, only open fence and dirt. At the light by the gas station the whole block looked shallower, as if somebody had taken a chunk out of the model and forgotten to patch the camera angle.

He turned the radio on, then off again when a man cheerfully announced a sale on after-Christmas mattresses. He drove with the window cracked half an inch because the car smelled like wet gypsum and his own coat.

In his pocket, the screw knocked lightly against his keys every time he took a turn.

He tried, once, to think in the big moral terms that had organized him for so long. What had this made him. What had Dominic been. What did complicity mean when the alternative had been getting fired twelve days earlier by a man whose replacement would have signed the same form with less hesitation. The thoughts came up flat, useless, pre-chewed. He did not need philosophy. He had seen the sign come down. He had seen Mort's jaw. The morning had converted everything abstract into inventory.

At a red light he found himself looking at his right hand on the wheel.

Scar across the left knuckle. Broad fingers. Dominic's hands. He had been looking at them for months now, these hands that chopped onions in Sarah's kitchen more cleanly than his own ever had, these hands that knew how to knot a tie faster than he did, these hands that answered Tommy's frightened midnight knocking and had emptied both accounts because that was what Dominic's life required of whoever occupied it. The same hands had held the pen in Victor's office. The same hands now kept one absurd surviving screw in the coat pocket like a relic.

He put his thumb and forefinger into the pocket and rolled the screw between them at the next light. The left-hand thread ran against the grain of expectation. Tighten the wrong way. Loosen the wrong way. A design built around the fact that some pressures shook ordinary things apart.

He thought of Mort years ago at the counter, explaining that ordinary stores didn't waste shelf space on screws like that.

He pulled into the lot at the apartment building just after two. Sarah's Civic-sized parking space joke crossed his mind and died there. He sat another minute with the engine off. Somebody above him was running a vacuum. Somewhere down the block a dog barked in exactly the same rhythm it had barked the day before. The world had not widened to accommodate what had happened. It never did.

When he opened the apartment door, warmth hit him with the layered domestic smells that had become the atmosphere of the life he wore now: toasted sesame oil, rice, the faint detergent Sarah used on the dish towels, radiator heat with its dry-metal edge.

Sarah was in the kitchen with one sock on and one bare foot tucked up against the cabinet toe-kick, balancing against the counter while she shook leftover rice into a skillet. She had a pencil pushed through the knot of her hair, not because she was grading anything but because if she set a pencil down it migrated. A cardigan hung open over a Yale T-shirt she'd stolen from some free box in the teachers' lounge years ago because the cotton was good. The radio on the fridge was low enough to be only drums.

She glanced over her shoulder when he came in.

"You're back early."

"Yeah."

"I found the mushrooms before they turned," she said. "So that's dinner."

"Okay."

He hung his coat carefully instead of dropping it over the chair the way Dominic apparently used to, a habit he'd only learned because Sarah once rolled her eyes and said, not angrily, that coats had hooks for a reason. Dust came off the sleeve in a faint gray stripe onto his hand. He rubbed it away before she turned back.

"You want tea?" she asked.

"No. I'm good."

She stirred the rice, broke up a clump with the wooden spoon, and let the silence sit for a few seconds. It was not the old silence from the worst stretch of the fall, the one dense with her observation and his panic. This one was thinner, workable, the silence of two people who had not solved anything but had learned the outlines of each other's damage well enough to move around it in a kitchen.

"How was your day?" she asked.

He had the screw in his pocket. He put his hand around it through the fabric and felt the wrong-way threads press into his palm.

"Fine," he said.
