# Chapter 2

Nate waited until Sarah's breathing settled back into the deep, even pull of sleep before he moved. He did it in stages, like he was disarming something. First he slid one borrowed foot out from under the sheet. Then he got Dominic's weight up on one elbow. Then he lifted himself clear of the mattress and stood there in the dark with the air conditioner grinding in the window and Sarah a warm shape behind him, one arm curled under the pillow that still smelled like whatever shampoo she used. It had been the first thing he noticed after the blackout. It was still there now, maddeningly ordinary.

He had expected, if the thing worked at all, some version of arrival. A warehouse. An alley. Dominic in front of him. Maybe blood if he was lucky, maybe a speech if he was stupid enough to think he would get one. Instead he had gotten sheets, a sleeping woman, and a body that answered to a different set of weights.

The apartment was lit only by the digital clock on the cable box in the living room and the faint green numerals on the microwave. He had stared at both long enough from the edge of the bed to confirm they still said July 11, 2005. The date had not changed because he wanted it to. The year had not snapped back after some waiting period. He had murdered a man by deleting him and woken up in the middle of that man's life.

The bathroom door clicked louder than it should have. Nate froze with his hand on the knob and listened. The air conditioner kept grinding. A car passed outside. Sarah did not move.

He turned on the light.

The face in the mirror was Dominic Altieri's, but that sentence meant less in glass than it had on Nate's laptop for all those years. In the ALTIERI folder it had been a grainy headshot on a company page, flat enough to hate. Here it had pores, beard shadow, a small nick in the chin from shaving or something older, and a nose that bent a little to the left where somebody had broken it years ago and it had healed crooked. Dark hair stuck up in the back. His mouth was slightly open in the expression of a man who had gotten out of bed too fast and not yet caught up to it.

Nate stepped closer. Dominic stepped closer.

He put a hand to the glass and felt how broad that hand was, the square heel of the palm, the thick fingers, the scar slashing pale across the left knuckle. His own hands had always looked underfed to him, all tendon and knuckle, like the hands of a man who spent his life pointing at screens and promising he'd start going to the gym next week. These were no prettier. They looked built for contact with the world. A stupid thought, but there it was.

He pushed his hair back. The hairline held. He bared his teeth. Straight enough. No glasses. He reached automatically toward the bridge of his nose to adjust a pair that did not exist and touched skin instead. The motion hit him harder than the mirror had. His body had done it before he could stop it. Some old Nate reflex, left behind with a face that no longer existed anywhere.

He said, very softly, "Jesus Christ."

The voice came out lower than his, rough with sleep, a man's voice he had heard only in clips and imagined from photographs. It filled the tiled room and made his stomach go loose. He tried again.

"Nate."

The stranger in the mirror said it back.

He should not have done that. The name felt illegal in this apartment. He swallowed and tried the other one.

"Dom."

That was worse.

He stripped the borrowed T-shirt off and took inventory because inventory at least sounded like an action a living person took. Medium build, maybe a little softer through the stomach than the photos had suggested, shoulders wider than Nate's, chest darker with hair, thighs heavier. He turned sideways and saw a life he had stepped into without instructions. He looked at his cock because of course he did, because waking up with another man's face still left the rest of the paperwork to review. He almost laughed at the obscenity of it, the bureaucratic thoroughness of the universe. Congratulations on the murder. Here was the body package. Please inspect all included parts.

The laugh died before it got out. There was nothing funny in the fact that Sarah had put her hand on this body in the dark believing the man inside it was still there.

He pissed and missed the angle, a sharp stripe against porcelain before he corrected. Even that was wrong. He stood there bare-legged in a stranger's bathroom feeling stupid in a way that reached all the way to the bone. People talked about inhabiting someone else's life like it would be some deep, mystical thing. Mostly it was bad calibration. Wrong center of gravity. Wrong reach. Wrong way the stream hit the bowl. His own body knowledge had become legacy software with no compatible hardware.

The sink held two toothbrushes in a ceramic mug from some museum gift shop, both of them damp from the night before. Sarah's was blue. Dominic's was white with a fraying strip of green rubber on the handle. A black elastic sat around the faucet neck. A bottle of her shampoo stood beside his shaving cream, and the sweet clean smell of it lifted off the steamless room anyway because it was already in the towels, in the pillowcase, in the shirt he'd taken off. The apartment had the density of a life shared by two people who kept using the same corners of the world until the corners learned them.

He opened the medicine cabinet. Aspirin. Antacids. A travel-size packet of tissues. A small bottle of Visine. An old prescription bottle with Dominic's name on it from a dentist. There was no answer in there, no printed card that said YOU ARE HERE and nothing under it about how to get out.

The living room was one room away and a century off. He moved through it slowly, touching nothing at first. One-bedroom mid-rise, just like the outline said in his head, except outlines were cleaner than actual life. Actual life meant a pair of Sarah's sandals near the couch, one tipped on its side. A stack of magazines on the coffee table. A television thick enough to need its own posture. A silver DVR box with a green clock. A desktop computer in the corner on a cheap laminate desk, beige tower underneath, monitor with a back thick as a cinder block. Bills and junk mail leaned in a ceramic tray by the front door under a bowl with keys in it. The place smelled faintly of coffee grounds, fabric softener, and whatever the downstairs neighbor had smoked into the hall.

He went for the bowl because keys felt foundational. Car keys, apartment keys, two loyalty tags for places he did not know, and a brass saint medal on a split ring worn smooth at the edge. Dominic's wallet sat under them. Nate opened it with more care than he would have handled a gun.

Driver's license first. Dominic Altieri, the same face as the mirror but flattened and made official, the Connecticut seal ghosting the corner. Address matching the apartment. Height five-ten. Forty-three dollars in cash. Two credit cards. A health insurance card. A laminated supermarket membership card. No miracle instructions. No explanation of why Dominic, of all people, had been the endpoint his machine picked. He knew why, of course. He had supplied the obsession. He had fed the coordinates with so much hate that the machine had gone where the hate was looking. The universe, apparently, had terrible autocomplete.

He kept the license out and studied the photo until it started to blur. Thirty-one. Dominic had been thirty-one and alive a few hours ago. Nate had been thirty-four and miserable. Now thirty-one beat thirty-four by knockout because thirty-four had been vaporized or collapsed or otherwise converted into an engineering problem nobody could solve because the engineer in question was gone.

He put the wallet down and picked up the flip phone.

It felt insultingly small in his hand, not elegant and retro the way old tech looked in photos, just cramped. The outer screen showed the time and a battery icon. He flipped it open and was greeted by a menu that seemed to assume he had grown up apprenticed to it. Contacts. Messages. Recent Calls. Settings. Games. He hit the wrong button twice and ended up in something called Tools. He backed out, then went forward again by mistake. He was already getting angry at plastic.

Recent calls gave him a list of names and numbers, little salvageable pieces of Dominic's life. Sarah. Ma. Ray. Tommy. Home. A number with no name. Another with just Kassos on it. He read them like a burglar casing a house. Sarah's name had three calls yesterday. Ma had called twice. Ray at 5:12. The phone log had the intimacy of a bloodstream. People reached for Dominic all day and the device kept score.

He shut it and immediately patted his own thigh for the rectangle that should have been there. Nothing. Thin cotton pajama pants and empty pocket. He stood still with the phantom gesture hanging off him.

The absence of the smartphone humiliated him in practical ways. He already understood the year. What kept catching at him was the missing search box in his hand. No maps. No notes app. No weather. No camera. No instant way to ask the internet how to use a percolator or what Dominic Altieri had majored in or whether it was possible to reverse a consciousness transfer with household materials and desperation. Twenty years fell hardest in the places his hand wanted to go without asking him first.

He sat at the desktop and pressed the tower's power button. The machine woke with a fan whine and then the Windows XP startup sound bloomed into the room, absurdly lush, like a corporate choir announcing his imprisonment. He winced and looked down the hall toward the bedroom.

No movement.

The screen took its time. Blue welcome screen. Desktop icons. Internet Explorer, Outlook Express, My Documents, something labeled Quicken, a folder called Photos, the Recycle Bin. He clicked the blue e in a panic of habit and listened in disbelief as the modem began its insectile screaming through the phone jack. It sounded like two robots being tortured in a fax machine. Nate lunged for the speaker knob and turned it down too late because the noise had already happened and would either wake Sarah or not.

Nothing from the bedroom.

He disconnected anyway. There was no version of this where he quietly Googled his way out of murder before dawn. The page had barely loaded enough to show a white background and a sliver of MSN at the top. The internet existed, technically. So did sailing ships.

He opened My Documents instead. Bank statements. A resume. A folder of digital photos. Several Word documents named with things like `tenants_notes` and `meeting_july`. He hovered over all of it and then pulled his hand back. Every click felt expensive. If he opened too much now he would lose the morning to panic and be in the exact same place, only with more file names in his head. He needed basics first. He needed not to get caught staring at Dominic's desktop like a raccoon at a vending machine.

The kitchen waited with its own indictment: a stainless steel percolator on the counter, a can of ground coffee, sugar in a ceramic jar, filters under the sink, though why a percolator needed filters he had no idea. The machine looked less like an appliance than a polished prop from a movie where everybody smoked indoors and lied convincingly. Nate took the lid off, examined the basket stem thing, the chamber, the loose parts, and wished with a violence disproportionate to the moment for one stupid pod machine, one cheap modern sacrament, one button that said coffee and meant it.

He reached for the absent phone again, this time not for comfort but for instructions. Empty pocket. Right. Murder had rolled back the operating system too.

He filled the thing halfway with water, then doubted that immediately. Too much? Too little? He unscrewed the coffee can and got hit with the dark oily smell of grounds, the smell of adult mornings already underway before he had earned them. He spooned some into the basket, then dumped half back because it looked excessive, then added a little more because now it looked weak. The whole exercise had the confidence profile of a man wiring a bomb by vibes.

When he plugged the percolator in, nothing happened for three seconds. Then a thin metallic tremor started low in the base.

He leaned down as if listening would help.

The apartment gave him details instead of answers. A framed photo on the bookshelf of Dominic and Sarah at some shoreline place, wind in her hair, his arm around her shoulders. A stack of lesson plans clipped together with a binder clip on the dining table, Sarah's handwriting in the margins, the words SUMMER SCHOOL in block letters at the top of the first page. A dry cleaner receipt in Dominic's wallet. Utility bills. A box of elbow macaroni in the pantry. The white noise of the air conditioner fighting July. Each object carried the same message in a different format: Dominic had been here yesterday. Dominic had been expected here today. Dominic would have continued, if Nate had not used him as a target the way a bored god used a map pin.

He went back to the bathroom and looked at himself again because he kept hoping the second viewing might trigger some loophole. It did not. Dominic still had Dominic's face. Nate still did not.

He braced both hands on the sink and forced himself to think in sequence.

He could not explain anything to Sarah because the true explanation was impossible and the false ones would multiply too fast. He could not walk into Dominic's job cold unless Dominic's job turned out to be something forgiving like lighthouse keeper or man who silently moved boxes in a warehouse. The odds were not good. He could not call anyone from 2025 because he was in 2005 and, more importantly, he no longer existed there to take the call. He could not go looking for his own fourteen-year-old self because that was insane even by the standards he had just set.

What he could do was learn the shape of the life he had stolen before other people started quizzing him on it.

Objects first. People second. Let everyone else talk more than he did. Ask questions when asking questions seemed normal. Admit nothing specific. Stay home if possible. Search the apartment. Search the computer. Search the phone. Build Dominic from residue.

It was not a moral plan. It was a survival plan. Morality had had its turn in the workshop with the copper coil and the AI prompt and the months he had spent feeding a grievance until it became a machine. This was after.

The alarm in the bedroom went off at six-thirty with the clipped beep of a device that believed in getting to the point.

Nate shut off the bathroom light and stood in the doorway to the hall, heart punching at the wrong ribs.

* * *

Sarah slapped at the alarm twice before she found the button that killed it. "God," she said into the pillow, voice thick with sleep. Then she rolled onto her back and blinked toward him from the bed.

"You're up early," she said.

There it was, the first line delivered to Dominic and accepted by default.

Nate made himself answer on the first beat. "Yeah."

She pushed herself upright and gathered her hair in both hands, twisting it once before she let it fall again. There was a blue ink stain on the side of her right hand, near the thumb, as if she had fallen asleep grading with the pen still uncapped. She squinted toward the window unit like she was personally offended by its noise, then looked back at him.

"Did you sleep at all?"

"Not much."

"Mm." She swung her legs out of bed. "I told you not to read that stupid zoning packet in bed."

He had no idea whether Dominic had done that, but it sounded plausible and, more to the point, she was giving him a piece of himself for free. Nate nodded like a man annoyed to be correctly known.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

She stood, stretched hard enough to make her shirt ride up a little at the waist, then padded past him to the bathroom in a faded Quinnipiac T-shirt and thin drawstring shorts. Before she went in she reached up, touched his cheek with the back of her fingers, and kissed the other one in passing.

"Morning, Dom."

Nate forgot for a second how faces worked. He managed "Morning" just before the bathroom door shut.

He stood there after, touching the spot she had kissed. What got him was the ease of it, how casual it had been, how automatic, a move she had made a hundred times with no ceremony attached, the kind of affection that existed because Dominic had been alive long enough with her for habit to do some of the work love used to do. Nate had not earned even the smallest millimeter of it, and there it was on his skin anyway.

The percolator on the counter gave a sudden hollow pop. He turned too fast and banged his hip on the edge of the table, misjudging Dominic's width. A binder clip jumped off Sarah's stack of papers and skittered across the floor.

"You okay?" she called through the bathroom door, toothbrush already in her mouth.

"Yeah," he said. "Just hit the table."

"The table usually wins."

Nate bent for the binder clip and stayed down a second longer than he needed to, fingers flat on the floorboards. She had said it through toothpaste, already halfway into the next thing, not even checking whether he was hurt.

He retrieved the binder clip and put it back on the stack, trying to recreate the exact arrangement as if she might notice a half-inch of displacement and pronounce sentence. The top page was a worksheet about the Progressive Era. Red pen marks ran down the margin in a neat, unsentimental hand. Not cruel, exactly, but unwilling to indulge nonsense. One student's answer had been circled with a small note beside it: `specifics, please.` The words should not have hit him as hard as they did.

Sarah came out brushing her teeth, hair gathered now in a rough ponytail. She moved through the kitchen with the confidence of a person whose body knew the cabinets without consulting her. Mug from the second shelf. Spoon from the drawer by the stove. Lunch container from the drying rack. She rinsed, spat, then frowned at the percolator.

"Did you make coffee?"

"I tried."

She took the lid off, looked in, and laughed once through her nose.

"Dom, there's no basket in here."

He stared at the open percolator. She was right. The basket piece sat on the counter by the sink where he had apparently set it down while reconfiguring the rest.

"Right," he said. "I was in the middle of..."

"Sure you were."

She rinsed the parts and rebuilt the thing in quick practical motions, coffee first, stem through the center, basket seated properly, lid on. Her teacher voice slipped in without becoming performative, clear and patient in spite of him.

"If you plug it in without the basket, you just get angry bean water."

"Good to know."

"It's not, actually. I wish I didn't know."

She wore a thin silver ring on one thumb and turned it absentmindedly while the water heated. Nate filed that away with everything else. Ring. Thumb. Quinnipiac shirt. Ink on hand. Hated weak coffee on principle. Taught summer school in July and corrected teenagers even in the margins of worksheets she had probably handed back already. He had wanted Dominic to be flat enough to kill. The apartment kept disproving that. Sarah did worse.

She opened the fridge and frowned in at the shelves. "Do you want the last yogurt or should I take it?"

Nate did not know what Dominic did with yogurt. "You take it."

"Wow," she said, turning to look at him. "Who are you and what have you done with my boyfriend?"

He went cold so fast the room changed dimensions.

Sarah saw something on his face, but whatever she saw read to her as sleep deprivation, not cosmic confession. She smiled, small and crooked.

"I mean because it's peach," she said. "You always say peach tastes like perfume."

"Right. Yeah."

"You look wrecked."

"Didn't sleep."

"I gathered."

She took the yogurt anyway, tucked it into her lunch bag with a packet of crackers and two folders thick with papers, and moved on. Nate stood there with his hands braced lightly on the back of a chair, trying to ease the blood back into circulation without looking like a man who had nearly confessed to consciousness murder over dairy.

The coffee began its wet burbling in the percolator. Sarah set two mugs on the counter and glanced at him. "Are you going in?"

He should have had an answer ready. Dominic, presumably, would have.

"Maybe later," he said. Then, hearing how vague that sounded, he added, "I want to see if I can shake this off first."

She nodded once. "Okay."

No interrogation. No follow-up. That should have relieved him. Instead it made the whole thing feel more precarious, as if trust itself were an unstable platform and he had just stepped onto it with muddy boots.

Sarah sat at the little dining table and opened one of the folders while they waited for the coffee. She uncapped a red pen with her teeth and began reading an essay without any theatrical sighing about it, just immediate work. Nate watched her eyes move and had the ugly, dislocating thought that Dominic had seen this exact sight so many mornings it had stopped registering as a miracle. The side of her face in cheap morning light. The crease between her brows when a sentence annoyed her. The way she tapped the end of the pen twice against the paper before she wrote a comment. Intimacy was apparently made mostly of repeated access.

"One of these kids wrote that Upton Sinclair was president," she said, still reading.

Nate made a noise he hoped was conversational.

"I know it's summer school," she said. "I know I'm not supposed to expect brilliance. But if you're going to make somebody president, at least pick somebody with the right vibe."

He almost laughed. "What's Sinclair's vibe?"

She looked up from the page. "Not president."

"Fair."

"Maybe secretary of being upset in public."

He laughed before he meant to, and it came out in Dominic's voice — deeper, warmer. Sarah glanced over, and for one terrible second Nate thought he'd laughed on the wrong note, that even amusement had a biometric profile she would know.

But she only smiled and went back to the essay.

"Thank you," she said. "I'm very funny before seven-thirty. After seven-thirty I become a bureaucrat."

"I can see that."

"You can't. You're asleep by then."

He nodded like he had been. The lie slid into place because all it had to do was agree with what she already believed. That, more than anything else, began to teach him how he might survive. Everyone else was already carrying Dominic around in their heads. He just had to stop crashing into the version they had.

The thought was useful. It was also sickening.

Sarah got up when the coffee was ready and poured for both of them. She took a sip, made a face, poured in a little milk from the fridge, and drank again. Nate copied the black part first because he did not know Dominic's coffee settings and the heat of it shocked his tongue. He had not brushed his teeth yet. The coffee hit his mouth like punishment and rescue at once.

"You put on a shirt?" Sarah asked.

He looked down. Sometime between the bathroom and the kitchen he had changed into one of Dominic's undershirts from the dresser because standing around in pajama pants had started to feel too intimate for panic. The shirt fit close through the chest. Not his chest.

"Yeah."

"Inside out."

He looked again. The seams were showing.

"Right."

She laughed, not loudly, and came around the table to tug the hem loose for him. "Hold still."

He did, because there was no safe alternative. She pulled it up and over his head in one efficient motion, then handed it back to him the right way without making a thing of the fact that his body had been briefly shirtless in front of her. Of course she did not make a thing of it. They lived together. She had seen this body all the time. Nate got the shirt back on while trying not to look like a man being flayed alive by domestic competence.

"Thanks," he said.

"You really didn't sleep."

"No."

"Try not to drive if you're this foggy."

"Okay."

She studied him a second longer than she had before, and he could feel the beginning of future trouble in that look. Nothing had clicked into place for her this morning. She was simply a person who noticed what the room gave her. Then she reached for her bag, checked the folders, checked for her keys, checked the stove even though it had not been on, and slung the strap over her shoulder. That, too, Nate filed away. She checked twice because she wanted the day to start on purpose.

"I'll be home around three," she said. "Maybe later if they trap us in curriculum planning hell."

"Okay."

"There's leftover lo mein if you want it."

"Okay."

"And if my mom calls the apartment, tell her I'll call her tonight. She still thinks summer school means I teach criminals in a basement."

"Got it."

She took another look at him, softer this time. "You sure you're okay?"

The truthful answer was so large it would have broken the walls.

He made himself stay in the smaller one. "Just tired."

"All right." She stepped in, kissed his cheek again, then his mouth, quick and familiar. "Get some sleep, Dom."

And then she was at the door, gathering keys from the bowl, phone from the counter, sunglasses from on top of the television. She frowned down at the sunglasses.

"Did you move these?"

He had not. But panic made him stupid and guilt made him eager. "No."

"Weird." She shrugged. "Anyway."

The door opened. Hallway light fell in a yellow slab across the carpet.

"Bye."

"Bye."

The door shut.

Nate stayed where he was until he heard her footsteps fade down the hall and the elevator clank open somewhere beyond it. Then he crossed to the window and nudged the blind with two fingers. Sarah came out of the building below in a white blouse and dark skirt now, hair pinned up tighter, bag on one shoulder, coffee mug balanced in the other hand. She moved fast without looking rushed. A woman on a mission to explain the Progressive Era to people who did not deserve her before eight in the morning.

She got into a car Nate assumed was hers. The engine turned over. The car pulled out.

Only after it was gone did he let the blind fall shut.

The apartment changed immediately. The quiet rose up as soon as she was gone. The air conditioner seemed louder because there was nothing human left to compete with it. The percolator clicked in cooling metal ticks. Somewhere upstairs a toilet ran. The place still held Sarah everywhere she had just been, but now it held Nate as the only person actively lying in it.

He sat on the couch too hard and felt the cushions answer Dominic's weight, not his. The wallet, the flip phone, and the keys were still on the coffee table where he had arranged them. They looked less like personal effects now than items logged into evidence.

He picked up the wallet again and took the license out. Dominic Altieri looked back at him in laminated indifference: thirty-one, five-ten, address matching the apartment. Sarah had kissed that face goodbye and gone to work assuming it would still be here when she got home. The day had not paused for Nate's shock. It had put Dominic's schedule in his lap and kept moving.

He had thought the hard part would be the machine: the field generator, the code written with liar confidence, the copper coil and magnetron and the coffee mug he had watched split at the glaze line on his apartment floor in 2025. Instead the hard part was this stupid Tuesday morning surviving the murder intact. There was yogurt in the fridge, lesson plans on the table, and a woman who checked the stove twice before leaving. Dominic's life had not turned dramatic just because Dominic was gone. It was still waiting to be lived.

Which meant the impersonation was viable, and viability was its own disaster. There would be lunch, work calls, Ma, Tommy, whoever Kassos was on the phone, bills in Dominic's name, people saying Dom from doorways and expecting an answer without hesitation. Sarah had let him pass over coffee, and that ordinary mercy made the scale of it worse.

He stood up too fast, caught himself on the sink, and waited for the room to settle. Dominic's hands whitened around the counter edge. There was not going to be a return trip, and there was no body in 2025, no missing Dominic to hand back. Sarah would come home tonight and expect him here, and somewhere in Bridgeport his fourteen-year-old self was still moving through an ordinary July morning with Mort alive and reachable to him, unreachable to Nate. The only future available was Dominic's unless Nate blew it up and took everybody in it down with him.

The thought that finally got him moving was not hope. It was workload.

He went to the little desk by the computer, found a legal pad in the drawer, and clicked a pen until it worked. His first attempt at writing looked wrong immediately. Dominic's hand was broader, the grip heavier. The pen moved with a confidence Nate did not own. He wrote DOM just to see it, stopped there, then filled the next lines with the names he already had: Sarah. Ray. Tommy. Ma. Kassos.

Under that he wrote, smaller, `Listen more. Don't volunteer. Search everything.` He stared at `Don't` for a second, irritated by how relieved he felt to see the contraction there, as if even the legal pad could tell when he was faking it. Then he put the pen down and flexed Dominic's fingers until the cramp eased.

The apartment around him offered none of a crime scene's mercy. Crime scenes were inert. This place was active. The phone could ring any minute. Someone could knock. Sarah could come back because she had forgotten something and find him standing over Dominic's paperwork like a thief in daylight. Every object in the room held information, and every object in the room could also expose him if he mishandled it.

Nate looked toward the bedroom where the unmade bed still held the hollow of two bodies.

Then he picked up the flip phone, opened it with his thumb, and started learning how to be the dead man he had made.
