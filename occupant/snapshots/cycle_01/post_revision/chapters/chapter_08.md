# Chapter 8

By mid-September the lunch hour had become the most manageable part of Nate's day.

The office wanted a competent Dominic Altieri. The apartment wanted a loving one. Eastbridge Plaza wanted almost nothing from him as long as he stayed out of Unit 7.

He started telling Ray he had errands and driving to the strip mall with the demolition date sitting in his head like a file he couldn't close. December 28. Four months and change. The number made everything at Kassos Development feel pre-recorded. Everyone in that office kept talking as if there were still time for the usual office things, the little delays and jokes and coffee refills, when Nate had already seen the date on paper in Dominic's file. He had the weird superstitious feeling that if he kept looking at the place in its current state, if he kept logging enough repetitions of the bell over Gail's door and the parking lot glare and the hand-painted L on Lazar's Hardware, maybe the future would have a harder time deleting it.

He parked near the diner because it was the closest he could get without walking into the store.

Gail's sat in the middle of the row, squeezed between the dry cleaner and the barbershop, with a sun-faded sign and a bell over the door that hit a tired high note every time someone came in. Inside: four tables, a pie case with one lemon meringue pie losing volume by the hour, a counter worn smooth at the edges, and stools that swiveled halfway before sticking. The place smelled like coffee that had been on since dawn, fryer grease, toast, and the damp cold air the air conditioner kept coughing out without ever quite winning.

Gail worked the counter alone most days. She was sturdy, fast, mid-fifties, her dark hair pinned up so tight it looked structural. A pencil lived above her ear until she needed it, at which point it vanished into her hand so quickly Nate never saw her reach. She moved with the speed of someone who had been carrying too much for too long and had made a separate peace with it. On his second visit he watched her catch a plate one-handed before it slid off the pass, slap a fresh napkin under it, and tell the customer reaching for it, "Give it a second, honey, unless you want fingerprints burned off," without sounding annoyed. It was half warning, half care.

The first day Nate ordered the turkey club because he panicked at the menu.

"White or rye?" Gail asked.

"White."

"Fries or slaw?"

He looked at the menu again even though the answers were right there. "Fries."

She gave him a quick flat look. "You can just say fries. I'm not grading you."

"Right. Sorry."

"Don't be sorry. Be hungry."

That was about the level of intimacy she required from him. He sat at the counter, ate the sandwich, drank the coffee, paid, left. The turkey club was good in the way diner food was good when nobody was pretending it was anything else: thick turkey, too much mayo, bacon that had gone a little soft under the tomato, toast cut corner to corner and held together with frilled toothpicks. It came with a pickle spear that leaned against the fries as if it had collapsed there.

On the third visit she nodded at him as he came in and said, "Turkey club?" without looking up from the register.

The relief that moved through him at being remembered for an order and not a biography was immediate and embarrassing.

At work, everyone who looked at him was running a comparison against Dominic. At home, Sarah did it even when she was trying not to. Gail had no baseline. To her he was just a quiet guy in office clothes who came in around twelve-thirty, sat on the counter side of the room instead of hiding at a table, and tipped decently. He did not have to authenticate. He did not have to know how Dominic took his coffee in a restaurant he'd supposedly been visiting for years. He could say three words in twenty minutes and nobody thought that meant anything.

Gail talked enough to make up the difference.

She talked to customers, to the dishwasher when he showed up late, to the coffeepot, to the television bolted high in the corner, to nobody in particular. She did not gossip with any air of secrecy. Information moved through her diner the way steam moved through the kitchen: visibly, constantly, and without asking permission.

That was how Nate learned the strip mall in pieces.

Al had been cutting hair next door for twenty-two years and was supposedly going to retire to Florida any day now, a claim Gail delivered daily with the confidence of someone narrating a joke she had fully worn through. "He says this every September," she told Nate while wiping the counter in front of him. "Then it gets cold and he says he can't leave till after Christmas, and then it's tax season, and then his daughter sends another brochure and he says Florida is full of lunatics. He'll die in that barbershop and make me cater it."

Mrs. Park at the dry cleaner had a son at the same high school where Sarah taught. According to Gail, the boy had already lost one sneaker and an algebra book in the first week back. "How do you lose one sneaker?" Gail asked the room at large. "I know kids are stupid. I was also a kid. But one sneaker?"

Cesar at the bodega stocked Cafe Santo Domingo because his wife, Marisol, insisted on it. "Nobody buys it except her cousins and one guy from Fairfield who comes in once a month and acts like he's discovered treasure," Gail said. "But Marisol says the other coffee tastes like cardboard boiled in sink water, so Cesar keeps a whole shelf of it and pretends it's a business decision."

Nate listened and ate and let these people arrange themselves in his head.

Sometimes one of them came through the diner while he was there. Al blew in smelling of talc and Barbicide, silver hair slicked back, little dark clippings dusting his smock, a folded Polaroid of two little girls tucked into the corner of his mirror-bright wallet when he paid for coffee. Once he stood at the counter counting out quarters and muttering about his left knee while Gail filled a to-go cup and told him to stop pretending the weather caused every pain in his body. "You're sixty-three and stubborn," she said. "That's the weather."

Mrs. Park came in on a Thursday afternoon in a cream cardigan with a lint roller tucked in her handbag like medical equipment. She drank tea, not coffee, and spent most of the five minutes she waited for it rebuttoning the cuff of her own sleeve because one pearl button had gone loose. When Gail asked after her son, she rolled her eyes in a way that made it clear affection and exasperation were roommates. "He says he can't find his gym clothes," she said. "I say clothes don't become invisible just because you're lazy."

Sarah would've liked her.

Cesar came in carrying a produce crate against his hip, plantains freckling in the bottom of it, price-gun labels stuck to two fingers. He set it by the pie case and asked Gail if she had change for a twenty. She asked if he had those potato chips she liked back in stock. He said if she meant the barbecue ones, yes, if she meant the unsalted ones, no, and no one in America bought unsalted anything by choice except one old man who should be grateful for what he got. Gail told him to say hello to Marisol. He said, "If I forget, she'll remind me she's married to an idiot," and took his change and the coffee Gail had already poured for him.

These people did not know Dominic Altieri. They did not know what Nate had done. They did not know he had come from twenty years ahead carrying a dead man's name and a demolition date.

To them he was a lunch customer.

He had not understood until then how tired he was of being observed by people who thought they knew him.

From stool six, if he turned a little, he could see across the parking lot to Lazar's Hardware. The L in LAZAR'S had faded more than the other letters, as if sunlight had developed a particular grudge against it. Sometimes the door stood open and he could see dim movement inside, a customer at the counter, a ladder display near the window, a hanging sign for paint rollers. Once he saw Linda in the back through a gap between shelves, small and quick, carrying a box against her hip. He never saw Mort from the stool, though he kept looking for him.

He did not go in.

Not yet had become one of the organizing principles of his life.

By the second week Gail no longer asked what he wanted.

"Turkey club, fries, coffee," she said when he sat down.

"Yeah."

"You don't ever get tired of it?"

"I know what happens."

She snorted. "That's one philosophy."

He almost smiled.

The coffee appeared before he asked for it, the sandwich a few minutes later, the check face down near his elbow after that. There was no danger in the routine. Nobody in the diner asked him where he'd been on a specific weekend in 2004 or whether he remembered the Cape Cod restaurant or why he suddenly seemed softer, less somewhere else. Gail asked if he wanted a refill. Al asked if the Red Sox had any pitching this year. Mrs. Park asked if the parking lot had always been that badly striped or if someone had gone blind with the paint machine. These were answerable questions. He did not have to be brilliant. He only had to be present.

That should not have felt like mercy.

It did.

***

A few days later Sarah ran a hand over his hair that morning while she was looking for her keys and said, "You're getting shaggy."

"Am I?"

"Yes. You have ears. I can see less of them than I used to."

She found the keys in the bowl by the door where they always were, kissed his cheek, and left for school with a canvas bag full of papers and what looked like an apple she would forget to eat. Nate stood in the apartment a few seconds longer than he needed to, touching the side of his head where she'd brushed him. Then he went to work, got through the morning without obviously disgracing himself, and drove to Eastbridge at lunch with Dominic's hair suddenly feeling like a problem.

The day had that early-fall quality Bridgeport got for about fifteen minutes before winter started thinking about it. The heat had backed off but not left. Dry leaves skated low across the parking lot and caught in the curb by the storm drain. The sky looked scrubbed. He ate half his sandwich at Gail's, drank one cup of coffee, told her he had to walk before he turned into an office chair permanently, and stepped back out into the lot.

It was the first time he'd let himself move through the strip mall without heading straight back to the Civic.

Seen from the parking lot instead of the diner stool, Eastbridge Plaza looked less like the ruin he'd built in his head and more like what it was: a tired commercial strip holding a lot of ordinary life together through habit. The nail salon had glossy posters of impossible hands sun-faded at the edges. A cigarette butt had been mashed into the mulch around the sad little shrub by the curb. Someone had taped a handwritten sign inside Gail's front window that said NO CHECKS and then, below it in smaller handwriting, SERIOUSLY. The paint on the concrete wheel stops had begun to peel. It looked held together by bad striping, taped paper, and people showing up anyway, and something in Nate's chest pulled tight at the sight of it.

For years in 2025 the strip mall had existed in his mind as a single catastrophic image, all consequence and no present tense, the place that had been destroyed. Standing there now, he could feel how incomplete that memory had been. Demolition flattened time. It made everything before the machines arrived seem like prelude. But the place was not living like prelude. It was living like itself.

He crossed to Al's barbershop because Sarah was right and because a haircut was the kind of neutral adult errand nobody asked follow-up questions about.

The place smelled of tonic, talc, old aftershave, and the blue chemical sweetness of the comb jar on the counter. Sports radio muttered under the buzz of clippers. Al stood behind a chair with one hand braced on the back and the other holding a comb. Up close he looked older than Nate had guessed from the diner, the skin around his eyes cut by small deep lines, his wrists roped and strong. A Polaroid of two girls with matching jack-o'-lantern pajamas was tucked into the corner of the mirror in front of him. One of them had her front teeth out. Both were grinning hard.

"You waiting on somebody?" Al asked.

"No."

"Then sit down. I'll finish with him and get you."

The man in the chair was maybe twelve and sulking with the total commitment only boys that age could manage. Al held his chin still with two fingers and said, not unkindly, "Your mother asked for around the ears, not this whole thing. Sit up."

The kid rolled his eyes and sat up.

Nate took the vinyl chair by the wall. Hair clippings gathered in the corners of the floor like little animal corpses. There were three magazines on the side table, one six months old, one older, one apparently permanent. On the bulletin board near the door a postcard for Daytona Beach had been pinned beside a handwritten note reminding someone named Anthony to pick up his church raffle tickets. The postcard had a crease down the middle and a coffee stain in one corner.

When Al waved him over, Nate climbed into the chair and looked at Dominic's face in the mirror, bibbed in a striped cape like a child.

"How you want it?" Al asked.

Nate realized, too late, that Dominic probably had an answer to that in some parallel version of this lunch hour that no longer existed. "Just shorter," he said. "Clean it up."

"I can do that."

The cape snapped around his neck. Al's hands were dry and sure. He clicked the clippers on and went to work without trying to manufacture small talk, which Nate appreciated more than he would've admitted. They went almost a full minute with only the radio talking. Then Al said, "You work around here?"

"Downtown."

"Mmm."

It was not agreement or disagreement. Just acknowledgment that Nate had answered less than he'd been asked and Al was not going to chase him for it.

"You just move?" Al said after another minute.

"Why?"

Al tipped Nate's head with two fingers and clipped around his ear. "You look at everything like it might've been moved overnight."

Nate let out a short laugh before he could stop it. "Maybe I did."

"Maybe you did what?"

"Move."

"All right." Al dusted the back of Nate's neck with a brush soft as fur. "If you need to know anything useful, ask Gail. If you need to know anything useless, ask me."

"That's a good division."

"It keeps everybody in their lane."

He jerked his chin toward the postcard on the bulletin board. "My daughter sent me that. Says I'm supposed to retire and come sit in the sun like a lizard. Meanwhile she keeps mailing me school pictures of those two monsters." His mouth twitched toward the mirror corner where the Polaroid sat. "I stay here, I can see them when I want and send them home when I don't."

"That sounds better than Florida."

"That's because you have a functioning brain."

Ten dollars later Nate stepped back out into the lot with clipped hair itching down his collar and a clear view of Lazar's Hardware straight ahead.

He did not go there. He turned first toward the dry cleaner.

Mrs. Park's shop was narrow and colder than the diner, the kind of cold built for fabric and metal hangers rather than people. Shirts rode slowly around the overhead track in their plastic skins. Mrs. Park stood at the counter in a neat blouse with a measuring tape around her neck and a square gold watch turned to the inside of her wrist. At a small folding table in the back, a teenage boy in a wrinkled school T-shirt was bent over homework with one earbud in and a chemistry textbook open under his elbow. Every few seconds he spun a pen between his fingers without looking up.

"No game until you finish the worksheet," Mrs. Park said in his direction, not raising her voice.

"I know."

"You say you know and then you don't do it."

"I'm doing it."

She glanced up and saw Nate by the counter. "Hello."

"Hi. I was just looking."

"Looking is free," she said. "Cleaning is not."

There was no smile with it, but no hostility either. Just precision.

Nate nodded toward the boy. "School started rough?"

Mrs. Park made a small face that translated easily. "School starts rough every year. He forgets he's a student all summer. Then September arrives and it's a terrible shock."

The boy said, without looking up, "Mom."

"What? It's true."

Nate found himself smiling for real this time. He said he'd be back sometime and left before she could ask his name.

Cesar's bodega was next, packed tight with things the fluorescent lights made both more and less appealing: stacked canned goods, bruising bananas, paper towels in a tottering column, lottery tickets, a rack of aspirin blister packs, a refrigerator humming with soda. Merengue played quietly from a radio near the register. Cesar stood behind the counter with a box cutter in one hand, cutting tape off a case of canned beans. He wore a navy sweatshirt despite the weather and had a strip of price-gun paper stuck to his thumb.

"You need something?" he asked.

"Gum."

Cesar pointed with the box cutter. Nate picked a pack of Spearmint and set it on the counter. Near the register, between the Maxwell House and the instant coffee, sat a small shelf of bright yellow-and-red Cafe Santo Domingo.

"That sell?" Nate asked, mostly because he wanted to hear Cesar talk.

Cesar looked at the shelf and then back at him. "Enough."

He rang up the gum. "My wife drinks it. So now everybody drinks it, whether they know it or not. She says the other stuff tastes dead."

"Seems decisive."

"She's a decisive woman." He shrugged. "Keeps me organized."

He handed Nate the change and nodded toward the hardware store. "If you need light bulbs, Mort has the good ones. Not the cheap garbage that burns out in a week."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah." Cesar bent back over the box with the cutter. "He'll talk your ear off, but you'll leave with the right thing."

Nate pocketed the gum and stepped back outside.

The door to Lazar's Hardware was propped open with a paint can. Even from the sidewalk he could catch the smell: sawdust, metal, WD-40, the faint rubber smell of floor mats warmed by years and summer. A customer stood at the counter holding a package of something small and silver. Mort, half-turned toward the wall of fasteners, was explaining the difference between two boxes with both hands. Nate couldn't hear every word from where he stood, only the cadence, the patient material precision of it. Mort lifted one box, shook it once, set it down, took the other. The customer nodded. Mort reached for a pencil behind his ear, wrote something on a scrap of paper, and handed it over.

The sight of him in motion still had the power to make everything else in the frame lose resolution.

Nate stood there long enough for the customer to glance toward the door and then kept walking, because standing outside your dead father's store staring at him through the opening was only a little less unhinged than walking in and saying hi, it's me, I murdered a man to get here.

He made a slow circuit back toward the diner and stopped at the edge of the lot before crossing. Gail was in her doorway smoking half a cigarette she was clearly not supposed to be smoking. She looked at his haircut and nodded once, approving the removal of excess.

"Better," she said.

"That's what I was going for."

She flicked ash into a coffee can. "You go into Mrs. Park's?"

"For a minute."

"She'll fix your life if you let her. Hem your pants, feed your kid, tell you your skin looks tired."

"Useful range."

"Very."

She took one more drag and stubbed the cigarette out halfway, annoyed at herself. "You see the hardware store?"

He kept his face as still as he could. "From outside."

"Mort's a good guy."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah." She pushed the door open with her hip and went back inside. "Whole damn place is good people, mostly. That's why it makes me so mad."

Nate stood there a second longer.

Whole damn place.

That was what had shifted without his permission over the last two weeks. The strip mall had stopped being one store plus collateral. It had become a network diagram drawn in coffee refills and lint rollers and school worksheets and a shelf of coffee that existed because one woman liked it and her husband was still trying to please her. It was gossip, extension cords, hair clippings, aspirin, tomatoes on white toast, a father telling customers which light bulbs weren't garbage. Demolition, in the file, was a date and a contractor and a line item. Out here it looked like severing a bunch of ordinary dependencies and pretending nobody would notice because the concrete got replaced.

He finished lunch late, drove back to the office, and spent the rest of the afternoon pretending to care about parking variance language while the smell of talc and deli turkey clung to him under Dominic's shirt.

***

The first time Mort sat down beside him at Gail's happened on a Thursday so ordinary Nate would've lost it instantly if it hadn't become one of the fixed points of his life.

He got there at twelve-thirty, took stool six, and Gail said, "Turkey club, fries, coffee," as if reading from a stable law of nature. He said yes. There were only two other people in the place, both at tables. The TV over the counter was tuned to muted local news. A delivery truck backed somewhere out behind the strip with a long periodic beep. Sun laid itself flat across the parking lot. Nothing announced itself.

The bell over the door rang and Mort came in carrying the weather with him, that faint outdoor coolness that had finally started to edge the heat back.

He wore work pants, a pale blue shirt with the sleeves rolled, and his reading glasses on the chain Nate remembered from childhood, the chain catching briefly at the hollow of his throat when he bent. There was sawdust on one cuff. His hands looked exactly as they had through the store window in July: rough, scarred, capable. He paused just inside the door to let his eyes adjust from the glare, and Nate had one of those stupid body moments where part of him expected to be recognized by blood magic, some buried paternal code, some impossible click of the universe admitting the administrative error and correcting it on the spot.

Nothing happened.

"Coffee, Mort?" Gail called.

"Yeah, thanks."

He came to the counter and took stool four, leaving one empty seat between himself and Nate. The empty seat made Nate want to laugh and be sick at the same time. Nineteen years. For nineteen years there had been no hand to shake, no body to occupy the next stool over, no chance of ordinary proximity. Now there was one stool's worth of space and the whole idiot machinery of time travel and murder and corporate paperwork fit inside it.

Gail set the mug down in front of Mort and then looked between the two men.

"Mort, this is..." She squinted at Nate. "Honey, I never asked your name."

Nate swallowed. "Dom."

"Dom," Gail repeated. "Mort runs the hardware store. Dom eats the same thing every day and acts like that means he has achieved wisdom."

Mort turned and offered his hand immediately, easy as breathing. "Nice to meet you."

Nate took it.

The grip was dry and firm and completely normal. That was the unbearable part. Brief pressure, a small pump, release. Nate had enough time in it to feel the thickened skin at the base of Mort's fingers and the scrape of a healing nick along one knuckle, and then Mort was already turning back toward the TV with his coffee.

"You too," Nate said, and was relieved his voice came out steady.

Mort lifted his coffee, blew on it once, and looked up at the TV. "You hear they're talking rain this weekend?"

"They've been wrong all week," Gail said.

"That's why I said talking, not doing."

Nate stared at his sandwich when it arrived, because if he looked straight ahead too long he might start acting like a person whose dead father was sitting two stools away discussing meteorology.

Mort glanced over after a minute. "You work around here, Dom?"

"Not far," Nate said. "Downtown."

"Office?"

"Yeah."

Mort nodded as if that told him enough to continue being polite and not one syllable more. "Lucky you. We got the delivery truck double-parked this morning and one guy leaned on the horn like we'd invented inconvenience personally."

Gail snorted. "Was it the red van?"

"Silver SUV."

"Then he was already mad before he got here."

They let that sit. Nate ate half a fry. Mort took another drink of coffee. On the TV, somebody's mouth moved through a headline nobody in the room needed.

Then Mort tipped his chin toward the sports section folded beside the napkin dispenser. "You watch baseball?"

"Some."

"Red Sox got enough pitching?"

The question hit Nate with the same weird double exposure everything in 2005 did. The answer existed. The whole season existed. He could, if he wanted, start talking from outside the frame, from the cheap god position of already knowing what happened. Instead he took a drink of coffee and said, "I don't know. Depends which Schilling shows up."

Mort made a face that suggested deep personal offense at Curt Schilling's ankle. "He hasn't looked right in a while."

"No."

"Bullpen's a headache too."

"Yeah."

Gail slid between them to refill Nate's cup. "You two gonna solve the American League or just mourn it?"

"We're pacing ourselves," Mort said.

That got a quick laugh out of her. Nate looked down at his plate again. Heat climbed his neck. Mort hadn't leaned on the joke or looked around for the laugh. He'd just said it and reached for his coffee, and the ease of that hit Nate harder than if he'd tried.

They talked a little longer about the Sox, then about the city repainting half the curb blue and none of the spaces straight, then about nothing at all. Nate learned that Mort took one sugar and then, some days, didn't use it. He learned that his father folded Sweet'N Low packets in exact quarters before setting them aside unopened. He learned the shape of Mort's pause before he answered a question he didn't think needed much of an answer.

It would've been possible to leave it there. That had probably been the wise move.

Instead Nate heard himself say, "You run the hardware store?"

Mort looked at him properly for the first time, not as the man in the next stool but as a person asking. "Yeah. End unit."

"How long you been there?"

"Sixteen years."

He took the unused sugar packet and turned it once under his fingers. Not nervous exactly. Something quieter than that.

"Started small," he said. "Linda and me. Built it up over time."

"That's a long time."

"Yeah." He nodded once. "It is."

Nate could feel Gail listening without looking at them. She wiped the same spot on the counter twice. One of the men at the tables looked up from his plate and then back down. Nobody knew anything; the room had just caught the weight in Mort's voice.

He should have stopped. He didn't.

"Gail said she's mad about something with the plaza," Nate said, keeping his voice casual enough that he hated himself for practicing it all summer. "What's going on?"

Mort's fingers flattened the sugar packet against the counter. When he answered, the words came quiet and even.

"They're redeveloping."

Gail put a plate down harder than necessary at the far end of the counter.

Mort kept his eyes on the coffee. "They sent a letter. That's how we found out. Sixteen years we're there, and nobody asked us."

Nobody asked us.

The sentence sat between them with no ornament on it at all.

Nate put his hand around his mug because he needed something solid and temperature-based to do. The ceramic was too hot. He held it anyway.

"That sucks," he said, which was not the right response but was close to the only human one he could get through the traffic jam in his throat.

Mort gave a small shrug. "Yeah. It does."

He seemed about to leave it there, then added, "You build up inventory, regular customers, special stuff people know to come to you for. Then somebody in an office decides they can do better with the land." Another tiny shrug. "Maybe they can. Just would've been nice if anybody asked the people already here."

Gail said, from the coffeepot, "They don't ask because then they'd have to hear the answer."

Mort huffed a breath that wasn't quite a laugh.

Nate stared at the grease shining on the edge of his plate. "Who's doing it?"

Mort looked over at him again, no suspicion in it, only the assumption that of course a stranger might ask. "Some outfit called Kassos Development."

He pinched the folded sugar packet once between finger and thumb.

"Guy named Altieri signed the letter."

Nate nodded.

That was all he could risk. Just a nod, like he was storing the information for no reason other than conversation. Inside him everything went very still. It felt like a server room after the power died, the residual whine cutting out all at once.

Mort took another sip of coffee. "Probably never set foot in the place."

Nate looked at his father and almost said no.

Not because he had any way to prove Mort wrong. Because Mort was saying it to Dominic Altieri's face. Because Nate had been orbiting the place for weeks and still hadn't managed to walk in. Because there was something obscene about correcting him in the voice of the man who'd signed the letter, and his body seemed to lag half a second behind the room.

He bit the inside of his cheek and tasted blood, faint and metallic.

"Well," Mort said after a moment, the subject already beginning to close on him in the way pain closed when he had no use for public performance, "we'll see. Linda's doing inventory like it's the end of the world. Which maybe it is for shelving."

Gail pointed at him with the coffeepot. "You tell Linda I saved her a piece of pie."

"I will."

Then, because he was Mort and could not leave a practical line alone once it offered itself, he nodded toward Nate's fresh haircut. "Al do that?"

"Yeah."

"Looks better."

"Thanks."

"He's good."

"Seems like it."

"Talks too much."

"That's because you don't talk enough," Gail said.

Mort smiled into his coffee. "One of us has to keep the noise level legal."

They sat there a few more minutes after that, mostly quiet. Nate ate the second half of his sandwich even though his stomach had started refusing jurisdiction. The ordinary talk returned and frayed and returned again. Gail argued with the TV weather. One of the table customers paid and left. The bell rang. A truck shifted gears outside. Mort checked the time on the clock over the grill and set his mug down.

"I should get back," he said.

Nate reached for his wallet before the motion had fully become thought.

"Get his too," he told Gail.

Mort looked over. "You don't have to do that."

"It's fine."

"No, really."

"It's fine," Nate said again, because if he had to provide a reason he'd start saying insane things like because I can't tell you who I am so let me at least buy you a coffee.

Mort studied him for one second, then gave the small nod of a man accepting a favor without making a performance of refusing it three times. "All right. Appreciate it."

"Yeah."

Gail rang the check up, tore it, took Nate's cash, and gave him a look that said she had registered the whole exchange and would store it wherever she stored things.

Mort stood, adjusted the glasses chain against his shirt, and picked up the to-go cup she'd poured for him without asking. "See you around, Dom."

"Yeah," Nate said. "See you."

The bell rang behind Mort on the way out. Through the window Nate watched him cross the parking lot toward Lazar's Hardware with the coffee in one hand, shoulders set a little forward from habit, not age. Mort let himself into the store with his elbow because both hands were full, and the door swung wide enough for Nate to get one more brief rectangle of the place inside: ladder endcaps, fluorescent light, the dark line of the counter. Then the door closed.

Nate got to his car before the shaking started in earnest.

He sat behind the wheel with both hands locked around it, Dominic's broad fingers whitening over the textured plastic, the scar across the left knuckle gone pale. The cup of diner coffee in the holder shivered against its lid. He'd missed the key slot on the first try and left the key hanging there crooked. Across the lot, life went on with its obscene administrative calm. Somebody came out of the nail salon laughing. Gail wiped a table. A delivery van idled at the curb. Mort was back in the store Nate had spent half his life remembering only at the moment of its destruction.

Nobody asked us.

Guy named Altieri signed the letter.

He put his forehead briefly against the top of the steering wheel and smelled vinyl, stale sun, the faint ghost of Al's talc from the haircut, deli turkey on his hands, coffee cooling beside him. He had bought his father a coffee. That was the whole transaction. A stranger named Dom had bought Mort Lazar a coffee and watched him carry it back into the building that was going to be taken from him.

He lifted his head, started the car, and backed out of the space with his hands still shaking hard enough to rattle the coffee lid.
