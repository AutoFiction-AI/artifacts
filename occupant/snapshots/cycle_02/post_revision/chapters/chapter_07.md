# Chapter 7

With everyone else gone, the fluorescent hum seemed to carry farther. Nate glanced at the phone beside Dominic's keyboard. 7:13.

Kassos Development always felt temporary after hours, like the warehouse it had been before somebody laid carpet tile over the concrete and called the result professional. The fluorescent panels buzzed overhead. Desktops whirred in their sleep modes with that hot-plastic fan noise older computers made when they were technically resting but still clearly doing something. The break-room coffee had been sitting on the burner since lunch. Its smell had gone past stale and into scorched.

Nate sat at Dominic's desk and waited for the last sound of another person.

He had heard Ray leave ten minutes earlier, the outer door thudding shut hard enough to rattle the glass in Victor's office. Then there had been the smaller sounds that only existed once the talking stopped: air coming through the vents, a printer settling somewhere near reception, the building making the little metal clicks it made when the day heat started leaving it. That was it. There were no phones, no voices, and no footsteps coming back because somebody had forgotten a wallet or a legal pad or their stupid little stress ball shaped like a house.

The Eastbridge Plaza file sat in front of him, thicker than it had any right to be. He had opened it once, weeks earlier, just enough to find the tenant list and his father's name on it. That had been bad enough. He had closed it after that like the folder itself was hot. Every morning since, it had been there in Dominic's stack, a plain manila thing with a typed label on the tab, waiting with the patience of paperwork. It was still there when he got coffee. Still there when he came back from lunch. Still there when he pretended to understand whatever Ray was talking about. It had become one of the permanent objects in his field of vision, like the phone or the monitor or the framed photo of Dominic and Sarah at some beach he had never been to. Tonight he finally pulled it all the way over.

He did it carefully, which was stupid. The file did not require delicacy. It was a folder full of copies. It was not an explosive device. It was not even original pain. The real letter had gone to the strip mall in June. The real damage had started before Nate had ever arrived in Dominic's body. But his fingers still treated the cardboard edge like something that might cut deep if he moved too fast. He opened it.

The first section was the property summary, and it was so ordinary that for a second he hated it more than he would have hated open malice. The summary listed the address, parcel numbers, existing square footage, current tenancy, existing improvements, and proposed redevelopment. Somebody had paper-clipped a yellow routing slip to the front, with initials in three different pens. The whole thing looked like every boring administrative packet Nate had ever had to touch in his own life, the same stackable confidence that came from fonts chosen to make decisions look inevitable.

He started reading anyway. Real estate English was not a language exactly. It was a filtering system. Anything human went in one side and came out the other as a category. Businesses became tenants. Buildings became assets. The strip mall where Nate had spent half his childhood, where Gail gave him free fries if Mort was running late and where the barber kept a jar of lollipops on the counter and where the hardware store smelled like cut wood and rubber mats and WD-40, became an underperforming retail parcel with redevelopment upside. The phrase was on page two, typed cleanly in the executive summary: redevelopment upside.

He read the phrase twice. It had the same dead smoothness as corporate emails in 2025 about right-sizing and restructuring and operational efficiencies. Nate had sat through enough all-hands meetings at his support job to know the genre. Nobody said people were getting fired if they could say the company was optimizing headcount. Nobody said a neighborhood store was going away if they could say the site was being repositioned for higher-value use. It was not even lying, exactly. It was worse than lying. It was making the damage grammatical.

The next page held a rent roll, with unit numbers, square footage, lease dates, monthly rents, and security deposits. Little ruled boxes full of numbers were supposed to tell the story better than the businesses themselves. The nail salon was there. Gail's Diner was there. Mrs. Park's cleaner was there, along with the barbershop, the bodega, and Lazar's Hardware.

Morton Lazar, guarantor.

That was the line that got him.

He registered the rent amount, the lease term, and the note that the hardware store occupied the largest unit in the plaza. Then his eye caught on guarantor and stayed there. His father reduced to the person financially responsible if the wrong thing happened, like the whole life of the store could be boiled down to who signed at the bottom if everything went bad.

Mort would have laughed at the word. It was salesman language, office language, the kind of word people used when they were trying to make obligation sound clean. He would have said something like Just say I pay the damn rent. Then he would have shrugged and gone back to counting boxes or cutting chain or showing somebody which screws they actually needed instead of the screws they'd grabbed.

Nate sat back in Dominic's chair.

The office chair had one arm that sat slightly lower than the other. He had noticed it his first week and then stopped noticing it because the body adapted, the way it kept adapting to Dominic's life without asking permission. Dominic's body knew where the desk edges were. It knew how far to reach for the stapler. It knew the particular tired angle to take in this chair. Nate hated that. He hated every small competence he inherited without earning it.

When he bent over the file again, the financial projections came next. Those were easier and worse. Nate did not know the first thing about development finance, but you did not need a license to understand a before-and-after column when the after column was much bigger. The packet listed current annual revenue, projected annual revenue, stabilized occupancy, estimated return, and tenant mix repositioning. There were charts in color. Somebody had added a note in the margin about projected parking ratios and another about traffic counts. The numbers marched down the page with complete indifference to what had to be erased to get them.

Lazar's Hardware showed up there too, not by name this time but by square footage and current rent, a problem to be solved by replacing old leases with better ones. The whole strip mall was an equation whose answer had already been chosen. Mort and Linda had done sixteen years in that store and the spreadsheet still looked at them and decided the rent was too low.

Nate thought of his father behind the counter, sleeves pushed up, reading glasses on the chain against his chest, running a finger down an invoice with grease under the nail from unloading stock. He thought of the hand-painted sign with the faded L. He thought of the wall of specialty fasteners, every drawer labeled by hand, not because Mort was quaint or meticulous in some artisanal way but because if you sold the wrong machine screw to the wrong guy he came back pissed off and now you had a problem on a Saturday. There was no box in the projections for any of that. There was only the current use and the projected use and the difference between them, which turned out to be enough to kill a place.

Stopping would not have improved anything, so he kept going. The site plans were folded twice inside the file. He spread them across the desk and had to move the keyboard out of the way. Paper crackled while blue and black lines, dimensions, and callouts crowded the sheet. Little circles marked light poles, and little rectangles marked parking spaces. On the existing conditions sheet the plaza looked vaguely like itself if he squinted. He could make out the long block of units, the parking lot, the sidewalk running the storefronts, and Unit 7 on the end, the hardware store, larger than the others.

The proposed plan made the whole place unrecognizable in exactly the way maps made murder look respectable.

The building shapes had been reworked into cleaner geometry. The old awkward angles were gone. There were landscaped islands now, and wider drive aisles, and arrows showing better traffic flow. Somewhere a civil engineer had spent an afternoon making sure cars could enter and leave more efficiently. Somewhere an architect had aligned boxes until the parcel looked modern. There was no smell on the page, no heat coming off the asphalt, no bell over Gail's diner door, no dust on the hardware store shelves. The plaza that had existed in Nate's bones since childhood had been translated into rectangles and setbacks and turning radii, and once it had been translated it became easy to replace.

He found the outline of the hardware store and followed it with his finger. On the proposed sheet the space where Lazar's sat was no longer a store at all. It was part of a cleaner frontage, broken into a new arrangement of units and striped parking in front. The exact front counter where Mort rang people up, the aisle with the paint scrapers, the back room where Linda did the books and opened deliveries and yelled if Nate touched anything she had already counted, all of it had become linework under a title block.

Nate pressed his thumb against the plan hard enough to leave a damp print.

The thumbprint shone on the paper. The old website photos had never done this. Neither had the abstract fact that Dominic Altieri worked for a development company, or even the sight of Mort alive through the store glass a month ago, though that had almost put him on the floor. This drawing did, with the old place emptied out and replaced by professional certainty. Someone had measured his father's ruin with an architect's scale.

He folded the plan back along its creases and moved on to the permits. Those were stamped and dated and somehow more obscene than the renderings. A city council rezoning approval from April sat on top of filing receipts, insurance certificates, utility coordination notes, and demolition contractor correspondence. The tone of all of it was businesslike, impatient, already in motion. There was no debate in the documents, not even the ghost of one. Whatever arguments anybody might have had about the strip mall had happened somewhere else, with other people, before the file reached Dominic's desk. By the time it got here the thing was procedural.

There was a schedule sheet clipped behind the permits.

That was where the chapter of his life he'd been pretending was still abstract finally stopped pretending.

He stared at the page for a while before reading down it. The schedule had rows and dates and responsibility columns, each task already assigned to somebody. It listed utility shutoff coordination, tenant access restrictions, a salvage window, temporary fencing, a final pre-demo inspection, mobilization, and demolition.

The mall died in administrative pieces before the walls ever came down.

Nate read the dates once, then again from the top, slower. The schedule called for tenant notifications in June, follow-up documentation in July, permit closeout and contractor coordination in August and September, final site prep in December, and demolition on December 28, 2005.

He checked the date in the corner of his monitor as if the computer might disagree with the file. Monday, August 15, 2005. The monitor light made the plastic bezel look yellowed, older than it had this morning. He looked back at the schedule. The date there was December 28, 2005.

He reached for the little desk calendar sitting beside the phone. It was one of those free bank calendars with a scenic lighthouse photo across the top and enough room in each square to write one dentist appointment if you wrote small. August was already more than half used up. He flipped ahead with his thumb to September, then October, then November, then December. By the count of page headings rather than the days bleeding out underneath them, the demolition was five months away.

He put the calendar down carefully. Until now the demolition had belonged to the past in the only way that had ever mattered to him. It was the thing that had happened. The thing that took the store, then took Mort three months later, then kept taking from Nate for the next nineteen years because resentment turned out to have a fantastic shelf life if you fed it regularly enough. Even here, in 2005, the demolition had still felt weirdly historical, as if he was living in the prequel to his own damage. He knew it was coming, but knowing had not been the same thing as seeing the date typed in black on a work schedule with checkboxes next to the tasks that would get them there.

December 28 was not history. It was a Wednesday at the end of the year with a contractor attached to it and people assigned to return calls.

The last section of the file held the tenant correspondence.

There were six copies, each on Kassos Development letterhead, each clipped behind a divider with the unit number handwritten on the tab. They all used the same body text, the same dead polite tone, and the same phrases about redevelopment plans, appreciated tenancy, and transition period. Somebody had mail-merged regret.

Nate found Unit 7 and pulled that one free.

Morton Lazar  
Lazar's Hardware  
Unit 7, Eastbridge Plaza

The name and address sat at the top of the page in a clean serif font that somehow made everything worse. It was Morton's full name. Nobody called him Morton unless it was a bank, the DMV, Linda when she was genuinely pissed, or paperwork. Nate had spent half his childhood thinking Mort was the name adults used because children weren't allowed the whole thing yet. Seeing Morton Lazar typed at the top of a formal notice felt like watching strangers use the family silver.

He read the first paragraph. Then the second. Then he stopped because the language was so obviously built to survive a lawsuit that it barely sounded like English anymore. The sentences ran through formal notice, redevelopment of the property, anticipated changes to tenancy status, access restrictions during pre-demolition operations, and further communication to follow. Every sentence had been sanded smooth until nobody in it had to own what was happening.

At the bottom, above a line printed with Dominic's name and title, was the signature.

Dominic Altieri  
Project Coordinator

The signature was in blue ink, not a photocopied black stamp. Dominic had signed the file copy by hand.

Nate held the page closer. He knew that signature. That was the part his body understood before his mind did. The big open D. The narrow tail at the end of Altieri. The way the t in the middle got crossed almost as an afterthought, a hard little slash that bit through the stem instead of resting on it. He had been making that shape for weeks now on courier receipts, timesheets, call slips, parking validations, stupid internal forms that nobody in 2025 would even have printed. At first he had copied it carefully from Dominic's license. Then less carefully. Then one day he had realized his hand no longer needed the reference. The muscles had the motion.

He set the letter down, reached for the yellow legal pad by the phone, and put pen to paper before he had fully decided to.

Dominic Altieri.

The name came out almost clean the first time. Nate had not practiced enough to explain that. Dominic's hand lived in the tendons and joints and pressure points now, and the body knew how it liked to sign itself. Nate stared at the fresh blue ink on the pad, then at the signature on the letter, then back again.

The slant matched. So did the heavy start and the impatient slash across the t.

He felt sick so fast and so specifically that for a second he thought he might actually throw up on Dominic's desk.

It was not generic nausea or movie guilt. The office coffee smell turned sour in his nose. The paper in his fingers went slick. The fluorescent light seemed to get louder. He put the pen down because the click of it closing felt dangerously far away, like he was operating his own hand over a laggy connection.

This was what the chapter of his life had been reduced to. For years he had pictured a gun, or a confrontation, or Dominic cornered and finally told why he deserved to die. Instead he was staring at a letter, a piece of office paper with his father's name at the top and Dominic's signature at the bottom, and now the signature belonged to Nate's hand because Nate had wanted Dominic dead badly enough to climb into his body and finish the job.

He lifted the letter again. Mort would have opened it at the store. Nate could see that with terrible clarity. It would have been midday maybe, or late afternoon if the mail had come late. Linda would have been in the back with a delivery or the books. Mort would have slid a thumb under the flap and pulled the paper free while standing at the counter, reading glasses hanging on the chain until the first sentence made him put them on. He would not have understood the legal phrasing right away because nobody understood legal phrasing right away. He would have read it once, then slower the second time. Maybe he would have called Linda out from the back. Maybe he would have said, in that flat voice he used when something was serious enough that volume would only waste time, that they'd finally put it in writing.

Nate did not remember the exact day in the original timeline. He had been fourteen. He remembered the demolition as a season, not a sequence, full of raised voices at night, bills on the kitchen table, his mother saying not in front of him when they both knew he was right there, and his father getting quieter. But here was the notice itself, the event flattened into paper. It was the day the thing entered the store, the moment the future stopped being rumor and became mail.

When he looked at the signature again, the question split in two: either Dominic had signed it because he believed in what the company was doing, or he had signed it because the job required signing it, and Nate did not know which possibility made him angrier. Being someone's son, eating his mother's food, keeping a framed photo of his girlfriend on the desk, none of that unsent the letter. Nate had seen enough of Dominic's humanity by now to know hatred did not stay clean under inspection. That did not change the line at the bottom of the page.

He put the letter down and touched the indentation in the paper where the pen had pressed hardest. There was a physical groove there. Dominic had leaned into the downstroke. Nate's fingertip fit it exactly.

The desktop tower under the desk let out a rough fan cough and kept going. Somewhere near reception a fluorescent tube flickered, steadied, flickered again. Outside the warehouse windows the parking lot had gone black except for the sodium light by the dumpster, which threw a long ugly orange stripe across the inside of the glass. Nate stayed bent over the file, both forearms on the desk, the letter between them like evidence in a trial nobody had asked him to attend.

He tried, because he was Nate and trying to think his way out of disaster was the only move he consistently had, to make the problem smaller by turning it back into process. Five months remained. Maybe that still meant something. Schedules slipped, permits hung, contractors missed dates, and city offices lost forms or sat on them. He knew this from tech support more than real estate: enough dependencies in a system and eventually one of them failed, tickets languished, approvals stalled, whole launches got pushed because one guy in another department never answered an email.

Then he flipped back through the file and the fantasy died fast. Rezoning in April, notices in June, contractor retained, insurance certificates filed, utility coordination already moving. Too many names in the margins, too many tasks already assigned, too many parts of the machine turning without him. Just dragging his feet was not going to save Lazar's Hardware. If he wanted to stop this, he had to figure out where the leverage actually was, and fast.

He went back to the schedule sheet and read it from the bottom up: demolition, mobilization, fencing, final inspection, utility shutoffs, completed notifications. No line for change of heart. The monitor said Monday, August 15, 2005. The page said Wednesday, December 28, 2005, and every step between those dates already had somebody's name next to it.
