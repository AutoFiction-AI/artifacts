# Chapter 9

For four days after watching Mort carry the coffee back across the parking lot, Nate turned Eastbridge Plaza into flash cards.

He had the project file open on Dominic's desk, a yellow legal pad beside it, and three stacks of printouts he had no business pretending to understand: pages from the Connecticut Department of Environmental Protection website, a lender memo copied from somebody else's binder, and a zoning packet with so much highlighted on it the yellow stopped meaning anything. He'd written phrases in block letters to make them stick. PHASE II. REMEDIATION ESCROW. APPEAL WINDOW. CARRYING COSTS. He'd drawn arrows between them like the arrows would supply the logic.

This was not that different from how he'd survived tech support, if he was being honest. The system would break, he would search the knowledge base, skim five threads from people smarter than him, memorize a few nouns, and then say them back to the customer in a tone that implied sequence and control. Usually that worked because the person on the phone was some regional sales manager who could barely restart a printer without calling IT. Victor Kassos was not a guy with a printer problem. Victor had built the thing Nate was trying to jam a screwdriver into.

He knew that. He kept going anyway.

Late September had started to cool off, but the office never changed with the weather. The fluorescent lights flattened everything into the same season: carpet tile, beige partitions, the dry breath of the air-conditioning, desktop towers giving off steady heat under every desk. When Nate came in early the place felt less like a company than a server room with chairs. The only sound was the hum of computers and, farther off, the cough-and-burble of the break-room coffee maker trying to become coffee.

He flipped back to the tenant list.

MORTON LAZAR, LAZAR'S HARDWARE, UNIT 7.

He had seen the line so many times that it no longer hit all at once. The first impact had happened weeks ago. Now it came in pieces: the hardware store, the end unit, the hand-painted sign. Mort at Gail's last week, stirring powdered creamer into diner coffee and telling a stranger in Dominic Altieri's body that some developer named Altieri had signed the letter. Nobody asked us, Mort had said. He had said it without heat, and the flatness made it worse. It wasn't anger. It wasn't even surprise anymore. It was just the voice of a man who had already started making room in his head for getting screwed.

Nate put the heel of his hand on the page and held it there until the paper warmed.

He could not sit at Dominic's desk for three more months, open the file when people asked, and then watch the building come down on schedule. He could not keep eating turkey clubs at Gail's and nodding along to gossip from people whose livelihoods were already in a demolition budget. He had done the math a dozen times and it always came back to the same thing: the only lever available to him was inside Kassos Development, so if he was going to pull anything, he had to pull it here.

The problem was that Dominic had known how the machine worked and Nate knew where the switches were painted on the outside.

He read through the environmental section again. The strip mall had a dry cleaner. Dry cleaners meant chemicals. Chemicals meant contamination. Contamination meant delay. That much felt intuitive, the way fake competence always started from something intuitive. He had found a report in the file from a firm called Trinity Environmental and half understood it. There had been testing. There had been language about historical use, subsurface vapor, mitigation recommendations. There was a line about no further action required contingent on remedial compliance, which sounded to Nate like either closure or a trap depending on the day. He wrote PERC RISK? in the margin and boxed it.

Then there were the permits. If the city had approved rezoning in April, maybe the city could revisit it. Cities revisited things all the time, didn't they? There were hearings, complaints, neighborhood groups. Somebody could claim traffic impact, parking, noise, wetlands, asbestos, something. He did not know which of those counted as real and which belonged to late-night message boards full of guys furious about condos.

Then there was the financing. There had to be a financial case against moving fast. A delay cost money, sure, but demolition cost money too. Tenants still paid rent. Lazar's Hardware and Gail's and everybody else were income on paper. It seemed possible that he could talk about risk in a general enough way that Victor would hear prudence instead of panic. If he could get the project paused for sixty days, thirty even, maybe that led to something else. Maybe he found another way in those thirty days. Maybe some bureaucratic fungus grew on the file and slowed the whole thing down.

He knew how thin that sounded. He kept sanding it in his head until it took on the shape of argument.

At eight-thirty Ray came in carrying his travel mug and the little plastic cup of pineapple he brought every morning because he was always, in some vague ongoing way, trying to eat better. By ten he'd usually be leaning over somebody's desk stealing Doritos, but the pineapple was part of the ritual. He set both down, shrugged off his jacket, and looked over at Nate's desk.

"You were in before me?" Ray said. "That's sick."

"Couldn't sleep."

"Yeah? You look like it."

Ray sat, peeled the lid off the pineapple, and speared one cube with a plastic fork. He was tall enough that even sitting down he looked cramped by office furniture. The summer tan had gone out of his face. There were pale half-moons under his eyes. Everybody here looked tired in a slightly different professional font.

Nate kept scanning his notes as if he were still reading them and not just letting his eyes move. "Victor around?"

"He's got a call till nine-thirty, I think." Ray chewed and swallowed. "Why?"

"Wanted to run something by him. Eastbridge."

Ray nodded once, not especially interested yet. "He'll probably have a hole after the lender call. If you want, I'll mention you're looking for him."

"No, I'll just catch him."

"Suit yourself."

Ray swiveled to his computer. Outlook chimed to life on both their screens. Nate watched Ray log in with the speed of habit, windows opening in an order that made sense to someone who belonged here. The screen in front of Nate looked like a stolen dashboard. He knew where the pedals were by now. He still didn't know the engine.

He spent the next forty minutes arranging and rearranging the same six points. He crossed out whole sentences because they sounded too much like him and not enough like Dominic. He wrote them again in a more measured tone, then crossed those out because they sounded like a college kid bluffing in a seminar. He tried saying one of the lines under his breath.

"I think we may want to revisit environmental exposure before we commit to demolition sequencing."

It sounded like a man wearing his father's suit to court.

He stood up and took the pad into the conference room. The room smelled faintly of dry-erase marker and the deli mustard from yesterday's lunch meeting. Somebody had left a legal box on one chair full of sales brochures for a Stamford condo project. Nate shut the door and tried again.

"I'm just looking at Eastbridge and wondering if we're underestimating a couple things."

It was better, and less robotic. It was still not Dominic, because Dominic would have known the couple things.

He pictured Mort's hand around the coffee cup at Gail's. The cracked thumbnail. The burnished gray of ground-in metal dust along the creases of the knuckles. He pictured the strip mall parking lot in that dead in-between hour after lunch, the barber with a cigarette out back, Cesar stacking Poland Spring cases with his radio on, Gail wiping down a counter that had been wiped down every day for twenty years and still held the shine of a thousand elbows. Victor saw square footage, abatements, financing commitments. Nate saw all of that now plus the specific place where Mort had stood two stools down from him and not known his own son.

He went back to his desk before he could spook himself out of it.

At nine-forty Victor's office door opened. Victor came out still holding a cordless phone, suit jacket off, shirtsleeves immaculate anyway. He ended the call with an easy laugh Nate couldn't imagine being able to produce on command and spotted him immediately.

"Dom. Give me two minutes."

Victor disappeared back inside. Nate's stomach did a small ugly drop, not dramatic enough to be called anything but very physical, like missing the last stair in the dark. He stood, took the legal pad and the Eastbridge file, then sat back down because standing there like a kid outside the principal's office felt worse. When Victor waved him in again, Nate rose so fast his chair clipped the desk.

Victor's office was warmer than the rest of the floor. It was not cozy, just deliberately warmer, as if he had negotiated a private climate. A clementine sat half-peeled on a cocktail napkin beside an open binder, the bright torn skin releasing a clean citrus smell that cut through the office coffee. There was a framed photo of Victor on a boat with two teenage girls in Yankee caps, and a tiny bronze turtle on the corner of the desk that looked rubbed at the shell from being picked up often.

"Close the door," Victor said. "Sit."

Nate sat.

Victor finished peeling the clementine in one neat spiral, dropped the peel onto the napkin, and looked at the file in Nate's hands. "Eastbridge."

"Yeah."

"Good. I've got Dreher's guy coming in this afternoon and I'd rather know if we're missing anything before he starts pretending he invented concrete." Victor smiled and separated an orange segment with his thumbnail. "What do you have?"

It was almost enough to make Nate believe he belonged there. The easy invitation. The assumption that they were already on the same side of the page. He opened the file on his lap because putting it on Victor's desk felt like an admission he needed the text in front of him.

"I was looking through the environmental stuff again," he said. "And I think we might want to slow down before we lock demolition. Just because of the dry cleaner."

Victor waited.

"There's contamination exposure there. Potentially. Perc. Solvent migration, maybe. If DEP gets interested late, or if a lender gets nervous, we could be pushing a site issue into a worse position than it needs to be. So I was thinking maybe it makes sense to hold till we have a cleaner review."

Victor nodded once, not agreement, just motion. "What review do you think we don't have?"

Nate had expected resistance, not that question. "A fuller one."

"Meaning?"

"A more thorough environmental look."

Victor held the orange segment in the air for a second, then ate it. He chewed, swallowed, dabbed his fingertips on the napkin. "We did the Phase One in February. We did the targeted subsurface in March because the dry cleaner scared the lender, which you know because you were on that call. Trinity wrote the memo in April. Remediation reserve is already in the budget. Lender signed off in May. So when you say cleaner review, what exactly are you asking for?"

Nate felt the legal pad softening under his palm. "I'm saying if we reopen it now on our terms, that's better than having it reopened later by somebody else."

"By whom?"

"The state."

"Which office in the state?"

Nate looked at Victor.

Victor didn't rescue him. Something in the room changed by a degree, small enough that if Nate had not been watching for danger he might have missed it. Victor was still pleasant. He was also no longer helping the sentence happen.

"DEP," Nate said finally.

"DEP already signed off on the remediation protocol. Again. You know that." Victor reached for the file, and Nate had to hand it over like a student surrendering a test. Victor flipped directly to a tab halfway back, not looking for it, just arriving. "Page seventy-two. This letter. That's the state telling us to proceed as long as the vapor barrier gets installed before foundation work. Which it will, because it's in O'Rourke's scope and I enjoy paying people to do the job we hired them to do."

He slid the page over. Nate saw the letterhead, the dense paragraph block, the signature he had skimmed and not retained.

"Okay," Nate said, because there was nothing else to say that didn't sound worse.

Victor leaned back. "Okay. So it's not environmental. What is it?"

Under all the fake nouns he'd brought in with him sat the real thing, swollen and unsayable: his father's store, Gail's, the barber, the whole doomed little strip of people Victor could turn into carrying costs. He had not walked in planning to say any of that, which was good, because even thinking it inside Dominic's face felt insane.

"I just think," he said, starting over, "we're moving fast on a fully occupied center."

"We are."

"And if we pushed the demolition date a quarter, maybe two, we'd keep rent rolling while we close up a few loose ends. Optics too. These are small businesses. Christmas week is rough timing."

Victor smiled a little then, not mean, not kind either. "You think we're demolishing a beloved village green."

"That's not what I said."

"No, but it's what you mean." Victor folded his hands. "A quarter costs us six figures in carry alone, before contractor rescheduling, before lender extension fees, before the anchor tenant asks whether we're capable of reading a calendar. The center is fully occupied because we've been collecting rent while entitlement ran. That's the only virtue of a strip mall this tired. You don't leave money on the table during predevelopment and then get sentimental when it's time to clear the site."

Nate heard the words six figures and anchor tenant and felt his own argument flatten like wet cardboard.

"I'm not talking about sentiment," he said.

"No?"

"No. I'm talking about risk."

"Then talk to me about risk."

Victor said it lightly, generously even. Nate felt his stomach drop at the nice tone. Victor wasn't shutting him down. He was inviting him further into the hole.

Nate tried anyway. "Tenant pushback. Local press. If people make noise about relocation or notice or-" He heard himself reaching. "-or if someone says the process was rushed-"

"The process was rushed?" Victor said. "Dom, they got notice in June. We have signed acknowledgments in the file. We offered relocation stipends to the tenants we thought might take them. Most didn't, because most people don't move until they are moved. Council approved the rezoning in April. Appeal period expired months ago. What process are you imagining gets unrushed by waiting?"

Nate stared at him.

Victor picked up another orange segment. "If this is about the diner, Gail had options in July. She didn't want them. If it's about Lazar, he was never moving that store voluntarily. He was going to die behind that counter if someone let him. Which, to be clear, I respect. But respect doesn't finance a project."

The mention of Lazar hit Nate so fast his mouth moved before he had anything ready to put in it.

"Maybe he's got a point."

Victor's hand stopped halfway to his mouth.

For a second the room held still around the little bright piece of fruit between his fingers.

"About what?" Victor asked.

Nate knew he should back off. The right move was obvious: nod, let it die, get out. He made the wrong move instead.

"About the notice. About the time people get. Six months on paper isn't six months in real life. You know, if somebody's been somewhere sixteen years-"

"This isn't coming from Lazar."

"No."

"Then where is it coming from?"

Nate's mind opened to a white blank square.

Victor waited. He did not blink much. Nate noticed that now. He noticed the warmth had not left the room but had withdrawn from the conversation, leaving the temperature intact and nothing else.

"I've been looking at it more closely," Nate said. "That's all."

"You've been looking at Eastbridge more closely for two months. It is your file." Victor set the orange down untouched. "So let's do this clean. What changed?"

Nate heard the blood in his ears. It wasn't a roar. It was more like the hiss of fluorescent lights when you notice them too hard.

"Nothing changed. I just think maybe we're exposed in ways we don't need to be."

"On environmental, no. On process, no. On financing, definitely no, unless you want to explain to Levinson why we pushed the schedule after locking the demolition contract." Victor tapped the file once. "Are you worried about a specific tenant?"

Nate said nothing.

Victor tilted his head. "Dom."

Nate had crammed the file, the memos, the state letter, the rezoning packet, all of it, until the words sat in his head like copied code snippets from a forum post. They looked like function. They did not run. He knew that now with complete humiliating clarity. He had walked into a room with a stack of keywords and mistaken them for knowledge. There was no graceful way out of that once somebody started asking follow-ups.

"Look," he said, because people said look right before collapsing. "If we know the dry cleaner parcel has contamination and we know the tenants are going to scream and we know the timing is bad, I don't get why we wouldn't at least consider a short delay."

Victor's expression barely changed. If he'd laughed, Nate could have hated him cleanly. Instead Victor looked like a man discovering a fault in a structure he'd trusted.

"Contamination's accounted for," Victor said.

The part about tenants got a small lift of one shoulder, nothing more.

"And the timing isn't bad. It's the schedule. We move it now, we pay for it. We move it after locking demo, you can explain that to Levinson."

Nate looked down at the file.

Victor pressed the intercom button on his phone. "Can you send Ray in for a second?"

The floor dropped another inch.

He had a second to think maybe Victor was bringing Ray in for some unrelated reason. Then Ray knocked and stepped inside, travel mug still in hand.

"Everything okay?" Ray said.

"Sure," Victor said. "Dom's raising concerns about Eastbridge timing. I just want to make sure I'm not missing something on the lender side. If we push demo into Q1, what's that do to us?"

Ray glanced at Nate. It was not a long look, but it was enough. Nate saw the moment Ray understood he was being asked to stand in the gap Nate had opened.

"It screws the extension," Ray said. "Levinson built release schedule off December. If we slide past year-end, we're paying for the privilege and giving Fairfield an excuse to start re-trading."

Victor nodded. "And on environmental, the dry cleaner?"

"Already reserved. Unless Trinity missed something huge, which they didn't." Ray shifted the mug from one hand to the other. "Why? Did something come back?"

"No," Victor said. "That's what I was clarifying."

Ray looked at Nate again, harder this time. "No."

Victor turned back to Nate. "Anything else I should know?"

The humiliation was so total it went almost abstract for a second. Nate could see himself from outside it: thirty-four years old in his own vanished life, thirty-one in this stolen body, sitting in a warm office with an orange on the desk while two men who actually did this work spoke over and around his bluff. He thought, with the flatness of real embarrassment, that he had managed to be bad at office politics in two separate timelines.

"No," he said.

"Okay." Victor's tone brightened a fraction, which was worse than if it hadn't. "Thanks, Ray."

Ray didn't move right away. He waited one beat, as if Nate might salvage something, then left.

Victor let the silence sit there long enough to stop being an accident.

"Are you feeling all right, Dom?"

The question sat on the desk between the folded orange peel and the bronze turtle, small and impossible to step around.

Nate looked up.

Victor's face had gone still in a way Nate had only glimpsed before, beneath the jokes and the stories and the handshakes and the everybody's-family bullshit. He was not asking out of kindness. Or maybe he was asking out of a kind of kindness, the kind that assessed damage before deciding whether it could still be used.

"I'm fine," Nate said.

"You don't look fine."

"I didn't sleep much."

"One night?"

"A few."

Victor nodded as if this were useful data. "Is something going on at home?"

Nate almost laughed and wondered which home Victor meant.

"No. Nothing major."

"Tommy?"

"He's Tommy."

That got the smallest exhale from Victor, not quite amusement. "That he is." He folded the napkin over the orange peel with tidy fingers. "Listen to me. You have always been good on Eastbridge because you don't confuse noise with leverage. The tenants can yell. Gail can call the paper. Lazar can glare at the moon. None of that changes what the city approved, what the lender funded, or what the contractor is scheduled to do. Your job is to know the difference. If you're losing that distinction because you're tired or distracted, take a day and get yourself straight."

Nate said nothing.

Victor kept going in the same mild voice. "What you cannot do is walk into my office with half-formed concerns and no paper under them. That wastes my time and it puts you in a bad position, which I don't like to see. If there's a real issue, bring it to me once. Bring it complete. You understand?"

"Yeah."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

Victor held his eyes another second, then let him go. "Good. Go get some coffee that doesn't taste like old socks and try again tomorrow."

The audience had ended. Nate stood, took the file because nobody told him not to, and found that his legs worked normally, which felt insulting. He'd wanted at least the dignity of visible collapse. Instead he walked out of Victor's office at an ordinary speed and closed the door with his hand steady on the knob.

The open floor hit him all at once with the phones, the keyboards, someone laughing by the copier, a printer spitting out a packet three desks over. Nobody was looking at him, which made it worse because it meant the disaster had not been grand enough to justify itself as spectacle. It had just been professional incompetence, the kind people absorbed and then filed under concern.

He sat at Dominic's desk and opened the file again because he didn't know what else to do.

Page seventy-two held the state letter in cold black type, exactly where Victor's thumb had landed. He read it, and the language stayed dead on the page. He turned to the budget tab and found the remediation reserve, an amount circled in Dominic's neat handwriting from months ago. He had seen the number before. He remembered seeing it. He had just not known what it meant, which turned out to be a more dangerous kind of ignorance than not seeing it at all.

Across the aisle Ray was typing with the short hard keystrokes of a man pretending not to be pissed off yet. Nate stared at his own notes.

PERC RISK?
PRESS?
Q1 DELAY?

They looked pathetic now, the notebook of a kid trying to reverse-engineer adulthood from overheard conversation. He tore the top page off the pad, folded it once, then again, and kept folding until it turned into a damp little brick in his hand.

He had imagined, stupidly, that the truth of the strip mall might have force in itself. That if he carried enough of it into Victor's office, if he just looked hard enough at the people under the paperwork, the paperwork might blink first. Instead the truth sat where it had always sat: on one side of the ledger as a cost somebody else would pay.

At eleven-ten his phone rang and he nearly jumped out of his skin. It was a contractor asking about a Fairfield change order Nate knew how to route but not how to discuss. He transferred the call to voicemail, wrote down the man's name, and stared at the receiver after he hung up like it had personally insulted him.

The morning kept happening around him. He had gone into Victor's office thinking he was forcing a turning point and come out in time for ordinary Tuesday bullshit.

At noon he stood up to get coffee and saw Ray already waiting by the break room door.

Ray didn't say anything. He just jerked his chin toward the stairwell.

Nate followed him.

* * *

The stairwell smelled like concrete dust and stale cigarette smoke dragged in on people's clothes. Somebody had stubbed out a Marlboro on the outside windowsill and left the butt there, damp from last night's rain. Ray let the door swing shut behind them and turned around.

"What the hell was that?"

He didn't raise his voice. He didn't need to. The concrete carried everything.

Nate leaned against the wall because standing in the middle of the landing felt too much like being inspected. "I was trying to slow it down."

"No, I heard that part." Ray looked at him for a beat. "I'm asking what you were doing."

Nate watched a drop of old coffee slide down the outside of Ray's mug and darken the paper sleeve. "Eastbridge is a mess."

"Every project is a mess."

"This one more than most."

"Okay. Then bring something real."

Nate said nothing.

Ray took a breath through his nose and let it out hard. He looked tired in the stripped-down stairwell light, less office-ready, more like a guy in his thirties with a mortgage and not enough sleep. Up close Nate could see he'd nicked himself shaving under the jaw and stuck a piece of toilet paper on it that he must have forgotten to remove. It made the anger feel more personal, less theatrical. Ray had not put on a face for this. He had just come in after him.

"Victor asked me last month if you were okay," Ray said.

Nate looked up.

"Not like just now. Casual. He said you seemed off. I told him you were tired, maybe stuff at home, whatever. I covered it because you've been weird, but weird I can work with." Ray tightened his grip on the mug. "That in there? That's not weird. That's you walking in on your own file sounding like you read three articles in a dentist's office."

"I know," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yeah."

Ray waited, pointedly, the way he always did when he thought the next thing should come without being dragged out. The silence widened between them. Fill it, some small sane part of him said. Say you're burned out. Say Tommy's a disaster. Say Sarah thinks you're acting strange. Say something a normal man could conceivably say to a coworker in a stairwell. Instead he stood there with Victor's orange smell still stuck in his nose.

"I haven't really had my head on straight," he said.

Ray shut his eyes for a second. "Jesus Christ."

"What?"

"Nothing. That's just not a sentence you say when you're about to ask somebody to trust you on a live deal."

"I didn't ask you to trust me."

"No, you kind of did. In front of Victor."

Nate rubbed the folded paper brick in his pocket until the edges started to give. "I thought maybe if I got him thinking about exposure-"

"Exposure to what?" Ray cut in. "You kept saying risk like risk is magic. Risk from who? The tenants? Gail? Mort Lazar calling Channel Eight? Come on, man."

The name hit again.

Ray saw something in his face and kept going, not softer exactly but less purely pissed. "If you've got a specific thing, that's different. If Trinity missed contamination, say that. If the lender changed position, say that. If Dreher's office is getting cold feet, say that. But you can't walk in there with vibes."

Vibes. Nate almost laughed. The machine in 2025, the AI, the whole rotten architecture of his life. He pressed his molars together.

"I know," he said again.

"Then what's happening?"

Nate looked past him at the wire-mesh window in the stairwell door, the slice of parking lot visible through it, gray sky pressing down on hoods and windshields. He had lived inside Dominic's life for a little over two months and still every direct question felt like an electrical panel with the cover off. Too many exposed things close together.

"I'm just not-" He stopped. Started again. "I'm not as on top of this as I should be."

It was small and true. The kind of truth he could sometimes get away with because it bled enough honesty to distract from the larger lie.

Ray frowned. "No shit."

Nate let that sit.

Ray shifted his weight. "Is it Sarah?"

"No."

"Tommy?"

"No."

"Are you sick?"

"No."

"Using?"

Nate looked at him. "What?"

"I'm asking."

"No."

Ray nodded once, like crossing items off a list he wished he didn't need. "Okay. Then here's where I'm at. I don't care if you're having a midlife thing or family stuff or whatever else. I actually don't. But if Victor thinks you forgot how to read your own file, that's not just your problem. It splashes. You get that, right?"

Nate did get it. His face went hot anyway. Ray was not being noble here. He was being a guy trying to keep his boss from deciding the floor had gone soft under him too.

"Yeah," Nate said.

"And I like you. You know that. I don't want to watch you eat shit in there."

Nate almost said, Too late. Instead he asked, because this was what he always did when cornered, "What would you do?"

Ray looked mildly offended by the question. "I wouldn't have gone in there with nothing."

"No, I mean now."

Ray blew out a breath. "Now? You shut up for a couple weeks. You do the work in front of you. You answer emails. You don't freelance policy. If you're fried, take Friday, sleep for twelve hours, tell Sarah to hide your phone, whatever. Then come back and act like you know what you've been doing since March."

Since March. Dominic had known what he'd been doing since March. Dominic was ash in a timeline Nate could not even point to. Nate's silence must have read as resistance, because Ray's face hardened again.

"I'm serious," Ray said. "Victor likes you. That buys a lot until it doesn't. Don't make me explain you twice."

The toilet-paper square under his jaw had come loose at one corner. He peeled it off, looked at the tiny spot of dried blood on it, and balled it into the paper sleeve from the mug. It was such a tired little gesture that Nate felt suddenly, irrationally, guilty toward him.

"I won't," Nate said.

Ray studied him. "You want me on Eastbridge calls for a while?"

The offer sat there between them, practical and humiliating at the same time. It was help, but it was help that admitted Ray no longer trusted him to keep his footing.

Nate should have said yes. Take the help, keep your head down, stop making it worse. Instead Dominic's pride or Nate's panic or just plain stupidity got there first.

"No. I'm okay."

Ray gave him a look that translated cleanly to sure you are. "Right."

He opened the stairwell door, then turned back once more.

"Eat something," he said. "You look like hell."

Then he was gone.

Nate stayed where he was another minute, listening to the door settle back into its frame. He could hear office noise on the other side in muffled bursts, as if the company were happening underwater. He took the folded page out of his pocket and opened it. The paper had gone soft with sweat, the marker bleeding through where he'd boxed PERC RISK? He ripped it in half, then again, and dropped the pieces into the stairwell trash.

It didn't help.

When he got back to his desk, the Eastbridge file looked less like a weapon and more like the kind of adult object that kept revealing how far outside adulthood he actually was. It was not because he couldn't read, and it was not because he was stupid. There were whole worlds of knowing embedded in it that had to be lived at desk height, on calls, in meetings, through a hundred boring repetitions, until words like reserve and sequence and entitlement stopped being words and became reflex. Dominic had had that reflex. Nate had a grudge and a search habit.

He made himself work through the afternoon anyway. He returned the contractor's call and limited himself to saying he'd pull the change order and get back to him. He found the change order after ten minutes of panicked searching through folders. He answered two emails by cannibalizing old language from old project threads and half-finished templates. At three-thirty Victor walked across the floor with a client and did not look at him. The omission bit harder than another confrontation would have. It meant Victor had already decided what to do with the information for now: watch.

By five-fifteen the office had thinned out. Ray left without stopping at Nate's desk. He lifted two fingers in a half-wave on the way to the elevator and kept moving. It wasn't broken, exactly. It was strained, with the thread still there and pulled taut enough to sing.

Nate sat another ten minutes after everyone he could see was gone. The building made settling noises around him. A copier door slammed somewhere deep in the floor. The air-conditioning clicked down a notch for evening. He picked up the Eastbridge file and slid it back into the drawer. For one stupid second he imagined locking it there like that changed anything.

Outside, the parking lot smelled faintly of wet leaves and old oil. The sky had gone early-autumn flat, not dark yet but drained. He got into the Civic and set the file on the passenger seat before realizing he didn't need it at home and shoving it onto the floorboard. The radio came on to an ads-and-static mix he shut off immediately.

He drove the ten minutes to the apartment with both hands on the wheel and no idea what he was going to say if Sarah asked him anything direct.

By the time he got upstairs, he had narrowed his possible responses to three: Fine. Long day. Victor's an asshole.

Only one of those sounded like Dominic. Only one of them also sounded like a man who could get through dinner without having to manufacture a coherent version of himself in real time.

The apartment smelled like garlic and soy sauce and the faint dusty warmth of the radiator that hadn't quite come on yet but would soon. Sarah was at the kitchen table with a stack of blue books and a grocery-store rotisserie chicken pulled apart onto a plate beside her. She had one shoe off and the bare foot curled around the rung of the chair while she wrote comments in the margin of a student's essay. Her hair was up in a loose ponytail that had started to come apart, and there was a streak of green ink on the side of her hand where she'd brushed against something without noticing.

She looked up when he came in. "Hey."

"Hey."

"There's rice in the pot if you want to start eating. I had a kid today argue that the Missouri Compromise proved Jefferson believed in compromise as a moral principle, which is almost impressive. How was your day?"

"Fine."

Sarah looked at him for half a second longer than the word deserved.

"Okay," she said.

Then her eyes went back to the essay. No second question, no what happened, no teasing follow-up about whether fine meant actually fine or work-fine or family-fine. She just said okay and returned to grading.

The silence that followed had shape. It spread into the room and sat down with them.

Nate set his keys in the dish by the counter and stood there, not moving toward the rice, not taking off his jacket. Usually Sarah's talking filled the apartment in a loose practical way, with student stories, Renata calling, something Tommy had done, something on the radio. Tonight there was only the scrape of her pen, the little click of the kitchen clock, the soft wet tear of chicken skin when she pulled off another piece and set it on the plate without looking.

He took his jacket off finally and hung it on the back of the chair across from her. "How many of those?"

"Twenty-seven."

"That's rough."

"Yeah."

Another silence settled between them.

He got a bowl from the cabinet. The rice had gone a little gummy in the pot, clumping against the sides. He spooned some out anyway. Sarah stood, crossed to the stove, and turned the burner under a pan of bok choy back on.

"You want this?" she asked.

"Sure."

"Okay."

Her voice wasn't cold. That would have been easier. Cold had edges. This was attentive restraint. The kind that said she had noticed something and was deciding not to help him with it.

He sat. She put bok choy on his plate, then sat again and returned to grading. One of the essays made her exhale through her nose.

"This kid used 'therefore' three times in one paragraph," she said. "He thinks it's a magic word."

Nate gave a short sound that could have been a laugh if it had made it all the way out.

"I know," Sarah said, still looking at the page. "I should be supportive. He's fifteen."

"No, that's fair."

"Mm."

The apartment had never felt large, but tonight the little one-bedroom seemed to have extra corners in it, places for all the unasked things to stand and wait. Nate looked at Sarah's hand moving over the blue book, firm and economical. She did not doodle in margins or make decorative marks. She circled, underlined, wrote exactly what she meant. When she turned a page she smoothed it flat with the side of her hand first, a habit so ordinary it would have vanished if he had not been watching her like a man trying to learn a language from kitchen noise.

He took a bite of rice and realized he wasn't hungry. The food sat in his mouth like packing material.

"You okay?" he asked, because he couldn't stand the asymmetry of being the one under observation.

Sarah looked up.

He heard the move as he made it, old and automatic. Ask about the other person, turn the light, make concern into a tunnel out of the room.

"I'm grading sophomores," she said. "So no, not spiritually. But functionally I'm fine."

"Right."

She held his eyes another second, then nodded toward his plate. "Eat before it gets cold."

He did.

The silence did not break so much as thin out around practical things. She asked him to pass the soy sauce. He asked if she wanted more rice. She said no. The fridge kicked on. Someone in the hall laughed and kept walking. At some point Sarah moved from the table to the couch with the remaining essays and tucked one foot under herself, still grading, still not pushing. The restraint felt deliberate enough that Nate wondered if this was how she handled students on the edge of lying: give them room, let them hear themselves in it.

He carried his bowl to the sink. Water ran over the rice and turned cloudy.

"You don't have school stuff tomorrow morning, right?" Sarah asked from the couch.

"No."

"Can you take the recycling down if you go out before me?"

"Yeah."

"Thanks."

Nothing else followed.

He stood at the sink with his hands in the dishwater longer than necessary. The apartment window over the counter showed a slice of parking lot and one tree starting to turn, a few leaves gone red at the edges like the color had been testing itself there first. Somewhere below, a car door slammed. The sound carried up and went flat against the glass.

If she had asked, he might have lied better. Or worse. He might have said Victor was on him about Eastbridge, which was true enough to start trouble and false in all the ways that mattered. He might have said he was tired. Instead she let the one word he'd chosen sit where he'd dropped it and went back to grading.

He dried the bowl and put it away.

Sarah was reading now, green pen tucked behind one ear, the graded stack on the coffee table beside her. The lamp threw a soft cone of light over the page and left the rest of the room dim. She looked comfortable there, familiar, like a woman in her own home at the end of a long day. He had entered that home by killing the man she had built it with. On bad nights the fact sat in him as a hard moral statement. Tonight it felt more mechanical and more sickening than that, like a misrouted signal that had overwritten the correct one and then kept propagating through everything connected to it.

Sarah glanced up. "You're hovering."

"Sorry."

"It's okay. Just either sit down or keep moving. You're making me nervous."

"Right."

He sat at the far end of the couch.

She went back to reading. After a minute she held out her hand without looking. "Can you grab me the water?"

He passed her the glass from the coffee table. Their fingers touched for a second. It was ordinary contact, the sort of thing that had become both routine and unbearable.

"Thanks," she said.

"Yeah."

At work Victor had gone watchful. Ray had started checking his answers. Tomorrow he'd go back to Dominic's desk and do what Ray had told him to do: answer emails, keep his mouth shut. Here Sarah just kept reading, green pen moving in short exact strokes, not rescuing him and not letting him off either.

Nate looked at the space she left between them and tried to build a sentence that could survive the trip across it. Nothing made it out.

Sarah turned another page.
