# Chapter 2

The trauma room had no room in it.

Bay One held a boy no older than ten with restraint foam dried white at the corners of his mouth and one shoe missing. Bay Two held an old man on oxygen, his chest lifting shallow and fast under a paper gown that had gone damp clear through. In Bay Three a woman from lane six kept pressure on her scalp with a wad of towels that had once been white and now looked like weak tea. The floor drain under the sink burped pink water every time somebody rinsed an instrument tray. Summer heat had made the plastic mattress covers tacky. The overhead lights buzzed hard enough to feel in the back teeth.

Marisol stopped in the doorway and took stock the way she always did: what still moved, what could be cleared, what would become a report if it got any worse. A trauma-room runner named Celia Velez was counting sterile packs under her breath, tapping each one with a fingernail striped blue from cheap polish she had not had time to remove. Celia had wrapped pink kinesiology tape around her left wrist, and the tape had gone gray at the edges from powder and sweat.

"Not now," Celia said without looking up. "If this is about another heat complaint, they can sweat where they're standing."

"Case 4471-R," Marisol said. "I need him in here."

Celia looked up then. "You and everybody else."

Marisol stepped farther into the room. The air hit her face cold from the wall unit nearest the oxygen bay, but the cold never reached the hall. It only sat in this room with the disinfectant and the old iron smell, pretending there was a line somewhere between treatment and transport. She could hear the intake speakers outside repeating lane assignments in two languages, bright synthetic politeness over the drag of boots and the wet coughs from holding.

"Upper-arm restraint bruising, right rib side, guarding when he twists," she said. "If lane three crushed something and he decompensates after seal, it lands in your log and mine."

Celia gave her a long look. "You saw guarding or you want guarding."

Marisol did not answer that. She did not have time to make it sound less personal than it was. Gabriel was still in the loading lanes under somebody else's country code. Every minute he stayed there the car got closer to sealed, the file got closer to hard. Quiet corrections happened before steel doors shut and a train became timetable instead of yard.

"Clear me a table," she said.

"I don't have a table."

"Then clear me a wall and a tray."

Celia's mouth tightened. It was not refusal. It was arithmetic: who got seen, who waited, which body might still be walking when the southbound cars started to move. She jerked her chin at the rolling stainless cart by the sink. "Three minutes, maybe four. If he vomits blood I need to know whether it was before or after you got sentimental."

Marisol let that pass. The word sentimental had no business in the room, but she had already spent the authority that would have let her challenge it cleanly. She swiped her supervisor passcard over the trauma-room reader and felt the lock click. The system would show she had opened a medical bay against intake load. It was another trace in the system, another reason somebody could later ask why.

In lane three the air was ten degrees hotter. White paint on the car wall had blistered along the seam beside the holding bench, and the rust underneath showed through in a line as fine as a split lip. Officer Baca stood at the gate with a short-link restraint line hooked to one fist and a clear property bag looped over the other wrist. He kept worrying the pale groove beneath his wedding ring with his thumb, turning the band half around and back every time the queue jammed. A nicotine patch had started to peel from the side of his neck.

Gabriel sat on the bench with three others waiting for final car assignment. His shoulders were rounded forward against the chain clipped at his waist, but his head was up. He had always done that when he was trying not to give somebody the satisfaction of seeing pain land. The scar through his left eyebrow cut white through sweat and dirt. His fingers were still dark with ink at the nails as if he had been writing until the hands came down on him.

Marisol took in the rest in pieces because if she took it all at once she would lose the use of her own face. The shirt was twisted half out of his waistband. One sleeve had stretched where somebody had jerked him by it. Fresh bruising spread under the cloth from the side of his ribs toward his back, a low dark bloom already turning the color of bad plums. Thumb marks sat high on both upper arms, distinct enough to count.

Baca saw where she was looking. "He's ambulatory."

"He's going to trauma," Marisol said.

"For what."

"For not cracking open in my car."

Baca gave a short laugh that was not amusement. "You're pulling triage for a bruise now?"

Gabriel rolled one shoulder against the chain as if testing how much room Baca had left him. The movement pulled at his side, but he let the pain show just long enough to tell her he knew exactly what she was doing.

"I'm fine," he said. His voice was rough from heat and holding, but it still had that dry edge in it, the one that used to come out when he was thirteen and already knew which adult in a room was full of shit. "Load somebody who still wants your help."

Marisol stepped in close before Baca could take that as settled. She put two fingers under the hem of Gabriel's shirt and lifted. Heat came off his skin. The bruise on the right side was worse up close, the shape wide and ugly, with a yellowing edge where an earlier hit had already started to age under the new one. She set her hand against the lower ribs and pressed.

He sucked air hard through his teeth. It was enough.

"Tender," she said. "Right side. Possible fracture, possible spleen. He's not loading until med clears him."

Gabriel stared at her with something almost like contemptuous admiration, as if he could see every tool she was reaching for and hated that she still knew how to use them.

Baca shifted his grip on the chain. "You palpated him."

"That's what hands are for."

"You want Celia to sign that?"

"I want you to explain to compliance why you sent a possible internal upstairs because dispatch was making you nervous."

The property bag knocked against Baca's thigh when he moved. Inside it lay the usual intake subtraction: belt, shoelaces, folded papers, a cheap pen snapped in half at the clip. Beside them sat a small black recorder with a cracked screen and a mesh speaker grilled silver. Yellow evidence tape crossed it in an X. A sticker label read `UNDECLARED DIGITAL STORAGE` above the printed alias `RAFAEL AVELAR`.

For one second the room narrowed to that object. It was not because the recorder looked expensive. It looked old, worn smooth at the edges by a thumb. But it had been handled carefully before the seizure. Somebody had wrapped one corner in clear tape to keep the casing from splitting. The kind of repair people made to something they could not afford to lose because what lived inside mattered more than the machine.

Gabriel saw her see it. The line of his jaw changed.

"Property stays with custody," Baca said.

"Then custody comes with him," Marisol said.

Celia pushed through the lane gate with a pulse clip and a rolling tray, muttering multiplication tables under her breath as she counted syringes. When she saw Gabriel's side she stopped muttering.

"Well," she said. "That's more than a bruise."

"Thank you," Marisol said.

Celia clipped the pulse sensor to Gabriel's finger. "Don't thank me yet. If he starts dropping pressure in transit, I get to be right in writing."

Baca did not want writing. Nobody on the line wanted writing unless it could be pointed at somebody lower. He blew air through his nose, peeled the chain from the bench ring, and jerked his head toward the trauma corridor.

"Four minutes," he said. "Then he's back in queue unless he opens up on your floor."

"Move," Marisol said.

Gabriel got to his feet slower than pride wanted and steadier than pain allowed. The short restraint chain ran from his waist to cuffed wrists, forcing a cramped half-balance that made every step look like obedience even when it was not. Marisol took the corridor side, Baca the wall side. Celia rolled the tray ahead of them. Intake noise thinned as they crossed the threshold into the medical wing and became smaller sounds: the squeak of the tray wheel with a flat spot, the air unit knocking once before it caught, a man behind one of the curtains begging somebody not to send his daughter back into the bright.

Gabriel's shoulder brushed Marisol's arm on the turn. He did not lean on her. He would have fallen first.

In the trauma room Celia cleared the stainless cart with one sweep of a forearm, sending a stack of chart sleeves skidding to the floor. Baca hung the property bag from the IV pole hook and kept his hand on the chain while Gabriel sat. A custody radio clipped high on Baca's vest kept coughing lane codes into his left ear, and every few seconds he half-turned toward the corridor to catch the words over the wall unit and the old man's oxygen hiss. Up close, the ink on Gabriel's fingers had bled into the cuticles. There was dried blood in one ear. His lower lip had been split inside, not out; she could tell by the way he kept tonguing one side of his mouth.

Celia cut the shirt seam with blunt scissors and peeled the cloth back. The bruise along the ribs had the wide flat shape of baton work, not a stumble, and the skin above it was abraded raw where the restraint strap had ridden up and sawed during transport from the holding cage.

"Jesus," Celia said softly.

"Don't say that like he's unusual," Gabriel said.

Celia met his eyes. "You're not unusual. That's the problem."

She ran practiced fingers along the rib edge, gentler than Marisol had been. Gabriel went still in a different way then, the stillness of somebody bracing not against the pain itself but against giving strangers the sound of it.

"Nothing floating," Celia said. "Maybe a hairline. Maybe just deep bruising. He can breathe?"

"Can you?" Marisol asked before she could stop herself.

Gabriel turned his head toward her. "I could before you put on a show."

Celia straightened. Her gaze moved once between them and settled nowhere. "I need compression wrap and the portable scan from Bay Two. If either of you touches that property bag, I didn't see it and I'll deny I even know how hooks work."

"Celia," Baca said.

"Then keep staring at it," she snapped. "I need the machine."

She shoved the tray against the wall and disappeared through the curtain into the next bay. Baca stayed at the door with one boot planted against the threshold and his head angled toward the corridor, thumbing the radio when it spat back another custody count. The wall unit rattled, the oxygen mask hissed, and anything said low in the room had to fight its way through both. The recorder hung between Marisol and Gabriel at eye level, slowly turning on the IV hook from the draft of the wall unit.

* * *

He watched it instead of her.

"You should have let the shirt stay on," he said.

Marisol folded the cut edge of fabric over once, because her hands needed a task before they did something stupid like reach for his face. "I needed you in here."

"You needed me out of sight."

"Same thing for four minutes."

He laughed once, a dry scrape of sound that hurt him enough to make him stop.

Marisol forced herself into the voice she used when blackwater alarms tripped and three crews started shouting at once. "Listen to me. I can flag the intake injury, reopen the custody note, put you in observation. If I can get your file back into yard review before they lock the car list, I may be able to unwind the receiving-state assignment."

Gabriel finally looked at her full on. Heat had hollowed his face, sharpened it. Underfed, yes, but more than that: stripped down to the things he would not surrender. Tom's mouth when cornered. Her own eyes when she had been young enough to believe anger could feed a person.

"You may," he said. "That's a beautiful plan."

"Don't start."

"You started. Back there, with the ribs."

Marisol kept her voice down because Baca was six feet away with his radio to his ear, and because if she let it rise she would hear the plea inside it. "If I leave you in queue, the car seals. Once it seals, I lose half the options."

"What options."

"Delay. Review. Recode. A hold long enough to put hands on the paperwork."

He tipped his chin toward the hanging property bag. "And that."

She followed the look though she did not need to. "I saw it."

"Did you."

"What's on it?"

For the first time something in him shifted beyond fury. His attention tightened on her, precise and unwilling, as if he were deciding whether she had earned even that answer.

"Voices," he said. "Forty-seven hours, if they did not wipe it already. Testimony, field notes, checkpoint sounds, berth calls, destination codes read wrong and then read again like saying them twice made them legal. A woman from Arroyo Norte listing everything they took out of her pockets because she knew nobody would log it honestly. Two brothers from the salt camps who could tell which car they were on by the pitch in the floor when the blackwater tank shifted. A man who got assigned to Santa Avelina because the officer liked the sound of his dead grandfather's surname. Things with dates on them. Things with names."

The recorder turned once on the hook, its yellow tape catching the fluorescent light.

Marisol heard herself ask, "Why were you carrying it here."

"Because that's where the trains are."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer you get."

She took one step closer. "Gabriel."

He flinched at the name the way people flinched from cold water, not because it hurt more than heat but because it made the skin honest. His eyes cut to Baca and back.

"Don't do that in here," he said quietly.

Marisol felt the chemical burn scar on the inside of her wrist go tight as she curled her hand. "Do what."

"Use my name like it buys you something."

The oxygen mask in Bay Two hissed. Somebody dropped a metal tray behind the curtain and swore. The room carried on being exactly what it was: a place where bodies were patched just enough to move on schedule.

"I'm trying to get you off that schedule," she said.

"Quietly."

"Yes. Quietly, if possible."

Gabriel lifted his cuffed hands a few inches and let the short chain strike the stainless cart. The clink cut through the oxygen hiss and the wall unit, a small deliberate sound that made the whole room feel like evidence.

"That's the whole religion here," he said at last. "Quietly. Quiet loading, quiet bruises, quiet mistakes. Quiet enough that the platform still smells like lemon and bleach by morning and everybody can call it transport."

Marisol swallowed whatever first answer came up because it would have been the wrong one, something defensive and small. "I keep people alive on that train."

"For transfer."

"For the night. For the next stop. Sometimes that matters."

"I know it matters. I'm not stupid." He nodded toward the drain in the floor. "That's why this works. They hit people in the lane, and then people like you know exactly how much blood counts as an incident and how much just goes down there with disinfectant."

The words landed harder because he said them without heat. He was not accusing for effect. He was keeping accounts.

Marisol heard the old line rise in her head anyway, the one she had lived by so long it had worn a groove through her thinking: somebody would do this work. Somebody would keep the cars from turning into sealed rot. Somebody would clean the foam, clear the drains, get water into the right hands, keep a child with fever from riding three counties with nobody looking in. She could have said all of it. She had said versions of it to herself for twenty years while scrubbing other people's terror off surfaces designed to show no residue.

She did not say it, because in this room with his shirt cut open and his recorder hanging in a property bag between them it sounded like what it was. A worker's justification grown smooth from overuse.

"I know what the line is," she said.

"Do you."

"Don't mistake me for them."

That brought the heat back into him. It sharpened his mouth.

"Why shouldn't I." He jerked his cuffed hands as far as the short chain allowed. "They leave marks on people. You make the marks transportable. That's not the same job, maybe, but it's the same route."

She took the blow and kept standing because there was nowhere to set it down. "You think I taught them how to hurt you."

"No." His smile then was brief and mean and exhausted. "I think you taught them how not to see it as a problem once they had."

Baca shifted at the door. Marisol lowered her voice further.

"If I can get you moved into observation, I can buy time."

"For who."

"For you."

"No."

The word was immediate. Clean.

Marisol blinked. "You don't even know what I can do yet."

"I know enough."

"You don't know what I can move on this line."

"Then hear me clearer." He leaned forward despite the pull in his side, and now the pain showed because he no longer cared what it cost him to show it. "You don't pull me out by myself and call that fixing anything. You don't get to make me your private exception so you can go back upstairs and tell yourself the machine slipped once."

"That's not what this is."

"That's exactly what this is."

"You'd rather stay on the manifest."

"I'd rather the thing that happened to me have a name bigger than my file."

There it was, stripped of any request for gratitude or revenge. He was planting himself where she could not turn him into a private exception.

Marisol heard how the speakers outside kept repeating intake instructions in the same bright, clipped voice no matter who cried over them. She heard a gurney wheel click each turn because somebody had taped the hub instead of replacing it. Sound was how Gabriel had always sorted a room. As a child he had known her key in the apartment lock from the third-floor landing. He had known his father's cough from two blocks away. Now he was talking about destination codes and floor pitch and voices that would vanish if the recorder vanished, and the pattern was so old it made something cramped and ugly move under her ribs.

"Who are these people," she asked.

"People you transport."

"Don't be clever with me."

"I'm not being clever. That's the whole point." He tipped his head toward the recorder again. "They were on the line before me. Some got moved south. Some got deferred. Some just came apart in the paperwork after a medical hold and then nobody could find where the body went. I was carrying copies. Backups. Whatever word makes you feel less panicked."

His breathing had gone shallow with the talking. Celia had been right about the bruise. Deep enough to make speech expensive.

Marisol found herself looking at the strap abrasion crossing his side. The skin was torn in two places where the restraint had rubbed it raw. If she wet gauze and laid it there it would sting first, then cool. The knowledge rose in her with the old stupid force of motherhood, immediate and practical and useless at the scale of the room.

"Who picked you up," she said.

"Checkpoint team east of the yard."

"Why were the papers on you."

He gave her a look that said she was skipping over the actual problem on purpose. He was right. She was.

"Because I had them," he said.

"Gabriel."

"Because I had them."

She wanted to shake him then, not for insolence but for the terrible resemblance between his answer and the kind of answer she herself would have given at twenty-seven if somebody with authority asked for the wrong truth. She settled for gripping the edge of the stainless cart until the cold bit her palm.

"If I can't fix the paperwork in the yard," she said, "I can still alter where you sleep tonight. I can route you into medical observation, I can get a fresh scan, I can stop them from sealing you in Car Eight for this leg."

"And then what."

"Then I keep moving."

"By yourself."

"By whoever I need."

Baca swore under his breath when the radio cracked alive again, turned fully toward the corridor, and pressed two fingers to the earpiece while somebody barked for lane counts. Gabriel used the opening at once, leaning over the stainless cart until the short chain drew taut. For those few seconds Baca was all back and profile, listening away from them.

Gabriel leaned toward her over the stainless cart and kept his voice inside the machine hum. "If you move me alone, I tell the first officer who asks exactly why Supervisor Anaya suddenly cares about Case 4471-R. I tell them your real name and mine. I tell them you recognized me in intake yesterday and have been bending procedure since."

Marisol caught the edge of the stainless cart as the room lurched in her like a blackwater tank shifting too fast on a grade. By the time her stomach caught up, her mind was already counting the damage.

"You'd burn your own chance to get clear."

"Clear to where." He let out a short breath that might have become a laugh if his ribs had allowed it. "I'm already on the train."

She kept one hand locked on the cart and let the cold bite her palm while memory misfired anyway: the small boy who used to sleep with a cassette recorder under his pillow because he liked to tape the night trains two miles off and play them back slower in the morning. The boy was gone. Or rather he was present in the exact wrong way, grown into a man whose habits had sharpened instead of softened. The same attention to sound. The same refusal to let adults rename what he had plainly heard.

"What do you want from me," she asked.

"Not that."

"Say it plain."

He licked the split inside his lip and winced. "If there's anything left in you that isn't company, don't save me quiet. Get that recorder out. Keep the names from being scrubbed with the rest. If you can do more than that, prove it somewhere I can see."

There was no room in the sentence for mother. That was the point. He was setting terms the way he would have set them with any compromised adult asking for trust she had not earned.

Marisol opened her mouth and found nothing fit to put in it. Sorry was too small. I love you was impossible here, obscene almost, with Baca at the door and the alias tag on the recorder and the bruise opening purple under the room's bad light. The best she could summon was process.

"If I touch property chain-of-custody," she said, "they audit the room."

"They should."

"You don't know what that costs."

"I know what this costs."

He raised his cuffed hands again, a fraction, enough to indicate not just himself but the room, the line, the whole clean apparatus of damage control around them. Then he let them fall back against the short chain with a clink that sounded indecently small.

Celia came back through the curtain carrying a compression wrap and the portable scanner under one arm. Sweat had darkened her scrub top between the shoulder blades. She took in the silence first, then the distance between them, then Gabriel's face.

"Time is up," Baca said after he killed the radio, relieved now that the matter could return to ordinary violence and ordinary paperwork.

Celia set the scanner on the cart, thumbed it awake, and squirted a cold stripe of gel under Gabriel's right ribs before he could brace against anything but pain. The probe clicked once against bone. Gray shapes opened and closed across the small screen.

"Hold still," she said.

Gabriel bared his teeth and did.

Celia ran the probe along the lower rib edge, then higher, then back again until she trusted what she was seeing. "No free fluid that I can see. No obvious splenic spill. Maybe a hairline rib, maybe muscle torn deep. This rig won't rule a slow bleed, and it doesn't buy me an observation bed without a physician read."

Marisol did not move.

"Supervisor," Baca said.

Gabriel kept his eyes on her. Whatever else he had learned since she last had any right to ask, he had learned not to rescue the person with power from the consequences of using it.

"Do your note," Marisol said to Celia.

Celia wiped the gel away with a wad of gauze, then wrapped the ribs tight enough to support and loose enough to pass for humane. Gabriel shut his mouth on the pain this time. When Celia lifted the property bag to note the seized electronics in the medical addendum, the recorder swung close enough for Marisol to read every scratch and strip of tape on the casing. The alias label flashed under the fluorescents. Forty-seven hours, he had said, voices with dates on them. The bag spun once from Celia's grip and settled.

"Observation?" Baca asked.

The question hung there, simple and lethal. If she said yes, she would need more than what the room now held. The portable scan had shown no free fluid and nothing dramatic enough for Celia to own a hold. To force observation Marisol would need a doctor, a stronger chart, and a fight she could not hide. Baca would log the deviation. Celia would resent it. Gabriel would burn the secret on contact. If she said no, he went back into queue bruised, wrapped, and carrying the only thing in the room that might outlast both of them in a property bag marked for seizure.

Marisol looked at her son. He gave the smallest shake of his head.

"Cleared for monitored loading," she said.

The words came out in her public voice, clipped and exact. She hated how competent she sounded.

Baca nodded as if he had known all along this would settle into sense. He rehooked the short chain, took the property bag, and tugged Gabriel to his feet. Gabriel stood carefully, protecting the right side now that the wrap had made the pain more legible instead of less.

At the threshold he stopped and turned back just enough to make Marisol meet his eyes one last time.

"The guards leave bruises," he said. "You built the part that lets everybody else call them manageable."

Baca jerked the chain. Gabriel did not look at her again.

Celia stripped the paper backing from another wrap with her teeth. "Next time," she said without malice, tired almost to transparency, "bring me a body you actually mean to keep."

Then she was gone behind the curtain and the room reclaimed itself: oxygen hiss, cart wheel squeak, fluorescent buzz, the floor drain swallowing diluted blood one pink mouthful at a time.

Marisol stood alone beside the empty stainless cart until the intake speaker outside announced final southbound staging. Her passcard trace was in the system now. Baca had seen too much urgency. Celia had seen more than that. Gabriel had refused the one version of mercy she knew how to offer and named the job beneath the job with a precision she could not scrub down into clerical language.

When she stepped back into the corridor, the heat hit her full in the face. At the far end Baca turned Gabriel toward the loading gate, the property bag with the recorder knocking lightly against his knee as if it held nothing heavier than laces and a broken pen. Marisol watched them disappear into the white-painted passage where rust kept showing through no matter how often the crews came at it with bleach. By Caldera Junction, she'd be expected upstairs with a tray in her hands and nothing visible on her face. The sentence she had been using to keep herself functional since dawn finally failed.

This was not a mistake inside the line.
