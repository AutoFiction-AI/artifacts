# Chapter 6

South of Caldera, at Marsh Crescent, the outage took the fans first, then the strip lights, then the little courtesies that made custody look orderly.

The emergency bars along the corridor floor glowed a bad hospital green. Everything above them sweated in the dark. White wall panels that looked clean by daylight showed their seams at night, rust pushing through the paint in hairline blooms. The train had been standing still for almost an hour on the marshland siding, nose angled into black water and reeds, with the traction line dead and the backup system doing just enough to keep locks live and nothing else. There was no refrigeration in the galley, no proper air, and no false sense that movement itself was mercy.

Heat sat inside the cars like a second load. It pressed Marisol's shirt flat against her back and worked salt into the chemical burn scar on her wrist until the skin there prickled. Mosquitoes worried at the torn patch of screen over the crew vestibule window, whining so close to her ear that more than once she slapped at nothing and hit the metal wall. Beneath her boots the floor no longer carried the steady travel hum of the line. Instead it gave her the slow settling sounds of stalled tanks and strained couplings: a hollow shift in the blackwater below, a tinny pop from cooling bolts, somewhere farther down a latch knocking softly every time a body leaned against the wrong door.

She sat on an overturned waste drum in the service alcove with a flashlight, a dead comm tablet, and Gabriel's recorder laid across her thigh. The little machine looked cheap enough to have come out of a corner pharmacy, except Gabriel had wrapped a band of white labeling tape around the back and written a date block in his careful square hand. The adhesive had gone gray with pocket lint. There was dried something on one corner of the case, not blood, maybe coffee, maybe the syrup from canned fruit. She had been carrying it since Caldera, first in the inside pocket of her supervisor jacket, then in the locked drawer where she kept spare seals and incident blanks, then in her bra for the walk between cars when the audit trail on property storage started feeling too easy to read. She had stolen it because it was his. She had not listened because once voices entered a room, they changed the room's size.

A child cried twice in the family bunks and was hushed by somebody too tired to soothe for long. From one of the locked cars came the rubbery sound of a cough that would not clear. A guard swore under his breath when his flashlight clipped the jamb. Then the corridor went back to the marsh and the insects and the heat, those old patient things that needed nothing from policy.

Marisol should have been making another round. She knew the numbers already. Toilet load on Cars 6 through 8 was edging up because people kept drinking warm water and then losing it. Trauma room sink was low but usable. The portable bleach tank in sanitation was down to a quarter and the mop heads would sour by morning if the air stayed dead. The deep-clean hold at Caldera had bought time and cost all of them sweat, overtime, suspicion, and a harder push south once dispatch got the line moving again. That was the arithmetic she understood. The sequence in her head stayed operational: delay here, pressure there; vent this, seal that; carry the spill forward; do not call attention to the part of the machine that has started to smell like itself.

The recorder rested under her palm with a dull trapped warmth, as if it had its own body heat.

Paloma appeared in the doorway with two paper cups pinched between the fingers of her steady hand and a basil leaf stuck to the damp front of her scrub top as if it had ridden there all shift. Her braid had half come loose, silver threads escaping along her neck. The tremor in her right hand showed only when she transferred one of the cups to Marisol, a fine shiver at the wrist and then gone.

"Water with salt," Paloma said. "Don't ask me to call it cold."

Marisol took it. The cup was wet and nearly warm. "How bad?"

"For now?" Paloma leaned her shoulder against the frame and listened to the train the way other people listened to weather. "One toddler has heat rash over his whole back because his mother thought the synthetic blanket meant official and kept it on him too long. One old man is trying to faint with dignity. A girl in Car 7 bit a guard because he wouldn't let her mother hold the sick bag. I admire her, medically speaking."

"Any real emergencies?"

"You mean any emergencies that count on paper." Paloma sniffed once and pushed the basil leaf off her shirt with the back of her wrist. "Not yet. If the line doesn't come back before dawn, the family bunks are going to turn into a kennel and then into a clinic."

She tipped her chin toward the recorder. She did not ask where Marisol had got it. That was one of the things Marisol loved and resented in her oldest friend. Paloma could make room around a fact without pretending not to see it.

"You should at least sit by a doorway," Paloma said. "You're going to bake in here."

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine. You're just vertical." Paloma rubbed at the bridge of her nose with a knuckle that smelled faintly of camphor. "If dispatch wakes up and starts lying again, send someone for me before they decide sweat isn't a health issue."

Then she was gone, soft-soled and compact, carrying her tired body back toward other people's fevers.

Marisol drank half the salted water, which tasted like the rim of a cheap pot, and set the cup on the floor. The recorder waited.

Gabriel had handled evidence since before she understood he had built a life out of it. When he was fourteen he used to rescue labels from jars and stack them on the kitchen table to soak the glue off in a saucer, because he said paper deserved a second use if it had not lied yet. She had yelled at him then for making a mess in the only place they could eat. She could see the room now with the kind of accuracy that came only when she was trying not to think about it: the worker-housing kitchenette with one drawer that never shut flush, the fan in the window coughing hot air back at them, rice crusting in the cooker because she had picked up an extra wipe-down at the corridor clinic and got home too late, Gabriel bent over a thrift-store cassette player with a butter knife because he had lost the screwdriver again. She had come in still smelling of ammonia and old urine under the bleach, and he had looked up and asked whether dead batteries died all at once or got quieter first. She had not answered. She had gone to the sink and scrubbed her hands until the skin over her knuckles looked boiled.

Now she thumbed the play button.

For a second there was only hiss and the marsh's damp pressure around it. Then Gabriel's voice came through the tiny speaker, drier than it sounded when he had been standing in front of her with restraint marks on his ribs, but unmistakable. He tapped the casing once, a habit she remembered from all the cheap electronics he had tried to keep alive past their usefulness.

"Field file fourteen," he said. "South shelter overflow, loading week. First names only unless you want the rest on record. If you want me to stop, say stop. If you want water first, say that too."

There was a rustle, cloth or wrapper paper. Then a woman's breath, close enough to fuzz the mic.

The speaker's tin grain made Gabriel sound farther away than a few locked cars, already half translated into evidence. Even so, he started with water, with permission to stop. Marisol could not remember the last official question on this train that had offered a body that order.

"All right," Gabriel said. He spoke softer now. "Start wherever it sticks."

The woman laughed once, without humor. "It sticks in my pocket."

She gave her name as Amparo. Her voice had the grain of somebody who had spent years speaking over machinery and children both. Somewhere behind her on the recording a baby kept making the wet, irritated sound of a child too hot to cry hard. Amparo said they had let her keep the pocket things until intake because the first officer on the bus was lazy and the second one was flirting with a woman from records. She listed them slowly, as if inventory might make theft reversible.

"A bus token with the blue heron on it. One key with chipped green polish because it used to belong to my sister and I can never tell mine from hers by touch. Half a cinnamon stick in foil for my stomach. A receipt from Mercado Las Torres with soap, tomatoes, and powdered milk. The corner of a school picture where my girl is laughing before they tell her to stop showing all the gums in front. I had folded it small. Very small. Smaller than a finger joint. They gave me back the key because they said a key is harmless if the door is gone."

Marisol pressed her thumb into the raised seam of the burn scar on her wrist until the sting sharpened. Intake liked to call this stage property control, as if the right phrase could turn pocket-litter theft into procedure. She had watched officers shake receipts, prayer cards, antacid packets, and children's barrettes into trays with the bored care of people clearing drains.

She stopped talking. On the tape the baby started up again, then hiccupped itself quiet.

"What did the tag say?" Gabriel asked.

"Santa Avelina."

The name tightened the back of Marisol's throat. Outside, something brushed the torn screen and the mosquitoes changed pitch, a thin electric whine. Santa Avelina had started as salt-swollen paper in her closet; now the line used it like bleach, pouring it over whoever they wanted to rename clean.

"Do you have anyone there?"

"I've got a dead grandmother who once knew a man from there. That's how much I've got there."

Marisol sat so still that sweat gathered under her knees and ran down the backs of her calves. She knew the property bags Amparo meant. Clear plastic with a white strip for barcode and initials. She had signed for cartons of them. She knew the way intake clerks reduced a life into the categories on the preprint: jewelry, paper, medicine, miscellaneous. She had watched them mark family photos as paper and inhalers as plastic and wedding bands as personal metal as if the right noun made confiscation tidy.

At home, when Gabriel had been little, she had turned his pockets out over the bathtub before wash because if she fed screws or coins into the machine downstairs the super took the repairs out of the laundry deposit. She remembered marbles. Bread ties twisted into chains. A dead beetle in a matchbox with holes poked through the lid. Once, a whole tangerine gone soft and furry in the pocket of his school pants because he had decided to save it for later and forgotten. She had stood there after a double shift peeling moldy fruit from cloth with two fingers while he slept face-down on the foldout couch, his heel hanging over the edge, the room thick with the bleach smell she carried home no matter how long she showered. She had thought then that the difference between work and love was simple. At work you cleaned what strangers left in their bodies and went back the next day. At home you cleaned what your own people dropped without meaning to and called that ordinary life.

On the recorder Amparo said, quieter, "My daughter thinks Santa Avelina is made up because the name sounds painted on."

Gabriel did not fill the silence too fast. That was part of his skill, and hearing it made something bitter move in Marisol's chest. He had learned how to wait better than she had. On the line, silence was usually where lies went to breed. Gabriel had turned it into room.

"What's your daughter's name," he asked, "if you want it kept with yours."

"Lidia Mendez Ruiz," Amparo said immediately, like a hand slamming down to keep a paper from blowing away. "Write it properly."

The tape clicked again, brief and deliberate, while he marked something down.

Marisol looked at the blank back of an incident form on her lap, the one she had meant to use if the toilets overflowed before power returned. Without thinking too hard about it, she uncapped her grease pencil and wrote:

Amparo

Lidia Mendez Ruiz

The wax dragged on the paper fibers. Her handwriting looked too official for names.

She let the tape run.

The next voice belonged to a man who cleared his throat first as if about to enter a church argument. He called himself Leandro and then apologized for his breathing, which Gabriel told him not to do. You could hear from the edges of his words that he was smiling while angry, a trick Marisol knew from dockworkers and debtors and men who had learned not to hand fear over cleanly. He said he had fired brick for nineteen years inland and had never once stood close enough to salt water to smell it right.

"The officer spun the monitor and asked the room what they thought I looked like," Leandro said. "Like it was a fucking game. One of them says my hands are boat hands. These are kiln hands. Look."

There was a scrape, maybe him holding his palms up uselessly to a machine that could not see.

"Then the woman with the silver nails says Santa Avelina takes men with prior maritime family linkage, and I said I don't have prior maritime anything, I've got a cousin who drowned in an irrigation trench and an uncle who sold knockoff fans from the back of a truck, and she says, close enough, there's a surname packet attached."

"Did they show you the packet?" Gabriel asked.

"No. They showed me my own face on the screen like that settled it. Like if I looked tired enough I might have come from anywhere."

He laughed then, a short dry bark that turned into coughing. When he came back he sounded older.

"Write this part down. They chose a country because somebody liked the shape of the word in their mouth."

Marisol closed her eyes.

Tom Avelar had kept his papers in a round butter-cookie tin with a lid that no longer fit right. Dock license, vaccination card, union dues receipts from the months when there was union work to be had, Gabriel's birth certificate folded into quarters, and at the bottom the Santa Avelina parish record from a grandfather none of them had ever met. Salt had swollen the edges of all of it. After Tom died she could not bear to throw the tin away, partly because there were still practical uses for the documents and partly because grief, when you were broke, had to live in containers you already owned. Years later somebody on the line had scanned the parish seal and turned the old paper into a clean legal hook. A dead man's family folder had become a route code. She had kept that tin in the back of her closet beside extra bleach tablets and winter socks. She had never once imagined the train would reach inside it and come back with her son.

On the tape Gabriel asked, "What do you want beside your name?"

Leandro answered without hesitation. "That I make brick, not boats. That my daughter hates dust storms and chews the collars off her shirts. That I can't swim."

Marisol wrote his name too. Then the daughter: Ines, age nine, chews shirt collars. She did not know whether that detail belonged in a record that could save anyone. She wrote it anyway because it was the only truthful thing that had entered her hands all day.

The outage held. Outside, something big moved in the reeds and left a dragging sound through the shallows. The train answered with a shudder from the couplings, a complaint traveling through forty locked tons held still on bad temper and dead power. Sweat had dampened the collar of her uniform enough that the fabric rasped when she turned her head. Somewhere overhead a mosquito found the warm hollow behind her ear and fed until she slapped it, smearing a small crescent of blood she could not tell was hers.

She thought about the deep-clean order at Caldera, the way she had signed it with flat competent strokes and watched the crew wheel in the disinfectant drums. She had told herself delay was neutral, or at least neutral enough. Time was time. An extra six hours before departure. An extra six hours to call, to think, to improvise, to save Gabriel from the paperwork built around his father's name. But while she had been buying those hours, families had stayed longer in strapped vinyl seats. Children had slept longer in the heat. Guards had stayed on shift long enough to get mean in that unfocused tired way that left bruises for stupid reasons. There was no clean delay inside this machine. There was only where the weight landed.

The tape hissed, clicked, moved on.

"This one?" Gabriel said. "You can tell it or your mother can."

"I can tell it." The voice that came next was high and careful, not babyish, a child working hard to sound old enough to be believed. "You have to not say I cried a lot."

"I can leave the count off," Gabriel said, and Marisol could hear the smile in him even through the static.

"No, say I cried some, not a lot. My name is Luz Elena. I'm eight and a half."

There was a pause while someone in the background murmured that she was seven. Luz Elena snapped back, "I'm eight in November and that's halfway." Then, more composed: "They tied my mother first because she wouldn't let go of my brother's shoe. The foam smells bad after somebody throws up on it. It smells like orange cleaner and old spit and the pennies after you hold them in your hand all day. They turned the pad over but the smell stays in the middle."

Marisol saw the foam at once. Gray restraint blocks with wipeable covers, inventory code stenciled in black, always advertised in company training as hygienic because the outer skin did not absorb. That was only true if you cared more about surface than what lay warm underneath.

"Did they clean it before you had to lie there?" Gabriel asked.

"They wiped the top. Not the crack part." Luz Elena lowered her voice as if sharing a recipe. "The crack part keeps it."

Behind the child, a woman made a sound so tired it was almost a laugh.

"And the tag?" Gabriel asked after a moment.

"Mine says Santa Avelina too, but my mother says that's not where my grandmother came from and anyway my grandmother's dead, so she's not available to pick us up."

Gabriel breathed in through his nose, let it out slow. "Do you want your brother's name in too?"

"Mateo," Luz Elena said. "He throws one sock away every time he's mad, so if he gets there with only one sock, that's still him."

Marisol put the recorder on pause because the service alcove had tilted under her, just enough to make her reach for the wall.

The metal beside her shoulder was slick with condensed heat. It smelled faintly of wet dust and the cheap lemon compound they used on public-facing surfaces when investors rode along. She kept her hand there until the car settled. Her body still expected the old sway and had to correct when the floor stayed still.

Then somebody shouted from the family bunks, into the dark without reaching for a radio. "Supervisor. We need a bag. Now."

Marisol shoved the recorder under the dead comm tablet and went with her flashlight and the half roll of absorbent pads from the alcove shelf. The corridor floor bars turned every shin bone green. In Car 7 the young guard with the bitten hand stood uselessly in the aisle, thumb wrapped in gauze, a paper sick bag clenched in his good fist while a woman on the lower bunk tried to hold a little boy upright over a blanket already slick with vomit. The child had brought up everything he had and kept heaving anyway, orange water and ropey spit, each convulsion knocking his forehead against his mother's collarbone. A girl beside the ladder, maybe ten, watched the guard with her mouth set hard enough to bite again.

"Why is that still in your hand?" Marisol asked.

He lifted the bag a little, defensive even now. "She threw it."

"Then let her throw the next one full." Marisol held out her palm until he gave it over. "Get me two more and a mop head that doesn't already stink."

He muttered something about protocol and moved because route supervision still carried in her voice. Marisol passed the bag to the mother, stripped the soaked blanket down to the vinyl, and pressed absorbent pads into the seam where the sour liquid had run. The bunk had already taken the smell into its stitched middle: stomach acid, orange cleaner from some earlier wipe-down, the sweet rot of heat. The mother kept saying, "I'm sorry, I'm sorry," every time the boy gagged. Marisol told her to save her breath and hold him higher. The girl stayed planted by the ladder, watching every move like she was memorizing who got to touch what.

By the time the guard came back, too slowly, with the extra bags and a dry mop head, the blanket was in a leak sack and the boy had gone limp with the ugly quiet that came after vomiting. Marisol told the guard to fetch Paloma and to leave the bags with the family this time. She waited until he nodded before she left. The smell stayed behind her anyway. It lived in the bunk seam now.

Back in the service alcove, her hands carried sour heat even after she wiped them on sanitizer gauze. Maintenance had never only meant getting people to morning. It meant preparing damage for transport, drying it, bagging it, and naming it something smaller than what it was so the train could claim dispatch-ready status and the company could invoice another clean departure.

She started the tape again.

Voices came now in shorter cuts, maybe because Gabriel had been interrupted, maybe because he had been forced to record fast. A woman named Soraya whose whole contribution was, "If they put me under another dead man's paperwork, then write that I still have my own teeth and they're mine." A teenager laughing while he described braiding bread-bag ties together because you could not confiscate what looked like trash. Somebody in the back asking if the recorder could catch accent and Gabriel answering, "Enough for someone to know you didn't come from where the tag says." Each fragment left a body-shaped dent in the air.

Marisol wrote as much as she could catch: names when they gave them, details when the names blurred under static, own teeth, bread ties, can't swim, one sock missing on purpose.

The grease pencil softened in her fingers from the heat. Wax collected under her thumbnail. By the time the battery warning light blinked red on the recorder, the back of the incident form was full and she had started on a waste-log duplicate from the clipboard pocket. It was obscene, maybe, to use company paper for this, but then obscenity had been the house style of RailPassage for years. Everything the line did was built on the premise that the right form could launder almost any fact.

The last clear stretch of tape carried Gabriel's voice alone for a moment. He must have thought he had stopped recording or else meant to leave himself in as a marker. His breathing was close to the mic. Then he said, not to a witness exactly, maybe to the room, maybe to himself, "If they change the destination, start with the name again. Start there every time."

The recorder clicked dead.

From the cars ahead came a brief wave of sound, collective discomfort passing through bodies at once, a step short of panic. A baby. A curse. Somebody hammering twice on a locked door with the flat of a hand. Then quiet gathered over it again, thin and ugly.

Marisol looked at what she had written. The names sat on the page in the same upright script she used for bleach requisitions and injury summaries and seal-breach reports. Official handwriting. Work handwriting. She hated that. She hated that even here, even alone, the first tool available to her was the same one she used to make pain legible to payroll and invisible to conscience.

But the paper was not empty anymore.

Amparo, with the key that opened nothing here.

Lidia Mendez Ruiz, gums showing in the photo.

Leandro, kiln hands, can't swim.

Ines who chewed her shirt collars to threads.

Luz Elena, eight and a half by her own rules.

Mateo, one sock gone when angry.

Soraya, her own teeth.

More half-caught names crowded the margins, some with question marks because the static had eaten a syllable, some with only a bunk note or a detail that might have to stand in until she heard them again. It was not enough. It would never be enough. The train carried hundreds. The line had carried thousands before this week and would carry thousands after unless something happened large enough to force the truth out where it could not be cleaned up before inspection.

She had spent days telling herself the story in the smallest possible way: Gabriel on her line, Gabriel under bad paperwork, Gabriel needing time. The recorder had broken that scale. If she kept risking anything on this train, it could not be to pry one son out of hundreds. It had to be for the people the line kept trying to flatten into tags, bunk numbers, and transportable mess.

Marisol folded the pages once, careful along the crease so the grease pencil would not smear too badly, and slid them inside the plastic sleeve behind her dead comm tablet. The hiding place would not survive scrutiny. It only had to survive the next round. Her body already knew the motion from years of tucking unofficial things inside official ones. She hated that knowledge too.

Outside, the marsh breathed its rotten-sweet breath through the broken screen. The mosquitoes kept coming. The train sat in the heat with its locks alive and its people awake inside it.

Marisol picked up the recorder again though the battery was dead, just to feel its weight. Then she rose from the waste drum, knees stiff, shirt damp, the salted water gone metallic at the back of her tongue. There was another round to make. There were children in hot bunks, an old man trying to faint politely, guards getting stupid with fatigue, tanks filling under the floor. All of that remained true.

At Car 7 the girl by the ladder was still awake, face washed green by the floor bars, watching the aisle as if she had been posted there. Before Marisol checked the bunk seam or counted the remaining sick bags, she took the waste-log duplicate back out and asked for the boy's name. The girl gave it. Marisol wrote that down too, folded the damp duplicate into quarters, and slipped it inside her waistband before taking her flashlight on to the next car.

The train could make them cargo by dawn. It did not get them nameless first.
