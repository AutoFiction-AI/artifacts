# Chapter 12

The pathology cooler sat unlatched on the stainless counter, sweating meltwater into a widening crescent.

The room was not dirty. It was wrong. The ordinary braid was there, the camphor from Paloma's sleeves, the basil she kept alive in clinic cups along the narrow sill, the bitter slick of disinfectant rising off the drain grate, the heat starting to wake in the metal cabinets while the yard wall still held back the sun. Under all of it sat the colder pathology note that did not belong before transfer hour.

Marisol shut the door with her hip and checked the wall lens above the handwash sink. The red dot held steady. Corridor surveillance was live, as usual, but the room camera was still pointed three degrees too high after last week's maintenance and caught more fluorescence than counter. She had left it that way on purpose. Maintenance would not open a ticket for a camera that only missed the lower right corner, where Paloma kept the sharps tray and now the cooler.

Paloma had changed into a clean scrub top with lemons printed near the hem, a gift from some niece she never stopped mentioning and never seemed to visit. The fabric was already damp between her shoulder blades. Her silvering braid had darkened with sweat at the nape. When she reached for the cooler latch, the tremor in her right hand made the metal chatter once against the lid before she got it still with the heel of her left palm.

"You can still stop me," she said.

Marisol had already gone over the timing so many times it had started to sound like prayer and then like the kind of lie people told themselves before an accident. Audit swabs on the southbound layover hit at 06:18. The assay printer in sanitation took four minutes to kick back a quarantine code if a live sample crossed the threshold. Another six, if the code was amber or above, and dispatch had to freeze the consist, notify corridor health, and begin outside-call documentation. In every drill Marisol had ever sat through, a disputed source with a pathology lot number locked both cars in place until corridor health classified the breach; the emergency-variance clause was for burst pipes and galley spills the line could call accidental on its own. If the freeze happened before the reconciliation packet sealed at eight, the file lock on the high-risk transfers stalled with it. That was the hinge, a clock inside a clock with nothing just or merciful about it.

"If we stop now," she said, "they seal those files by breakfast."

Paloma looked at her for a beat too long. Fear made her tidier. Marisol had seen it for years. The older woman arranged gauze by size when her pulse climbed. She straightened labels that already read straight. Now she turned the cooler so the tag faced square to the counter and peeled back the lid as carefully as if she were setting a table.

Inside, nested in cold packs and absorbent pads, sat the preserved samples they were not supposed to touch except to transfer, destroy, or testify over. Every tube had a barcode strip and a handwritten date. Paloma's world in miniature: other people's blood, stool, sputum, the things bodies made when systems failed. She lifted one amber-tagged vial from its foam slot.

Marisol read the half-peeled label upside down. `MC-17 / enteric panel / hold hotel cluster`. The sample was three weeks old. Paloma had said it was still potent enough. It was still enough to turn a routine swab into a corridor-level problem if it landed where the assay would believe it.

"This will point back here if they chase lot numbers," Paloma said.

"Only if they get time to chase them."

"They always get time to chase workers."

Marisol did not answer because it was true and because speaking after truth like that usually made her say something useless. She checked her slate instead. Car 11 blackwater pressure was steady. Car 9's latch alarm had resolved. Gabriel's restraint tier was still flagged for morning count. Naila's bunk row was marked for nutrition distribution at 06:30. The warm glass flattened all of it into obedient icons, as if a restraint tier and a breakfast line weighed no more than light.

Paloma cracked the vial seal. The smell was faint at first, sour and almost sweet, then rose hot and intestinal once air hit it. Marisol's body reacted before her mind did. Her shoulders went tight. Her tongue pressed against the back of her teeth. She had spent half her life teaching herself the difference between disgust and information. This was information.

Paloma drew a pipette, squeezed up a cloudy ribbon, and touched two drops onto the inner lip of the sterile swab tube waiting for the audit pass. Two drops barely glazed the plastic; in a sterile tube, that was all it took. Then she capped it, wiped the outer plastic with alcohol, and slid it back onto the tray beside the untouched kits as if it had always belonged there.

"Once that prints," Paloma said, "there is a record of a source that does not match the train."

"Once it prints, there is a quarantine code."

"You keep saying the second half like the first half vanishes."

Marisol looked at the basil on the sill because she could not look at Paloma just then. The leaves had gone soft at the edges from the heat. Paloma pinched them every morning anyway, muttering over them in the same tone she used on children with fevers and old men who knew exactly what their scans meant. She used that tone on bodies, plants, and old machinery. She used it on anything neglected but not yet gone.

"I know what the first half is," Marisol said.

Paloma gave a dry little laugh with no humor in it. "Do you?"

The knock came exactly on schedule. There were two raps, one pause, and a badge scrape. The audit team had arrived.

Marisol moved first. She opened the door and let in the sanitation verifier with his tablet and disposable sleeve guards. His name was Rafiq Mendoza. He wore the same black ballpoint tucked behind his ear every day and still insisted on carrying a paper checklist folded in quarters inside his breast pocket because, in his words, screens died at the exact moment management needed proof they had once worked. Today the paper corner showed above the pocket seam like a little white flag.

"Morning, Supervisor," he said. "Doctor."

"Morning," Marisol said. "You have the trauma sink, waste locker, and restraint rinse."

"And Car 11 latch handles. New order from compliance."

That made Paloma's eyes flick once toward Marisol. Car 11 was Gabriel's car. It also made the sample path cleaner than Marisol had hoped. If the code hit a restraint-tier handle, Teresa would not be able to shrug it down to a stained counter or one careless orderly. She would have to move. That was the point.

Rafiq pulled gloves on, tapped his slate awake, and began the sequence in the bored, practiced rhythm of a man who had done the same humiliating work too long to pretend it meant hygiene alone. He swabbed the sink lip and the locker latch, scanned the barcode, added the time stamp, and initialed the field.

Marisol stood with her own slate in hand and felt each second as pressure in the scar inside her wrist where the bleach had eaten her skin years ago. She knew exactly what the corridor floor sounded like when a decision passed through it. Right now it was only wheels on distant ballast, a lift gate whining open, the tannoy apologizing in two languages for delays nobody had asked about yet.

Rafiq reached for the doctored tube without knowing it was different. He broke the sterile cap, swabbed the restraint-rinse basin, returned the swab to the tube, and fed it into the portable assay reader clipped to his belt. The little machine blinked green.

Thirty seconds passed. Then forty. Then ninety.

Paloma put both hands flat on the counter to hide the tremor. Marisol watched the numbers on the reader scroll through their checks and thought, with sudden stupidity, of all the times she had trusted small lights more than human voices.

The reader gave a hard double tone and flashed amber, then red. `Q-AMBER ENTERIC / ROUTE HOLD`.

Rafiq looked up, annoyed first, then alert. "That's not possible."

"It is now," Paloma said, too quickly.

Marisol was already moving. She raised her slate, logged the code, and hit the freeze request before Rafiq's own system finished populating the field. Her voice came out in command register, clipped flat from years of loud rooms and spilled things.

"Route hold on the southbound consist. Contamination trigger in restraint tier. Seal trauma room intake, stop all nonessential movement, and notify corridor health per code."

The tannoy's polite synthetic voice cut out mid-sentence. A lower alarm rolled through the car walls, not loud but absolute, the sound the line used when it needed every worker to remember liability before breakfast.

Rafiq stared at the red screen, then at the tube in his hand. "Supervisor, this lot code says trauma-room stock. Why is a trauma-room swab on a restraint rinse?"

There it was: the first half.

Paloma took one step toward him. Marisol stopped her with the smallest movement of two fingers against her own thigh.

"Because the rinse basin serves both lanes," Marisol said. "Log the hold."

Rafiq did, because protocol was older in him than curiosity. The hold went live. Somewhere under the floor, pumps shifted state. Somewhere farther down the train, custody officers started cursing. Marisol could feel the whole corridor tense around a piece of plastic no bigger than her thumb.

Then the first reply came back from compliance, and it was Teresa's voice instead of corridor health.

"Supervisor Anaya," the comm said, polished and already cold, "keep that room sealed. I am on my way."

The line clicked dead.

Paloma let out a breath through her nose. "She was waiting."

Marisol looked at the cooler, the open foam slot where the amber vial had sat, the meltwater widening beneath it like a stain that had not yet decided its final shape. "Then we make her wait too."

***

Teresa Ordonez arrived nine minutes later with her jacket still buttoned despite the heat and her reading glasses in one hand instead of on her face, which meant she had come fast enough to forget where she had put them. She never looked disordered from a distance. Up close, Marisol could see the bitten edges of her cuticles under the clear polish and the smudge where foundation had settled into the crease beside her nose.

With her came Internal Security Officer Bellido, a narrow man in a pressed shirt with a coffee ring drying on one cuff and the flat expression of somebody who thought suspicion itself counted as competence. He chewed licorice gum in tiny deliberate bites. Even from where Marisol stood, she could smell the medicinal sweetness cutting through bleach and enteric sour.

The trauma-room threshold became a room inside the room. Teresa remained just outside the yellow tape line, never letting the soles of her shoes cross into liability if she could help it.

"Explain the discrepancy," she said.

Marisol held her slate at her side. "Q-amber enteric hit on restraint-rinse sampling during morning audit. Hold was logged immediately. Corridor health needs to be notified if they have not already replied."

"I did not ask for the script, Marisol."

Paloma folded her arms. Her right hand tucked under her left elbow to hide the tremor. "The script is the law."

Teresa put on her glasses. "Doctor Rivera, at the moment the relevant law concerns chain of custody. Officer Bellido."

Bellido stepped to the counter, glanced at the assay reader, then at the cooler, then at Paloma. He did not touch anything yet. He liked the look of not touching things. "Cooler seal is broken," he said. "Temperature logger shows a four-minute access window at 06:12."

"I open my cooler every morning," Paloma said. "I'm a medic, not a priest."

"You open it under log, not under freeze."

"The freeze had not begun."

Marisol cut in before Bellido could enjoy himself. "The hold is active. The response now is quarantine, not theater."

Teresa shifted her gaze to Marisol without moving the rest of her face. "The response now is containment. Those are not synonyms."

She extended one manicured finger toward the slate in Marisol's hand. "Show me the raw lot code."

Marisol did. Teresa read it, read it again, and her mouth settled into the shape it took whenever numbers became useful against people.

"This vial originated off-route," she said.

"The assay did not know that."

"The assay does not need to know. Investigators do."

Marisol heard the tannoy outside cycling into a hold notice and then failing halfway through the second language because somebody had cut the generic script to start pushing live orders. Boots moved fast in the corridor. Metal doors clapped. The whole train had gone from morning routine to controlled embarrassment in under fifteen minutes. If they could keep it embarrassed long enough, outside inspectors would step in and the file lock would miss its hour. That had been the plan.

"Corridor health has a mandated response time," Marisol said. "You can't split a consist under suspected enteric exposure without external review."

Teresa looked almost sorry when she answered, which was one of the things Marisol hated most about her.

"I can quarantine contaminated units and transfer custody to reserve stock under emergency variance if the exposure source is disputed and the route remains commercially critical. The addendum lets compliance classify it as disputed pending external review. It does not require me to keep the whole consist idle while corridor health drives out here."

Marisol had read it. She had read every ugly appendix they shoved across a desk if it affected drains, bodies, or dispatch. But that was the turn she had counted on Teresa not daring. In every drill disputed source had meant hold everything until corridor health named the breach. Teresa was using the same words to split the train before the call could land.

"The reserve stock on Spur C has dead air until maintenance brings the second bus online," she said. "You move restrained passengers through that bridge in this heat and you're stacking them in a hot box."

"Then we will move them quickly."

Paloma barked a sound that was not laughter. "That's your medical plan."

Teresa's glasses flashed. "My medical plan, Doctor Rivera, is to prevent an unverified sample discrepancy from freezing the entire southbound corridor during reconciliation week. Your role is to tell me whether any individual currently requires non-ambulatory transfer."

Paloma's face changed then. The fear lost its neat edges. Coarseness came in under it, exactly as Marisol had always known it would if somebody mishandled a body in front of her.

"My role," she said, "is to tell you that if you put seventy-odd people through a dead-air bridge at noon with cuffs on and masks on and officers trying to make up time, one of them is going to fold on you. Maybe more than one."

Bellido finally touched the cooler. He tipped the lid farther back with a pen and studied the foam slots. "Missing vial."

Neither woman spoke.

Teresa took off her glasses again and rubbed the bridge of her nose. For one second she looked her age and tired and merely human. Then the managerial shell slid back into place.

"Seal the cooler. Pull Rivera's access log, call history, and storage inventory. Supervisor Anaya, you will coordinate transfer readiness on Spur C under direct compliance supervision. Doctor Rivera, you will accompany the move without independent access to pathology or trauma-room storage pending interview."

"That's suspension by another name," Paloma said.

"No. Suspension is paperwork. This is preservation of evidence."

"And when the evidence is me?"

Teresa's voice stayed level. "Then I advise you to make your answers very precise."

The worst part was that she was not shouting. The worst part was that every sentence came dressed as administrative reason. Marisol knew how much harm could fit inside tone like that. She had built a career around it.

"Outside inspectors are already on the clock," Marisol said. "You can't outrun the call."

Teresa turned to her. "I do not need to outrun it. I need to ensure that when they arrive, the contaminated units are sealed, the passengers are accounted for, and the only plausible breach point sits exactly where it belongs."

Her eyes moved, just once, to Paloma.

In that glance Marisol saw Spur C, the dead-air bridge, the sealed trauma cabinet, and Paloma already being fitted into the shape of the breach.

Marisol stepped closer before she could think better of it. "This was your audit path. Your extra swab order on Car 11 did this as much as anything."

"Be careful," Teresa said softly.

"No. You be careful. You're about to cut power to the only car with live trauma storage and then push a restraint transfer through a bridge that already runs ten degrees hotter than ambient. We have one portable AED on this half of the consist, and it's in that room."

"Then bring it."

Bellido shook his head. "Evidence room stays sealed until inventory."

Paloma said, "There. You hear yourself?"

Teresa mislaid her glasses again, patted one pocket, then the other, then found them still in her left hand. The gesture was so quick most people would have missed it. Marisol did not miss small dropped things when hierarchy got frightened.

"We are done arguing," Teresa said. "Move the passengers."

The order went out three minutes later. Spur C would take the transfer. The move was a split-consist emergency transfer. Suspect units were sealed for review. Custody would shift to reserve stock before corridor health arrived.

In the corridor outside the trauma room, workers and officers pivoted with the obedient speed institutions called professionalism. A sanitation porter Marisol had trained herself six years earlier stood by the junction door with a roll of red tape and tears drying angry on his cheeks because he had just learned he would be entering a biohazard lane for no extra pay. A young custody officer kept adjusting the neck of his vest where the fabric had rubbed his skin raw, not from fear maybe, just from heat and bad stitching and a job he would later pretend had always been inevitable. Somewhere farther down, a child started crying and did not stop.

Paloma reached for Marisol's wrist as Teresa turned away. Her fingers were cold from the cooler.

"This is where it breaks," she said quietly.

Marisol looked at the open trauma room, at the sealed cabinet where the portable monitor sat, at the little basil cups sweating on the sill in a line like witnesses nobody had subpoenaed yet.

"Then stay close," she said.

Paloma's mouth twisted. "That has not worked out very well for either of us."

***

By the time they brought the first detainees onto Spur C, the heat had climbed from oppressive to punitive.

The service bridge between the sealed car and reserve stock had always been a cheap piece of corridor engineering, corrugated metal walls, grated floor, no side vents worth naming, built for equipment carts and the occasional escorted overflow, not for moving bodies in batches with their wrists fixed and their fear already up. Once the consist split and the suspect units went dark, the bridge lost its little breath of conditioned air and became a long aluminum throat swallowing heat from both sides.

Marisol knew every failure in it. The left latch stuck if the frame swelled. The overhead strip lights browned out when the second power bus lagged. The floor bolts near the midpoint had been replaced with cheaper heads after spring inventory and now caught on soft shoe rubber. None of those defects had mattered enough to halt dispatch. That was the logic she had lived inside for twenty years: nothing counted until a body gave enough its exact measurement.

Custody officers moved people in linked groups. Masks covered mouths. Plastic cuffs bit into wrists. Property pouches were still separated, because dignity could always wait for the manifest to catch up. Bellido had ordered the pace doubled. Teresa stood under the spur awning with a slate in one hand and a sealed-water bottle in the other, not drinking from it, just holding it cold against her wrist.

Naila came through in the second group, cropped hair dark with sweat, the two missing fingernails on her right hand making her grip on the rail look careful and oddly delicate. She counted under her breath as she moved, each number tied to the next with the same small certainty she used when she knotted wrapper strings. She was at seven, then eight, then nine when Gabriel turned his shoulders behind her to keep an older man from being jostled too hard by the officer at his back.

Marisol recognized the old man from the last three days of rounds before she remembered his name. He was Julián Arce. His intake card had said sixty-eight, though the line shaved years off and added them back whenever it needed labor or pity. He had a nicotine-yellow moustache and shirt buttons repaired with bright blue thread that looked done by somebody else's hands. In every hold since Caldera he had been folding biscuit foil into tiny silver fish for the bunk children because his own fingers would not stay idle. One of the little girls had tucked one behind her ear yesterday like jewelry.

Now his face had gone the bad chalky red Marisol had seen before in boiler rooms and summer loading bays. He was still trying to be polite.

"Señora," he said to no one person and everyone. "A little slower."

Gabriel said, "He's not keeping up because he can't breathe."

The officer behind them shoved the line forward with his forearm. "Keep moving."

Marisol stepped into the lane. "Give me a non-ambulatory tag on him."

Teresa did not raise her voice. She never needed to. "He is not tagged non-ambulatory."

"Then tag him now."

Paloma was already there, having slipped the edge of Bellido's supervision by doing what she always did when systems failed: moving toward the worst body first. She put two fingers under Julián's jaw, then to the side of his neck. Her braid had come half-loose. Her right hand shook visibly now and she ignored it.

"Irregular and fast," she said. "He's cooking. I need him seated and I need water, shade, and my kit."

Bellido answered from three steps away, "Your kit is sealed."

Paloma looked up at him with open hatred. "Then get me a fucking chair."

No one moved fast enough. That was the thing Marisol would remember later, more than the collapse itself: the half-second pauses in eight different people while authority sorted itself out. Teresa checking whether yielding once would invite chaos. Bellido calculating liability. The officer with his hand on Gabriel's shoulder not wanting to break line. Marisol already moving and still somehow not having moved soon enough.

Julián tried to take one more step. His knees softened instead. The paper fan folded from his meal notice slipped from his hand and skated under the bridge rail. He did not fall cleanly. He folded in parts, chest first, then one shoulder banging the corrugated wall, then his head clipping the latch housing with a dull metal knock.

The line bunched. Somebody screamed, and the sound came from one of the children three groups back.

"Down!" Paloma snapped. "Lay him flat."

Marisol and Gabriel reached him at the same moment from opposite sides of the lane. Gabriel's fingers, still stained blue-black from old pen ink ground into the nail beds, slid under the old man's shoulder while Marisol got a hand behind the neck and felt the skin there, dry and furnace-hot. There was no sweat left. The worst sign sometimes was when the body ran out of water enough to stop shining.

Julián's mouth worked twice before sound came. "Fish," he whispered.

Gabriel bent close. "What?"

"Pocket."

Then the old man's eyes rolled wrong.

Paloma shoved the officer aside with more force than Marisol would have believed she had left in her. "Mask off. Now."

Bellido said, "Possible enteric exposure. Barrier protocol stays in place."

Paloma rounded on him, feral. "Then you can tell his corpse the protocol worked."

She ripped the mask free herself. The elastic snapped against his cheek. Marisol got the cuffs shifted enough to free his arms forward. The pulse at his throat went from frantic to scattered, a relay clicking in bad weather. Paloma pressed her stethoscope to the old man's chest though she hardly needed it. Marisol could see the heart trying to beat through cloth and bone without rhythm, not pumping so much as fluttering in separate arguments.

"Julián," Paloma said, loud by his ear, "stay with me. Open your eyes."

Naila had stopped two bodies up and turned, still as a post despite the shouting around her. "His pills are in property," she said. "He asked since morning."

That landed in Marisol like a tool dropped on bone. Of course the pills were in property. Intake had stripped everyone into pouches and numbers, and whatever the old man took for his heart sat somewhere labeled and unavailable while policy decided whether it counted as contraband or care.

Julián jerked once, a hard animal arch, and then his chest went loose.

Paloma swore, flat and ugly. She started compressions on the grated bridge floor because there was nowhere else to start them. The first push bounced with the metal under them. The second found depth. The third broke cartilage. Marisol heard it go.

Gabriel flinched and did not let go.

"Bag him," Paloma said.

"You don't have oxygen," Marisol said.

"I know what I don't have."

She kept working. She counted out loud because the body needed count even if the room did not. One, two, three, four. Her right hand trembled between cycles and came down true anyway. Sweat dropped from her chin to the old man's sternum. A custody officer finally arrived with a resuscitation bag stripped from the reserve car wall, but no oxygen canister, just hot air and cheap plastic. Bellido hovered, still refusing to step into the compression arc. Teresa stayed under the awning, speaking rapidly into her slate to somebody up the chain who would later claim the whole sequence had been under review the entire time.

Marisol looked up once and saw the reserve stock doors still closed because the second bus had not come live. Inside those cars the air was dead. Outside, the line of restrained people backed up down the ramp, masks damp, shoulders pressed together, fear turning the whole bridge into one breathing animal.

"Open the reserve doors," she shouted.

"They are not cleared," a maintenance voice yelled back.

"Then clear them."

Nobody answered with anything useful.

Paloma bagged twice, checked for response, went back to compressions. Julián's eyes had opened but not in any way that meant seeing. A thin line of spit ran from the corner of his mouth into his moustache. On the twelfth compression of the next cycle, urine spread warm under his trousers and ran through the grate onto the ballast below, sharp with dehydration and old kidneys. The smell hit the heat and came back up stronger.

Marisol found herself thinking stupidly, clinically, the way she did when something had gone too far to meet with feeling first: severe dehydration, likely existing cardiac history, prolonged heat exposure, restrained transfer, delayed intervention, no AED. Facts stacked themselves because if they did not, she would have had to name the other thing: cause, participation.

Gabriel did not look at her. He kept his hand on Julián's shoulder as if weight alone could call somebody back.

"Come on," he said, low and furious, to the old man or the system or both. "Don't do it like this. Not in front of them."

The portable monitor should have been ten yards away in the trauma room cabinet. The AED should have been under the seal Bellido had refused to break. Shade should have been available. Water should have been in hands, not on Teresa's wrist. Half the book of should was sitting in Marisol's chest by then, useless as prayer.

Paloma went on until her arms shook too hard to hide it. She checked again. Nothing. She pressed two fingers into the throat, moved them, pressed harder as if the right angle might conjure a pulse by force of contempt.

Then she sat back on her heels.

The whole bridge seemed to tilt.

"Time?" Bellido asked, already reaching for official language.

Paloma stared at him. "Go to hell."

"Doctor Rivera, I need a time."

She gave it because the dead did not stop needing facts once they stopped needing air. Her voice was shredded. "Eleven thirty-seven."

Marisol heard the number and felt it hit like a stamp on wet paper. 11:37. Early enough for the transfer to continue. Early enough for a body to become paperwork before corridor health ever stepped onto the spur.

One of the little silver fish had worked free from Julián's shirt pocket during compressions. It lay by his ribs, foil catching the bridge light, flattened on one side where somebody's boot had clipped it in the rush.

Naila made the sign of the cross with two fingers and then, because she was nineteen and practical and had already learned not to leave the room inside other people's scripts, she said, "His name is Julián Arce."

Bellido looked up from his slate. "I've got the manifest."

"Then use it," she said.

No one struck her for it. No one had space.

The transfer resumed twenty-one minutes later.

It resumed because Teresa ordered it resumed, because corridor health was still forty minutes out, because the reserve doors finally opened with a cough of stale air, because one dead old man did not count as grounds to stop a commercially critical route when the policy language had already been bent far enough to survive him.

Marisol helped move the rest because what else existed in the hour after failure except the same bodies still needing hands. She got people into the reserve stock, forced water up the line where she could, had one officer cut three sets of cuffs looser when fingers started swelling blue, and carried the paper fan from under the bridge rail without knowing why until she realized it was because leaving it there felt like part of the killing.

When she finally stood again outside the sealed car, her uniform was stiff with someone else's sweat and her palms smelled of old skin, plastic, and the iron tang compressions left behind.

Teresa approached only once the line was moving.

"Do not touch anything else without authorization," she said.

Marisol looked at her and truly looked. She saw the careful makeup, the fatigue under it, and the expensive glasses hanging now from a cord she had apparently fetched from somewhere in the interim because she had needed her hands free for command. Teresa had made herself orderly again. That alone made Marisol want to spit.

"He died on your bridge," Marisol said.

"He died during a medically supervised transfer under contaminated conditions initiated by a falsified sample."

The sentence was beautiful in its construction. Every clause was a place to hide.

"Say his name."

Teresa's jaw set. "Not here."

"Why not here."

"Because I am trying to prevent panic."

Marisol laughed then, one short cracked sound that scared her more than shouting would have. "You've got a corpse on ballast and seventy people smelling him through paper masks."

Teresa's face did not move. "And now I have an internal breach in my trauma room."

She turned away before Marisol could answer. That was power too: deciding which sentence got the last air.

***

By midafternoon the sun had shifted off Spur C but left all its work behind in the metal. The sealed suspect cars sat on the side track with yellow tags on the doors and no visible movement inside. Corridor health finally arrived, inspected a scene Teresa had already cut to pieces, and asked for records instead of bodies because records were what still remained intact enough to carry authority.

Paloma's suspension meeting happened in the medication alcove outside the trauma room because Bellido had decided his office computer ran too hot and Teresa had decided dignity was optional.

Marisol stood three feet away with a box of returned supplies in her arms because someone had told her to move them and she had obeyed out of old habit before realizing obedience was now just another form of standing still. Inside the box were saline bags, expired electrolyte gels, a cracked blood-pressure cuff, and two basil cups Bellido had removed from the sill as if leaves might contaminate evidence.

Paloma looked smaller without the room around her. She did not look weaker. She looked smaller, as if the suspension had already started stripping square footage off her life. Her braid had been redone, rough and fast. Dried sweat crusted the collar of her scrub top. The tremor in her right hand returned each time she tried to hold it still at her side.

Teresa read from her slate.

"Pending investigation into chain-of-custody irregularities, unauthorized pathology access, and conduct materially compromising corridor safety, your contract privileges are suspended effective immediately. Badge access is revoked as of fourteen ten. Housing protections attached to route employment are stayed for seventy-two hours pending review. Work authorization notices will issue separately."

There it was, the personal part sitting inside the bureaucratic part like a blade in a sleeve.

Paloma listened without interrupting. That frightened Marisol more than if she had started cursing again.

"Do you understand the terms?" Teresa asked.

Paloma looked not at Teresa but at the box in Marisol's arms, at the basil, and at the saline that had not been on the bridge when she needed it. When she answered, her voice was almost calm.

"I understand you are taking my room before you have proved anything."

"I said housing protections are stayed pending review."

"That means you are taking my room with more paperwork."

Teresa's mouth tightened. "If you cooperate, the review may be swift."

"That's not the same as fair."

Bellido held out the signature pad. Paloma ignored it for a second long enough to make him uncomfortable, then took the stylus. Her hand shook so badly on the first try that the line she made looked like a heart monitor going bad. She steadied with the left hand and signed again, slower, angrier.

"I need my blood-pressure tablets from locker three and my street shoes," she said. "The clogs stay here."

"An escort will retrieve approved items."

"And the basil."

Bellido blinked. "The plants?"

"Yes, the plants. They're living things. That used to matter in the trauma room."

Teresa glanced at Marisol as if the absurdity should embarrass them both. Marisol did not give her that.

"She can take the basil," Marisol said.

"You are not in this meeting."

"You've made damn sure of that."

Teresa ignored the interruption with visible effort. "Doctor Rivera, you may collect personal effects under escort. Pathology storage remains sealed. You are not to contact route staff about this matter outside counsel or formal interview."

Paloma laughed softly then, an exhausted sound. "You think friendship is evidence tampering now."

"At the moment, yes."

When Bellido reached for Paloma's badge, Paloma unclipped it herself. She looked at it a second before surrendering it, thumb rubbing once over the scratched plastic photo taken ten years earlier when her eyes had held more sleep. Then she put it in his hand like something dead.

Marisol should have said something better than what came to her. Should have found a sentence worthy of twenty years of shared shifts, of soup in break rooms, of basil cuttings and wound dressings and the way Paloma had once stitched the inside of Marisol's wrist without filing the injury because insurance would have asked too many questions.

What came was workman's plainness.

"I'll get your shoes," she said.

Paloma looked at her finally and really looked. Her tired eyes, always a little kind even when she was furious, had gone hard with pain and with the knowledge that kindness was expensive now.

"Get the old man's property too," she said. "Before they turn him into incident language."

Then Bellido touched her elbow and she shook him off on instinct. The gesture was too slight to count as resistance, but it reminded the room she still occupied her own body for the moment.

They walked her down the corridor past the trauma-room door. She did not turn her head, but as she passed Marisol she said, so low only she could hear, "Bodies are not theory, Sol."

Marisol stood with the box until the plastic saline bags cut a red groove into her forearm. Then she set it down, took the basil because Bellido had forgotten to mark it, and went looking for Gabriel.

They had put the high-security detainees in the reserve car's rear bay after the transfer, closer-packed than before because the line had lost usable space to quarantine and embarrassment. The air inside was marginally cooler now that the bus was stable, but it still held the cooked smell of too many people forced to share one bad decision. Synthetic politeness drifted from the overhead speaker every few minutes, apologizing for service changes no one aboard had chosen.

Gabriel sat on the lower bunk with his back to the wall, elbows on knees. The bruises intake had left on his upper arms had yellowed at the edges. There was a fresh streak of somebody else's blood dried on his cuff. Naila stood near the aisle post beside him, silent, one hand wrapped around the wrapper string she had been using to count transfers all day. When she saw Marisol, she did not move to block her or welcome her. She just watched, weighing whether this was one more useless exchange to survive.

Gabriel looked up once. That was all. There was no space in his face for surprise.

"Paloma's suspended," Marisol said.

"I know."

"They tied it to the cooler access."

"I know that too."

His voice was quiet in a way that always made other people underestimate how close anger sat under it. Weakness had nothing to do with it. His quiet felt like a drawer shut carefully around knives.

Marisol held the basil cup because she had forgotten she was holding it. Dirt had tipped onto her thumb. "Julián Arce's pills were still in property."

Gabriel's mouth twitched once, not toward humor. "You found that fast."

"Paloma told me to."

"Good for Paloma."

The meanness of it was earned. Marisol took it. "He asked for them since morning, Naila said."

Naila answered before Gabriel did. "He asked since yesterday afternoon. He said the white ones slowed the birds in his chest."

Marisol nodded because if she tried to say sorry now it would sound like a woman asking language to do labor it could not do. "How many white ones?"

"Seven in the pouch," Gabriel said. "Case number on the outside. Name smaller below it."

He looked at her then, full on, and Marisol saw exactly how near they were to losing each other again, not to estrangement this time but to arithmetic. To his conclusion that she had finally become what he had accused her of being from the start: a woman who could count bodies and still proceed.

"We were trying to stop the train," she said.

"You did stop it. For twenty-one minutes."

"Teresa cut the quarantine."

"And we handed her the blade."

Marisol put the basil on the edge of the bunk because her fingers had begun to cramp around the cup. Soil marked the metal. "If we'd gotten outside eyes on the spur in time, the reconciliation lock would've missed."

Gabriel's laugh was worse than anger. It sounded tired and disbelieving. "Listen to yourself. You still sound like the forms when you panic."

She heard it too. Lock, spur, timing: the language of mechanism arrived first because mechanism was what she had built her courage out of. Underneath it sat the old man on the bridge with his heart failing like bad current.

"His death is on Teresa," she said, and hated how incomplete it sounded the instant it was spoken.

"No," Gabriel said. "Not just on Teresa."

The car around them went quiet in the specific way crowded spaces did when everybody was pretending not to hear and hearing every word anyway. Somewhere down the bay, a child coughed in sleep. The speaker clicked and failed to announce anything.

Gabriel leaned forward. His ink-stained fingers laced together so hard the knuckles whitened.

"You don't get to hand this all upward because it got ugly," he said. "That's exactly how they built the line. Everybody does the part they can live with and sends the rest higher. She rushed it. Bellido sealed the kit. The officers shoved. Intake took his pills. The route cooked him. And you and Paloma gave them a fire to run through. That's the truth now."

Marisol kept her eyes on him because looking away would have been cowardice and because he had earned witness from her if nothing else. "I know."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

His jaw worked. For a second she thought he might shout. Instead he got quieter still.

"Then know this too. The dead belong partly to your ledger now. Mine too, maybe, because I helped hold him up and I helped build this with you. But don't come in here asking me to make it clean so you can keep going."

The words hit harder because he did not raise them.

Naila unwound one turn of string from her fingers. "If we stop speaking his name, they keep him," she said.

Gabriel shut his eyes briefly. "I know."

"If we keep speaking it," Marisol said, "they keep Paloma anyway."

"Yes," Naila said. "That's the kind of day this is."

There was more wisdom in that spare sentence than Marisol felt she deserved to stand near. Naila's face stayed still, but grief had gone into the precision of her posture, into the way she held the string as if counting was now the only wall between memory and procedure.

Marisol said, "I need to know if you're out."

It was the most honest question she had asked all day.

Gabriel looked at the dirt she had left on the bunk from the basil cup, then at his own hands, then past her toward the window where the sealed cars glared white in the late light and rust showed through the seams no amount of paint ever kept covered for long.

"I can't answer that while he's still warm," he said.

Refusal would have been simpler. What Gabriel gave her was truth without comfort.

Marisol picked up the basil again because leaving it felt wrong and because carrying small living things was sometimes the only work that did not insult the dead. At the aisle she stopped.

"His fan is with property," she said. "One of the silver fish too."

Gabriel did not turn. "Then don't let them throw them away."

She went from the reserve car to the property cage because Paloma had told her to and because orders from the people she had failed still felt more solid than anything management said. The cage sat beside the inventory office under failing fluorescent tubes. Plastic pouches hung by case number in long rows that clicked softly when the fans turned on. There were shoes without laces, cheap combs, a hearing aid in a crackled case, and a child's sock stiff with old wash and not enough soap. The corridor could take a person down to four ounces of object in under an hour if it had paperwork ready.

Julián Arce's pouch was still warm from the yard. Someone had tagged it for transfer casualty review and set it apart from the others on a gray tray. Marisol opened it because nobody stopped her and because sometimes rank still worked by muscle memory even when it should not have.

Inside lay seven white tablets folded in wax paper, a pocket comb with two broken teeth, the paper fan made from the meal notice, and three little foil fish flattened nearly smooth from his body weight and the bridge floor. On the outside of the pouch the printer had put the case number in large type and the name beneath in letters half the size.

Marisol took a marker from the desk and wrote `JULIAN ARCE` across the top in block capitals so dark they bled through the plastic and blurred the tiny printed case number beneath. The gesture would not raise him, get Paloma her room back, or wash the smell of hot urine and failed compressions from her hands.

Small, useless, necessary, it meant the next person to touch the pouch would see his name first.

Then she stood under the failing fluorescent light with the basil in one hand and the dead man's property in the other while the train, delayed but not stopped, groaned somewhere beyond the wall like a machine clearing its throat before asking the night shift for more.
