# Chapter 7

By the time Marisol came down the service stairs at Caldera Junction, the platform already looked stripped for evidence.

A folding compliance table had been opened under the tin awning where crew usually smoked after shift. Two portable printers sat on it beside sealed evidence envelopes, a biometric reader, and a sweating cooler of bottled water that nobody had been invited to touch. Teresa Ordonez stood behind the table in an immaculate white shell uniform that had gone dark under the arms anyway. Her makeup held. Her cuticles did not. She kept worrying one bitten thumb with the edge of a thumbnail while a junior audit clerk beside her peeled at the blister on her heel through the slit in a brand-new shoe.

The train had rolled in forty minutes earlier with hot axles, a night of outage stink still trapped in the connector joints, and blackwater sloshing low in the tanks. Marisol had not slept. She still had mosquito bites up both forearms from Marsh Crescent. The dried salt on her collar rubbed like sandpaper each time she turned her head. She saw the compliance table, Teresa, the security pair in dark vests, and knew the deep clean had finished becoming a story.

Crew were lined up along the awning posts with their passcards already out. No one was pretending this was routine. Ivo stood halfway down the row, heavy shoulders bent, grease under his nails still black after a scrub. He had a paper cup of machine coffee in one hand and the folded court letter about his child-support review peeking from his shirt pocket because he read bad news more than once when it did not change. Paloma had come over from the trauma room in a fresh gown already damp at the spine. Her silvering braid was still pinned tight, but wisps had escaped around her ears. The room-smell of camphor and disinfectant came with her into the open air.

Teresa saw Marisol and did not raise her voice.

"Supervisor Anaya. At last."

There were people all along the platform, but Teresa made the two words sound private and disciplinary at the same time. Marisol crossed the concrete. Heat came up through the soles of her shoes. Somewhere under the cars a pump kicked, failed to catch, and kicked again.

"We had a rolling-stock outage at Marsh," Marisol said. "The connector fans died and Car 8 backed up on blackwater alarms. We came in hot."

"You came in late," Teresa said. "You came in with medical timestamps that do not match corridor footage and sanitation holds that do not match dispatch notice. Give me your card."

Marisol had imagined arguments on the ride in. She had built them out of valves, doors, temperatures, the old competence that had gotten her promoted one rung at a time over twenty years of mess nobody else wanted. None of those arguments survived the sight of Teresa's compliance table and the crew lined up to watch. Marisol slid her passcard from the lanyard and set it on the table.

The junior clerk took it with both hands, as if it were something breakable. The blister on her heel had burst and bled through the nude stocking into the shoe lining. She fed the card into the reader. Green lit first. Then amber. Then a hard red bar crossed the small screen.

Teresa put on her reading glasses, discovered they had been in her breast pocket all along, and looked down at the tablet.

"As of now," she said, "your credentials are reduced to sanitation-supervision access on this route only. No manifest editing. No property-room access. No red-seal restraint inventory. No unscheduled car transit beyond sanitation and service corridors. If you need to enter a custody or medical zone, you will do so under escort and with written justification attached to a time stamp. Blackwater readiness now requires secondary sign-off from compliance until further notice."

No one moved. Marisol heard the words as if Teresa were bolting metal over doors inside her body. Manifest editing gone. Property room gone. Red-seal key authority gone. No walking the length of the train because she knew where she had to be. No drifting near Car 8 on a pretext. No reaching Gabriel on a lie that looked enough like work to pass.

"That's not necessary," Marisol said. "You know what the Marsh segment does to the clocks when power drops. We had detainees vomiting in the heat. We had a restraint line jam at the trauma threshold. I logged what mattered."

Teresa looked up. Her face stayed composed, but the patience had thinned enough for the plain woman under it to show through.

"What mattered was chain of custody," she said. "What mattered was that a route supervisor did not create thirty-nine loose minutes around a delayed car already flagged by dispatch. You do not get to decide, after the fact, which minutes matter."

The people along the awning kept their eyes lowered in the way workers did when they wanted to see everything. Marisol could feel them taking stock of the new balance. A supervisor being corrected was one thing. A supervisor being publicly unlocked from her own authority was another. It rearranged the air.

Teresa picked up the card and laid a narrow orange strip across the printed supervisor band. The adhesive went on crooked. She peeled it back, straightened it with irritated precision, and pressed again.

"Wear this visibly," she said. "If any door logs you outside your temporary scope, security will respond, not me."

She handed the card back. Marisol clipped it to her damp shirt. The plastic hit her sternum with no weight at all, which was worse than if Teresa had hung a brick there.

"Director," Marisol said, because the crew was listening and she would not beg by first name in front of them. "Car 8 still needs an experienced hand. That crew knows my voice. We have passengers who calm faster when they know the same person is coming through the door."

"Then they will have an opportunity to demonstrate flexibility."

Teresa tapped her tablet twice. One of the security officers stepped forward. He was a broad man Marisol knew as Barrera, a custody sergeant who wore a pink elastic around his wrist for his little girl's braids and snapped it against his skin when he was bored. He handed Teresa a transfer slip without speaking.

Teresa read from it.

"Case 4471-R is being moved to restraint tier three pending reconciliation review and contact-irregularity assessment."

Marisol felt the platform tilt half an inch under her shoes. She kept her face level because Barrera was watching and Teresa would enjoy less than a flinch. She had known it might come to this the minute the audit table appeared. Knowing a blow was possible did not take the shape out of it when it landed.

"On what basis?" she asked.

"On the basis that your route has already demonstrated poor boundary discipline," Teresa said. "On the basis that a delayed case with disputed ancestry documentation now carries elevated exposure. On the basis that I do not intend to explain another preventable irregularity to investors during heat season."

The transfer team was already at the connector. Marisol heard cuffs before she saw them: metal against metal, a drag and answer drag through the tunnel between cars. Gabriel came into view between two officers in dark vests. His hands were boxed in black transport mitts attached to a waist chain. The bruise along his ribs showed through the scrub shirt where sweat had darkened the cloth. Somebody had split the scab at his left eyebrow again during the move. Blood had run a clean thread down the side of his nose and dried there. He was thinner than the first day she saw him in intake. Or maybe higher-security restraints simply drew the body tighter, pared it down to what could still be controlled.

He did not turn his head much. That was the part that told her how bad the new tier was going to be. Gabriel always looked where he wanted, even when it cost him. Now he kept himself forward because he knew exactly how far a guard's patience reached inside a transfer tunnel.

The service door nearest the connector had always opened for Marisol. She had spent years walking through it with mop handles, med coolers, meal bins, broken latch reports, things that made the route keep pretending it was just a difficult workplace instead of a machine for moving human beings out of sight. When Gabriel's chain caught briefly on the threshold rail, she stepped toward the door on reflex, card already in hand.

The reader flashed red. The lock did not click.

The sound was small, a dead electronic refusal, but it carried. Barrera's mouth moved at one corner. Teresa did not smile. She only said, "The door is functioning correctly."

Gabriel must have heard that. He lifted his face then, not fully toward her, just enough for the old scar to catch the light and for Marisol to see recognition pass over him like contempt held under discipline. Then Barrera jerked the chain loose and the team took him on into the higher-security car, where the mesh partitions were narrower and the water came through timed nozzles because even thirst had to be metered when the company had decided someone was a management problem.

Marisol stood with her useless card in her hand until the connector doors sealed.

Teresa closed the tablet cover.

"You are not suspended," she said, giving the words the careful shape of mercy. "I advise you to understand the difference."

Marisol looked at the orange strip on her badge, at Barrera's pink elastic, at the junior clerk shifting weight off the blistered heel she would have to squeeze back into her shoe for the ride home. Everybody on the platform had some petty damage strapped to them. That was part of how the line kept running. The damage did not make Teresa gentler. It only made her better at pricing what other people could afford to lose.

"What do you want me doing?" Marisol asked.

"What you should have been doing already," Teresa said. "Keep the route sanitary, keep your paperwork exact, and stop treating procedure as a place where you can improvise a personality."

She moved on to the next worker. The audit did not need Marisol's humiliation any further. It had entered the crew on its own.

Marisol stepped away from the table because standing still would have looked like pleading. Ivo did not meet her eyes as she passed. Paloma did, and what Marisol saw there was not pity. It was the flat alertness of someone in a medical room who has just heard the wrong monitor change pitch.

***

By noon the heat had turned every metal surface in the trauma room tacky under the hand.

Marisol was there because sanitation still opened for her. Paloma had called down for a mop and absorbent powder after a transfer patient vomited over the edge of the treatment cot. Under the new rules, it took a custodial escort to get Marisol through the first medical door, but once she was inside the room the officer stayed out in the corridor, grateful for any excuse not to breathe the place.

The patient was a man in his fifties with graying stubble and one church shoe still on, black leather polished by habit if not by current circumstance. The other foot wore only a thin transport sock turned inside out at the heel. His name on the chart rack was Rufino Salcedo. He kept apologizing every time pain made him louder.

"Forgive me," he said through clenched teeth as Paloma cut the shirt away from his shoulder. "Forgive me, doctora. I know you have work."

"Shut up and breathe," Paloma said, not unkindly. "Apologies do not reduce dislocations."

Rufino gave a wet laugh that broke in the middle. Somebody had dragged him hard enough during the tier transfer to pull the humeral head half out of the socket. His right shoulder stood wrong under the skin, a pointed little geography of damage. There was also a split on his lower lip and a ridge of raised flesh where the transport cuff had chewed the wrist. He smelled of old sweat, cheap soap, and the cumin that had gotten into the seam of his church shoe years ago and never left. Marisol wondered who packed formal shoes for a transfer corridor. Then she saw the careful shine on the toe and understood that Rufino had not packed them for the corridor. He had packed them because some habits were the last dry things a man could carry.

Paloma glanced at the med drawer, then at the clock over the sink, then at the chart. Her right hand trembled once in the air before she steadied it with the left.

"Hold him," she said.

Marisol set the mop aside and came to the cot. Rufino's skin was furnace-hot. When she braced his chest, she could feel the little skipped catches in his breath where pain kept trying to turn breathing into something smaller.

"This will go fast," Paloma said.

"No, no, wait," Rufino said. "Please, just tell me when."

"Now."

Paloma pulled the arm down and out. The shoulder went back with a thick, damp clunk that Marisol felt through the mattress frame. Rufino screamed once, high and young, and then bent sideways to vomit bile over the cot rail. Marisol got her hand under his forehead before he hit metal. The bile splashed her sleeve and ran warm into the seam at her wrist, right over the old chemical-burn scar. Outside in the corridor the custody officer swore and took two disgusted steps farther away.

Rufino sobbed twice without tears. Then the pain changed shape. Marisol knew that kind of change. A body never got kinder after a joint went back in. It simply stopped being one emergency and became another.

Paloma reached for the locked med drawer.

The officer at the door leaned in just far enough to make a rule.

"Director froze controlled pulls until audit clears," he said. He had a strawberry cough drop bulging one cheek and the red outline of a wedding ring on a finger that had gone bare for shift. "No ketorolac, no tramadol, no nothing without compliance sign-off."

Paloma kept her hand on the drawer.

"He needs pain control."

"Then call compliance."

"Compliance can come listen to his shoulder for the next four hours."

The officer shrugged. He was not cruel in any original way. He was a tired man with a cough drop and an order that cost him nothing personally. That was worse. Original cruelty had to be maintained. This was just transfer of burden.

"Chart it," he said. "If they clear it, they clear it."

He stepped back out before Paloma could answer. The trauma-room door swung almost shut, leaving only the strip of corridor light and the smell of hot uniforms.

Rufino had gone gray around the mouth. He whispered, "Please," not theatrically, not even very loudly. He sounded embarrassed to still exist at the center of the room.

Paloma opened the chart and stared at the last entries.

"What time did they bring him in?" Marisol asked.

"Officially? Eleven thirty-eight." Paloma looked at the clock. "The drawer freeze posted at eleven twenty."

Rufino was shaking now, not from fear alone but from the body discovering fresh pain all at once now that the shoulder sat where it belonged. Paloma's braid had come loose another inch. Sweat tracked down the side of her face into the collar of the gown. Marisol knew that look on her. It was the look Paloma got when a person was being treated as paperwork first and tissue second.

"Don't," Marisol said quietly.

Paloma turned.

"Don't do what?"

"Don't do anything for me."

That made Paloma give her a very brief, exhausted smile.

"This isn't for you."

She pulled the drawer, counted by touch, drew the dose, and set the syringe on the tray. Then she took up her pen and wrote in the chart with neat, balanced letters that did not hurry even though the lie had already begun. Marisol saw the line as it formed.

11:17 - ketorolac IM administered after transfer injury, observation continued.

Paloma capped the pen. Her hand trembled after the fact, not during.

"If compliance asks," she said, "he got here before the freeze. He was already under observation before your missing minutes turned into a sermon."

Marisol looked at the chart, then at the syringe.

"You don't know that helps me."

"I know exactly what it helps."

Paloma gave Rufino the shot. He flinched, cursed softly, then apologized again because apology was apparently lodged in him deeper than pain. Paloma slapped a cotton square over the injection site and held it there longer than necessary.

"Stop saying sorry," she told him. "Save your mouth for breathing."

Rufino nodded. The tremor in his jaw eased by degrees.

Paloma stripped the gloves off and dropped them into the bio bin. She did not look at Marisol while she spoke next.

"He's your son."

Marisol did not answer. The room had the hot, chemical stillness that came after vomiting was wiped but before the air admitted it had changed. Water knocked in the pipes somewhere above the ceiling panels. Rufino had closed his eyes, either from relief or from the need to leave the room without moving.

"You don't have to tell me," Paloma said. "I saw your face when they moved him. I've known you too long to mistake that kind of stupidity."

There was no point pretending now. Not with the chart still wet, the syringe wrapper open, Rufino half sedated on a lie Paloma had chosen for reasons larger than friendship and smaller than revolution.

"Yes," Marisol said.

Paloma nodded once, as if confirming a symptom she had already diagnosed.

"All right."

That was all she gave the fact. No gasp, no pity, no speech about blood. She took the soiled pillowcase from under Rufino's head and rolled it inward to keep the bile contained.

"I wondered why you would risk housing debt and your own job for one detainee you barely knew," she said. "It's uglier, but it makes sense."

Marisol leaned both hands on the cot rail. It had rust pitting under the paint near one bolt head. Someone had cleaned around it for years without ever getting the rust out. Her whole life on the line seemed to sit inside that little red-brown bloom: managed, never removed.

"I didn't ask you to cross for me."

"I crossed for his shoulder." Paloma looked at her then, tired eyes hard. "And because if Teresa starts pulling every loose minute around that car, she won't stop at you. She will crawl through my drawer counts, my retainer, my visa file, every basil plant I've ever tried to keep alive in that room. So don't make the mistake of thinking care and self-interest are enemies. Most decent things on this line are both."

That sounded more like Paloma than anything noble would have.

Rufino stirred and touched the toe of his church shoe with the inside foot as if checking it was still there. Even half drugged, he did not want to lose it.

"Thank you, doctora," he murmured.

"If you thank me again, I will reduce the other shoulder out of spite," Paloma said.

Rufino almost smiled.

Marisol picked up the mop and cleaned the bile properly because work was still the shape her hands understood. Paloma filed the chart in the rack with the fresh lie buried inside it among temperatures, pulse rates, and intake times. When the floor was done and the absorbent powder had gone pale with fluid, Marisol rinsed the mop head in the slop sink. Gray water spiraled, hit the trap, and went on down the route she knew better than any prayer.

Paloma was washing her hands when she said, "Listen to me."

Marisol turned.

"If you're going to keep doing whatever this is," Paloma said, very quietly, "you don't get to do it like a mother running on instinct. Instinct is what gets people dragged between cars. You do it with numbers, timing, and an exit for the innocent, or you stop."

Marisol thought of Gabriel disappearing into tier three with the mitts on his hands. She thought of the red flash on the door reader and Teresa saying it was functioning correctly. She thought of Rufino trying to apologize his way out of pain.

"I heard you."

Paloma dried her hands. The tremor was back now that the choice had been made and could not be unmade.

"Good," she said. "Take that shirt to laundry. If audit sees bile on your sleeve, they will chart even your vomit against me."

***

The all-hands meeting happened in the crew cafeteria at the worst hour of the afternoon, when the roof trapped heat low over the tables and the vending machines glowed like exhausted altars.

Nobody had enough appetite for the steam-table food. They still took trays because shifts had taught them to eat when eating was possible, not when hunger arrived. Marisol sat with a sweating cup of station coffee and a bowl of rice that had gone stiff at the edges. The orange strip on her badge made people look twice before they looked away. It was not formal demotion. It was worse. It was visible uncertainty.

Teresa stood near the condiment counter with a printed memo in her hand and her glasses on top of her head where she had forgotten them again. A floor fan turned beside her, clicking on one bent blade. Workers filled the room in patchy silence. Ivo took the chair across from Marisol but angled himself half out into the aisle as if ready to leave at the first bad sentence. Lina Paredes from laundry sat two tables over, blue fish-market gloves still stuffed in her back pocket from the station side job she worked on weekends. She had a crescent of silver hair dye at one temple where she had done her own roots in a bathroom mirror and missed a spot. She kept smoothing the same folded housing-office notice on the table as if ironing paper flat could lower the amount printed on it.

Teresa did not waste time pretending the meeting had a benevolent purpose.

"You all know we are under route-integrity review," she said. "What some of you may not know is how quickly repeated delays become payroll consequences."

The room changed at once. Not louder. Tighter.

"The corridor benchmark for heat season allows a very narrow band of discretionary overtime before recovery review begins," Teresa continued. "If this route misses recovery targets, overtime will be restricted to director-approved emergency labor only. Housing subsidies tied to corridor employment can also be recalculated at recovery rate rather than concession rate. Contract renewals and visa protections attached to active line service may then be reviewed in sequence."

No one had to ask what reviewed in sequence meant. It meant first the hours, then the room, then the paperwork that let a person keep the room. It meant the line reached into your paycheck and your mattress with the same hand.

Lina spoke without raising her hand. Fear had taken care of protocol.

"Recovery rate starting when?"

Teresa looked at her.

"Next cycle, if we do not recover."

Lina pressed the folded notice flatter. "My mother sleeps in that unit. She has the fan by the window because of her lungs."

"Then I suggest all of us take recovery seriously," Teresa said.

She said it with that managerial softness that always carried the shape of threat more efficiently than shouting would have. Marisol watched the room absorb it. A dishwasher stopped scraping trays. Somebody at the back whispered a curse and then did not repeat it. Ivo sat very still. When he got frightened enough to calculate, he stopped joking entirely.

Teresa kept going.

"I know many of you support extended family. I know summer earnings matter. They matter to me as well. But I will not permit undisciplined holds, undocumented favors, or personal improvisations to put this route into a spiral that harms the very workers who keep it operational."

Undocumented favors. Personal improvisations. She never said Marisol's name. She did not have to. The orange strip hanging off Marisol's chest had already become a paragraph.

"Any worker who observes deviations in timekeeping, med pulls, sanitation notices, or custody movement is expected to report them up the correct chain," Teresa said. "That is not disloyalty. That is the difference between earning through summer and watching payroll claw back what your families already spent."

She folded the memo once and set it on the condiment counter beside the mustard pump. The meeting was over. No vote, no questions worth asking. Just information delivered in the language the company trusted most: rent, hours, visas, mothers on fans by windows.

People did not rise immediately. They stayed where they were a few seconds longer, the way detainees sometimes stayed seated after a door opened because movement had become too costly to waste. Then the room came apart into low talk.

Lina looked straight at Marisol across the aisle.

"Was it your car?" she asked.

There it was. No accusation in the grammar. No deference either. Just a worker trying to locate the hand around her own throat.

Marisol set down her cup.

"My car had the hold," she said.

"And now they want two hundred more off my check if they call it recovery." Lina's voice stayed level, but she kept rubbing the housing notice with her thumb until the paper softened. "I can take a late train. I can't take my mother down three flights in August because somebody wanted to prove a point."

Marisol could have denied, soothed, gone procedural. She knew all the old supervisor lines. We're working it. Payroll always overstates first review. Let me check the numbers. None of them would have sounded like truth in that room.

"I know what they're threatening," she said.

"Do you." Lina stood up with her tray still full. "Because when they threaten you, they threaten a salary. When they threaten me, they threaten a door code."

She left before Marisol could answer. Her fish-market gloves knocked against the back of her thigh with each step like a small, practical metronome counting labor nobody saw in the official chart.

Ivo watched her go.

"She's not wrong," he said.

His voice stayed low, almost conversational, which made it land harder. Marisol looked at the court letter in his pocket.

"I didn't say she was."

"No. But you still have the habit of thinking fear makes people stupid. Usually it makes them exact."

He stirred his coffee with the handle of a plastic spoon because the spoon bowl had cracked off. Grease under the nails, neck burn scar half hidden by collar, mechanic's gait even while sitting still. All of him built from things under strain.

"Do you think I caused this?" Marisol asked.

Ivo considered that longer than comfort required.

"I think somebody made Teresa look slow," he said. "I think she billed that feeling to the rest of us before the hour was over. I think the housing office has been waiting all summer for a reason to pretend their deductions are neutral math."

That was not absolution. It was not refusal either. It was a machinist's answer, all force transfer and load path.

"And you?" Marisol said. "What are you waiting for?"

He let the cracked spoon handle go. It spun once in the coffee and stopped.

"A number I can live with."

There was a whole sad life inside that line. Pension vesting, court review, overtime, the child whose support letter he reread like prayer or punishment. Marisol had asked him for one lie already. She heard, in the space after his answer, exactly how expensive a second one might be.

At the back table two sanitation attendants stopped talking when she looked over. One of them, Tomas from pump maintenance, kept a folded city bus map in his wallet because he still did not trust the corridor app to tell him when service had been cut. The other, Celia Morn, had a lunch bag covered in cartoon strawberries because her granddaughter had declared plain black too depressing for work. Both of them glanced at the orange strip on Marisol's badge and then away, quickly, not wanting to be caught being caught.

Marisol knew that look. Detainees gave versions of it when rumor moved faster than orders. Workers gave it when payroll notices hit. It was not hatred. Hatred had heat in it. This was colder. This was people measuring distance from a thing that might spread.

"They think I'm radioactive," she said.

Ivo snorted once without humor.

"No. Radioactive pays cleanup premiums."

For a second she almost smiled. Then he rubbed the bridge of his nose and added, "They think you're the sort of problem management will solve by taking it out of their rent."

That was closer.

The fan clicked. Trays knocked. Somewhere outside, a coupling slammed hard enough to rattle the condiment packets. Teresa walked past the cafeteria windows with two audit staff behind her, already moving on to the next room she intended to make orderly by threat.

Marisol looked down at her badge. The orange strip had already started lifting at one corner from sweat. Under it, half hidden, was the old supervisor band with her name and clearance level still printed like a memory of authority the machine no longer recognized.

She took her untouched bowl to the return rack and went back out into the corridor. The route smelled of fryer oil, bleach, wet uniforms, overheated plastic, and the thin sour note of fear that no cleanser ever quite got. Her downgraded card opened the sanitation cage on the first try. Green light there. Bleach tablets, mop heads, absorbent powder, all the things the company still trusted her with because they cleaned up after other people's decisions.

Beyond the cage door, down the corridor that led toward the restraint cars, a pair of attendants saw her and lowered their voices. One stepped aside to let her pass, too quickly, as if proximity itself could bill them later. Marisol kept walking.

What Teresa had done at the compliance table was simple enough: take access, narrow movement, make an example. That was management work. It had rules. What settled into Marisol now was uglier because it did not belong only to Teresa. It belonged to every worker whose room key, overtime slip, visa stamp, or parent on a fan by the window could be priced against one more unexplained delay.

She had known from the start that management would come for her if she touched the schedule. She had not understood until this afternoon that the line had trained the rest of them to come away from her too.

At the far end of the corridor a red security light pulsed over the sealed door to tier three. Her card would not open it. Neither, now, would the old habit of being needed. She stood there a moment anyway, listening to the vibration under the floor and the timed hiss of water somewhere behind the door, thinking of Gabriel in the narrower mesh, of Rufino trying not to lose one polished shoe, of Lina smoothing the housing notice flat with the side of her hand.

When she turned back toward the sanitation cage, workers coming off shift made space for her without touching. That was the part that stayed with her. Not Teresa's speech. Not the orange strip.

The space.
