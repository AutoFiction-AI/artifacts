# Chapter 3

The cafeteria at Caldera Junction smelled of fryer grease, wet denim, and the thin ammoniac sweetness that lifted off a crew after a full intake day. Southbound staging had already taken Gabriel out of Marisol's sight before the layover meal opened. She carried her tray with both hands and kept her face arranged for anyone who looked up. Her left knee had gone wooden on the metal stairs, and the stew trembled in its compartment like something barely set. The windows facing the commuter platform were filmed white from old detergent. Beyond them, on the maintenance track, the deportation cars sat in their patient row with the white paint failing at the seams. On the other side of the glass a commuter train slid in clean and silver, doors opening for people who still got to decide where they were going.

She took the corner table by the dead vending machine for the view of both doors. The route had trained that into her: clock the exits, sit where you could see who came through. Her hair was pinned so tight the skin at her temples ached. Bleach had dried the backs of her hands into a fine white nap, except over the old burn on her right wrist, where the skin never roughened evenly again. She set down the tray, sat carefully, and listened to the room: forks on plastic, boots dragging, a cook cursing in the steam line, the constant low vibration from the yard under all of it. The whole junction seemed built to remind a body that metal did not rest merely because people did.

Paloma arrived first, compact and square in her scrubs under an unbuttoned work shirt, silver braid darkened with sweat down her back. She carried her own tray in one hand and two little rinse cups in the other, each holding a basil cutting in water gone green at the stems. She set the cups in the strip of evening light on the table before she sat, more carefully than she set down the food.

"If I leave them in the dorm," she said, "those idiots will strip them for noodles."

Marisol looked at the cups. "You've brought the whole windowsill with you."

"I've got four survivors. That's not a windowsill. That's stubbornness."

Ivo dropped into the third chair with the rolling gait Marisol had known for years, his weight shifting half a beat ahead of every step, ready for the floor to betray him. He had grease under the nails no brush ever reached, and the small burn scar high on his neck had gone pink in the heat. A folded maintenance docket and an opened county envelope were crammed together in his coverall pocket. He slid a heel out of one boot under the table and made a face like a man removing a nail from lumber.

"What's dying?" he said.

"Everything on your tray," Paloma said. "Also those cuttings if the dorm boys find them."

Ivo looked into the stew. "No, those are safe. Nothing grows in that."

Paloma snorted. Marisol almost did. The laugh stayed shallow in her chest. Gabriel's face kept rising underneath every ordinary thing that tried to sit on top of it: the old scar through his eyebrow, the bruising under one eye already yellowing toward green, the clear property bag hanging from a hook in intake with the recorder inside it like an organ sealed for transport.

Paloma noticed what Marisol had not touched before Marisol did. "Eat while it's hot."

"It's not hot," Ivo said. "It's historically warm."

"Eat while it's historically warm, then."

Marisol broke the skin of the stew with her spoon. Steam rose carrying onion, powdered stock, and something that tried to be beef and never had the muscle for it.

"You stole those from the trauma room?" Marisol asked, nodding at the cups.

"I propagated them from the trauma room," Paloma said. "Stole is for narcotics and payroll."

"Same building," Ivo said.

He took the folded maintenance docket from his pocket and used it to level the bad leg of the table. His phone buzzed almost at once. He glanced at the screen, and the lines around his mouth eased before he could stop them.

"Lina sent me a picture from the school yard." He dug his phone from his breast pocket, thumbed to a blurred image, and set it between the cups. "Wanted to know if it's a kestrel."

Paloma leaned over. "That's a pigeon."

"A talented pigeon," Ivo said.

"A sick pigeon," Paloma said. "Look at the feathers."

He glanced at Marisol. "Tell her."

Marisol looked down at the screen because looking at a bird was easier than looking at anything with paperwork attached to it. The photo showed a lump of gray-brown feathers on a chain-link fence, breast puffed against the heat. In the corner a child had caught half her own thumb.

"Pigeon," she said. "Kestrels don't sit like that."

"Thank you," Paloma said.

Ivo took back the phone with exaggerated hurt. "My own table. Two women against me."

"Your daughter asked a question," Paloma said. "Now you can answer it without lying."

"She likes the hawk answer better."

"Children like candy better than soup too," Paloma said. "That doesn't make soup a conspiracy."

"This soup is absolutely a conspiracy."

The room around them kept up its own tired commerce. A group from the refit bay argued about coolant leaks. Someone at the next table was filing a torn nail with the edge of a keycard. A ceiling fan chopped hot air into slightly differently shaped hot air. Over the cafeteria speakers a synthetic voice announced the commuter departure for Platform C in English and Spanish, clipped and pleasant, a voice made for departure times and not for restraint foam slick with vomit, not for children in the white cars on the maintenance line.

Marisol ate under their eyes. In a room where worry was contagious, untouched food announced it too loudly. The spoon clicked once against her teeth. She kept seeing Gabriel in the trauma room, shirt lifted, ribs mapped under skin that had gone mean and lean from transport food. He had always been thin. Even as a boy he had looked built from long twine and grievance. It was the bruises that would not leave her alone. They had shape. She knew from one glance what buckle had made one mark and what grip had made the other. She knew too much about what left color on a body and how long each color took to clear.

Paloma had begun rubbing her right hand with the heel of the left, pressing the tremor down into the knuckles where it might pass for strain. The movement was small, practiced, almost private.

"You're favoring the knee again," she said without looking up.

"Everybody's favoring something," Marisol said.

"That wasn't the question."

Ivo pulled his boot back on and stamped his heel once. "My right foot feels like somebody hid a bolt head in the sole."

"That's because your fascia is shot and you buy boots like a martyr," Paloma said.

"I buy boots like a man with court arrears."

She lifted her eyes. "How much left?"

"Too much to be dignified, not enough to be dramatic."

"That's not a number."

"Six-eight, last time they mailed me something threatening."

Paloma made a face that mixed pity with diagnosis. "And you spent part of your layover at the pension office anyway."

"Had to. They lost a quarter again."

"A quarter of what?"

"A quarter year. On paper I apparently took three months off in 2037 to enjoy myself."

Paloma smiled. "Did you?"

"If I'd done that I wouldn't still be here eating a gravy crime under fluorescent light."

Marisol said, "It was the axle seizure in spring. They booked the shutdown wrong."

Both of them looked at her. The answer had come too fast, too exact. She could hear it herself, the way stress turned her into a woman who corrected labels while the room burned.

Ivo tore bread. "See, that's why I sit with you. I've got a memory problem and you were born mad at a ledger."

Paloma studied Marisol's face a beat longer than she needed to. "What did you come in here carrying?"

The question was soft. It went nowhere soft inside Marisol. She set down the spoon, wiped her mouth with the paper napkin, and bought herself the two seconds it took to lay a smaller lie over the larger one.

"A manifest mismatch," she said. "One of intake's scans bounced twice and came through with the wrong property count. If I don't fix it before southbound, it'll breed another six forms."

Paloma kept watching her. "Property count doesn't put that look on you."

"Blackwater alarm did," Marisol said. "Two of them. Car Nine burped back through the rear stack and the new kid on hose detail panicked and kinked the line. I spent an hour ankle-deep in somebody else's breakfast. There. Better story?"

It was too detailed, which made it more believable to anyone who did not know her stress well. Paloma knew it. Paloma also knew when not to pry where a body had already braced.

"Fine," she said. "Then after this you need heat on the knee and six hours horizontal."

"You prescribing horizontal now?"

"For workers? Constantly. Nobody listens."

Ivo pointed with his spoon. "See, that's why medics go mean. You keep giving orders to people who refuse repair."

"Mechanics don't refuse repair?"

"Mechanics postpone repair until catastrophic failure. It's a more honorable trade."

That got a real laugh out of Paloma, brief and low. It bent her over the tray for a second and showed the tiredness in her neck. Marisol held on to the sound. Keeping anything ordinary in place had become difficult work. Paloma smelling of camphor and disinfectant, Ivo grumbling about pension math, the three of them under a bad fan with a tray between them: it belonged to a world where the worst thing in front of Marisol was a swollen knee and not the fact that her son was sitting restrained two tracks over under a country's name that was attached to him by a dead man and a treaty formula.

Ivo ate two more spoons of stew, gave up, and pushed the tray away. "Storm's still sitting north."

"You can tell by your aching foot?" Paloma asked.

"By the coupler seals whining all afternoon. By the charge in the catwalk rails."

"Now the rails are gossiping to you."

"Everything gives warning before it quits."

Over the cafeteria speakers the synthetic voice cut in again, this time routing southbound exceptions: medical holds to trauma, property discrepancies to records, final reconciliation on Cars Seven and Eight before lock. Marisol's spoon stopped halfway down.

Paloma saw it at once. "Is it a body?"

Marisol set the spoon onto the tray. "It's a case."

"That wasn't the question."

The speaker rolled on to commuter boarding, bright and useless. Ivo was no longer watching the platform. He was watching Marisol.

"Sol," Paloma said, quieter now, "if this is one of mine, don't make that call on your handheld. Use the wall phone by pensions. Those route slabs rat on you cleaner than people do."

"It isn't medical."

Paloma's mouth tightened. "Bodies don't stop being medical because paperwork got there first."

Ivo wiped his fingers on the paper fork sleeve. "I've got an open coupler complaint on Car Eight and records still owes me the overflow sheet. If you need ten minutes off the map, you just remembered that for me."

"I didn't ask either of you for cover."

"You wore it in here," he said.

Paloma finished half her bread and wrapped the rest in a napkin for later. She wasted nothing edible and almost nothing green. "I've got to get back before intake decides another split lip is dehydration."

"You ever take a full break?" Ivo asked.

"Not since the company discovered understaffing."

"That's why your hand shakes."

"My hand shakes because I've been suturing idiots since sunrise," she said. "Different diagnosis."

She stood, gathering the cups. Her right hand shook once when she lifted the tray and she shifted the weight to the left without comment. Marisol stood too fast, as if the move had been waiting under her skin all meal, then regretted it when the knee caught and sent a hot grind up her thigh.

Paloma saw that and clicked her tongue. "Heat, Sol. Not stairs, not pride, not another shift of pretending your joints are made of policy."

"I've got one call to make."

"Then make it sitting down if you can."

"Look at you," Ivo said. "Medically bossed by a woman who can't finish a break."

"That's because I work with amateurs," Paloma said. "And if either of you touches my cups, I'll prescribe something ugly."

She left with her cups and wrapped bread, moving with that compact quickness she had even when exhausted, shoulders already turning toward the trauma-room corridor, already listening for the next body. Ivo watched her go.

"She's going to bolt herself to that trauma room one day," he said.

"She practically has now."

"Good. Somebody ought to keep one living thing on this route that isn't under inventory."

The sentence put a fine wire through Marisol's chest. She kept her face still. Ivo looked back at her then, really looked, the way a mechanic listened with his hands before he took a panel off.

"Car Eight?" he asked.

She folded the napkin once more, exact along the crease. "Don't do that."

He bent, pulled the folded maintenance docket out from under the bad table leg, and slid it across to her. "Then don't make me guess blind. Take this. If anybody asks, you're chasing my overflow sheet and signing the coupler note."

"You don't know what you're covering."

"I know it isn't a manifest mismatch."

There was the ghost of a smile at one corner of his mouth. It made him look younger and more tired at the same time. She had known that expression in a bunk once. In the junction light it was only an old tool left in the wrong drawer. He picked up his tray.

"I've got to check on that coupler complaint before night crew invents religion around it," he said. "If your call goes bad, find me on the platform."

"You should call Lina," Marisol said, because it was safer than answering what he had actually offered.

He grunted. "I will. I'll tell her what the experts said."

"Tell her pigeons survive everything," Marisol said.

"That's not a comfort."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

When he had gone, the folded docket was damp in Marisol's hand. The cafeteria grew louder for having fewer known faces in it. Marisol stacked her tray, left it at the return slot, and took the corridor toward the administrative wing where the crew phones hung. The hall was colder than the cafeteria because fewer bodies had breathed into it. Fluorescent tubes buzzed above her. A bulletin board outside the pension office held notices about hydration, a summer injury poster with a cartoon spine, and a payroll reminder about housing-deduction schedules. Someone had drawn a halo over the cartoon spine in blue pen.

She used the wall phone instead of her handheld. Route devices logged too cleanly, Paloma had named the risk aloud, and Marisol did not want this call attached to her name any more than it had to be. The handset smelled faintly of citrus sanitizer and older hands. She keyed in her supervisor number, then the case-services extension, then sat through the recorded menu in both languages while a synthetic woman explained that quality and compliance monitoring might occur. By the third prompt Marisol had dug the edge of her thumbnail under the paper label on the phone cradle and lifted half of it away.

A live voice came on at last. It was female, tired, neutral, mid-shift somewhere far from the heat in the yard.

"Corridor case services. Employee number, please."

Marisol gave it.

"How can I help you, Supervisor Anaya?"

"I've got a live southbound file out of Puerta Seco. Case four-four-seven-one-R. The receiving-state designation appears mismatched to subject history. I need a correction before release."

Keyboard clicks. Marisol could hear each one land hard and separate, little teeth on glass.

"Did intake validation clear the ancestry packet?" the voice asked.

"Yes. That's the problem."

"If the packet validated, the designation is not treated as a clerical error."

"It is an error."

"It falls under case-integrity review, Supervisor."

Marisol leaned her forehead briefly against the cool cinder-block wall. "Open one."

"This desk does not open review on a live route."

"Then who does?"

"Regional compliance can submit a case-integrity request. A court order can interrupt status. Receiving state refusal can interrupt at transfer."

Each sentence arrived smooth and properly spaced, no malice in it, which made it worse. A human voice had said the words, but the shape of them had already been decided somewhere else, in a template, in a training file, in a room full of people who never had to stand two feet from a restrained body and watch a bruise come in.

"I need somebody to look at the file now," Marisol said. "The train is at Caldera. We roll south tonight."

"I can log concern language and timestamp the contact."

"That won't hold dispatch."

"No, Supervisor."

"Then what good is it?"

There was the smallest pause, a worker checking which answer belonged to the slot.

"It preserves sequence," the woman said.

Marisol looked at the bulletin board across from her: hydration poster, payroll notice, cartoon spine with its stupid halo. She kept hearing the phrase preserve sequence. The line knew how to preserve sequence. It preserved sequence on restraint logs, on waste manifests, on the chain of custody for a child's inhaler taken at intake and never returned in the same country. Sequence was what systems said when they meant nobody was coming.

"Put it in, then," she said.

"I need concern language."

Marisol shut her eyes. What language could hold it? My son is not from the place you are sending him. A dead man's paper is driving a live body south. I saw the marks your intake team left on him and I need them not to become foreign soil. Instead she heard herself say, clipped and procedural because that was the only register the phone respected:

"Subject history appears inconsistent with designated receiving state. Request supervisory review of ancestry validation and attached source documents prior to transfer."

More keys.

"Logged," the woman said. "Ticket number K-nine-two-seven-one-six."

"Does regional compliance see it tonight?"

"I can't guarantee review time."

"Can you flag urgency?"

"It's already classified as active transport."

That was as far as the desk would bend. Marisol thanked her because years on the route had trained politeness into the muscles that took abuse and kept moving. She hung up before the woman could offer any scripted closing. The dial tone returned, level and indifferent. In the silence after it, the junction gave her back its real noises: a brake squeal from the commuter line, a cart rattling over a floor seam, someone laughing too hard two doors down.

She stood there with the useless ticket number in her head and understood the narrowness of what had just died. Hope had already taken hits. What failed here was her faith in a quiet clerical fix, the sort of back-channel adjustment that kept operations from embarrassing themselves. If she took this upward now it would not be a fix. It would be a file, a named challenge, a record with her own access badge all over it. Teresa would see it. Maybe she had to. Maybe that was next. But something in Marisol had already started moving sideways, away from permission. The folded docket crackled when she shifted her grip. Ivo had bought her ten minutes. Paloma had taken the cleanest audit trail out of her hand. Neither of them had asked for the truth in words, which made the debt feel heavier, not lighter.

The property room sat thirty feet down the hall behind a wire-glass door with a keypad most supervisors knew by feel. She knew which digits stuck in humid weather and which one needed the nail, not the pad. Inside, the air was stale with plastic, laundry powder, old pockets. Metal shelving ran to the back wall. Clear bags hung from hooks or lay in bins under car numbers: belts, phones with batteries taped to them, medicine bottles, cheap jewelry gone gray at the clasp, one pair of dentures in a cloudy cup, a canvas shoe with cartoon stars on the toe. Beside the door a movement ledger hung from a clipboard under a dead barcode wand. Anything pulled for manifest confirmation was supposed to be scanned out or signed by hand, because the wand missed half its uploads once the weather turned wet. Its violence was domestic: the shaved-off household matter of a life the state wanted portable.

Marisol let the door close softly behind her. She listened first. There were no steps in the corridor and no voices close, only the refrigeration unit kicking on in the adjoining records closet and the far murmur of the junction beyond the wall.

She found Car Eight's overflow bin where intake had shelved the unreturned electronics and paper items pending southbound manifest confirmation. The tags were in numerical order until they weren't. She moved them with the same hands she had used for twenty years to sort what could travel with a body and what had to be logged away from it. Her own breathing sounded too loud in the room.

Then she found 4471-R.

The bag was lighter than she had expected, a clear zip pouch with a barcode sticker curling at one corner. Inside sat the recorder she had seen hanging in intake, matte black, thumb-sized, with its batteries taped in a smaller inner sleeve and a strip of masking tape on the back carrying Gabriel's block print: NORTH / JUNE / WITNESS 3. The writing undid her more than the machine did. He had always labeled things as if disorder were a personal insult. As a boy he wrote his name on cassette cases, on school notebooks, once on the underside of a kitchen chair because he said everything in the apartment migrated when she was at work and somebody had to mark the survivors.

Marisol slid the bag onto the metal shelf and held still until the tremor left her fingertips. This was theft. The route had a thousand words for seizure and none for return.

She peeled the tape strip back from the battery sleeve with painful care, the adhesive making a tiny dry sound in the room. She took the batteries first and put them in the coin pocket of her work pants. Then she opened the larger zip, removed the recorder, and held it in her palm. It was warmer than the room from trapped heat in the plastic, absurdly light for something that had already pushed her out of the last safe excuse. It was a field tool, a witness box small enough to disappear in a closed fist.

The handle clicked before she could rehang the bag.

"Supervisor?"

The voice came through the wire glass, male, bored, close. "You in property? Records needs the Car Eight overflow sheet before they lock."

Marisol flattened herself against the shelving, recorder in hand. Beside the door the movement ledger hung open under the dead barcode wand, blank beside 4471-R. If he opened up, he would see the pouch gaping on the shelf and her with contraband in her fist.

"Battery sleeve split on me," she said. "Stay out unless you want caustic on your hands."

A muttered curse. "You need decon?"

"I need two minutes and a red drum."

His steps went off at once.

Marisol moved before the sound had cleared. She shoved the empty sleeve back into the pouch, looped an orange hazard tie through the zipper, and yanked open the drawer under the scanner cradle until she found a swollen handset battery wrapped in old evidence tape. In the movement ledger she wrote 4471-R / BATTERY LEAK / ISOLATED PENDING DISPOSAL and set her initials beside the time. Then she tore the Car Eight overflow sheet off the clip and folded it once. The lie would buy one glance, maybe two. It would also put her badge, her hand, and a false hazard call on the discrepancy when morning inventory caught up.

When the porter came back rolling the red drum, she had the pouch rehung and the scrap battery pinched inside the torn sleeve. She opened the door only wide enough to drop it in and pass him the overflow sheet through the gap.

"Tag that bin and leave the shelf alone till day crew," she said.

He looked at the sleeve, then at her face, and did what most workers did when given a chance to avoid extra contamination: he chose distance. "Copy, Supervisor."

She shut the door before he could ask anything else.

She tucked the recorder inside the waistband of her work pants, under her shirt, where the hard rectangle rested against the soft lower belly she still thought of as the part of her body Gabriel had once occupied without asking permission. The thought came and she nearly flinched from it. She shut the property-room light off, keyed the door behind her, and walked back into the corridor at the pace of a woman who had completed a boring errand.

On the platform side window, dusk had gone from yellow to a flat iron blue. A commuter train pulled out, taking its clean passengers home. Farther down the yard she could see Ivo near the edge of the maintenance lane, one shoulder against a post, phone to his ear at last. He was listening more than speaking, head bent, the posture Marisol had seen once before when Gabriel was ten and had split his chin on a sink and Ivo had stood outside the clinic calling his daughter to explain why he'd miss dinner. In the trauma-room corridor on the other side, Paloma was setting the little cups on a sill under a light, turning the leaves toward whatever weak brightness the building gave.

The recorder pressed against Marisol with each step, small and undeniable. The hotline had given her a ticket number and nothing else. The machine at her waist had weight. It could be carried. It could be hidden. It had survived somebody else's hands. By the time she started up to the records alcove, it had already warmed to her body.
