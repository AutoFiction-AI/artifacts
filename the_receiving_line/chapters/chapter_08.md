# Chapter 8

At the sealed tier-three door, the amber light on Marisol's downgraded passcard blinked twice before the custody officer lost patience and slapped his own badge over the plate. The lock released with a flat metal cough. Heat pushed out of the seam hard enough to salt the inside of her nose. Somebody had already vomited. Bleach sat over it and did not win.

The announcement was still repeating in the car's clipped synthetic English and Spanish, the same four lines cycling over the ceiling speakers until the words stopped meaning anything except that they were happening to bodies: full biometric reconciliation, aisle compliance, prepare tags, remove nothing from your berth. The floor thrummed under her boots with the long steady vibration of southbound speed. The car swayed left, then right, and the portable scan units answered with little irritated chirps every time their handlers had to reset their stance.

This was Teresa's answer to the audit discrepancies: scan the whole car again, wash the paperwork through a second machine, and call it truth.

Marisol stepped in sideways because a line of knees already clogged the first third of the aisle. The air was body-hot, wet with old breath and overheated plastic. Children had been pulled down from upper bunks too early and were now drooping against mothers' thighs or staring at the scanners with the glassy patience of the over-tired. Men in wrist restraints sat along the right side under the windows, tags hanging from their necks. Families and overflow detainees were stacked into fold-down bunks on the left, three high, blankets kicked into ropes. White paint had split along the berth seams and rust showed through in freckles and tears.

Gabriel was four rows in, on the right, one wrist clipped to a floor ring because Teresa's audit had promoted him from inconvenient son to known problem. Fresh yellowing bruises sat under the darker ones on his ribs where the intake team had grabbed him days ago. He had lost more weight in one week than Marisol wanted to count. When he looked at her he did not look surprised to see route supervision present for the rescan, only tired and alert at the same time, already reading the order in the room before it reached her in words.

Across from him, berth 8-C, Naila Quispe was sitting cross-legged on the lower bunk with her back against the wall panel and a strip of silver wrapper flattened over her thigh. Someone had once printed tropical fruit on it in cheerful orange. She had folded the strip into narrow lengths and tied little overhand knots down the middle, one after another, enough to make a rough counting cord. Two fingernails were missing from her left hand, old damage, the nail beds grown over shiny and pale. She kept touching the knots with her thumb, not nervously, just checking where she was.

She had also counted the room faster than anyone else.

Marisol saw it happen in the first twenty seconds. Naila watched the guards instead of the scanner. She watched which one favored his right knee when the car lurched, which one had his baton already unsnapped, which handheld reader was still wrapped in dock plastic because nobody had finished the setup before they rolled. She tipped her chin once toward the backup unit, once toward the sweating hands of the first detainee in line, then toward the vent above the door that was blowing air too warm to dry anything.

"Left reader misses more," Gabriel said, his voice low enough to pass for a cough.

Naila did not turn her head. "When the car leans east."

"That's not east," said the young woman with the toddler at her knees, because fear made some people precise about stupid things. She had a cherry-red plastic comb shoved sideways through her braid and a stain of mashed banana dried on the shoulder of her shirt. Her little boy was barefoot except for one sock with the fox rubbed nearly blank at the heel. He kept trying to put his fingers into the oval holes of the berth ladder.

Naila glanced at him and hooked the ladder one rung higher with her foot so his hand could not get pinched if the car jolted. "Then when it leans window-side," she said. "Keep his hands off the metal."

Marisol knew better than to step into a car full of people and imagine silence meant passivity. Silence was work. Silence was counting. She also knew what a rescan in a moving car could do once fear got traction. The aisle was too narrow for clean force and too crowded for clean anything. If the restraint team had to come in shields first, somebody would crack a skull on the berth frame before they even reached the back rows.

"This doesn't need to turn into a scene," she said, keeping her voice pitched for the nearest bunks and the nearest guard at once. "Tags ready. Hands dry if you can. We get through it and we're done."

Naila looked up then, finally, and Marisol understood why Gabriel had been listening to her. There was nothing theatrical in the girl's face, no hunger for martyrdom, only stillness and, behind it, an arithmetic that had already run ahead.

"Hands dry?" Naila said.

She lifted her palm. Sweat shone in the center line.

"You felt the air in here."

The first scan failed with an angry beep. The guard holding the reader cursed and wiped the thumb plate on his sleeve. The detainee, an old man with one lens missing from his glasses, flinched before he realized nobody had hit him. A child laughed once, too sharp, then covered her own mouth because everyone else was quiet.

Naila bent to the woman with the fox-sock boy. Her voice dropped lower. Marisol only caught pieces.

"Not first."

"When I touch your shoe."

"Sit heavy. Not sideways."

The wrapper cord vanished into the woman's fist. A second strip of foil passed upward without hands seeming to move. Gabriel said nothing. He did not need to. He was watching the aisle with the fixed, recording attention he gave destination codes off freight.

Marisol moved closer to him under the pretext of checking restraint spacing. "Don't start this in motion," she said.

Gabriel gave her the shortest look. "They did."

"You know what I mean."

"I know exactly what you mean."

Up front the second scan failed too. The reader chirped again. The guard slapped the side of the device. His partner, a narrow man with sweat moons under the arms of his vest, barked the next berth number louder than he needed to. Volume would not dry skin or clean the audit trail Teresa was scrubbing in real time.

Naila slid off the bunk when her number came. She did it so neatly, one foot then the other, that Marisol thought for a split second she had abandoned whatever she was calculating. Then Naila put both knees down in the middle of the aisle and sat back on her heels.

She settled there with the plain economy of someone setting a load where it would hold. At the narrowest point between the first scanner and the next row of bunks, she laid her hands flat on the tread plate.

"Stand," said the guard with the reader.

Naila shook her head once. "No."

He reached for her elbow. Before his fingers closed, the young mother with the comb sat too, one arm behind her for balance, the fox-sock boy shoved up against the bunk support behind her knees. Then the old man with the broken glasses lowered himself more slowly than either of them, muttering because his left hip did not want to bend. Then two boys from the upper berth slid down and folded themselves cross-legged by the drain seam. Somebody in the back said, "Here too," not loud, just enough, and bodies started dropping in place along the aisle the way sacks settle when a load strap is cut.

The effect was worse than shouting. Shouting gave security a single source. This turned the whole car into an obstruction.

The guards stopped pretending it was one girl with bad timing. One unclipped his baton. Another hit the intercom tab at his shoulder and said, "Car C aisle refusal, multiple noncompliant, request extraction team."

Marisol's mouth went dry in the wrong place, at the back by the hinge of the jaw, the place bleach fumes always caught after too many hours in the tanks. She could feel her left knee already calculating the narrow turns she would need if the team came in fast.

"Nobody touch anyone yet," she said.

The guard nearest her gave a little incredulous laugh. "Supervisor, that decision went by you."

Maybe it had. Maybe that was the point.

The synthetic announcement cut off. For one clean second the car held only the floor-vibration and the breath of eighty people trying not to waste air. Then Teresa's real voice came over the speaker, polished even through distortion.

"This is Director Ordonez. Aisles must remain clear for verified custody movement. You are creating avoidable risk for yourselves and for minors in the car. Stand now and the review remains administrative."

Nobody answered her. Naila kept one hand on the floor and lifted the other just enough to tap the tread plate twice. Marisol heard it pass back in metal through the berth frames. Two more people sat, and then three more did.

Administrative, Marisol thought. Teresa kept choosing the noun to make a baton in a sealed car sound like paperwork.

She looked at the door, at the custody officer waiting outside it, at the red status light already live above the frame. There was still time to keep the restraint team out if she could turn delay into procedure before Teresa turned procedure into force.

Time was what she knew how to buy.

* * *

By the time Teresa reached the car door, the refusal had climbed almost halfway back through the bunks.

Marisol made herself the bottleneck before Teresa could make herself the solution. She rolled the sanitation caddy crosswise into the entry lane, yanked the lid off the top compartment, and splashed two inches of stale bleach solution across the threshold grate with enough wasteful urgency to make the threshold look contaminated. The smell hit at once, hard and false-clean. Then she crouched and started unscrewing the drain cover with her multi-tool, giving the grate the fussy attention she reserved for a failure point that could take off a foot.

It bought her twenty seconds before Teresa realized she was not hurrying.

Teresa appeared in the doorway with her tablet in one hand and her reading glasses shoved up into her hair, where she had plainly forgotten they were. Her uniform looked impossibly dry for this part of the train. Behind her, three restraint officers in white hard-shell vests waited with shields stacked against their thighs. One of them was chewing clove gum. Marisol could smell it even through the bleach.

"Supervisor Anaya," Teresa said. "Move that cart."

Marisol did not look up. "Threshold grate is loose on the right bolt. If a shield edge catches it, somebody loses a foot."

"Is that documented?"

"It is now."

Teresa's gaze flicked to the open compartment, the tool in Marisol's hand, the acid-white puddle feathering out over the metal lip. When she spoke again her voice was still level, but the consonants had hardened.

"You were instructed to witness a reconciliation event, not create a contamination theater."

"This car has two children on floor level, one toddler, three elderly bodies with mobility problems, and a live scanner error rate high enough to trigger duplicate reads. If you run a charge through here, you will be lifting people by whatever comes away first."

Teresa stepped closer. "Then clear the aisle."

"Give me five minutes."

"For what."

That was Teresa at her coldest: not a question, but a demand that the next sentence declare itself worth existing.

Marisol straightened enough to face her. "For the heat to come down, for those readers to stop throwing false misses, and for me to get the mothers standing without you cracking a child against the bunks."

Teresa's eyes moved past her into the car. She was good at that, Marisol had to give her that much. She saw the room in terms of liability spread: where the camera would catch a bruise, which injuries would look accidental if the report language stayed narrow, and which worker in the doorway could still be made to absorb the blame.

"You are visible in this obstruction," Teresa said. "Think carefully about whether that is your intention."

Behind Marisol, somebody farther back began whispering berth numbers in Spanish. The numbers moved like water under a door. Naila had her head turned now, not toward the front, but toward the families on the upper bunks. She kept touching shoulder, shoe, rail. A boy with a split lip already swelling from something earlier climbed down and sat beside the old man with the broken glasses. Gabriel shifted in his floor clip and said, not to Marisol, not to Teresa, "Close the gaps. Don't leave holes for boots."

The line tightened. People on the bunks started folding their legs in instead of leaving them dangling into the aisle, making room for the next body to sit. Someone passed back a half-empty water pouch. Someone else tucked a sleeping baby higher into the crook of a blanket so she would not roll.

There was no speech, no chant, only labor.

Marisol understood then that whatever was happening had already moved past her. She could affect timing. She could narrow the shape of harm, maybe. Leadership had already passed into the room's own hands.

That should have made the next choice easier. It did not.

If she stood aside and let Teresa send the restraint team in now, the car would break all at once. If she openly took the side of the refusal, there would be witnesses in the room but not outside it, and RailPassage would call the whole thing coordinated employee misconduct before the blood dried. If she stalled and stalled and stalled, maybe the order would climb high enough to get deferred to Caldera, where there were junction cameras, cafeteria workers, maintenance crews, commuters crossing the north platform. It would not be justice or even safety, only more eyes, more angles, and less ownership.

It was a thin plan. She took it because it was the only one she had in reach.

"You want this car readable?" she said to Teresa. "Then stop heating it up. Those face lenses can't hold a lock if the people in front of them are sweating into the scan ring and the car is pitching."

"The train is not pitching."

The car rolled through a turnout and the whole corridor gave a short ugly heave. One shield banged the door frame. The officer with the clove gum swore and reset his footing.

Marisol pointed at him. "That. Exactly that."

Teresa's mouth flattened. "Move the cart."

"Five minutes."

"You are not in a position to bargain."

"I'm not bargaining. I'm telling you what breaks first."

The custody officer by the door shifted, waiting for Teresa's nod. Marisol saw it in the corner of her eye and dropped the drain cover back into place with more force than needed. The clang rang through the car like a dinner pan in a prison tier. Heads turned, which was good. She wanted them to see her, and she wanted Teresa to see them seeing her.

Marisol lifted her voice, not toward Teresa now, but toward the seated line.

"Anybody with a child, keep the child up on the bunks if you can. Keep fingers away from the floor seam. Shoes on. No loose straps in the aisle."

It sounded like crowd control because it was crowd control. It also gave the refusal a minute more to organize itself around protecting smaller bodies. The young mother with the comb hauled her little boy backward between her calves and shoved his one good sock onto his foot with jerky angry motions. Naila nodded once at Marisol, not gratitude, not alliance, simply acknowledgment that the instruction was usable.

Teresa heard it too.

"That is enough," she said.

She put her glasses back on with one irritated motion, though they had been in her hair the whole time, and read from the tablet in the scrubbed cadence she used when she wanted language to disinfect what was about to happen.

"This is your final instruction. Clear the transport path or corrective extraction will proceed under family-preservation protocol and medical review."

"Family-preservation," Gabriel said from the restraint ring, and laughed once, short and ugly.

Teresa did not look at him. "Mr. Avelar, you will remain silent."

"Wrong name."

One of the officers took a step forward. Marisol rolled the sanitation caddy another six inches into the lane so he had to stop or knock it over.

"Don't do this in here," she said.

Now Teresa did swear, the first crack in the shell. The word came out low and private, escaping because reality had finally stopped honoring her vocabulary.

"Move it, Marisol."

For a second Marisol thought Teresa might actually say her full name the old way, the sponsor's way, the way she used to when a younger Marisol had been useful and grateful and too busy rising to ask what she was rising inside. Instead Teresa handed the tablet to the custody officer, extended one immaculate palm, and said to the restraint team, "Minimal tools. Pull from the front. Get the aisle open."

The white-vested officers raised their shields.

From the floor, Naila spoke for the first time at full hearing distance.

"Heads down," she said. "Children high. Lock your arms."

Then the team came in.

* * *

The first hard sound was a shield cracking against a berth edge, polycarbonate on steel because the entry lane was too narrow for a clean line. The second sound was the sanitation caddy tipping and spilling gray mop water over everybody's boots. Then the batons started hitting metal, bench legs, hands, whatever moved in front of them.

Marisol grabbed the fallen cart by one handle and yanked it aside before someone went over it face-first. The officer with clove gum shoved past her with his shield up at throat height. She hit the side of it with her forearm and felt the shock all the way into the old chemical burn on her wrist. Another officer came lower, driving the shield into knees. Bodies folded. Somebody screamed immediately. Somebody else started praying in a language Marisol did not know.

"Lift them," one of the guards shouted.

"You can't lift a packed aisle," Marisol shouted back. "You'll stack them."

No one listened because the point had already stopped being movement. The point was to teach a lesson.

Naila had wrapped one arm through the bunk ladder and the other around the old man's shoulders to keep him from taking the shield edge full in the chest. The missing nails on her left hand flashed wetly as she tightened her grip. She did not kick or swing. She made herself harder to separate. That alone enraged them. The officer in front drove the shield into her shoulder once, twice, trying to break the hook of her arm. She kept her head tucked and said, "Hold," so quietly Marisol almost missed it under the noise.

The young mother with the comb had turned half around, making a wall with her own body so the fox-sock boy could stay behind her legs. When the shield hit her, she slid but did not let go of the berth post. The officer with the baton struck down at her wrist, not wild, not blind, precisely where her grip was.

Bone made a small thick sound under the skin. Her hand went sideways.

She screamed from somewhere so deep in the body it sounded torn out rather than produced. The boy began shrieking too, not words, just the raw animal note children hit when they have seen a body become wrong in front of them. Her broken hand bounced once against the tread plate, fingers still trying to close on nothing. The cherry comb fell out of her braid and skittered under the bench.

Marisol dropped to her knees beside them before she had thought it through. The tread plate was hot through the fabric of her trousers. She shoved the child backward onto the lower bunk, got one hand under his ribs and one behind his head, and yelled, "Keep him up. Keep him off the floor. Don't let him touch her wrist."

The mother tried to curl around the arm and could not. Vomit came up pink and stringy and splashed onto Marisol's sleeve.

Above them the charge had already gone jagged. One officer was trying to drag two seated boys apart by the backs of their collars and succeeded only in slamming their faces into each other. The narrow guard with the sweat moons had his baton caught in a blanket knot and was cursing as an elderly woman bit his thumb through the glove. A scanner went down and skated under the bunks, still beeping under there with petty faith in administrative accuracy.

Gabriel had gotten one foot under him against the restraint ring and was hauling uselessly at the clip on his wrist. "Take me," he shouted. "If you want the organizer, take me."

A guard backhanded him across the mouth for that. Blood jumped bright onto his chin.

Marisol did the ugliest thing first because it was there. She pried the young mother's good hand away from the broken one and pinned both elbows against the berth support so the woman would not keep wringing the fracture into itself. "Look at me," she said. "Look at me. Breathe on the count. One, two, three, out. Again."

The woman's eyes were wide and furious and almost gone with pain. "My son."

"He's here."

"Don't let them."

"I know."

It was not enough to know.

A shield edge clipped Marisol's shoulder blade hard enough to drive her into the berth frame. Sparks ran up her neck. She heard Teresa at the doorway talking about proportional response and line of movement in the tidy cadence of a meeting. Then Naila cried out for the first time.

Marisol turned in time to see the officer with the clove gum wrench Naila's arm free of the ladder and fling her sideways. She hit the opposite berth with the side of her head. The metal lip opened the skin above her eyebrow in a fast clean line. Blood came at once, sheeted into her eye. She tried to push herself up. The officer struck her with the baton across the upper back and then again across the shoulder when she did not flatten fast enough.

That was when people stopped trying to remain seated and started trying not to let others be taken.

The aisle became one body with too many elbows. Hands came out from under benches. Someone grabbed a shield rim from behind. Someone else threw the half-empty water pouch and it burst against a visor, making the officer flinch and jerk his head away, blinded for one stupid second by plain water. The old man with the broken glasses went over onto his side and disappeared under knees. A teenage girl on the upper bunk started kicking the wall with both heels, not to injure anyone, just to make noise, terrible noise, enough to wreck command.

Marisol lunged for Naila because Naila was closest to the boots now. Her knee failed halfway down. Pain shot up the familiar metal-stair path from kneecap to hip and she nearly pitched onto the floor herself. She caught the berth rail with one hand, shoved with the other, and got her body over Naila's ribs just as a guard came forward to stamp the girl's wrist loose from the ladder rung she had found again by instinct.

The boot hit Marisol's thigh instead.

"Supervisor," the guard said, shocked enough to hesitate.

"That's a medic load now," Marisol barked, pure command voice, the one that had once moved crews through blackwater spills and burst valves. "Back off if you don't want brain injury on your incident sheet."

It worked for one beat because it was work language and because she was old enough in the corridor to still sound official when she chose the right verbs.

One beat was all she got.

Teresa saw her covering Naila and understood the scene for what it would look like later. "Pull Anaya clear," she said at once.

Hands grabbed Marisol under the arms. The chemical burn scar on her wrist scraped against a shield edge and lit up white-hot. She kicked back, connected with somebody's shin, and heard a grunt. Then she was on her feet, dragged two steps toward the front while the whole car rocked under the weight shift of too many struggling bodies.

In those two steps she saw enough to know what her delay had purchased: preparation.

The team had come in with more shields than the first call required. The custody officer in the doorway was already filming body positions on Teresa's tablet. The back rows, who might have stayed confused if the charge had come earlier, had used the time to join and were now taking the same blows in larger number. The children had mostly made it onto the bunks because Marisol had yelled early, and for that she would be grateful later in the cheap private accounting that let people survive work like this. But the smallest bodies were still not safe. They were simply seeing everything from a slightly higher angle.

Gabriel was shouting her name now, not Mama, not anything soft enough to let her hide. "Marisol. Marisol."

He was handing her witness, the one task she could still do right.

The officer holding her loosened his grip because the car lurched again. She twisted free and shoved back into the aisle, not toward Teresa but toward the old man, who had finally been dragged clear enough to breathe but not clear enough to sit, toward the mother, whose boy was trying to crawl off the bunk to get to her, and toward Naila, who had rolled onto one elbow and was blinking blood out of one eye while keeping the other on the people behind her, still counting even now.

"Under the seats," Naila said hoarsely to the children. "When they swing, under."

She stayed practical to the last inch.

By the time the restraint team got the front half of the aisle nominally open, the floor looked like a butcher's sink after a rushed rinse. There was water, blood, vomit, and silver wrapper strips mashed flat under boots. The portable scanner had been retrieved and wiped on a guard's pant leg. Teresa gave the order to hold position, not to stop. The distinction sat in the car like another weapon.

People were crying now, some from pain, some from the humiliation of being made audible. The refusal had not broken cleanly. It had been beaten into pieces.

Marisol stood in the middle of it with somebody else's blood drying tacky across her forearm and knew with perfect accuracy how RailPassage would code this by nightfall: transient noncompliance, minor corrective force, one family-preservation injury, maybe two if Paloma refused the downgrade hard enough.

* * *

Paloma came in six minutes later with a trauma bag, a splint roll, and her silvering braid half out of its tie from running the length of the train. Camphor and disinfectant came with her. So did her right-hand tremor, small but visible when she snapped on gloves. She took one look at the aisle, found the bodies losing blood and air first, and braced the tremor by pinning the trauma bag against her thigh.

"Who can't breathe?" she said.

No one answered first because everybody thought the answer had to be somebody else.

Paloma pointed. "You. Blue shirt. Ask him again in Spanish. You, move your boot. If she vomits on her back I will let her aspirate in your custody report and use your full name."

The guard moved his boot.

Marisol got the fox-sock boy off the berth and onto her hip because he had finally gone limp enough to cling rather than thrash. He smelled of sweat, old formula, and the sweet rot of hot upholstery. His mother, Celia, lay against the bunk support with her broken wrist cradled in the crook of her shirt. The deformity was obvious now, the hand dropped wrong at the joint, swelling fast around the intake band. Each pulse seemed to pump the skin tighter.

"Her name is Celia," Marisol said.

Paloma's eyes flicked up once, and that was good; Celia had become a name, not a category.

"Celia," Paloma repeated, crouching. "Look at me, querida. I'm going to touch the wrist. I know. I know."

Celia's lips peeled back from her teeth. "Don't let them take him."

"Nobody is taking him from my sight for the next minute," Paloma said. Then, because fear had tipped over into emergency and her stress had roughened her speech exactly the way Marisol knew it would, she added, "And if anybody tries, they can fight me for him."

The boy buried his face in Marisol's neck. His remaining fox sock dragged against her hip. She bounced him once, awkwardly, because it had been years since she had held a child that size and because everything old in the body came back stupidly fast. One of his tears ran down under her collar. She felt it cooling against the heat trapped in her uniform.

Across the aisle, Naila sat propped against the berth frame while blood streamed from above her eyebrow and down the side of her nose. It had pasted her cropped hair to her temple. Somebody had tried to hand her a wad of blanket to hold to the cut, but she had given it instead to the old man with the broken glasses, who was using it on his mouth where a tooth had sliced him from inside. Even now she was not hoarding what worked.

Marisol crossed to her when Paloma had Celia's arm stabilized between her knees.

"Hold this," Paloma said without looking up, and jammed a packet of gauze into Marisol's free hand.

Marisol crouched in front of Naila. Close, the girl looked younger and older at the same time. She was nineteen in the bones of the face, older in the way she had already learned not to waste words on pain that would not stop because it was named correctly.

"Press here," Marisol said, folding the gauze over the cut.

Naila did not take it. "The old man first."

"He's got pressure."

"He'll swallow blood."

"So will you."

Naila considered that, then let Marisol press the gauze in place. Her skin was hot as pipe metal left in sun. Under the blood and sweat Marisol caught a faint smell of sugar from the fruit wrapper the girl must have hidden earlier, saving sweetness for the right moment or the right child.

"You saw them stack outside," Naila said.

She stated the fact flatly.

"Yes."

"Next time count the door, not the scanner."

Then she closed her good eye and turned her face into Marisol's hand, not from trust, Marisol thought, but because pressure hurt less than bleeding.

Teresa was still at the front of the car, now dictating into the tablet for the incident log. Her glasses had slipped down her nose. One cuticle bled where she had worried it open without noticing.

"Corrective entry executed after repeated lawful instruction," she said. "Medical contractor on scene. Juvenile bodies preserved from trampling by immediate staff intervention. Rescan to resume in controlled order once the aisle is secured."

Gabriel laughed again, then spat blood onto the floor by his own boot.

"Controlled order," he said. "There it is."

Teresa turned her head at last. "Mr. Avelar, one more disruption and you will be hooded for the remainder of the segment."

"Then call me by the right name while you do it."

His lip was split. Blood had dried dark in the scar through his eyebrow and fresh blood lay brighter over it, a bad copy over old record. One sleeve had ridden up where he had fought the floor ring, exposing purple restraint marks around the wrist bone. Marisol saw Teresa register all of that only as future wording. Subject became verbally escalatory. Subject resisted correction. Subject required additional stabilization.

Teresa looked at Marisol instead. "You will submit your incident statement before end of shift."

Marisol wiped Celia's son's tears off her collar with the back of her wrist and left the smear there. "You already have one."

"I have the start of one."

"You have the whole thing."

Teresa's expression did not change, but she took half a step nearer, lowering her voice so the car would have to strain to hear. "Do not confuse proximity to suffering with innocence in it."

That landed because it was true enough to bruise. Marisol had proxied suffering for twenty years. She had cleaned it, coded it, vented it, bleached it, signed for it, made it move. She might have said something useful back if Gabriel had not spoken first.

"She knows," he said.

Silence opened a small hard space around the words.

Paloma was taping Celia's wrist to a molded splint now, her movements quick and brutal because gentleness would have taken longer and hurt more. The old man with the broken glasses was breathing through his mouth and holding the blanket to his lip. Naila had one hand over the gauze at her brow and the other flattened over the wrapper knots in her lap, beaten bloody and still unwilling to lose count.

Gabriel shifted against the restraint ring and looked directly at Marisol.

"You keep thinking time is mercy," he said.

His voice was not raised. That made everyone hear it.

"It isn't. Not if they still own the door."

Marisol opened her mouth, closed it, and opened it again.

"I was trying to keep them out until Caldera."

"For who."

"For witnesses. For cameras. For a chance that this would not stay in their file language."

Gabriel nodded once, blood on his teeth. "And in the meantime?"

He tipped his chin toward Celia, toward Naila, toward the old man with the blanket to his mouth, making her look where she was already looking.

"In the meantime they lined up outside, got their shields set, chose where to hit, and called it preservation. You bought them organization. You bought them a better report. If they still own the room, the delay is part of the beating."

No one in the car moved. Even Teresa did not interrupt, maybe because to stop him would make the line audible again, maybe because some part of her believed in letting people hear a truth right before she filed it under the wrong heading.

Marisol felt the words where she usually felt bad air pressure in a clogged pipe, the moment before backflow. Minutes were not neutral; they belonged to whoever held the locks, and she had let work teach her otherwise because otherwise was survivable.

"I kept the children off the floor," she said, and hated how small it sounded as soon as it existed.

Gabriel did not let her keep even that without measure. "Yes," he said. "You did. Good. They got to watch from higher up."

The boy on her hip gave a broken hiccup in his sleep and tightened his fingers in the fabric at her shoulder. Celia turned her face toward them, not fully conscious, following the sound of her son's breathing. Naila opened one eye and watched Marisol over the blood-caked edge of the gauze, not cruelly, which was worse.

Paloma rose with a grunt and stripped off one glove. "Celia needs the trauma room for films and pain control. Naila needs that eyebrow closed before it cakes dirt in. The old man needs his mouth checked for fragments. If you people are going to resume your holy scan, do it around me or do it in hell."

That, at least, stopped Teresa for another beat.

Outside the car, somewhere farther up the corridor, another announcement started for another compartment. It carried the same polished courtesy, the same two languages, the same request for orderly compliance. The sound came thin through the steel and made Marisol's skin crawl worse than shouting.

She looked down at the silver wrapper cord in Naila's lap, the little knots mashed but still countable. She looked at Celia's cherry comb lying under the bench where it had been kicked during the charge. She looked at the scanner on the seat, cleaned and ready to continue, one corner spider-cracked from the fall but still functional. That was the corridor's genius: it could leave damage everywhere and still preserve function.

Marisol knew how to scrub blood out of the tread plate before it darkened. She knew how much water to use if she wanted the pink to vanish without spreading. She knew which code on the incident form would bury a broken wrist under transport friction and which phrase would make a split brow sound like an unruly lunge into fixed infrastructure. She knew the smell of hot iron after a baton strike because the berth rails held it for minutes. She knew too much about how harm stayed operational.

In that moment the knowledge sat in her like contamination.

Teresa took back her tablet from the custody officer. "Medical transfer for three," she said. "Then we finish the reconciliation."

The pronoun landed wrong. Teresa was folding blood into process with that we.

Marisol bent and picked up Celia's red comb from under the bench. Two teeth were broken off it. She set it on the berth beside the sleeping boy so his mother would see it when the pain medication hit and she came back through the fog looking for proof of what was still hers.

Then she stood in the ruined aisle with the blood, the mop water, the wrapper foil, and the scanner chirping ready, and felt the old reflex reach for the caddy, the rag, the nearest lie that would make the damage look procedural. She let it pass. She left the pink water in the bucket, the cracked scanner on the seat, and Celia's broken comb in plain view beside the sleeping boy. When the scanner chirped again, Teresa had to step around the blood to reach it. Blood didn't stop the line.
